### Summary
# **Kroski, E. (2020). Why create digital breakouts? Opportunities for libraries. *Library Technology Reports, 56*(3), 8-9.**

Kroski's chapter is a short practitioner rationale for digital breakouts in libraries, published as chapter 2 of the *Library Technology Reports* issue *How to Create Free Digital Breakouts for Libraries*. The larger issue defines digital breakouts as online, escape-room-like experiences built with free web tools, lock forms, puzzles, clues, narratives, learning objectives, and virtual settings. Chapter 2 focuses specifically on why libraries might create them and where they fit within library service.

The central argument is that digital breakouts offer libraries a flexible way to inform, instruct, and engage patrons while still providing a game-like pastime. Kroski frames them as related to live escape room adventures, but with important differences: they are not bound to a physical room, they can often be played individually, they can be distributed widely online, and they can be built around a single subject, skill set, grade level, or learning objective. This makes them attractive for libraries that want the engagement of escape rooms without the space, staffing, props, and throughput constraints of live events.

The chapter names several library applications. For information literacy and bibliographic instruction, librarians can design puzzles that require players to locate catalog items, explore databases, identify fake news, find peer-reviewed articles, cite sources, and practice using library resources. For curriculum support, a breakout can be tailored to a class, discipline, school subject, or university course and can help librarians build faculty partnerships. For staff training, digital breakouts can replace or supplement handouts and presentations with an interactive, gamified training experience.

Kroski also emphasizes critical thinking and problem solving. Like escape rooms, digital breakouts require players to reason, make inferences, deduce patterns, try, fail, adjust, and try again. In the library context, this matters because the puzzles can make research and resource-use skills feel purposeful rather than procedural. The chapter further identifies outreach and marketing as service opportunities: because digital breakouts are portable and web-distributable, they can introduce patrons to library resources, services, events, and programs at little or no cost. Kroski also points to STEM learning, including coding and math puzzles, as a natural fit for inquiry-based online breakout design.

For the GBLS project, this source is useful because it extends the escape-room literature into a digital and low-cost format. It gives practical language for libraries that want to use game structures for instruction, outreach, curriculum support, youth programming, or staff learning but cannot host a physical escape room. It also connects well with implementation-oriented sources: a digital breakout still needs a narrative, a clear learning objective, appropriately difficult puzzles, testing, and assessment, but the format changes the operational constraints.

The limitations are straightforward. The chapter is not an empirical study, does not report participant outcomes, and makes some claims about retention and engagement without formal evidence. It should therefore be cited as a practitioner design rationale and service-model source rather than proof that digital breakouts improve information literacy or staff training. Its best use in the review is to show the range of breakout formats and the reasons libraries adopt them, while leaving effectiveness claims to studies with assessment data.
