# Summary

Ellyssa Kroski’s 2020 guide, *How to Create Free Digital Breakouts for Libraries*, published in *Library Technology Reports*, provides a comprehensive instructional framework for librarians to design and implement digital breakout games. The text defines digital breakouts as immersive online experiences analogous to physical escape rooms, utilizing free web-based tools to simulate locks, puzzles, and narratives. The primary objective is to equip library professionals with the technical skills to create these games for purposes ranging from information literacy instruction and curriculum support to staff training and community outreach.

The article does not present empirical data or a formal study; rather, it functions as a practical manual. Kroski outlines the six essential components of a digital breakout: a narrative backstory, locks (number, word, color, or directional), puzzles and challenges, specific learning objectives, a resolution with rewards, and a virtual setting. She argues that unlike live escape rooms, digital breakouts are not constrained by physical space or group size, making them highly scalable for individual patron engagement and efficient for teaching complex skills like database searching or citation practices.

The core of the text is a step-by-step technical walkthrough using the Google ecosystem. Kroski details how to establish Student Learning Outcomes (SLOs) to drive game design. She demonstrates how to configure Google Forms as "lock kits," utilizing response validation features to ensure players can only proceed by entering correct answers derived from solving puzzles. The guide covers various puzzle creation methods, including generating jigsaw puzzles via Jigsaw Planet, building interactive quizzes with logic jumps in Google Forms, and creating spreadsheet puzzles with conditional formatting in Google Sheets to reveal color codes. Additionally, the author lists numerous third-party resources for creating ciphers, hidden messages, and memory games.

Kroski provides concrete examples of breakouts created by other librarians to illustrate applicability across different library types. For instance, she cites a school library breakout designed to teach catalog navigation and another aimed at promoting diverse reading materials. The text emphasizes that these tools are cost-effective, requiring no budget expenditure beyond existing software access. The implications drawn are that gamification can increase information retention and patron engagement. By transforming static instruction into interactive problem-solving tasks, libraries can foster critical thinking and provide memorable learning experiences. The guide concludes with resources for further development, positioning digital breakouts as a versatile, low-cost strategy for modern library service delivery.
