# Summary

This article, published in *Against the Grain* in February 2019, is a philosophical opinion column by Jared Alexander Seay titled "Considering Games in Libraries and Such — Space Guardians: New Year’s Musings of How Libraries of Old (Including but Not Necessarily Old Librarians) Have Returned Just in Time to Save Us from Digital Anxiety." The piece does not present empirical research or technical data; rather, it offers a reflective narrative on the intersection of library services, analog media, and digital well-being.

The author begins by recounting a personal experience weeding 90% of his institution's print reference collection. While acknowledging the efficiency and searchability of online databases compared to physical encyclopedias, Seay expresses a sense of loss regarding the tactile satisfaction and "soothing book aroma" associated with browsing physical materials. He notes that while digital tools offer speed, they lack the sensory engagement of print, leading to a personal feeling of nostalgia for "librarian work" involving physical books.

Transitioning from this personal anecdote, Seay connects his feelings to a broader cultural trend observed during the holiday season. He describes listening to a board game podcast, *Shut Up and Sit Down*, which he found soothing. He interprets this reaction as evidence of a deeper yearning for tactile stimulation and face-to-face human interaction, contrasting these analog experiences with "digital drowning." Seay cites Douglas Rushkoff’s concept of "digiphrenia" (from the book *Present Shock*), defined as digitally provoked mental chaos resulting from the dissonance between digital selves and analog bodies. According to Seay, the resurgence of board games, vinyl records, and other analog media is a societal response to this information overload and anxiety.

The core argument of the article posits that libraries are ideally situated to capitalize on this trend. Seay references Rushkoff’s suggestion that society needs "safe spaces for uninterrupted contemplation" to mitigate digiphrenia. He argues that while coffee shops and board game cafés are emerging to fill this niche, libraries have a "three thousand year head start" in providing safe, comforting gathering places. By integrating board games and makerspaces into their collections, libraries can satisfy the public's longing for analog interaction and community engagement.

Seay concludes by suggesting that librarians are becoming relevant again by acting as "guardians of analog." He speculates that the popularity of analog games might eventually drive a resurgence in print books, creating a "mega trend" that combines both forms of analog media. The article ends with a personal commitment to engaging with physical media and human company, framing these actions as positive, retro responses to modern digital stress.

In the context of Games-Based Library Services (GBLS), this article provides a theoretical and philosophical justification for integrating games into library collections. It frames board games not merely as recreational items but as essential tools for combating digital anxiety and fostering community connection. The author’s perspective supports the inclusion of analog games in libraries as a strategic response to contemporary societal needs, reinforcing the library's role as a physical sanctuary for human interaction in an increasingly digital world. However, readers should note that these claims are based on personal reflection and secondary citations rather than primary empirical evidence or statistical data regarding user behavior or outcomes.
