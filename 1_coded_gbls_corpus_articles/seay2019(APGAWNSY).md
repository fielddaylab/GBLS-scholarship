### Summary
# **Seay, J. A. (2019). Considering games in libraries and such: Space guardians: New Year's musings of how libraries of old (including but not necessarily old librarians) have returned just in time to save us from digital anxiety. *Against the Grain, 31*(1), 51.**

Seay's "Space Guardians" column is a reflective practitioner essay about libraries, analog media, board games, and digital anxiety. The Zotero metadata dates the item to 2021, but the attached PDF itself appears in the February 2019 issue of *Against the Grain*. The piece is not a research article or program report; it is a short philosophical column. Its usefulness for the GBLS project lies in the way it articulates one rationale for analog game services: board games and other tactile, co-present practices can function as a response to digital overload and a renewed need for shared physical spaces.

The column begins with Seay describing the experience of handling print reference books while weeding a reference collection. This becomes a meditation on what libraries gain and lose when reference use moves online. Online sources are faster and more searchable, but Seay emphasizes the sensory and contemplative qualities of browsing, touching books, turning pages, and moving through physical collections. That nostalgia is not the central GBLS point, but it prepares the larger argument: libraries remain important because they are not only access systems but also places where bodies, materials, attention, and people meet.

The gaming thread enters through Seay's holiday listening to a board game review podcast. He describes the idea of cross-table, face-to-face interaction, wooden blocks, dice, and tactile play as soothing during a break from digital media. The column connects this feeling to Seay's recurring argument that the board game resurgence reflects a broader longing for analog interaction. Board games are presented not simply as entertainment objects, but as media that restore slower, social, tactile, and embodied forms of attention.

Seay then draws on Douglas Rushkoff's *Present Shock* and the idea of "digiphrenia," or digitally provoked mental chaos. In Seay's reading, the resurgence of board games, vinyl records, analog practices, and physical library gathering spaces responds to information overload, constant digital immediacy, and the dissonance between digital selves and analog bodies. For GBLS, this is useful as practitioner language for why analog games have value even in technologically sophisticated library environments.

The column's library argument is that libraries have a long history as safe, contemplative, public, physical spaces. Seay suggests that coffee shops and board game cafes may be responding to social longing for analog activity, but libraries have a much deeper institutional history of offering gathering places for attention, reading, conversation, and shared cultural materials. Board game collections and makerspaces become part of this renewed analog mission. In a playful phrase, Seay imagines librarians as "guardians of analog."

For the GBLS review, this source supports the conceptual terrain rather than a program model. It helps explain why analog game services should not be treated as technologically backward. A board game collection, tabletop club, or game night may meet needs for attention, social connection, tactile engagement, and relief from digital saturation. It also strengthens the community-space argument: games can help libraries function as places where patrons gather around shared materials and interact face-to-face.

The limitations are clear. The piece is anecdotal, nostalgic, and speculative. It does not assess a board game program, survey patrons, or analyze library outcomes. It should therefore be used as a light practitioner framing source, not as evidence that analog games reduce anxiety or improve well-being. Its value is rhetorical and conceptual: it connects analog play, library space, and community presence in a way that complements more concrete sources on board game collections, recurring play groups, and library-as-commons services.
