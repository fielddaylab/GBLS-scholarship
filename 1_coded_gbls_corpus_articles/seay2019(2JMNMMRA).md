# Summary

This article, titled "Considering Games in Libraries and Such — On using a Game to Actually Teach Something. Part I: The Accidental Learner," is a reflective practitioner piece by Jared Alexander Seay, published in *Against the Grain* in November 2019. The text serves as an introductory commentary on the integration of games, specifically escape rooms, into library instruction and educational settings. It addresses the author's personal and professional contemplation regarding the efficacy of game-based pedagogy, moving from theoretical appreciation to practical application.

The primary objective of the article is to explore whether using games to teach actually works and to what degree. Seay acknowledges a "panic attack" or existential crisis often felt by educators when transitioning from theoretical support for gamification to the complex reality of implementation. He notes that while he has long extolled the virtues of games in teaching, the practical application presents significant challenges, such as balancing theme, content, and mechanics.

To investigate these challenges, Seay describes a pilot project rather than a formal empirical study. Earlier in the summer preceding publication, he was contracted to design an escape game for a Science Summer Camp. This opportunity provided a low-stakes environment to test game design principles without the immediate pressure of formal academic assessment. The target population consisted of science campers aged 11 to 17, divided into similar age groups. The theme of the camp was "Mars and Stars," focusing on space exploration and Martian colonization. Seay designed an escape room scenario centered on a crash landing on Mars, requiring participants to survive through problem-solving and cooperation.

The methodology was exploratory and anecdotal. Seay spent six weeks designing the activity, prioritizing "fun" and engagement over explicit instructional objectives for this specific instance. He notes that the goal was simply "fun," which allowed him to focus on the experiential aspects of game design. The data derived from this event consists of observational reflections and participant feedback indicating high enjoyment and engagement.

The findings reported are qualitative. Seay states that participants "had a blast" and learned significantly about Mars science, mission planning, and the dangers of space exploration. Crucially, he highlights the acquisition of soft skills, specifically cooperation and problem-solving under pressure. The central insight drawn from this experience is that "content may be king, but experience is the goddess." Seay argues that while content is integral to the core concept, it is the positive experience and emotional engagement that hook participants and facilitate learning. He references a public speaking quote about grabbing emotions before minds, suggesting that educational success depends on securing both "hearts and minds."

The implications for library services are significant. Seay concludes that game-based education can work effectively when the design prioritizes user experience. He posits that while learning itself may not always be fun, it is easier to learn while having fun. The article sets the stage for future applications in formal library instruction, with the author expressing a desire to apply these lessons to library skills training. The text ends by teasing Part II of the series, which will likely delve deeper into the specific mechanics and design processes used to integrate learning objectives with game elements. This piece contributes to the Games-Based Library Services literature by providing a practitioner’s perspective on the initial hurdles and rewards of implementing escape rooms in educational contexts, emphasizing the primacy of experiential design over pure content delivery.
