### Summary
# **Seay, J. A. (2019). Considering games in libraries and such: On using a game to actually teach something. Part I: The accidental learner. *Against the Grain, 31*(5), 72-80.**

Seay's "The Accidental Learner" is a reflective professional column about designing an escape-room-like learning game. The Zotero metadata dates the item to 2021, but the PDF appears in the November 2019 issue of *Against the Grain*. The article is not a formal study, but it is useful for GBLS because it addresses a practical question that often sits underneath game-based library services: does using a game to teach actually work, and how does a designer turn a traditional lesson into a playable learning experience?

Seay frames the column through his own uncertainty after receiving a small teaching grant to create escape rooms for teaching. He describes the moment when game-based pedagogy becomes real implementation: ideas that sound persuasive in theory must be turned into practical mechanics, themes, content, puzzles, and experiences. This makes the column useful as practitioner reflection on design labor. Game-based instruction is not just "make it fun"; it requires decisions about whether theme or content leads, how mechanics support learning, and how fun and instruction can be integrated.

The column's main example is an escape game created for a science summer camp with the theme "Mars and Stars." Because the activity was framed as a fun elective, Seay felt less pressure to prove learning outcomes, but he still designed it as more than mindless fun. The scenario became a crash-landing-on-Mars survival game. Participants were science campers aged roughly 11 to 17. The design used theater, theme, role-play, puzzles, cartography skills, "deep thinking," colorful lights, and the survival premise to create engagement.

The strongest design insight is Seay's distinction between content and experience. He argues that content may be central, but experience is what hooks participants and stays with them. Before designers can reach learners' minds, they often have to capture attention and emotion. In the Mars game, participants reportedly had fun and learned about the science, necessities, danger, and adventure involved in planning and executing a Mars mission. They also practiced cooperation and problem solving under pressure. For the GBLS project, this language is useful because it connects affect, memory, collaboration, and problem solving to game-based learning design.

The column also introduces the idea of accidental learning. Seay suggests that the game did not make learning fun by turning every fact into entertainment; rather, participants learned while having fun inside the larger experience. This distinction matters for library instruction. A good GBLS design may embed catalog searching, database use, source evaluation, or space navigation inside a meaningful scenario so that the learning is necessary to progress, rather than treating the learning content as a quiz pasted onto a theme.

For the GBLS review, this source pairs with Seay's "Ultimate Library Instruction" vignette and with stronger empirical studies of escape rooms. It provides a practitioner account of the transition from game enthusiasm to design practice and helps explain why reflection and transfer must be designed into game-based instruction. It also supports the idea that libraries may need prototypes, playtesting, and cross-domain examples before they can build effective library-specific games.

The limitations are substantial. The column does not include assessment data, sample size details beyond a broad age range, learning measures, or a replicable lesson plan. The Mars game was not a library program, though Seay explicitly frames it as preparation for library-skills instruction. The source should therefore be used as design reflection rather than evidence of effectiveness. Its main contribution is vocabulary for the craft problem of making learning playable without reducing either the learning or the play to decoration.
