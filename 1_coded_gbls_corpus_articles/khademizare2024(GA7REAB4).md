# Summary

This qualitative study by Khademi Zare, Varnaseri, and Bayati (2024) investigates the potential applications of gamification for attracting audiences to public libraries, with a specific contextual focus on Iran. The authors posit that despite adherence to international standards, public libraries face challenges in engaging diverse populations, particularly youth and women in rural areas, due to low awareness and utilization rates. To address this, the study aims to identify how gamification—a strategy involving the use of game design elements in non-game contexts—can enhance library services, foster community involvement, and improve competitive appeal in the digital era.

The methodology employed a qualitative approach, utilizing semi-structured interviews with experts in gamification and game design. Participants were selected through purposive and snowball sampling techniques, resulting in a final sample of 13 experts who had at least two years of experience in the industry. Data saturation was achieved to ensure comprehensive coverage of perspectives. The collected interview transcripts were analyzed using content analysis, following the six-step thematic process defined by Braun and Clarke (2006). This rigorous analytical framework allowed for the identification of patterns and themes related to gamification applications in library settings.

The findings revealed 16 main themes and 56 sub-themes categorizing the benefits and applications of gamification in public libraries. Key areas identified include promoting reading habits by creating enjoyable and motivating experiences; enhancing book lending rates through attractive processes and incentives; and improving user experience by simplifying administrative tasks such as membership registration and seat reservation. The study highlights that gamification can transform library spaces into interactive environments that foster social recognition, intrinsic motivation, and a sense of achievement. Specific applications include using badges and leaderboards to encourage reading, progress bars to visualize volunteer contributions, and ranking systems to promote responsible behavior regarding the timely return of borrowed resources.

Furthermore, the authors emphasize the role of gamification in marketing and brand loyalty. By leveraging user networks through invitation links and social sharing, libraries can expand their reach via word-of-mouth strategies. Gamification also supports educational activities by reducing learner fatigue and increasing engagement through team-building and reward mechanisms. The study suggests that implementing a dedicated digital platform or application is essential for effectively integrating these gamified elements, allowing for personalized interactions and real-time feedback.

The implications of this research are significant for library policymakers and practitioners. The authors argue that gamification can help libraries remain relevant by transforming them into dynamic hubs for cultural, scientific, and social activities. They recommend further empirical research to test the effectiveness of specific gamification prototypes in controlled settings, comparing outcomes such as engagement, knowledge retention, and user satisfaction against traditional non-gamified systems. While the study provides a robust theoretical framework and expert-derived applications, it acknowledges that the precise adaptation of game elements to local cultural contexts requires additional investigation. Overall, the article contributes to the growing body of literature on games-based library services by offering a detailed, expert-informed roadmap for implementing gamification to enhance audience attraction and service quality in public libraries.
