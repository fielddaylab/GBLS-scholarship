# Summary

The provided source text is not an empirical research article but rather a promotional and informational document associated with the Games Round Table (GameRT) of the American Library Association (ALA). Authored by Molly Porter, Amber Sewell, George Bergstrom, and Danielle Costello in 2024, the text functions as a resource guide for International Games Month (IGM). It does not present a hypothesis, methodology, or data analysis; therefore, it contains no empirical findings regarding games-based library services. Instead, its primary objective is to aggregate and disseminate links to relevant digital resources, community standards, and contact information for librarians interested in gaming initiatives.

The document begins by identifying the authors and the central theme: International Games Month. It provides a direct link to the official IGM website hosted on the ALA domain. Following this, it lists several "Game RT Articles," specifically referencing check-in posts and wrap-up reports from previous iterations of the event, including 2019 and 2020. These links suggest a historical continuity of the event and offer readers access to past reflections on its execution, particularly noting the transition to virtual formats in 2020.

A significant portion of the text is dedicated to showcasing "Selection of Past IGM Events." This section includes URLs to various external platforms and library-specific guides. Examples include an itch.io game jam, event pages from the Erie County Public Library, Middlesex Community College, Brookline Library, Valencia College, and Nottingham City Libraries. These links serve as practical examples of how different institutions have implemented games-based programming, ranging from digital game creation jams to physical library clubs. This curation provides a snapshot of diverse implementation strategies across academic, public, and international contexts, although the text itself does not analyze or compare these implementations.

The document also establishes community norms through a "GameRT Twitch Policy." This five-point policy outlines behavioral expectations for online interactions, emphasizing kindness, respect, and zero tolerance for hate speech. It grants moderators the authority to remove disruptive users and provides mechanisms for reporting harassment. This section highlights the importance of maintaining safe and inclusive digital spaces within library-led gaming communities, particularly on streaming platforms like Twitch.

Finally, the text offers multiple avenues for further engagement and support. It lists resources for the GameRT blog, Facebook groups, public resources, and the main website. A specific resource document link is provided, along with an email address (gamert@ala.org) for inquiries. The inclusion of these contact points underscores the role of the Games Round Table as a central hub for information and support for librarians involved in games-based services.

In the context of a literature review on Games-Based Library Services, this source is relevant not for empirical data but for its role as a primary resource directory. It illustrates the organizational infrastructure supporting IGM and provides direct access to case studies and community guidelines. However, it does not contribute original research findings, statistical data, or theoretical arguments regarding the efficacy or impact of games in libraries. Its value lies in its utility as a navigational tool for practitioners seeking to implement or learn about games-based programming.
