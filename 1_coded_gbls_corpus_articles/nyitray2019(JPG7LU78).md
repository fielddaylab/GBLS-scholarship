# Summary

Kristen J. Nyitray’s 2019 article, "Game On to Game After: Sources for Video Game History," serves as a bibliographic guide for librarians seeking to support research in video game studies. The author begins by contextualizing the ubiquity of video games, citing statistics that 60% of Americans play daily and noting the industry's significant economic impact. Nyitray defines video games broadly as interactive experiences displayed on screens, distinguishing early analog experiments like *Tennis for Two* from true video games such as *Computer Space* and *Pong*.

The article argues that video game historiography has evolved from simple chronological accounts aimed at younger audiences to critical, transdisciplinary studies. This shift recognizes games as complex artifacts converging computer science, arts, humanities, and social sciences. Nyitray emphasizes the importance of preserving diverse materials—including source code, hardware, corporate records, and ephemera—to support research into technological innovation and cultural heritage.

The core of the article is a curated list of resources categorized by type. In the **Reference** section, Nyitray highlights Leonard Herman’s *Phoenix IV* for its comprehensive chronological narrative and Brett Weiss’s multi-volume guides for their detailed documentation of home video games from 1972 to 1990. Matt Fox’s *The Video Games Guide* is noted for its evaluative overviews and thematic appendixes.

Under **Visual Histories**, the article points to Evan Amos’s *The Game Console* and Van Burnham’s *Supercade*, which utilize high-quality photography and primary source materials to document hardware evolution and industry innovators. The **Histories** section includes works like Dan Ackerman’s *The Tetris Effect* and Tristan Donovan’s *Replay*, the latter praised for its global perspective that counters "Americentric" biases. Steve L. Kent’s *The Ultimate History of Video Games* is acknowledged as a popular, accessible entry point despite its age.

The **Culture and Critical Studies** section features theoretical works by Tom Bissell, Ian Bogost, and Raiford Guins. These texts explore games as art forms, address the challenges of critiquing interactive media, and examine the "afterlife" of obsolete games. Jane McGonigal’s *Reality Is Broken* is included for its argument that gaming skills can be applied to solve societal problems.

In the **Industry** category, Nyitray reviews Blake J. Harris’s *Console Wars* and Jason Schreier’s *Blood, Sweat, and Pixels*, which detail corporate rivalries and development challenges. Carly A. Kocurek’s *Coin-Operated Americans* is highlighted for its analysis of gendered narratives in gaming. The **Art** and **Music** sections recommend resources that treat game design and soundtracks as significant cultural expressions, including Tim Lapetino’s *Art of Atari* and Michael Austin’s edited volume on music video games.

Finally, the article discusses **Digital Collections**, specifically the Learning Games Initiative Research Archive (LGIRA) and the Internet Archive Console Living Room. These initiatives provide access to vintage games through emulation and digital preservation, facilitating research without requiring physical hardware. Nyitray concludes by noting the growing academic infrastructure for game studies, including new university programs and publisher series, underscoring the need for libraries to maintain robust, diverse collections to meet increasing scholarly demand. The article does not present empirical data but rather synthesizes existing literature to guide collection development and reference services in the field of video game history.
