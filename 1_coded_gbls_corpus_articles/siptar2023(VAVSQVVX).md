# Summary

This article is a scholarly review of Sarah Pavey’s book *Playing Games in the School Library*, published in *Vjesnik bibliotekara Hrvatske* (Journal of Croatian Librarians). The reviewer, Dejan Šiptar, evaluates the book’s effectiveness as a practical guide for school librarians aiming to implement game-based learning and gamification strategies. The review is structured around a chapter-by-chapter analysis of Pavey’s work, assessing its theoretical foundations, practical examples, and contextual applicability.

The review begins by outlining the book’s structure, which includes a foreword by Richard Gerver, an introduction, and ten chapters. Pavey distinguishes between "learning through play" (game-based learning) and "gamification," noting that the latter does not necessarily involve games but rather game elements like points or leaderboards. The reviewer highlights Pavey’s integration of six pedagogical theories—behaviorism, cognitive constructivism, social constructivism, modeling, multiple intelligences, and brain-based learning—to ground her practical advice in established educational science.

A significant portion of the review focuses on the frameworks Pavey uses to evaluate game quality. The SAMR model (Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, Redefinition) is used to assess the technological integration of games, while the ARCS model (Attention, Relevance, Confidence, Satisfaction) and Bloom’s Taxonomy are applied to physical and mixed-media games. The reviewer notes that Pavey provides concrete examples for various game types: simple presentation-based games, digital games (including video games, coding, and social media), physical games (quizzes, card games), mixed-media games (using QR codes or GPS), and live-action role-playing games. These examples are drawn from librarians worldwide, offering a diverse range of practical applications.

However, the review critically addresses the book’s limitations regarding its contextual specificity. Pavey writes from the perspective of a UK school librarian, which introduces biases that may hinder applicability in other regions, such as Croatia. Specific issues include references to English language learners (relevant in the UK but less so in homogeneous Croatian classrooms, though noted as potentially relevant for Ukrainian refugees), the use of Dewey Decimal Classification, and recommendations to purchase equipment via Amazon. Furthermore, many recommended digital tools are not available in Croatian, posing a barrier for younger students.

Despite these limitations, Šiptar concludes that the book is a "very useful and practical manual." The theoretical clarity and global examples provide a strong foundation for librarians to adapt concepts to their local contexts. The review emphasizes that while direct replication of UK-specific activities may not be feasible, the underlying principles and creative ideas can inspire original, localized game-based lessons. The final chapter’s focus on collaboration with teachers, administration, and parents is also highlighted as crucial for securing institutional support for these initiatives. Overall, the article positions Pavey’s work as a valuable, albeit contextually limited, resource for advancing games-based library services.
