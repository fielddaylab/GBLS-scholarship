### Summary
# **Broussard, M. J. S. (2014). Using games to make formative assessment fun in the academic library. *The Journal of Academic Librarianship, 40*(1), 35-42. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.acalib.2012.12.001**

Broussard argues that academic library instruction needs more formative, in-the-moment assessment and that educational games can make that assessment less intimidating for both students and librarians. The article begins from a familiar pressure in higher education: libraries are expected to show evidence of learning, but many common assessment tools are summative, large-scale, expensive, and too late to improve learning during the session itself. For instruction librarians, especially those working in "one shot" settings, Broussard argues that formative assessment is particularly valuable because it lets librarians and students exchange feedback while there is still time to adjust instruction.

The article's conceptual move is to connect formative assessment and game-based learning. Broussard defines formative assessment as frequent, informal feedback used by the instructor to modify instruction while learning is still happening. Educational games, in turn, are treated as environments where players try out skills, receive feedback, revise strategies, and move toward harder tasks. Drawing on game studies and education literature, Broussard argues that games and formative assessment share eight elements: active learning, feedback, motivation, moratoriums, scaffolding, critical thinking and self-assessment, social learning, and stealth assessment. This makes games promising not because they add novelty to instruction, but because they can turn evidence of learning into part of the activity itself.

Broussard grounds the argument in two information literacy games developed at Lycoming College's Snowden Library. *Secret Agents in the Library* was a one-hour freshman composition game that mixed online activities with physical exploration of the library. Students worked in teams of "rookie agents" to investigate an intruder in the library's "information mainframe," using reference materials, books, databases, and MLA citation style along the way. The game ended with a clicker-based debrief, allowing the librarian to identify confusion and provide mini-instruction when needed. *Goblin Threat* was an online plagiarism game in which players cleared rooms by finding hidden plagiarism goblins and answering questions about citation and academic honesty. Incorrect answers produced mini-tutorials and repeat opportunities; correct answers advanced the player and gave progress feedback.

The article is especially useful because it treats feedback design as instructional design. Games can provide specific, frequent, immediate, and adaptive feedback through scores, sounds, mini-tutorials, progress reports, access to new spaces, and facilitator coaching. In Broussard's examples, wrong answers do not simply mark failure; they create chances to revise understanding. The concept of moratoriums is also important: game spaces can let students practice imperfectly with reduced consequences, which is especially appropriate in library sessions where the goal is preparation for later research work rather than graded performance during the library visit.

Broussard also emphasizes that games alone are rarely enough. Debriefing, clicker review, discussion, journaling, and other wraparound assessments help players transfer game experience into real-world information practices. This point matters because several of the library games discussed in the article are technically simple; Broussard argues that good learning design is more important than technological polish. Carefully written questions, purposeful scaffolding, social play, humor, role play, and post-game reflection can all make simple games instructionally meaningful. At the same time, games are not appropriate for every learning objective, and library games require faculty buy-in, attention to design flaws, and early alignment between objectives and game activities.

For the GBLS project, Broussard provides one of the clearest bridges between game-based library instruction and assessment. The article supports a view of GBLS in which games are not only outreach tools or engagement devices but also environments for observing learning, correcting misconceptions, encouraging self-assessment, and making formative feedback feel like help rather than evaluation. It also strengthens the review's recurring claim that facilitation and reflection are core infrastructure. The evidence base is not experimental; the article is primarily conceptual and illustrative, using prior literature and two local game examples rather than a controlled outcome study. Still, it gives GBLS a vocabulary for designing information literacy games around active learning, feedback, scaffolding, safe failure, debriefing, and assessment beyond attendance.
