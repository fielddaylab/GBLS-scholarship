# Summary

Mary J. Snyder Broussard’s 2014 article, "Using Games to Make Formative Assessment Fun in the Academic Library," addresses the gap between established educational practices and library instruction methodologies. The primary objective is twofold: to advocate for greater adoption of formative assessment among instruction librarians and to demonstrate that educational games serve as effective vehicles for such assessments. The author argues that while summative assessments (e.g., LibQual+, Project SAILS) are prevalent, they are often resource-intensive and occur too late to improve immediate student learning. In contrast, formative assessments provide continuous feedback during the learning process, allowing instructors to adjust instruction in real-time.

The methodology consists of a qualitative synthesis of literature on formative assessment and game-based learning, followed by a case study analysis of two information literacy games developed at Lycoming College: "Secret Agents in the Library" and "Goblin Threat." The author identifies eight pedagogical elements shared by both fields: active learning, feedback, motivation, moratoriums (low-stakes environments), scaffolding, critical thinking/self-assessment, social learning, and stealth assessment.

The population for the case studies includes freshman composition students at Lycoming College for "Secret Agents in the Library," a hybrid physical-digital game designed to teach research skills. For "Goblin Threat," an online plagiarism prevention game, the data encompasses usage by hundreds of Lycoming students and over seventy external institutions. Quantitative data reveals that "Goblin Threat" received over 54,000 page visits between January 2011 and the time of publication. Qualitative data includes observations of student engagement, focus group feedback during development, and post-game satisfaction surveys.

Key findings indicate strong alignment between game mechanics and formative assessment principles. For instance, games provide immediate, specific feedback (e.g., sound effects, mini-tutorials after incorrect answers) which mirrors the ideal characteristics of formative feedback. The "moratorium" concept is illustrated by allowing unlimited attempts in "Goblin Threat," creating a safe space for students to practice without grade penalties. Observations from "Secret Agents" showed high intrinsic motivation, with students engaging in role-playing behaviors beyond game requirements. Survey results for "Goblin Threat" indicated that students found the experience enjoyable and preferred it to traditional instruction methods. The author notes that while technical limitations often constrain library games to simple formats (e.g., multiple-choice), effective pedagogical design is more critical than technological sophistication.

The implications for library practice are significant. Broussard recommends that librarians consciously integrate formative assessment into one-shot sessions, utilizing games to foster intrinsic motivation and reduce assessment anxiety through "stealth assessment." The article emphasizes the necessity of aligning game activities with learning objectives from the initial design phase and incorporating debriefing sessions to facilitate knowledge transfer. The author concludes that increasing awareness of these methodologies can lead to more effective, engaging, and responsive library instruction.
