# Summary

This document outlines the development, implementation, and outcomes of a six-module Virtual Reality (VR) Art & Design Curriculum designed for incarcerated youth in Washington State. Authored by a team from the University of Washington Information School Digital Youth Lab, the report details an educational intervention delivered in May and June 2019 at Echo Glen, a juvenile rehabilitation center. The primary objective was to introduce VR technology and digital art making to marginalized youth through an anti-oppressive framework, culminating in a co-curated public library exhibit.

The methodology centered on a participatory design approach, prioritizing safety, trauma-informed practices, and inclusivity. The curriculum avoided personal storytelling to prevent triggering past trauma, focusing instead on abstract world-building and art creation. The program involved two cohorts of 12 participants each, aged 13–21. Despite active recruitment efforts for female participants, the demographic was predominantly male, with only one regular female attendee. The setting presented unique challenges, including strict security protocols, limited access to students prior to the program, and difficulties in obtaining parental consent for research components due to family separation. Consequently, empirical data collection was limited; while 13 educators and librarians were interviewed, only three youth participated in a focus group due to consent barriers.

The curriculum consisted of six two-hour sessions covering VR introduction, world-making (using *Tiny Town VR*), future building (*Fantastic Contraption*), music visualization (*Fantasynth*, *Beat Saber*), art creation (*Tilt Brush*, *SculptVR*), and exploration. Each session combined VR experiences with non-VR activities such as drawing, block building, and discussion to ensure all participants remained engaged. The physical setup was carefully designed to mitigate vulnerability; VR users faced the room with their backs to bookshelves rather than other students, and small groups spectated via computer screens.

Findings were primarily qualitative and anecdotal. The school principal reported notable increases in student engagement and positive social behavior. Youth feedback highlighted VR’s ability to lower artistic barriers; one participant noted that individuals who could not draw traditionally produced "reasonably good" work in VR. The program concluded with the development of "Here But Not Here," an exhibit installed at Snoqualmie Library for four weeks. This exhibit utilized a co-curation model, sharing curatorial authority with the youth participants. Themes derived from student feedback, such as VR being "realistic" and "trippy," guided the exhibit’s narrative.

The authors conclude that this curriculum offers a replicable model for library services in restrictive or marginalized settings. They emphasize the importance of anti-oppressive principles, including acknowledging power dynamics and centering community voices. While the research component faced significant limitations, the educational program succeeded in fostering creativity and engagement. The report suggests that VR can serve as a valuable tool for social-emotional learning and inclusive programming in libraries, provided that safety, accessibility, and ethical considerations are rigorously addressed.
