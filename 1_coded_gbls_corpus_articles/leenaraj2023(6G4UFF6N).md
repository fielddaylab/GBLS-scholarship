## Purpose
The study aims to develop and evaluate a gamified mobile application ("CMU Journey") for first-year student orientation at Chiang Mai University, specifically to promote knowledge of library services. The research seeks to answer three questions: (1) Can students effectively acquire knowledge of library services using the gamified app? (2) Is knowledge retention longer with the gamified app compared to traditional e-learning? (3) Does the gamified orientation improve intrinsic motivation for first-year students?
Evidence: "This study aims to research and develop a mobile application using the gamification approach for first-year library orientation in Chiang Mai University. There are three research questions..." (p. 142)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employed an explanatory sequential mixed-methods design. The quantitative phase involved a comparative experiment with two groups: an experimental group using the "CMU Journey" mobile app and a control group using traditional e-learning. Both groups completed pre-tests, post-tests (immediate), and delayed post-tests (one month later) to measure knowledge acquisition and retention. Intrinsic motivation was measured using the short version of the Intrinsic Motivation Inventory (IMI). The qualitative phase involved thematic analysis of open-ended feedback from participants regarding their user experience.

## Population and Data
The population consisted of first-year students at Chiang Mai University, Thailand. A total of 60 participants (34 male, 26 female) were recruited in-person and via Facebook. Participants were randomly assigned to either the experimental group (n=30; 17 male, 13 female) or the control group (n=30; 17 male, 13 female). Data included pre-test/post-test scores on library service knowledge, IMI questionnaire scores (Perceived Competence, Interest, Effort), and qualitative feedback from survey forms.

Confidence: high

## Findings
**Knowledge Acquisition:** No significant difference was found between the gamified app group and the e-learning group in immediate knowledge acquisition (p=0.036, not significant at 0.01 level). Both groups showed improvement from pre-test to post-test.
**Knowledge Retention:** The gamified app group demonstrated significantly better knowledge retention one month later compared to the e-learning group (p<0.001). The mean score for the experimental group remained higher than the control group after one month.
**Intrinsic Motivation:** The e-learning group showed no significant change in any IMI subscales. The gamified app group showed a significant increase in "Interest" (p<0.001) but no significant changes in "Perceived Competence" or "Effort."
**Qualitative Feedback:** Experimental group feedback was largely positive, describing the game as "fun," "addictive," and "innovative." Some negative feedback cited physical tiredness from walking. Suggestions included adding more game mechanics (multiplayer, varied question types) and fixing technical issues (GPS accuracy, battery drain on Android). Control group feedback noted e-learning was helpful but sometimes boring or too long, with some system login issues.

## Implications
The authors conclude that while gamification does not necessarily improve immediate knowledge acquisition over e-learning, it significantly enhances long-term knowledge retention and intrinsic interest. They suggest that gamified mobile applications are a viable strategy for promoting library services to first-year students, particularly for sustaining engagement and memory over time. Future work should expand sample sizes, test across multiple universities, and refine game elements (e.g., levels, social features) to maintain long-term engagement.
Evidence: "In summary, this study shows that it is worth implementing gamification to promote library services that can enhance learners’ knowledge retention and intrinsic motivation..." # Summary

This study by Leenaraj et al. (2023) investigates the efficacy of a gamified mobile application, "CMU Journey," for orienting first-year students to library services at Chiang Mai University in Thailand. The research addresses the limitations of traditional orientation methods, which are often passive and fail to sustain student engagement or long-term knowledge retention. The primary objective was to compare the gamified app against traditional e-learning modules regarding knowledge acquisition, knowledge retention, and intrinsic motivation.

The methodology utilized an explanatory sequential mixed-methods design. A total of 60 first-year students were randomly assigned to either an experimental group (using the CMU Journey app) or a control group (using e-learning). The CMU Journey app employed mechanics inspired by *Pokémon Go*, requiring students to physically navigate the library campus, locate virtual monsters via GPS, and answer questions about library services to earn points and rewards. Data collection involved pre-tests, immediate post-tests, and delayed post-tests (one month later) to assess knowledge of library rules and resources. Intrinsic motivation was measured using the Intrinsic Motivation Inventory (IMI), focusing on perceived competence, interest, and effort. Qualitative data were gathered through open-ended surveys analyzing user feedback.

The quantitative results revealed distinct differences between the two interventions. Regarding immediate knowledge acquisition, there was no statistically significant difference between the gamified group and the e-learning group (p=0.036), indicating that both methods were equally effective for short-term learning. However, in terms of knowledge retention, the gamified group significantly outperformed the control group after one month (p<0.001). This suggests that the active, location-based engagement facilitated by the game helped solidify memory of library services over time.

Concerning intrinsic motivation, the e-learning group showed no significant changes in any IMI subscales. In contrast, the gamified group demonstrated a significant increase in "Interest" (p<0.001), though no significant changes were observed in "Perceived Competence" or "Effort." This indicates that while the game made the orientation more enjoyable and engaging, it did not necessarily alter students' perceptions of their ability to use the library or their perceived effort required.

Qualitative feedback from the experimental group was predominantly positive, with students describing the experience as "fun," "addictive," and "innovative." Many noted that the game motivated them to explore the library even if they initially lacked interest. However, some participants reported physical fatigue from walking and technical issues related to GPS accuracy and battery consumption on Android devices. Suggestions for improvement included adding multiplayer features, diverse question types, and deeper content challenges. The control group found e-learning helpful but often "boring" or too lengthy, with some experiencing login difficulties.

The authors conclude that gamified mobile applications are a valuable tool for library orientation, particularly for enhancing long-term knowledge retention and fostering intrinsic interest. They acknowledge limitations, including the small sample size (n=60) and single-site context, which restricts generalizability. Future research should involve larger, multi-institutional samples and incorporate additional game elements to sustain engagement beyond initial orientation events. This study contributes to the literature on Games-Based Library Services by providing empirical evidence that gamification can effectively bridge the gap between immediate instruction and long-term user adoption of library resources.
