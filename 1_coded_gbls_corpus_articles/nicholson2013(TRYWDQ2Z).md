### Summary
# **Nicholson, S. (2013). Playing in the past: A history of games, toys, and puzzles in North American libraries. *The Library Quarterly, 83*(4), 341-361. https://doi.org/10.1086/671913**

Nicholson provides a historical synthesis of games, toys, puzzles, and play in North American libraries from the nineteenth century through the early 2010s. The article directly challenges the idea that gaming is a new or anomalous library service. Instead, Nicholson argues that libraries have supported games for more than 150 years through programs, collections, recreation, education, community engagement, school support, research collections, digital literacy, and outreach to underserved patrons. The article is not a comprehensive census of every gaming program, but a documentary history drawn from research articles, library magazines, newspaper notices, event announcements, professional books, and historical examples.

The article begins by defining games broadly as forms of play with goals and structure, including board games, card games, computer games, role-playing games, console games, and other forms. Nicholson distinguishes between collections of games that patrons may borrow or use and gaming programs in which players gather at a specific time for facilitated play. This definitional move is useful for GBLS because it prevents the review from reducing "games" to videogames or treating game-based service as only event programming. In Nicholson's account, games can be materials, services, social occasions, instructional tools, research objects, and collection-development problems.

The historical arc starts with rational recreation and moral betterment in the nineteenth century. Libraries and related social-reform institutions used controlled recreational spaces, game rooms, billiards, chess, and morally themed board games as alternatives to public houses and gambling. The Mechanics' Institute Library in San Francisco, founded in 1854, is identified as home to the oldest still-existing chess club in the United States. Nicholson uses chess to show both continuity and resistance: libraries have long hosted strategic play, but some have also treated games as inappropriate disruptions, as in a New Rochelle case where a patron was arrested after playing chess at a reference table.

During the Great Depression, Nicholson shows, libraries responded to economic stress by supporting shared recreational resources. Toy libraries emerged when children needed access to toys and games, and some early models explicitly linked borrowing to responsibility and character formation. Puzzle contests also strained and activated libraries: patrons used dictionaries and reference works to solve prize puzzles, and libraries responded either by limiting access, protecting materials, teaching research strategies, or turning puzzle-solving into a reference display. These examples matter because they show that game-related services have long involved the same tensions that contemporary GBLS faces: resource allocation, staff time, noise, damage, patron demand, public criticism, and opportunities for instruction.

The article then traces games through public, school, academic, and special collections contexts. Public libraries used games to reach boys' clubs, young people, seniors, and other patrons not already engaged by ordinary services. Children and teens created their own games in library programs; summer reading programs used game structures, puzzles, mysteries, points, and prizes; and intergenerational programs used older adults' game knowledge as a community resource. School libraries collected and facilitated games for instruction, including treasure hunts, Dewey Decimal games, Jeopardy-style quizzes, board-game research projects, classroom game collections, and standards-aligned recreational games. Academic libraries built game collections for research, curriculum, and leisure, from playing-card archives and gambling collections to undergraduate videogame collections supporting game studies and design programs.

Nicholson's digital history is especially helpful because it places videogames inside a longer library-service continuum rather than treating them as a rupture. Libraries used computer games to introduce children and adults to computers, created circulating Atari 2600 collections in the early 1980s, hosted Dungeons & Dragons, Scrabble, video-game trading days, CD-ROM collections, virtual-world interests, Wii bowling for seniors, and large videogame collections. By the late 2000s, ALA initiatives, National Gaming Day, practitioner books, and the Games and Gaming Round Table helped normalize gaming as "just another library service."

For the GBLS project, Nicholson is a cornerstone historical source. It supports the review's argument that game-based library services are not a fad but a recurring adaptation of library missions to changing cultural forms, leisure practices, technologies, and patron needs. It also supplies a unifying service logic: games and supporting materials are information containers that can be selected, described, circulated, facilitated, assessed, and connected to other library resources. Nicholson's conclusion is also a useful caution. Gaming should not be justified only as a lure or reward, because extrinsic reward structures can weaken intrinsic motivation; instead, gaming services should be aligned with library goals and evaluated like any other service. The article's limitations are those of a historical survey: it compiles illustrative evidence rather than measuring outcomes. Even so, it gives GBLS the long view needed to frame contemporary videogame, tabletop, escape-room, VR, maker, and gamification work as part of a durable pattern of libraries supporting play, learning, exploration, and community.
