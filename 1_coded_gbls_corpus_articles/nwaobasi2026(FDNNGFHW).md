# Summary

This conceptual article by Nwaobasi et al. (2026) addresses the persistent challenges of low user engagement and poor retention rates associated with conventional library orientation and information literacy (IL) instruction in academic libraries. The authors argue that traditional methods often fail to actively engage students, resulting in limited effective use of library resources. To address this gap, the study proposes a theoretical framework for integrating gamification—the application of game design elements such as points, badges, quests, and leaderboards into non-game contexts—into library instruction.

The methodology is explicitly conceptual and design-based, relying on instructional systems design principles and established learning theories rather than empirical data collection. The authors synthesize four key theoretical foundations: Self-Determination Theory (emphasizing autonomy, competence, and relatedness for intrinsic motivation), Constructivism (viewing learning as an active process of knowledge construction), Experiential Learning Theory (highlighting the cycle of experience, reflection, and action), and Behaviourism (focusing on reinforcement through rewards and feedback). These theories underpin a four-stage model comprising input (design phase), process (implementation phase), engagement mechanisms (motivation and interaction), and output (learning outcomes).

A central contribution of the article is the detailed mapping of specific gamification strategies to distinct library instruction domains. The authors present a functional framework linking library functions to targeted IL skills through specific game mechanics:
1. **Library Orientation:** Digital scavenger hunts are proposed to foster spatial and functional awareness of library facilities, reducing cognitive overload for novice users through scaffolded exploration.
2. **Catalogue Training:** Quest-based missions are designed to improve search strategy development and proficiency in navigating metadata and classification schemes.
3. **Database Use:** Challenge-based tasks involving time or relevance constraints aim to enhance advanced research skills, including the use of Boolean operators and filters.
4. **Citation Training:** Badge-based quizzes serve as positive reinforcers for academic integrity, encouraging accurate referencing practices (e.g., APA, MLA) through immediate feedback and reward systems.
5. **Source Evaluation:** Scenario-based simulations require learners to assess information sources based on authority, currency, and bias, thereby developing critical thinking skills.
6. **Reference Services:** Role-play games simulate help-desk interactions to increase service awareness and improve user-librarian communication dynamics.

The authors claim that this structured approach transforms passive learning into active engagement, aligning with student-centered pedagogical models. They posit that gamification enhances motivation by leveraging both intrinsic factors (interest, autonomy) and extrinsic motivators (points, badges). The study identifies independent variables (gamification elements, instructional design, technology platforms), mediating variables (student engagement, motivation, system interaction), and dependent variables (IL skills, library usage frequency, academic performance support).

While the article provides a comprehensive theoretical justification and practical mapping for gamified library instruction, it explicitly acknowledges the absence of empirical validation. The findings are derived from conceptual analysis and literature synthesis rather than experimental testing. Consequently, the authors conclude that while the proposed model offers a scalable methodology for enhancing user engagement and IL acquisition, future research must focus on empirical testing to validate these claims in real-world academic library settings. The study positions gamification not merely as an entertainment tool but as a rigorous instructional strategy capable of supporting lifelong learning and academic achievement.
