# Summary

This article by Jessica Varsa (2022) provides a descriptive account of the inaugural FanFest hosted by the Oconee County Library, part of the Athens Regional Library System in Georgia. Held on April 2, 2022, at the Oconee County Civic Center in Watkinsville, the event was designed as an admission-free, all-ages festival inspired by comic convention traditions but focused on showcasing local talent and fostering community connection. The primary objective of the report is to document the structure, content, and initial success of this library-led initiative, highlighting its potential as a model for community engagement through pop culture and fandom.

The methodology employed in this text is observational and narrative. It does not present empirical data analysis, statistical testing, or comparative studies. Instead, it functions as a case report detailing the logistical execution and qualitative outcomes of the event. The population under consideration comprises the attendees of FanFest, with the text reporting that "over 350 participants" attended the day-long event, which ran from 9:30 a.m. to 5:30 p.m. No further demographic data regarding the age, gender, or background of these attendees is provided in the source text.

Key findings center on the diverse programming and collaborative nature of FanFest. The event featured a wide array of activities including live musical performances by acts such as the Rebecca Sunshine Band, Sacred Bull, and Razzi King; author appearances by Joey Weiser (known for *Mermin* and *Dragon Racer*) and Will Smith (creator of *Legacy A.D.*); and interactive gaming stations run by the University of Georgia Student Board Game Club. Additionally, the festival included a cosplay contest, vendor markets featuring local artists and businesses, food trucks, and educational workshops, such as acting sessions led by local actress Toi Mekhi. The involvement of student volunteers from the Oconee County High School Beta Club and partnerships with organizations like Gonzoriffic Films underscore the library’s role in bridging community sectors.

The article also notes that FanFest served as a wrap party for Season 2 of the *Classic City Vibes Podcast*, produced by the Athens Regional Library System, thereby integrating digital media promotion with physical event attendance. A door prize incentivized newsletter sign-ups, linking event participation to ongoing library communication channels. The text emphasizes that the event was made possible through support from the Oconee County Library Friends and various community partners, reflecting a collaborative approach to resource management.

In terms of implications for Games-Based Library Services (GBLS), this case study illustrates how libraries can utilize gaming and fandom elements—such as board game clubs, cosplay, and comic book culture—to attract broad audiences. The success of FanFest suggests that such events can serve as effective tools for community building, promoting local arts, and enhancing library visibility. However, the article does not measure long-term impacts on library usage, user satisfaction, or educational outcomes beyond the immediate attendance figures. It remains a descriptive record of a single event, offering qualitative insights into the feasibility and appeal of pop-culture festivals in public library settings without providing quantitative evidence of sustained engagement or behavioral change among patrons. The absence of data on participant demographics or feedback limits the generalizability of these findings, though the reported attendance of over 350 individuals indicates a strong initial community response.
