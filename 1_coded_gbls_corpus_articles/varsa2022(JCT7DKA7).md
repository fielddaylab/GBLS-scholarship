### Summary
# **Varsa, J. (2022). Oconee County Library puts on inaugural FanFest. *Georgia Library Quarterly, 59*(3), 1-2.**

Varsa provides a short practitioner report on the first FanFest hosted by Oconee County Library, a branch of the Athens Regional Library System in Georgia. The event took place on April 2, 2022, at the Oconee County Civic Center in Watkinsville and was supported by the Oconee County Library Friends, staff, and volunteers. It was admission-free, lasted a full day, drew more than 350 participants, and was framed as a community celebration in the tradition of comic cons.

The article defines FanFest as an all-ages festival that brings together live music, guest authors and artists, a cosplay contest, gaming, vendors, food, and other activities. Its distinguishing feature is localism. Rather than building a program only around nationally recognizable franchises or commercial fandom culture, the library used the event to showcase local authors, musicians, gaming organizations, vendors, food trucks, student volunteers, and community partners. Varsa notes that many participating organizations were already library-system partners and represented the diversity of northeast Georgia.

The service model is spatially and organizationally broader than a single in-library program. Oconee County Library used a partner venue, the Oconee County Civic Center, from 9:30 a.m. to 5:30 p.m. The main hall held vendors who could display books and products while talking with attendees. The auditorium hosted three music acts, while the lawn supported food trucks and sword demonstrations from the Athens School of Arms. The program also included workshops, panel discussions, book signings, live music, and other activities.

Gaming appears as one part of a broader fandom and creative ecosystem. The University of Georgia Student Board Game Club ran a gaming table, while local high school Beta Club volunteers greeted attendees and distributed event schedules. The report does not describe the specific board games, facilitation methods, learning outcomes, or attendance at the gaming table, so it should not be treated as evidence for a game program's impact. Still, the presence of a university board game club is important for GBLS because it shows how libraries can use existing local gaming expertise inside a larger festival format rather than staffing every activity internally.

FanFest also functioned as a platform for local creative production. Featured guests included graphic novelists, musicians, filmmakers, a local actress leading acting workshops, comic creators, face painting and henna artists, and a podcast wrap party for Classic City Vibes, an Athens Regional Library System podcast about local musicians, visual artists, literary artists, and other creatives. A donated merchandise door prize was tied to electronic newsletter sign-ups, connecting event excitement back to ongoing library communication.

For the larger GBLS project, Varsa's value is as a compact example of game-adjacent public-library festival design. It complements Virello's larger Southington Library ComiCon case by showing a smaller, local-talent version of the same model. In both cases, games are not isolated from library service; they sit beside cosplay, comics, music, crafts, authors, vendors, food, displays, contests, and community organizations. This matters because many GBLS programs will not be pure game programs. A board game table, video game tournament, cosplay contest, or playful demonstration may be one node in a wider cultural event that advances library goals around access, belonging, local creativity, partner visibility, and community gathering.

The limitations are clear. The article is only two pages, does not include formal evaluation, and gives little detail about planning, budget, staffing, accessibility, safety policies, marketing, or post-event outcomes beyond the attendance figure. Its usefulness lies in documenting a replicable event frame: an admission-free, all-ages FanFest that uses a partner venue, local creative networks, student volunteers, a university board game club, food, music, workshops, and library communication channels to make fandom a public-library commons.
