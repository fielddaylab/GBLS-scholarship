### Summary
# **Madray, A., & Catalano, A. (2010). The Curriculum Material Center's vital link to play and learning: What's the connection? *Education Libraries, 33*(2), 11-17.**

Madray and Catalano connect children's play, learning, cognitive development, and Curriculum Materials Center (CMC) collection work. Writing as academic librarians responsible for information literacy, education liaison work, and the management and collection development of a CMC, they argue that play-related materials in schools, homes, and libraries are vital to learning. The article is a professional-practice essay rather than an assessment study, but it is directly useful for GBLS because it treats games, toys, manipulatives, puzzles, realia, and related resources as legitimate library instructional materials.

The opening literature frame draws on Froebel, Fred Rogers, Piaget, Vygotsky, Bodrova and Leong, and other child-development and educational-psychology sources. The authors emphasize that play supports neurological, cognitive, physical, motor, language, social, emotional, self-help, problem-solving, leadership, and literacy development. Their argument is not that play is extra decoration around learning, but that play and learning operate as co-equals in childhood development. This frame helps explain why CMCs and education libraries might collect playful materials alongside books and curriculum guides.

The article then situates this work historically in Curriculum Materials Centers, formerly Curriculum Laboratories. A CMC is described as a physical location within an academic library or teacher-education campus where pre-service and in-service teachers can experiment with, evaluate, and use instructional materials. The authors link CMCs to National Council for Accreditation of Teacher Education requirements and to ACRL/EBSS collection guidance. Recommended CMC resources include flash cards, music, games, puzzles, pictures, books on tape, big books, little books, models, videos, charts, maps, manipulatives, realia, toys, computer software, and board and psychoeducational games.

A central practical claim is that games and manipulatives are among the most popular CMC materials across P-12 content areas. These materials support multiple forms of skill building and appear in English language arts, geography, history, science, math, fine arts, foreign languages, counseling, physical education, and special education. For GBLS, this matters because the article locates game-based service not only in youth programming or leisure collections, but also in academic-library support for teacher preparation, standards-aligned lesson design, and classroom practice.

The subject-area examples show how wide the service model can be. Math learning can involve water play, block play, card games, musical chairs, group games, board games, Pizza Fractions, and Unifix blocks; games can make repetition more intrinsically motivated than worksheets and provide immediate feedback. Science learning can use thermometers, scales, microscopes, telescopes, rocks, shells, blocks, water play, experiments, racing games, and other active-learning tools. Social studies can use role play, geography globes, Monopoly, Civilization, Oregon Trail, Pandemic, The Making of the President, and other board or electronic games. Music, physical education, special education, and children's literature are also treated as domains where play, movement, pretend play, story play, and accessible toys can support development.

The collection-development guidance is especially relevant to libraries. Madray and Catalano stress that CMC librarians should not merely buy and house games and toys. They should choose materials that fit classroom time constraints, age levels, content areas, state and national standards, different learning styles, and the practical realities of circulation. They caution against highly technical items, special batteries, or many small pieces that may be lost. They recommend collaboration with education faculty, subject-area conferences, literature review, journals such as *School Library Journal*, vendor catalogs, and attention to discovery learning, creativity, problem solving, and developmentally appropriate design.

The article also treats promotion and instruction as part of collection work. CMC librarians can create displays of toys and books by content area, demonstrate games and toys during library instruction, offer workshops on play and selecting developmentally appropriate toys, host open houses, and support teacher-created materials. The conclusion is that educators can create games where they do not yet exist, but that requires understanding learning theory, child development, subject matter, and classroom use. The CMC librarian's role is therefore advisory and instructional as much as custodial.

For the larger GBLS project, this source is useful because it provides a collection-based model for play-oriented educational service. It predates much recent GBLS language, but it shows that academic libraries serving education programs have long collected and taught with playful learning materials. Its limitations should be kept visible: it is a conceptual and professional-practice article, not a study of outcomes, circulation, patron demand, or implementation barriers. Still, it strengthens the review's argument that game-based library service includes curricular collections, teacher preparation, manipulatives, standards-aligned selection, and librarian-led guidance on how games and toys become learning tools.
