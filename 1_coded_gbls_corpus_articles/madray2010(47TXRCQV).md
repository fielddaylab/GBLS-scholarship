## Findings
The authors assert that play is integral to cognitive, social, and emotional development, citing theorists like Froebel and Rogers. They identify games and manipulatives as highly popular and effective CMC resources across subject areas:
- **Math:** Games provide intrinsic motivation and immediate feedback compared to worksheets; computer gaming camps improved student attitudes toward math, though not necessarily test scores (Ke, 2008).
- **Science:** Play encourages inquiry and problem-solving; basic toys (blocks, water play) teach physics and measurement concepts.
- **Social Studies:** Role-play software and board games (e.g., Monopoly for economics, Civilization for history) facilitate active learning of complex concepts.
- **Music/Physical Education:** Music aids motor skills and memory; PE games develop gross motor skills and sportsmanship.
- **Special Education:** Pretend play and textured toys support social and language development for students with disabilities.
The authors also note that CMC librarians must select materials that fit classroom time constraints (20–40 minutes) and align with curriculum standards, avoiding overly technical or fragile items.
Evidence: "Kamii and Kato (2006) discussed the value of games over worksheets... Ke (2008) indicated that students developed more positive attitudes toward math learning through a five-week computer math gaming camp..." # Summary

Madray and Catalano (2010) present a comprehensive overview of the intersection between play-based learning and the operational role of Curriculum Material Centers (CMCs) within academic libraries. The authors, serving as education liaisons and CMC librarians, argue that play is not merely recreational but a fundamental component of cognitive, social, and emotional development in children. Drawing on the works of educational theorists such as Friedrich Froebel, Jean Piaget, and Lev Vygotsky, the article establishes that learning occurs when the brain processes experiences from play, which enhances neurological and motor skills. The central thesis is that CMCs serve as vital links between theoretical child development research and practical classroom application for pre-service and in-service teachers.

The article details the historical mandate of CMCs by the National Council for Accreditation of Teacher Education (NCATE) and guidelines from the Association of College and Research Libraries (ACRL), which require collections to support P-12 curricula across various subjects and learning styles. The authors emphasize that games, manipulatives, and toys are among the most utilized materials in these centers. They provide subject-specific examples of how play materials facilitate learning:
*   **Mathematics:** The authors cite Kamii and Kato (2006) to argue that games offer intrinsic motivation and immediate feedback, superior to repetitive worksheets. While Ke’s (2008) research showed that computer gaming camps improved students' attitudes toward math without significantly raising test scores, the authors suggest that positive engagement is a precursor to learning. They recommend manipulatives like Unifix blocks and commercial games like Monopoly for teaching basic concepts.
*   **Science:** Play encourages natural inquiry. The authors note that Severeide and Pizzini (1984) found play integration improves problem-solving and achievement in science. Simple items like thermometers, rocks, and blocks teach empirical concepts such as physics and measurement.
*   **Social Studies:** Role-playing software and board games are highlighted for teaching complex historical and economic concepts. For instance, Monopoly is used to illustrate market exchange and financial management, while games like *Civilization* and *Oregon Trail* contextualize history.
*   **Music and Physical Education:** Music aids in gross motor skill development and memory, while PE games foster cooperation and physical health. The authors suggest CMCs direct teachers to specialized curricula rather than housing bulky sports equipment.
*   **Special Education:** The article stresses the importance of inclusive materials. Pretend play toys (e.g., doctor kits) and textured items support social and language development for students with disabilities, allowing them to express themselves and engage in sociodramatic play.

A significant portion of the text is dedicated to the practical challenges of collection development. Madray and Catalano warn against selecting materials that are too time-consuming for standard class periods (20–40 minutes) or overly fragile/technical. They advocate for "authentic" games that are fun first and educational second, citing Harris (2009). The authors also highlight the importance of collaboration with faculty to ensure materials align with state standards and developmental appropriateness.

Finally, the article outlines strategies for promoting CMC collections. Librarians are encouraged to move beyond passive housing of materials by conducting workshops, creating displays, and integrating demonstrations into library instruction sessions. The authors conclude that by guiding teachers in the selection and use of play-based materials, CMC librarians directly contribute to effective pedagogy and improved student outcomes. The piece serves as a professional guide for academic librarians seeking to optimize their CMC collections to support evidence-based, play-integrated teaching practices.
