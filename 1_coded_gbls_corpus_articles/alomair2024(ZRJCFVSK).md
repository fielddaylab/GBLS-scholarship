## Population and Data
The population consisted of 120 intermediate school students aged 12–15 from Jouf, Saudi Arabia. Participants were selected based on parental consent and requisite computer/internet skills. The sample was divided into an experimental group (n=60) and a control group (n=60). The study took place over two six-week periods (t1 and t2) during the second trimester of 2023. Data included scores from quizzes integrated into the system (experimental) or paper-based (control), standardized evaluation tests, and Likert-scale surveys completed by students and six English instructors.

## Findings
The results indicated a statistically significant improvement in SLO achievement for the experimental group compared to the control group. Specifically, 72% of the experimental group showed an increased rate (IR) in Knowledge Learning Outcome (LO1), versus 55% in the control group. For Skill Learning Outcome (LO2), 90% of the experimental group improved, compared to 53% in the control group. Survey results revealed that 75% of the experimental group strongly agreed that the framework enhanced their motivation, whereas only 45% of the control group felt similarly. However, dialectal variation posed a challenge for some students. Instructors (86%) endorsed the framework as a supportive adjunct to traditional teaching but cautioned against using it as the sole pedagogical tool due to technical barriers and the risk of diminishing the teacher's role.

# Summary

This study by Alomair and Hammami (2024) addresses a critical gap in foreign language learning (FLL) literature: the tendency of gamified and game-based approaches to prioritize student motivation while neglecting the rigorous enhancement of Student Learning Outcomes (SLOs). The authors propose and evaluate a novel framework that integrates gamification mechanics, card games, agent technology, and microlearning strategies to improve proficiency in listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

The methodology employed a quasi-experimental design involving 120 intermediate school students (aged 12–15) from Saudi Arabia, divided equally into an experimental group (n=60) and a control group (n=60). The experimental group utilized the proposed FLL framework, which centers on a Card Drafting Agent (CDA) developed using the Java Agent Development Framework (JADE). This agent retrieves content from a MySQL database to generate randomized card sets aligned with specific learning outcomes. Each card set includes microlearning content and quizzes for four language skills, plus a "prize card" to enhance engagement. The control group received traditional instruction. Data were collected over two six-week periods using achievement tests (quizzes and standardized evaluations) and surveys assessing motivation and dialectal challenges.

The findings demonstrate that the framework significantly improved SLO achievement. For Knowledge Learning Outcome (LO1), 72% of the experimental group showed improvement compared to 55% in the control group. For Skill Learning Outcome (LO2), the improvement rate was 90% in the experimental group versus 53% in the control group. Furthermore, survey data indicated higher motivation levels in the experimental group, with 75% strongly agreeing that the approach enhanced their persistence, compared to 45% in the control group. However, the study identified dialectal variation as a barrier for some students. Instructors largely endorsed the framework (86%) as a valuable adjunct to traditional teaching but advised against exclusive reliance on it due to technical access issues and the importance of teacher-led instruction.

The authors acknowledge several limitations, including the relatively small sample size constrained by time, the lack of qualitative data, and the study’s focus solely on English language outcomes. They recommend future research expand the participant pool, incorporate mixed-methods approaches, and test the framework’s applicability across different academic disciplines and diverse linguistic backgrounds. This work contributes to Games-Based Library Services (GBLS) literature by providing empirical evidence that structured, agent-driven gamification can effectively bridge the gap between engagement and measurable educational outcomes in language acquisition.
