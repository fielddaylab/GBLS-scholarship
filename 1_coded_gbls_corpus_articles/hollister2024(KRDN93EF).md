### Summary
# **Hollister, J. M. (2024). Virtual libraries in the new metaverse: An exploratory study on community libraries in Meta Horizon Worlds. *International Journal of Knowledge Content Development & Technology, 14*(4), 113-133. https://doi.org/10.5865/IJKCT.2024.14.4.113**

Hollister explores the presence and status of libraries in the emerging metaverse by studying public library-themed worlds in Meta Horizon Worlds, a social virtual reality platform owned by Meta. The study begins from the observation that libraries have long experimented with virtual worlds, especially Second Life, where librarians created digital collections, programming, reference services, and LIS education activities. The newer metaverse landscape raises a related but updated question: if library work moved into earlier virtual worlds, what does library presence look like in a contemporary VR platform built around socializing, games, exploration, and user-created worlds?

The project is framed as a pilot case study guided by two research questions: to what extent libraries are present in Meta Horizon Worlds and what characterizes those libraries. Hollister collected data from June to October 2024 through unobtrusive observation of public worlds that included the word "library" in their title or description. All in-platform visits were conducted in VR with a Meta Quest 2 headset. The author also collected descriptive data from world listings, took screenshots from a first-person perspective, and recorded field notes after each visit. To avoid involving human subjects directly, the author joined only publicly listed worlds and avoided interaction with other users by entering empty worlds or using closed/private sessions when needed.

The sample included 44 worlds after removing worlds that were empty, inaccessible, or created by Meta employees. The worlds were categorized by their creators in varied ways, most often as hangouts or art spaces, with others marked as puzzle, narrative, adventure, exploration, party game, or uncategorized. All were designed for VR use with Meta Quest headsets, although some Meta Horizon access is possible through other devices. The worlds varied in age, update frequency, capacity, comfort rating, age rating, and number of editors. Hollister treats these descriptive details as evidence that library-world development in Meta Horizon Worlds is relatively new, uneven, and labor-intensive.

The qualitative content analysis identified four recurring categories: art and asset libraries, community libraries, personal libraries, and library aesthetics used for games and social spaces. Art and asset libraries were often not libraries in a professional institutional sense; they provided virtual objects, shapes, lighting fixtures, mechanics, portals, and other assets that world builders could reuse. These worlds are still important for GBLS because they use library language to organize access, attribution, and reuse within a creator economy. In a platform built around world-making, a library can become an infrastructure for sharing components rather than a repository of texts alone.

The community library category is the closest to conventional library service. Hollister found a small number of worlds designed to function as virtual community libraries by providing resources, exhibitions, social space, events, curated portals, educational materials, or cultural programming. Only two worlds appeared to have connections to real-world libraries: the Geisel Library Dr. Seuss Matrix and AANHPI Heritage World. Other worlds simulated library spaces around African and African American culture and history, banned books, reading and computer clubs, anti-bullying themes, performances, portals, and community events. These spaces were not generally equivalent to mature Second Life library services, but they show how library functions can be remixed in a VR social platform.

Personal libraries and library-themed game/social worlds show another important pattern. Many users built cozy reading rooms, personal library spaces, recreations of famous media libraries, bars, trivia games, escape-room-style spaces, hidden-key challenges, and social venues with books, shelves, fireplaces, stages, portals, or other library aesthetics. Hollister argues that the library aesthetic has enduring appeal as a symbol of meeting, reading, culture, welcome, and sociality, even when the world does not provide formal library services. For GBLS, that finding matters because it shows that the idea of the library circulates inside game-like worlds as both place and play material.

The discussion is cautious about institutional opportunity. Most virtual libraries in Meta Horizon Worlds were community-created rather than affiliated with real-world libraries, and few provided the level of digital resources or virtual reference found in earlier Second Life library projects. At the same time, the worlds suggest possible future directions for libraries that want to meet users where they are in social VR. The study flags several barriers: platform instability, the shutdown of other VR platforms such as Altspace and Mozilla Hubs, limited interactive book functionality, content-development labor, headset cost, accessibility concerns, privacy concerns, motion sickness, limited high-quality content, and the need for stakeholder partnerships, training, and continuous assessment.

For the GBLS project, Hollister's article extends the virtual-world strand beyond Second Life into the newer commercial metaverse. It is not an assessment of a library-run program and does not show that patrons benefit from institutional VR libraries. Its value is descriptive and strategic: it maps what "library" currently means inside one game-like social VR platform and shows that virtual libraries can appear as asset repositories, community resource spaces, personal identity spaces, social venues, puzzle games, or aesthetic environments. GBLS should therefore treat virtual libraries not only as digital branches but also as worldbuilding, curation, access, community, and play spaces shaped by platform affordances and constraints.
