## Findings
The 44 sampled worlds were categorized into four emergent themes: Art & Asset Libraries, Community Libraries, Personal Libraries, and Library Aesthetics (Games & Social Spaces). Only two worlds (4.55%) appeared to have direct connections to real-world libraries ("The Geisel Library Dr. Seuss Matrix" and "AANHPO Heritage World"). Most worlds were community-created rather than institutionally affiliated. The average age of the worlds was 1.16 years, with an average of 82.66 days between creation and last update, suggesting ongoing development. Average world capacity was 14.02 participants. Few worlds offered interactive digital resources comparable to Second Life libraries; instead, many used library aesthetics for socialization, gaming (e.g., trivia, escape rooms), or as repositories for virtual assets/portals.

## Implications
The study suggests that while virtual libraries exist in Meta Horizon Worlds, they are currently nascent and lack the robust service infrastructure seen in older platforms like Second Life. # Summary

Jonathan M. Hollister’s 2024 article, "Virtual Libraries in the New Metaverse: An Exploratory Study on Community Libraries in Meta Horizon Worlds," presents an exploratory qualitative study examining the state of virtual libraries within the social VR platform Meta Horizon Worlds. The research addresses a gap in literature regarding library presence in newer metaverse platforms, contrasting them with established environments like Second Life. The primary objectives were to determine the extent of library presence in Meta Horizon Worlds and to characterize the nature of these virtual spaces.

The methodology employed unobtrusive observation and qualitative content analysis. Between June and October 2024, the researcher conducted site visits using a Meta Quest 2 headset, collecting screenshots and field notes from publicly listed worlds containing "library" in their titles or descriptions. From an initial pool of 49 identified worlds, five were excluded for being empty, inaccessible, or created by platform employees, resulting in a final sample of 44 worlds. Data analysis involved open, axial, and selective coding to identify recurring themes in world design and function.

The findings reveal that virtual libraries in Meta Horizon Worlds are predominantly community-created rather than affiliated with real-world institutions. Only two worlds (4.55%) showed clear connections to physical libraries: "The Geisel Library Dr. Seuss Matrix," which replicates a specific architectural landmark, and "AANHPO Heritage World," which offers educational resources on Asian American, Native Hawaiian, and Pacific Islander history. The remaining 42 worlds were categorized into four emergent themes: Art & Asset Libraries (repositories for virtual objects and building assets), Community Libraries (spaces simulating library services or hosting events), Personal Libraries (cozy, aesthetic reading rooms often inspired by media franchises like *Beauty and the Beast* or *The Breakfast Club*), and Library Aesthetics used for Games & Social Spaces (e.g., trivia games, escape rooms, or bars set in library environments).

Quantitative metadata from the world listings indicated that the average age of these virtual libraries was 1.16 years, with an average update frequency of every 82.66 days, suggesting active but ongoing development. The average capacity was 14 participants, and most worlds were rated for users aged 14 and up. Notably, few worlds offered interactive digital collections or reference services comparable to those in Second Life; instead, they often served as social hubs or asset repositories.

Hollister discusses the implications of these findings for library science practice. He notes that while the service infrastructure is currently limited, the persistent use of library aesthetics highlights the cultural value of libraries as inclusive meeting places. The study acknowledges limitations regarding generalizability due to the single-platform focus and subjective qualitative analysis. Hollister concludes that Meta Horizon Worlds presents an opportunity for libraries to expand their digital presence, provided that challenges related to content quality, accessibility, and hardware costs are addressed. He recommends that LIS professionals engage with XR technologies through training and partnerships to prepare for future integration of virtual services.
