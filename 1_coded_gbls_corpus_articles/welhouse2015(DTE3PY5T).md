## Findings
The authors found that existing literary plot classification systems were often too broad, too complex, or focused on motifs rather than central plots, making them unsuitable for video games. User studies confirmed that plot/narrative is a critical factor for game selection (ranked 7th most useful metadata element by 71% of survey respondents), yet no effective search mechanism exists. The developed controlled vocabulary uses a flexible VERB-OBJECT pair structure (e.g., "seek treasure," "prevent disaster") to describe narrative themes. Testing on 74 RPGs demonstrated that this structure allows for meaningful collocation of games with similar narrative elements, complementing genre categorization. The authors note that the vocabulary currently focuses on Western and Japanese RPGs because these genres have readily identifiable and complex narratives, serving as a stress test for the system.
Evidence: "Analyzing the transcripts revealed that 21 of the 56 interviewees mentioned story/plot/narrative as an important factor... 'Plot/Narrative' was ranked as the seventh most useful element with positive responses from 71% of all participants... The pair structure sets the stage for further refinement... Collocation based on plot elements can complement genre categorization and create interesting subcategories."

## Implications
The controlled vocabulary enables catalogers to provide standardized descriptions of game plots, facilitating better discovery and recommendation services in libraries. It supports scholarly research by allowing thematic collocation (e.g., apocalyptic imagery, survival). The authors recommend that the vocabulary be expanded to include other genres (shooters, survival horror) and suggest future work involving weighted plot pairs to indicate importance. # Summary

This article by Welhouse, Lee, and Bancroft (2015) addresses a significant gap in library and information science regarding the metadata description of video games. The authors argue that while plot is a defining feature of many video games, particularly Role-Playing Games (RPGs), existing organizational systems—including library catalogs and commercial game websites—fail to adequately describe plot information. Current systems rely heavily on genre labels, which are often inconsistent, overloaded with multiple dimensions, or too broad to support meaningful subject access. Furthermore, Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) are applied inconsistently to video games, leaving many records without useful subject access points.

The primary objective of the research was to create a controlled vocabulary for the "plot/narrative" element of video games. This effort is part of a larger project by the GAme MEtadata Research (GAMER) group at the University of Washington and the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) to develop a comprehensive metadata schema for interactive media. The authors sought to determine if existing literary plot classification systems could be adapted for video games and whether such metadata would facilitate meaningful collocation and search capabilities.

To establish the user need, the authors referenced prior empirical studies conducted as part of their larger project. These included persona analysis, semi-structured interviews with 56 users (including gamers, parents, scholars, and librarians), and an online survey of 1,257 participants. The findings indicated that plot/narrative is highly valued by users; in the survey, it was ranked as the seventh most useful metadata element, with 71% of respondents rating it positively. Interviewees expressed frustration with the inability to search for games by story content, noting that broad genre labels often obscure specific narrative interests (e.g., a parent seeking a game about knighthood rather than violence).

The methodology involved a domain analysis and card sorting technique. The authors first reviewed existing plot classification systems from literature (e.g., Polti’s 36 dramatic situations, Booker’s seven basic plots) and found them largely unsuitable for video games due to issues with granularity, complexity, or focus on motifs rather than central actions. They then collected candidate terms from a broad cross-section of RPGs, drawing from instruction manuals, in-game text, and online fan descriptions. Using card sorting, they organized these terms into a structured framework.

The resulting controlled vocabulary employs a faceted "pair structure" consisting of a VERB (a transitive verb describing the primary narrative action) and an OBJECT (a noun describing the central entity or concept). For example, a game might be described as "(Characters) seek treasure" or "(Characters) prevent disaster." This structure is designed to be flexible and extensible. The authors tested this vocabulary by cataloging a sample of 74 video games, primarily RPGs, which were chosen because their complex narratives provide a rigorous stress test for the system. The results demonstrated that the pair structure effectively allows for the collocation of games with similar narrative themes, offering a more nuanced discovery tool than genre alone.

The authors acknowledge several limitations and challenges. Granularity is a persistent issue; descriptions must be specific enough to be useful but broad enough to apply to multiple titles. Non-linear narratives and player choices complicate plot definition, as different playthroughs may yield different story outcomes. Additionally, cataloging plot information is labor-intensive, requiring significant time to understand complex stories, which raises questions about the feasibility for librarians without playing the games. The authors suggest that crowdsourcing could help mitigate this burden. They also note that the current vocabulary focuses on RPGs and will need expansion to cover other genres like shooters or puzzle games. Finally, they recommend supplementing the controlled vocabulary with free-text summary fields to capture nuances that do not fit the structured pairs and to address spoiler concerns through interface design.

In conclusion, the article presents a foundational step toward standardized plot metadata for video games. By providing a consistent framework for describing narrative content, this controlled vocabulary supports improved access, discovery, and scholarly analysis of video games as cultural artifacts.
