### Summary
# **Pan, Y., Adams, E. L., Ketterlin-Geller, L. R., Larson, E. C., & Clark, C. (2024). Enhancing middle school students' computational thinking competency through game-based learning. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 72*(6), 3391-3419. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-024-10400-x**

Pan, Adams, Ketterlin-Geller, Larson, and Clark examine whether a game-based learning environment can enhance middle school students' computational thinking competency, computational thinking self-efficacy, and engagement. The article begins from the claim that computational thinking should be treated as a broad problem-solving competency rather than as programming syntax alone. The authors argue that many K-12 computational thinking interventions emphasize coding concepts, while teachers need approaches that connect to curriculum standards and support computational thinking across disciplinary boundaries.

The intervention, Factory Craft, was built as a Minecraft-based learning system. The authors adapted a Minecraft mod, developed a node-based Minecraft Factory Planner, and integrated the system with Canvas so teachers could monitor student learning. In Factory Craft, students take the role of engineers on a planet called Tiny Town, where they construct efficient automated factories and contraptions to protect the planet from an upcoming meteor shower. The study focused on game levels tied to CSTA middle-school standards for data and analysis, including data collection, data encoding, data sorting, computational modeling, decomposition, abstraction, pattern recognition, and debugging. The design intentionally used Minecraft's crafting, mining, sorting, and organizing affordances while trying to reduce routine collection labor through custom contraptions.

The study used a one-group pretest-posttest design with 31 middle school students recruited from a summer camp at a nonprofit community center in Texas in summer 2022. The sample included 16 male and 15 female students, most of whom identified as Hispanic; students were in sixth, seventh, and eighth grades. The six-session procedure began with demographic, computational thinking competency, and self-efficacy measures; continued with four gameplay sessions of approximately 40 minutes each followed by engagement surveys; and ended with posttests for computational thinking competency and self-efficacy. Total gameplay time was about 160 minutes, facilitated by three researchers.

The measures combined released NAEP items aligned with CSTA standards, a shortened computational thinking self-efficacy survey focused on problem solving rather than programming, and the User Engagement Scale for focused attention, perceived usability, aesthetics, and satisfaction. The overall computational thinking competency gain was not statistically significant, although male students showed a significant subgroup increase. In contrast, computational thinking self-efficacy increased significantly overall. Students with limited Minecraft experience, eighth-grade students, and female students showed significant self-efficacy gains. Engagement results were mixed: students reported relatively high aesthetics and satisfaction, but perceived usability declined across lessons as tasks became more difficult and students had to learn both Minecraft and the separate Factory Planner mechanics.

The discussion is appropriately cautious. The authors suggest that students may have developed implicit game-based knowledge that did not transfer cleanly to far-transfer assessment items, and that three 40-minute game levels may have been too brief for measurable computational thinking competency gains. They recommend more support for transferring implicit knowledge into explicit problem-solving knowledge, longer gameplay, larger studies with control groups, unobtrusive analytics, qualitative triangulation, stronger validation of computational thinking measures, and attention to individual differences. They also note that complex game mechanics can draw engagement toward learning rules and controls rather than subject matter, so teachers may need more tutorial levels and supplementary practice.

For the larger GBLS project, the article is useful only as adjacent educational-game evidence. It offers design cautions that could matter for library game design: a game can raise self-efficacy without producing measurable content transfer, complex interfaces can reduce perceived usability, and learners with different prior gaming experience may need different scaffolds. Those points could inform library coding clubs, Minecraft programs, STEM outreach, or computational-thinking workshops. However, the article does not study libraries, librarians, library collections, library instruction, public programming, makerspaces, information literacy, or library users. Its setting is a summer camp/community-center educational intervention, and its claims belong primarily to K-12 educational technology and computational thinking research.

The source should therefore remain outside the main GBLS review. It may be retained as background for future work on Minecraft-based STEM learning, computational thinking, or educational-game assessment, but it should not be cited as direct evidence for game-based library services unless the review later includes a separate non-library adjacent-design evidence section.
