## Purpose
The study aimed to investigate the effects of game-based learning (GBL) on middle school students' computational thinking (CT) competency, CT self-efficacy, and game-based engagement. It further sought to examine how individual differences—specifically gender, grade level, and prior gaming experience—moderated these outcomes. The authors intended to address gaps in existing literature regarding the transfer of implicit knowledge to explicit skills and the specific impact of GBL on non-cognitive factors like self-efficacy.
Evidence: "To address this need, the current study investigated how game-based learning influenced middle school students’ learning processes, particularly on the development of computational thinking competency, self-efficacy toward computational thinking, and engagement during gameplay. Additionally, the study examined how these outcomes were moderated by individual differences." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Method
The researchers employed a one-group pretest-posttest quasi-experimental design. The intervention involved a custom-developed GBL system called "Factory Craft," built within the Minecraft Education environment using a modded version of "Lumberjack Tycoon" and a node-based planning application (Minecraft Factory Planner). Participants completed six 50-minute sessions over a summer camp period. Data collection included demographic surveys, pre- and post-tests for CT competency (using NAEP items) and CT self-efficacy (using a modified Weese and Feldhausen survey), and repeated measures of game-based engagement (using the User Engagement Scale) after each gameplay session. Statistical analyses included Wilcoxon Signed-Rank tests, paired samples t-tests, and repeated measures ANOVAs.

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 31 middle school students recruited via purposeful convenience sampling from a summer camp at a non-profit community center in Texas. The demographic breakdown was 52% male (n=16) and 48% female (n=15), with 94% identifying as Hispanic. Grade distribution included 12 sixth graders, 9 seventh graders, and 10 eighth graders. Participants were categorized by prior Minecraft experience into "limited" or "sufficient" groups based on self-reported duration and frequency of play.

## Findings
The study found no statistically significant overall improvement in CT competency scores from pretest to posttest (p = .111). However, male students showed a significant increase in CT competency (p = .041), while female students did not. Conversely, there was a significant overall increase in CT self-efficacy (p = .004). Female students demonstrated a significant gain in self-efficacy (p = .028), whereas male students did not show a statistically significant change. Regarding engagement, perceived usability decreased significantly across lessons (p = .046), indicating increased cognitive demand or frustration as tasks became more complex. Gender moderated engagement patterns, with females reporting lower usability in early lessons but higher usability in later sorting tasks compared to males. Prior gaming experience and grade level also influenced self-efficacy gains, with students having limited prior experience and 8th graders showing significant improvements.
Evidence: "The overall difference in students’ computational thinking competency was not statistically significant... General increases were observed for students across the subgroups, but the increases were only statistically significant for male students... We observed that students’ self-efficacy toward CT significantly increased after the gameplay experience... female students increased in CT self-efficacy... whereas male students did not increase significantly." (Results)

## Implications
The authors suggest that GBL is a viable alternative to traditional methods for enhancing CT, particularly for boosting self-efficacy. They recommend that educators provide additional time and practice opportunities for female students to develop CT skills, as they may require more exposure to reach proficiency levels comparable to males. Teachers should also scaffold complex game mechanics to maximize engagement with subject matter rather than game rules. Future research should focus on transferring implicit knowledge to explicit skills and utilizing unobtrusive analytics to triangulate self-reported data.
Evidence: "First, we propose that GBL serves as a viable alternative to some traditional paper-and-pencil activities... Specifically, we suggest that teachers leverage GBL to boost CT self-efficacy, particularly among female students. Moreover, we recommend that teachers offer additional time for female students who have not fully grasped the requisite CT concepts and skills..." (Conclusion and implications)

# Summary

This study by Pan et al. (2024) investigates the efficacy of game-based learning (GBL) in enhancing computational thinking (CT) competency, self-efficacy, and engagement among middle school students. Recognizing that traditional CT instruction often focuses heavily on programming syntax rather than broader problem-solving skills, the authors designed an intervention using a modified Minecraft environment called "Factory Craft." The study aimed to determine if GBL could improve CT outcomes and how individual differences such as gender, grade level, and prior gaming experience moderated these effects.

The methodology employed a one-group pretest-posttest design with 31 middle school students (52% male, 48% female; 94% Hispanic) recruited from a summer camp in Texas. Participants engaged in six 50-minute sessions involving gameplay focused on data analysis, algorithms, and the impacts of computing, aligned with CSTA standards. Data were collected via demographic surveys, CT competency tests (adapted from NAEP items), CT self-efficacy surveys, and repeated measures of game-based engagement using the User Engagement Scale.

The results indicated that while there was no statistically significant overall improvement in CT competency scores (p = .111), male students showed a significant increase in competency (p = .041). In contrast, CT self-efficacy improved significantly for the entire group (p = .004), with female students demonstrating a notable gain in self-efficacy (p = .028) despite not showing significant gains in competency scores. This divergence suggests that while boys may have performed better on objective measures of CT skills, girls experienced greater confidence in their problem-solving abilities through the GBL intervention.

Regarding engagement, students reported high levels of satisfaction and aesthetic appreciation but showed a significant decrease in perceived usability across lessons (p = .046). This decline indicates increasing cognitive load or frustration as tasks became more complex. Gender differences were observed in engagement patterns; females initially reported lower usability but improved in later stages involving sorting tasks, whereas males experienced consistent declines in perceived usability during challenging content-specific tasks.

The authors acknowledge several limitations, including the small sample size, lack of a control group, and reliance on self-reported data which may be subject to response biases. They conclude that GBL is effective for boosting CT self-efficacy, particularly among female students, and recommend that educators provide additional practice time for girls to bridge the gap between self-efficacy and actual competency. Future studies should incorporate control groups, larger diverse samples, and unobtrusive analytics to better understand the transfer of implicit knowledge to explicit skills in GBL environments.
