# Summary

The *2023 Public Library Technology Survey: Summary Report*, authored by Sara S. Goek and colleagues for the Public Library Association (PLA), presents a comprehensive empirical analysis of technology integration within U.S. public libraries. The primary objective is to assess the current landscape of library technology resources, digital literacy services, infrastructure capabilities, and staff support structures, while tracking evolution from the 2020 baseline amidst post-pandemic recovery and federal funding initiatives.

The methodology relies on a nationwide survey administered in fall 2023 via the Benchmark platform. The PLA invited all U.S. public libraries at the administrative entity level to participate. A total of 1,511 libraries completed the survey, yielding a 16.4% response rate. To ensure national representativeness, the results were weighted in post-processing to account for differences between respondents and the universe of all U.S. public libraries. The data is stratified by locale type: city (6% of entities), suburban (25%), and town/rural (69%).

Key findings indicate a significant expansion in technology access and services since 2020. Notably, hotspot lending has increased substantially, with 46.9% of libraries now offering internet hotspots for checkout, compared to 32.6% in 2020. Digital content provision is nearly universal, with 95% of libraries offering e-books and/or e-audiobooks. Furthermore, 95.3% of libraries provide some form of digital literacy training, ranging from basic computer skills to coding and maker technology. However, significant disparities exist by locale. City libraries are far more likely to possess advanced on-site technologies, such as maker production equipment (74.8% in cities vs. 33.5% in town/rural) and digital media production tools (58.9% in cities vs. 18% in town/rural). Similarly, city libraries are more likely to offer streaming media (92.9%) and language learning resources (85.9%) compared to their town/rural counterparts (46.7% and 40.2%, respectively).

Infrastructure analysis reveals persistent gaps in broadband connectivity. While 74.8% of libraries have fiber optic connections, speed disparities remain pronounced. City libraries subscribe to average download speeds over three times higher than town/rural libraries. Critically, 28.4% of all libraries subscribe to internet connections that fall below the Federal Communications Commission’s (FCC) revised broadband standard of 100 Mbps download and 20 Mbps upload; this figure rises to 35.4% for town/rural libraries. Barriers to improving connectivity include contractual lock-ins (40.1%), lack of funding (30.5%), and unavailability of faster speeds in the service area (30.4%).

Staffing and budget constraints emerge as critical limitations. Only 20.7% of libraries employ full-time IT staff, with many relying on consortia or contracted support. Open-ended responses identified limited staff capacity (54%) and insufficient funding (39%) as the primary challenges to providing digital literacy assistance. Additionally, 86% of libraries cited subscription costs as the top factor influencing their ability to offer digital content. Despite these constraints, 60.8% of libraries maintain dedicated budget lines for public access technology, with grants (61.9%) and local government funds (48.9%) being the primary sources.

The report implies that public libraries serve as essential hubs for digital equity, particularly for underserved populations lacking home broadband or digital skills. The authors emphasize the importance of federal funding mechanisms like E-rate and the Digital Equity Act in sustaining these services. They also highlight the emerging need for staff training in new technologies, such as artificial intelligence, noting that 61% of respondents planning future training focus on AI. The data underscores that while libraries have successfully expanded technological reach, systemic inequities in infrastructure and resources between urban and rural settings require continued advocacy and targeted investment.
