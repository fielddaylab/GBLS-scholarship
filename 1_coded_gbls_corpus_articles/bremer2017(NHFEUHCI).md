# Summary

This article by Peter Bremer serves as a narrative case study of a collaborative tabletop gaming event, termed "Game Day," organized jointly by the Rodney A. Briggs Library at the University of Minnesota Morris and the Morris Public Library. The primary objective was to extend library services beyond the university campus to engage the broader community, specifically aiming to bridge the perceived "town/gown" divide and attract diverse patrons through gaming programs. The author posits that such collaborations expand potential audiences and foster future institutional partnerships.

The methodology involves a descriptive account of the event’s planning and execution. The academic librarian initiated contact with the public library director, leveraging her interest in community outreach and innovation. Key logistical decisions included hosting the event at the public library to maximize accessibility for local residents who might be reluctant to visit the university campus. The event was scheduled for a Saturday afternoon in January to accommodate community schedules. Publicity efforts were extensive, utilizing posters, social media, local newspapers, radio, and library websites. Financial constraints were managed by securing funding from the Friends of the Morris Public Library and Briggs Library Associates for refreshments, as neither institution had a dedicated gaming budget.

The population consisted of attendees drawn from both the university and the surrounding community. The author reports that the event reached capacity, initially accommodating 40 people in the public library’s meeting room before additional tables were added. Demographically, the attendees included college students, adults, families, and young children. However, the author notes a significant gap in representation among teenagers, despite targeted publicity on social media platforms.

The findings indicate that the event was highly successful in terms of attendance and participant engagement. Observational data suggests that free food (pizza, snacks, soda) was a strong incentive for attendance. The variety of tabletop games provided—including titles such as *Risk*, *Monopoly*, *Forbidden Island*, and *Exploding Kittens*—facilitated interaction among diverse groups. The author highlights the positive social atmosphere, noting that participants enjoyed themselves and that staff were able to assist newcomers in selecting appropriate games.

Critical limitations and areas for improvement are explicitly discussed. First, the game collection was insufficient for younger elementary school children, as the academic library’s holdings skewed toward adult and teen-oriented titles. The author recommends acquiring simpler games like *Candyland* or *Trouble* for future events. Second, the failure to attract teenagers suggests that current publicity methods may not be effective for this demographic, prompting a recommendation to brainstorm targeted communication strategies or host separate teen-focused gaming events. Finally, the reliance on external funding for food highlights budgetary constraints inherent in such programming.

The implications of this study suggest that inter-library collaborations can effectively mitigate institutional barriers and enhance community engagement. The success of the event supports the broader argument that gaming programs are valuable tools for keeping libraries relevant and accessible. Future iterations of the program will focus on diversifying game collections to better serve younger children and refining outreach strategies to include underrepresented groups like teenagers. This case study provides practical insights into the logistical, financial, and social dynamics of implementing games-based library services in a collaborative context.
