# Summary

Aaron J. Elkins’s 2015 article, "Play! Why School Librarians Should Embrace Gaming in the Library," serves as a persuasive argument for integrating video games into school library collections and programming. The primary objective is to demonstrate that gaming is a powerful tool for developing 21st-century literacies, including critical thinking, problem-solving, collaboration, and information literacy. Elkins challenges the prevailing stereotype of gamers as exclusively young males, citing research indicating that adult women now constitute a significant portion of the gaming demographic (Santovec 2011). The article posits that by engaging with games, librarians can share common experiences with patrons, thereby fostering deeper relationships and better understanding of student interests.

The methodology is non-empirical; it functions as a professional guide grounded in a review of existing literature on game-based learning and library science. Elkins draws on the work of scholars such as James Paul Gee, who argues that libraries should provide access to digital media with the same intent as they do print materials, to support informal learning environments (Gee 2012). The article asserts that games offer "low price for failure" environments where learners can take risks and engage actively rather than passively receiving information (Gee 2008). It further claims that multiplayer gaming fosters collaboration that extends beyond the digital space, leading players to create modifications, strategy guides, and wiki pages, thus enhancing media creation and analysis skills (Hobbs and Rowe 2011; Gee 2003, 2005, 2012).

A significant portion of the article is dedicated to practical implementation. Elkins addresses the barrier of cost and accessibility by recommending specific games that do not require expensive consoles, such as *The Cave*, *Cogs*, *Monster Loves You!*, *Spaceteam*, and *World of Goo*. These titles are selected for their ability to target specific cognitive skills like spatial reasoning, moral decision-making, and collaborative problem-solving. The article emphasizes the equity implications of gaming in libraries, noting that providing access to these resources can help socioeconomically disadvantaged students who may not have opportunities to develop these literacies at home.

Elkins provides a framework for librarians to consider when starting a gaming program, including questions about collection development, circulation policies, and programming options. He suggests that strategy guides can serve as an entry point for reluctant readers and a way to gauge interest before investing in hardware. The article concludes with recommendations for further reading, citing works by Gallaway, Neiburger, and Nicholson, and urges librarians to "get their game on" by playing games themselves. This personal engagement is presented as essential for understanding the medium’s potential to enhance information literacy and capitalize on learners’ intrinsic motivation. The overall tone is encouraging and practical, aiming to demystify gaming for library professionals and position it as a vital component of modern library services.
