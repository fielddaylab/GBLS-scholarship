# Summary

The *Global Music Report 2026*, published by the International Federation of the Phonographic Industry (IFPI), provides a comprehensive analysis of the recorded music industry’s performance in 2025, alongside strategic insights into artist development, regional market dynamics, and policy recommendations. The report documents an 11th consecutive year of global revenue growth, with total revenues reaching an all-time high of US$31.7 billion, marking a 6.4% increase from the previous year. This growth was broad-based, with 57 out of 58 measured markets recording positive revenue changes.

The financial data reveals a continued dominance of streaming, which accounted for 69.6% of global revenues, surpassing US$22 billion. Subscription streaming was the primary driver, growing by 8.8%, while ad-supported streaming grew more modestly at 4.3%. Notably, physical formats experienced a significant rebound, growing by 8.0%—the fastest rate among all formats—largely driven by vinyl, which marked its 19th consecutive year of growth. In contrast, downloads and other digital formats declined by 5.0%, reflecting the industry's shift toward streaming consumption. Regional analysis highlights Latin America as the fastest-growing region (+17.1%), followed by the Middle East & North Africa and Sub-Saharan Africa (both +15.2%). Asia also showed strong improvement with 10.9% growth, while Europe and the USA & Canada posted more moderate gains of 5.6% and 3.5%, respectively.

Beyond financial metrics, the report emphasizes the evolving nature of label-artist partnerships through three detailed case studies: Tate McRae, Balu Brigada, and Olivia Dean. These examples illustrate a shift toward long-term, patient investment in artist development rather than short-term commercial exploitation. For instance, RCA Records’ support of Tate McRae involved balancing her dual identity as a dancer and songwriter, utilizing data-informed but instinct-led strategies to scale her success globally. Similarly, Warner Music’s partnership with Balu Brigada focused on preserving the band’s authentic creative vision while leveraging global infrastructure for touring and distribution. Capitol Records UK’s work with Olivia Dean highlights the importance of trust-based A&R support, allowing the artist to lead creative decisions while the label provided strategic guidance for album production and global rollout. These cases underscore that successful modern partnerships are characterized by flexibility, cultural authenticity, and sustained commitment.

The report also addresses critical policy issues through a five-point manifesto. First, it calls for the upholding of copyright laws to protect the economic and cultural value of music. Second, it urges governments to avoid undermining the competitive marketplace with unnecessary regulatory restrictions. Third, it advocates for responsible AI integration, insisting that AI developers must obtain authorization from right holders before using copyrighted content and must disclose training data. Fourth, it emphasizes the need for effective performance rights collection mechanisms globally. Finally, it highlights the urgent need to tackle streaming fraud, which dilutes revenues for legitimate creators, requiring coordinated action from streaming services, distributors, and law enforcement.

In conclusion, the IFPI report presents an industry that is financially robust but facing complex challenges related to technology and regulation. The findings suggest that future growth depends on maintaining strong copyright frameworks, fostering authentic artist-label relationships, and expanding paid streaming adoption in emerging markets. While the document does not directly address games-based library services, its discussion of synchronization revenues (which include gaming) and the importance of local market investment offers indirect relevance to understanding how music is utilized and valued across different media platforms, including interactive entertainment.
