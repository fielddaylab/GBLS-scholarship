# **Montola, M., & Stenros, J. (Eds.). (2008). *Playground worlds: Creating and evaluating experiences of role-playing games*. Ropecon ry.**

Montola and Stenros's edited volume *Playground Worlds* was published in conjunction with Solmukohta 2008 and belongs to the Nordic role-play/Knutepunkt book tradition. It is a large role-playing and larp design collection rather than library literature. The book gathers journalism, community reflection, design essays, production accounts, and research/theory pieces about tabletop role-playing, freeform, live-action role-playing, pervasive larp, parlor larp, game criticism, documentation, immersion, fantasy, medievalism, and role-playing pedagogy.

The editors frame the volume around "playground worlds": game worlds and ordinary-world play situations that can function as amusement parks, test laboratories, or efforts to expose and impose new perspectives on everyday life. The introduction emphasizes that characters do not exist without worlds and that role-playing games can create powerful experiences of special places and times. Examples include Dragonbane, Ground Zero, The White Road, Agabadan, A Nice Evening with the Family, Agerborn, Totem, and Sanningen om Marika. The editors also describe three design ideals in recent Nordic larp: powerful drama, the physically immersive 360-degree illusion, and pervasive larp, where play spills into streets and ordinary society.

The book is organized into three major sections. "Journalism & Community" includes accounts of the Role-Players' School at Osterskov Efterskole, role-playing game criticism, Dragonbane as ephemera and ruin, event production, work minimization, and British live-action role-play history. "Art & Design" turns to design process, high-resolution larping, hobo-drifter play, adventurous romanticism, jeepform, facade and family drama, narrativism, interaction design, indulgence, and parlor larp. "Research & Theory" includes essays on Ground Zero, adult pretend play, connections between fantasy play in children and adults, Allan Kaprow and happenings, Forge theory, broadcast culture and role-playing culture, larp and modernity, and fantasy/medievalism in role-playing games.

Several chapters are potentially useful as background for GBLS even though the volume is not directly citable as library evidence. Malik Hyltoft's chapter on Osterskov Efterskole describes a school that bases teaching on role-playing and other forms of game play, with narrative units, workshops, portfolio reflection, common ownership, and teachers designing learning materials around role-play structures. Design chapters discuss production labor, interaction design, parlor larp constraints, pervasive-game boundaries, and documentation problems. Theory chapters connect larp to adult pretend play, child fantasy play, performance art, immersion, character, and social worlds. These themes could inform librarians designing TTRPG/LARP programs, game design workshops, or reflective debriefs.

For the larger GBLS project, however, the volume's relevance is indirect. It can help a reviewer understand role-playing and larp as serious design cultures with traditions, vocabulary, production practices, documentation challenges, and theories of immersion and play. It also reminds GBLS work that role-playing services are not only about rules or entertainment; they involve worlds, roles, boundaries, labor, atmosphere, player culture, and post-event documentation. Those insights may be useful for program design, staff training, and comparison with library TTRPG literature.
