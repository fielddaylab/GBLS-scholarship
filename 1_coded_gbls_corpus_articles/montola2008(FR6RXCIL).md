## Findings
The text identifies three dominant design ideals in contemporary Nordic larp: 1) Powerful drama, focusing on dramatic arcs and character development; 2) Physically immersive 360° illusion, emphasizing environmental realism and indexicality; and 3) Pervasive larp, which blurs the line between game and reality by spilling into public spaces. The pedagogical case study finds that role-playing-based teaching at Østerskov Efterskole is feasible, resulting in low student fluctuation and stable staff retention, though grade comparisons were inconclusive due to lack of prior data. The text also highlights the difficulty of documenting ephemeral larp experiences and notes a shift from competitive "gamist" play toward experiential expression within the Nordic community.
Evidence: "Looking at the larps produced and played in recent years, it seems that there are currently three strong design ideals guiding larpmakers... The first and oldest of these ideals is powerful drama... The second design ideal, the physically immersive 360° illusion... The third and youngest design ideal is pervasive larp." (Introduction, pp. 6-7)

## Implications
The authors imply that role-playing games have matured into a legitimate artistic and academic field, capable of serving as tools for social exposure, education, and community building. The success of Østerskov Efterskole suggests that game-based pedagogy can effectively engage students, particularly those with special needs or histories of bullying, by providing alternative learning structures. The text calls for continued documentation and critical analysis of larp to build a historical record and theoretical framework, arguing that such discourse is essential for the medium's future development and recognition as art.
Evidence: "Documenting the games we have played is imperative if we wish to render our rich history visible... Larp is, indeed, growing up." (Introduction, pp. 5-6)

# Summary

The provided text, *Playground Worlds: Creating and Evaluating Experiences of Role-Playing Games*, serves as an introduction to a collection of essays documenting the state of Nordic live-action role-playing (larp) in 2008. Edited by Markus Montola and Jaakko Stenros, the volume aims to transition larp discourse from informal community opinions to structured academic and artistic analysis. The text is organized into three sections: Journalism & Community, Art & Design, and Research & Theory, reflecting a multidisciplinary approach to understanding role-playing games as creative works and social phenomena.

A central finding of the introduction is the identification of three prevailing design ideals in contemporary Nordic larp. The first, "powerful drama," prioritizes narrative structure and character arcs, utilizing techniques such as scripted events and thematic rules to evoke specific emotions. The second, "physically immersive 360° illusion," focuses on environmental realism and indexicality, striving for a setting where every object represents itself to create a sense of physical presence. The third, "pervasive larp," blurs the boundaries between game and reality by integrating gameplay into public spaces, often engaging bystanders or addressing social issues directly. These categories provide a framework for analyzing the diverse outputs of the Solmukohta community, which has evolved from disparate national scenes into a cohesive Nordic tradition.

The text includes a significant empirical case study by Malik Hyltoft on Østerskov Efterskole, a Danish boarding school that bases its curriculum primarily on role-playing and game-based learning. The study reports on the school's first year of operation, noting enrollment growth from 30 to 90 pupils aged 14–17. Pedagogical methods involve "narrative units" where students engage in full-scale larp, functional play, or teacher-led role-playing to cover multidisciplinary subjects. The data indicates low student fluctuation (ratio of 1.38 compared to a national average of 1.65) and high staff stability, suggesting strong engagement with the method. While grade comparisons were limited by a lack of prior data from students' previous schools, the overall average was comparable to national standards. The study highlights the effectiveness of this approach for students with special needs or histories of bullying, arguing that the immersive nature of role-playing fosters motivation and holistic learning. However, it acknowledges challenges in maintaining concentration during repetitive tasks and the dependency on strong teacher collaboration.

Beyond pedagogy, the text addresses the theoretical challenges of larp criticism and documentation. Jussi Ahlroth’s essay discusses the "Schrödinger’s Cat" problem in RPG criticism, where the act of observing or participating in a game alters the experience, complicating objective analysis. The authors emphasize the ephemeral nature of larp, noting that games cease to exist upon completion, making documentation crucial for building a historical record. Despite efforts to document specific games like *Dragonbane* and *Ground Zero*, a robust culture of critical writing remains underdeveloped.

The implications drawn by Montola and Stenros suggest that larp has matured into a legitimate field of artistic and academic inquiry. The shift away from competitive "gamist" play toward experiential expression reflects a broader cultural evolution within the community. The text argues for continued interdisciplinary research and documentation to validate larp as a medium for social engagement, education, and artistic expression. By providing a structured overview of design philosophies and empirical examples, *Playground Worlds* contributes to the legitimization of role-playing games within academic and professional contexts, offering insights relevant to games-based library services by demonstrating how immersive, narrative-driven environments can facilitate learning, community building, and critical reflection.
