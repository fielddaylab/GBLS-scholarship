### Summary
# **Martín-Martín, Ó., Manero, B., & Romero-Hernández, A. (2022). El videojuego como herramienta de alfabetización informacional en estudiantes universitarios [The video game as an information literacy tool for university students]. *Profesional de la información, 31*(4), e310402. https://doi.org/10.3145/epi.2022.jul.02**

Martín-Martín, Manero, and Romero-Hernández evaluate a locally developed serious video game as an autonomous information-literacy learning tool for undergraduate students. The article is directly situated in academic-library instruction: the lead author is affiliated with the Complutense University of Madrid library, and the game was designed from two years of real user queries recorded by the Biblioteca de la Universidad Complutense. The authors argue that university libraries play a central role in teaching information skills, but librarians often face short, voluntary, extracurricular training sessions and must look for more motivating and participatory methods.

The conceptual frame distinguishes gamification from game-based learning. Gamification uses game elements in non-game activities, while game-based learning makes the game itself the learning vehicle. The authors focus on serious games, whose design is tied to specific learning objectives. They connect the project to information literacy, media and information literacy, metaliteracy, DigComp, and the REBIUN adaptation of digital competence for Spanish undergraduate students. Their target competencies are in DigComp area 1, information and data literacy: navigating, searching, filtering, and managing digital information, data, and content.

The game, *El último examen* (*The Last Exam*), was built in Unity and published through Simmer.io for web play. Its narrative follows a university student who arrives at the faculty to complete the last exam before graduating but must first complete three tasks: obtain a library study manual after consulting the catalog, search a bibliographic database for research articles on a specified topic, and attend a library training session about the Mendeley reference manager. The game recreates familiar university spaces, including the library, a library training room, and an exam classroom. Characters include a librarian who provides bibliographic information, a training librarian, and a course professor. A transversal goal is to position library staff as relevant guides when students have information needs.

The design was grounded in 1,639 library queries recorded from February 2018 to February 2020. The authors divided those queries into local/policy information, such as loan periods and access rules, and more generalizable information-literacy needs, such as catalog searching, database searching, and bibliographic/reference-manager questions. They chose the generalizable categories for the game because they could apply across academic libraries. The learning objectives map to identifying the catalog as the main tool for locating a library manual, understanding call numbers as location information, identifying bibliographic databases as sources for articles, using Boolean operators and parentheses in a search strategy, and recognizing reference managers such as Mendeley.

The study used a one-group quasi-experimental pilot design with anonymous pretest and posttest measures. Fourteen undergraduate students, mostly first-year nursing students at the Faculty of Nursing, Physiotherapy, and Podiatry, participated in an intervention lasting under an hour: roughly seven minutes for each test and 15 to 30 minutes for the game. The questionnaire, validated by four expert librarians with information-literacy and library-training experience, included Likert items on library use and perception, 12 multiple-choice information-literacy questions, satisfaction with the intervention, and willingness to repeat it.

The results are encouraging but clearly preliminary. Participants' average information-literacy score rose from 53.57% on the pretest to 85.71% on the posttest, a statistically significant gain with a reported 32.14 percentage-point improvement. Satisfaction was high: 92.85% of participants were satisfied or very satisfied, and all participants said they would repeat the intervention. The activity also seems to have made students more aware of gaps in their own information skills: self-reported satisfaction with their ability to find information declined after playing, suggesting that the game surfaced previously unrecognized learning needs. However, the game did not significantly change library-use frequency or perceptions of the library as a fun/dynamic service.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is valuable because it offers a rare empirical example of a library-built video game tied to actual reference/training demand data and tested against information-literacy outcomes. It shows that a serious game can serve as an introduction, reinforcement, or assessment component within a broader library instruction program, especially when the tasks are aligned with real patron questions and recognizable library workflows. At the same time, the article names several cautions: professional-quality game development is resource intensive, local questions may not fit general-purpose games, participation was much lower than hoped, and voluntary standalone play may require marketing, rewards, and integration into formal instruction. Its strongest GBLS lesson is that games work best as part of an instructional design ecosystem, not as novelty objects detached from librarian-led learning.
