## Purpose
The primary objective is to evaluate the utility of a self-developed serious video game ("El último examen") as an autonomous learning tool for improving information literacy (IL) competencies among undergraduate university students. The study aims to determine if such games can effectively address common library queries and enhance specific digital competencies defined by the DigComp framework.
Evidence: "El presente trabajo plantea como objetivo principal evaluar la utilidad del videojuego... como instrumento de aprendizaje autónomo en alfabetización informacional... en estudiantes de grado universitario" (Section 1).
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employs a quantitative, quasi-experimental single-group pilot design. It utilizes a pre-test/post-test structure to measure changes in participants' knowledge and perceptions before and after playing the video game. The intervention involved a one-hour session where students completed an anonymous pre-test, played the game for 15–30 minutes, and completed an identical post-test. Statistical analysis included Shapiro-Wilk tests for normality and paired Student’s t-tests to assess significance.
Evidence: "se planificó un experimento piloto de caso único y metodología cuantitativa cuasi experimental... utilizando para la recogida de datos la cumplimentación anónima de un pretest inicial y un postest" (Section 2.1).
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 14 undergraduate university students (57.14% female, 42.86% male), aged 18–32. The majority (78.57%) were first-year Nursing students from the Faculty of Nursing, Physiotherapy, and Podiatry at Complutense University of Madrid. Data sources included: (1) an analysis of 1,639 user queries registered over two years at the university library to inform game design; and (2) responses from a validated questionnaire comprising Likert-scale items on library perception and multiple-choice questions on IL competencies.
Evidence: "participando 14 estudiantes... principalmente de primer curso del grado de enfermería" (Section 2.1); "Se han analizado y categorizado 1.639 consultas registradas en un período de dos años" (Section 2.2.1).
Confidence: high

## Findings
The intervention resulted in a statistically significant improvement in information literacy scores. The mean accuracy on IL questions increased from 53.75% in the pre-test to 85.71% in the post-test (t = -7.696, p < .001). Participants reported high satisfaction with the intervention (92.85% satisfied or very satisfied), and 100% indicated they would participate again. Notably, while perceived self-efficacy in information skills decreased from 89.86% to 64.28%, suggesting increased awareness of knowledge gaps, there were no significant changes in actual library usage frequency or general perceptions of the library as a dynamic space.
Evidence: "obteniendo una media de aciertos del 53,75% en la prueba inicial y un 85,71% tras la intervención... Sig. (bilateral) <,001" (Section 3.2); "el 92,85% de los participantes se mostraron satisfechos" (Section 3.3).
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that video games are effective complements to traditional library training programs, particularly for introduction, reinforcement, or evaluation of IL concepts. They suggest that while the game successfully improved competencies and motivation, low voluntary participation highlights the need for professional-grade development, marketing campaigns, and incentive structures. Future work should address local library-specific queries not covered by general IL frameworks and consider integrating games into broader instructional designs rather than relying on spontaneous adoption.
Evidence: "Se concluye que la incorporación de videojuegos como complemento a los contenidos de los programas formativos de las bibliotecas puede resultar provechoso" (Abstract); "la participación de los estudiantes fue muy baja, lo que pone en duda la capacidad de este tipo de videojuegos para atraer a los estudiantes de manera espontánea" (Section 4).
Confidence: high

# Summary

This article by Martín-Martín, Manero, and Romero-Hernández (2022) investigates the efficacy of serious video games as tools for fostering information literacy (IL) among undergraduate students. The study addresses the challenge libraries face in engaging students with traditional IL instruction, proposing game-based learning as a motivational alternative aligned with the European DigComp framework.

The methodology centers on a quasi-experimental pilot study involving 14 university students, predominantly first-year Nursing majors. Prior to the experiment, the authors analyzed 1,639 library queries from a two-year period to identify common information needs, which informed the design of "El último examen" (The Last Exam), a custom 3D video game developed in Unity. The game tasks players with navigating a virtual university environment to complete research-related objectives, such as using library catalogs, searching bibliographic databases with Boolean operators, and utilizing reference management software.

Data collection employed a pre-test/post-test design using an anonymous questionnaire validated by four library experts. The instrument measured three dimensions: library usage/perception (Likert scale), IL competencies (12 multiple-choice questions), and intervention satisfaction. Statistical analysis confirmed normality via Shapiro-Wilk tests, allowing for paired Student’s t-tests to evaluate changes in IL scores.

The findings demonstrate a statistically significant improvement in IL competencies following the intervention. The mean score on IL questions rose from 53.75% to 85.71% (p < .001), indicating that the game effectively taught targeted skills such as database searching and catalog navigation. Participant feedback was overwhelmingly positive, with 92.85% reporting satisfaction and all participants expressing willingness to repeat the activity. Interestingly, while objective knowledge increased, students' subjective confidence in their information skills decreased from 89.86% to 64.28%, suggesting the game helped them recognize previous knowledge gaps. However, the intervention did not significantly alter actual library usage frequency or general perceptions of the library as a dynamic space.

The authors acknowledge several limitations. The small sample size and demographic skew toward Nursing students limit generalizability. Furthermore, despite positive outcomes, voluntary participation was low, raising questions about the ability of such games to attract students spontaneously without institutional support. The study concludes that while video games are valuable for enhancing IL competencies and motivation, they should be integrated as complementary elements within broader instructional frameworks rather than standalone solutions. Future developments should aim for higher production standards, incorporate marketing strategies, and address local library-specific queries to maximize engagement and utility.
