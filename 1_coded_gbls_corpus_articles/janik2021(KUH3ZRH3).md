## Findings
The article argues that the player and the game object form a "bio-object" through intra-action, where neither entity has pre-existing, stable boundaries independent of their interaction. Key findings include:
1.  **Equality of Actants:** The player and the game are equal partners in meaning-making; the game is not merely a passive tool but an active agent that imposes constraints and generates responses.
2.  **Dynamic Agency:** Agency is not possessed by either the player or the game individually but emerges dynamically between them during play. This challenges anthropocentric views that reserve agency solely for humans.
3.  **Meaning Generation through Tension:** Meaning arises from the "rivalry" or tension within the bio-object, such as when players subvert game rules (modding, cheating) or when games exhibit autonomy (glitches, anti-piracy measures).
4.  **Post-Play Imprints:** The effects of intra-action extend beyond the immediate gameplay session, influencing player identity, fan culture (e.g., fan fiction, theories), and academic interpretation.
Evidence: "The game and the player become equal in this new unity... agency is not something that actants have and can use, but rather is a dynamic force that happens between them." (Sections: Intra-actions and Bio-Objects; Conclusion)

## Implications
The article suggests that adopting a posthuman, intra-active framework shifts game studies away from player-centered analysis toward a relational approach. It implies that researchers should view play as an ethical and aesthetic experience of communication with the "Other" (the game object). This perspective encourages recognizing the autonomy and materiality of digital artifacts, acknowledging that they co-create reality and meaning alongside players. It also highlights the need to move beyond anthropocentric language in analyzing human-technology interactions.

# Summary

Justyna Janik’s 2021 article, "Intra-acting bio-object: A posthuman approach to the player–game relation," presents a theoretical framework for re-evaluating the relationship between video game players and the games themselves. The primary objective is to move beyond traditional anthropocentric models in game studies, which often position the human player as the central agent controlling a passive digital environment. Instead, Janik proposes a posthumanist perspective that treats the player and the game as equal, co-constitutive partners in a dynamic process of meaning-making.

The methodology is purely theoretical, synthesizing two distinct conceptual frameworks: Tadeusz Kantor’s notion of the "bio-object" from avant-garde theatre and Karen Barad’s concept of "intra-action" from critical posthumanism and new materialism. Kantor’s bio-object describes a hybrid entity formed by the interaction between an actor and a stage object, where neither retains independent stability outside their relational encounter. Barad’s intra-action posits that entities do not pre-exist their relations; rather, they emerge through material-discursive practices. Janik argues that combining these theories allows for a nuanced understanding of gameplay as a performative act where both human and non-human actants define each other’s boundaries and properties.

The article does not employ empirical data collection or participant sampling. Instead, it utilizes qualitative examples from various video games—such as *The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim*, *Animal Crossing*, *Undertale*, and *Doki Doki Literature Club*—to illustrate how the bio-object functions in digital contexts. Janik demonstrates that during gameplay, a continuous "rivalry" or tension exists between the player’s desire for control and the game’s structural constraints or autonomous behaviors (e.g., glitches, anti-piracy mechanisms). This tension is not a failure of design but the very mechanism through which meaning is generated. For instance, when a player encounters a glitch in *Skyrim*, the game object reveals its materiality and autonomy, forcing the player to reinterpret their experience. Similarly, subversive play practices like modding or cheating represent attempts by players to reassert dominance within this relational dynamic.

Key findings emphasize that agency is not an inherent property of either the player or the game but a dynamic force that emerges between them. This challenges conventional game studies concepts that attribute agency primarily to human users. Janik asserts that both entities are transformed through intra-action: the player develops new skills and physiological responses, while the game object becomes a unique, playable artifact shaped by specific interactions. Furthermore, the implications of this relationship extend beyond the immediate session of play. The "imprints" of these intra-actions persist in fan cultures, academic analyses, and the player’s ongoing identity as a gamer.

The article concludes that viewing the player-game relation through the lens of the intra-acting bio-object offers an ethical and aesthetic framework for game studies. It encourages researchers to recognize the "voice" of the game object and to understand play as a form of communication with the "Other." By acknowledging the ontological difference between human and non-human actants while emphasizing their entanglement, this approach provides a more comprehensive understanding of how meaning is co-created in digital environments. This theoretical shift has significant relevance for Games-Based Library Services (GBLS) by suggesting that library-mediated gaming experiences should not focus solely on user outcomes but also consider the material and agential properties of the games themselves as active participants in the learning or engagement process.
