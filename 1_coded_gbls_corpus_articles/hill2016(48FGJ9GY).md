# Summary

Christa Hill’s 2016 article, "Play On: The Use of Games in Libraries," serves as a comprehensive literature review advocating for the integration of games into library services across public, school, and academic settings. The author addresses the initial skepticism some librarians hold regarding gaming—viewing it as irrelevant or a waste of resources—and counters this by synthesizing evidence that demonstrates gaming’s significant implications for community engagement, digital literacy, and educational outcomes.

The review begins by contextualizing gaming within library history, noting that games have been present in libraries for over a century, originally used for social reform and moral instruction (Nicholson, 2013). Hill argues that modern gaming continues this tradition by fostering community involvement. Gaming programs, such as game nights or tournaments, provide non-intimidating environments that attract patrons who rarely visit the library, particularly adolescent males and seniors. Evidence from Werner (2013) suggests that these programs make libraries more accessible and inviting, encouraging patrons to utilize other services after their initial engagement with gaming.

A central theme of the review is the educational value of games. Citing Phetteplace and Felker (2014) and Elkins (2015), Hill highlights that games promote active participation and contextualized learning. Digital games, in particular, help develop 21st-century skills such as critical thinking, problem-solving, collaboration, and media literacy. The article emphasizes the library’s role as a "social equalizer" by providing access to expensive digital technologies for less affluent communities, thereby helping to close the "digital gap" (Gee, 2012).

The review categorizes games into digital games, video games, role-playing games, and tabletop games. Each category offers distinct benefits; for instance, tabletop games are noted for their portability and affordability, while video games can serve as research sources or curriculum support in academic settings (Thomas & Clyde, 2013; Copeland et al., 2013). The article also discusses gamification—the application of game mechanics to non-game contexts—as a tool for enhancing information literacy instruction and library orientation (Phetteplace & Felker, 2014; Kim, 2015). Additionally, game creation by patrons is presented as a valuable activity that fosters design skills and self-expression (Powell, 2013).

However, Hill acknowledges several challenges in implementing gaming programs. These include librarian hesitancy due to perceived resource waste, noise management issues, theft of materials, budget constraints for high-cost hardware, and potential content challenges from parents (Nicholson, 2013; Werner, 2013; Elkins, 2015). To mitigate these issues, the review suggests collaboration with local gaming retailers and community clubs. Such partnerships can provide expertise, prizes, and volunteer support, reducing the burden on library staff and resources (Blodgett & Bremer, 2014; Werner, 2013).

In conclusion, Hill asserts that games are powerful tools for engaging patrons with ideas and each other. The article recommends that librarians seriously consider gaming programs, develop robust collection policies, and utilize documented successes from peer institutions to justify these initiatives. By integrating games, libraries can enhance their relevance, support educational goals, and foster a more informed and engaged community.
