## Findings
The authors find that abstract board games possess a "modular ontology" characterized by procedural reliability, visual flexibility, and material nonessentiality. The case study reveals that while checkers in the Marshall Islands holds significant social and cultural value (e.g., featured in festivals, acting as a social lubricant), the specific rule set (particularly regarding king promotion) does not carry inherent cultural meaning for the players; it is simply "how the game is played." The authors distinguish between intrinsic semantics (logico-analytical interpretation of formal rules) and extrinsic semantics (culturally specific imaginative interpretations). They argue that the reproducibility of the formal system allows abstract games to travel without cultural "baggage," facilitating cross-cultural dispersal.
Evidence: "In short, playing checkers in Majuro is a meaningful activity that is not informed by the unique set of playing rules... # Summary

This article by Danilovic and de Voogt addresses a theoretical gap in game studies regarding abstract board games, which have persisted for millennia with minimal alteration despite crossing vast cultural and temporal boundaries. The authors argue that traditional frameworks, which often assume a reciprocal relationship between players and the meaningful representation of game elements, fail to account for the stability of abstract games like checkers, senet, and mancala. To resolve this, the authors propose a "modular ontology" and a typology of meaning-making that separates the game’s formal system from its cultural interpretations.

The methodology combines theoretical analysis with a qualitative case study of checkers (*jekab*) in the Marshall Islands, conducted in 2017/2018. The researchers observed and interviewed seven adult male players from diverse atolls who participate in daily play and annual tournaments. The findings indicate that while checkers is deeply embedded in Marshallese social life—serving as a "social lubricant" and a component of cultural heritage—the specific rule variations, such as the mid-move promotion to king, hold no inherent symbolic meaning for the players. The rules are viewed simply as the standard way to play, unrelated to local cultural narratives or historical origins. This supports the authors' broader argument that abstract board games function as self-sufficient formal systems.

The authors define the modular ontology of abstract board games through three properties: procedural reliability (rules remain consistent regardless of context), visual flexibility (boards and tokens can be reimagined culturally without affecting gameplay), and material nonessentiality (the physical composition of the game does not alter its formal identity). They distinguish between "intrinsic semantics," which involve logico-analytical engagement with the rules, and "extrinsic semantics," which involve culturally specific imaginative interpretations. By treating abstract games as "virtual machines," the authors suggest that these games provide a stable structural framework that allows players to project diverse cultural meanings onto them without altering the underlying mechanics.

The implications of this framework are significant for understanding the cross-cultural transmission of games. The authors posit that the reliability of the formal system enables abstract board games to travel across borders unaffected by sociopolitical changes, potentially facilitating the spread of logical and mathematical thinking systems. This approach bridges archaeological evidence with social science perspectives, offering a nuanced tool for analyzing how play practices integrate local variability with universal structural constants. For Games-Based Library Services (GBLS), this distinction between formal structure and cultural meaning is crucial for understanding how abstract games can be utilized in diverse community settings without imposing specific thematic narratives, allowing for flexible, inclusive engagement.
