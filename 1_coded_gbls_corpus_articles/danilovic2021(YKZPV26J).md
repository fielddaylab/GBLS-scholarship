### Summary
# **Danilovic, S., & de Voogt, A. (2021). Making sense of abstract board games: Toward a cross-ludic theory. *Games and Culture, 16*(5), 499-518. https://doi.org/10.1177/1555412020914722**

Danilovic and de Voogt offer a theoretical account of abstract board games, especially traditional, nonproprietary games such as checkers, senet, mancala games, morris games, and other board games whose formal rules and simple boards have traveled across long periods and wide geographic areas. The article is not about libraries, but it has conceptual value for GBLS because tabletop programs and collections often include abstract games whose rules, materials, and cultural meanings do not operate in the same way as heavily themed modern hobby games.

The central problem is that many game theories assume meaningful representation. Game studies often treats games as systems in which players interpret rules, materials, fiction, theme, and social context together. Abstract board games challenge that assumption because they often lack figurative or decorative markings and may use arrangements of squares, holes, lines, stones, seeds, or counters with little culturally specific iconography. Yet these games have moved across borders, persisted for centuries or millennia, and been appropriated by many play communities without always preserving knowledge of origin.

The authors distinguish between reliable and variable qualities. The reliable properties of abstract board games are their self-sufficient formal systems: rules, legal moves, procedural structures, and mathematical relationships that can be reproduced across contexts. The variable qualities are the culturally specific meanings, materials, rituals, interpretations, and social practices that players attach to those formal systems. Abstract board games can therefore function as placeholders or virtual machines: the same formal structure can be implemented with different materials and imbued with different local meanings without changing the identity of the game as a rule system.

Their case of checkers in the Marshall Islands illustrates this distinction. Checkers, known as jekab in Marshallese, uses a rule set found in several Pacific contexts, but those rules do not by themselves explain the game's cultural significance. The game has become part of cultural celebrations, social identity, intergenerational play, and Marshallese heritage, yet the specific formal rule set likely traveled through other historical pathways. The meaning of play is real, but it is not reducible to the intrinsic logic of the rules.

The article develops a modular ontology of abstract board games with three properties: procedural reliability, visual flexibility, and material nonessentiality. Procedural reliability means the formal rules can be transcribed and reproduced across cultural contexts. Visual flexibility means the layout can be rendered in different visual or symbolic ways without changing the game. Material nonessentiality means the board and tokens can be made from many local materials because those materials are not essential to the formal identity of the game. The authors also distinguish intrinsic game semantics, or logico-analytical interpretation of formal rules, from extrinsic game semantics, or culturally specific symbolic interpretation of the game and its materials.

For the larger GBLS project, this source should be used as conceptual grounding rather than evidence of library outcomes. It helps explain why some tabletop games may be especially portable in library programs: their rules can be taught with low-cost materials, different boards, found objects, or culturally local tokens. At the same time, the article warns against assuming that portability equals cultural neutrality. The formal system may travel, but libraries still need to ask how local communities understand, name, teach, display, and value the game.

This is useful alongside culturally situated sources such as Bayeck and Bayeck on African board games. Bayeck and Bayeck caution against flattening local games into generic mechanic labels; Danilovic and de Voogt explain why some abstract game systems can also be formally reproducible while remaining locally meaningful. Together, they support a nuanced GBLS position: libraries can use abstract tabletop games as accessible, low-material, cross-context play forms, but they should still treat names, histories, materials, rules variants, and community interpretation as part of facilitation and advisory.

The limitations are scope-based. The article does not discuss libraries, patrons, programming, collections, instruction, or evaluation. It is a game studies and archaeology theory article. It should not be cited as evidence that abstract board games succeed in library settings. Its value lies in helping the review's conceptual terrain distinguish between game mechanics, materials, themes, and meanings when libraries select and explain tabletop games.
