# Summary

This qualitative study by Nelson and Matthews (2025) examines the social dynamics of Dungeons & Dragons (D&D) games hosted in public libraries, specifically focusing on how these programs balance individual player expression with community building. The research addresses a gap in existing literature, which often consists of practical advice for librarians rather than empirical studies of patron experiences.

The methodology involved participant observation and semi-structured interviews at two Midwest public libraries: Ulfgar, which hosts online games via Zoom, and Quarion, which hosts in-person sessions. The researchers observed multiple game sessions, recording fieldnotes on player interactions, spatial arrangements, and narrative progression. They conducted interviews with 14 participants, including players, Dungeon Masters (DMs), and library staff. The sample comprised five women and nine men, with no transgender participants identified. Data were analyzed using qualitative coding to identify themes related to individuality, community, and the library's role.

The findings indicate that D&D in libraries successfully fosters both individuality and community. Individuality is expressed through character creation, exploration of fantastical scenarios, and "spotlight" moments where players take heroic actions or solve problems creatively. For younger players, particularly at Quarion, the game provided a safe outlet for silliness and experimentation with identities and behaviors that might be restricted in their daily lives. Conversely, community is built through the collaborative nature of the game. Players demonstrated strong teamwork, often risking their characters to save others or consulting less vocal members during decision-making. The DMs were viewed not as authoritarian figures but as facilitators who ensured all players had opportunities to contribute to the shared narrative.

A critical finding is the unique value added by the library setting. Participants highlighted three main benefits: stability, resources, and inclusivity. Libraries provided a reliable schedule for games, which helped maintain player interest over time, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic when online continuity was crucial. Libraries also supplied essential resources, such as rulebooks, 3D printers for miniatures, and access to digital platforms like Roll20, lowering financial barriers to entry. Most significantly, libraries created an intentionally inclusive "safe space." Unlike commercial game stores, which some participants found intimidating or exclusionary, library games were described as welcoming to new players, women, and LGBTQ+ individuals. The presence of library staff helped maintain a neutral, supportive environment where experienced players mentored newcomers without condescension.

The authors connect these findings to broader theories of interest development and belonging. Library D&D programs trigger and sustain situational interest through consistent programming and resource provision. They also facilitate multiple paths to belonging, including communal relationships, achievement, and group membership. The study posits that by allowing diverse groups to experiment with the balance between individual autonomy and collective responsibility, library D&D games contribute to democratic practices. The authors acknowledge the limitation of a small sample size, which restricts generalizability, but argue that the insights offer valuable models for other libraries seeking to enhance their community programming. They recommend future research focus on how libraries can further support marginalized communities through gaming and explore the potential of role-playing games to foster empathy across different social identities.
