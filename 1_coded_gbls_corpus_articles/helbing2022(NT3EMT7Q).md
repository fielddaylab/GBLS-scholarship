## Findings
The formative assessments embedded in the activities indicated that learning objectives were met, as students successfully completed all tasks within the allotted time. Informal evaluations showed high levels of student enjoyment and engagement across all formats. Key logistical findings included:
*   **Time Investment:** Initial planning required approximately 12 hours; subsequent iterations required only 3–5 hours due to reusable components.
*   **Format Adaptability:** Escape rooms functioned effectively in team-based (optometry), individual hybrid (pharmacy), and fully remote individual (medicine/optometry) settings.
*   **Grading Implications:** Assigning points based on speed/ranking caused anxiety regarding fairness among optometry students. Providing completion-only points for pharmacy students reduced stress and allowed facilitators to focus on struggling learners.
*   **Accessibility:** Potential barriers exist for non-native English speakers due to spelling/grammar dependencies in clues; team formats may mitigate this.
Evidence: "The formative assessments embedded into the activities demonstrated that learning objectives were met... Observations of and interactions with the students while participating in the escape rooms revealed an overall high level of enjoyment and engagement."
Confidence: high

## Implications
Librarians can effectively use escape rooms to teach information literacy in health sciences, adapting to various constraints (space, budget, public health mandates). Important considerations include choosing between team vs. individual formats based on learning goals, managing the significant initial time investment for design, and carefully considering grading structures to maintain a low-risk learning environment. The authors recommend starting with simple clues to build confidence and testing activities with colleagues prior to implementation.

# Summary

This case report by Helbing et al. (2022) details the implementation of educational escape rooms within the Health Sciences Libraries at the University of Houston to enhance information literacy instruction for health professions students. The authors argue that traditional library lectures often fail to engage students or ensure skill retention, whereas game-based learning offers a low-risk environment for practicing critical thinking, problem-solving, and collaboration. The study presents four distinct iterations of escape rooms tailored to optometry, pharmacy, and medical students, demonstrating the adaptability of this method across in-person, hybrid, and fully online formats.

The methodology involved a backward design process, where librarians first established specific learning objectives—such as using PubMed, applying PICO frameworks, and locating journal impact factors—and then developed clues to assess these skills. The activities were piloted sequentially: an in-person team-based escape room for optometry students in 2019; a hybrid individual activity for pharmacy students in March 2020; a fully remote individual orientation for medical students in July 2020; and a fully online individual iteration for optometry students in February 2021. Each scenario framed the library search task within a realistic research proposal context, requiring students to solve puzzles to unlock a final code within a 45-minute timeframe.

The population included approximately 100 first-year optometry students, 130 second-year pharmacy students, and 30 first-year medical students. Data collection was informal, relying on facilitator observations of student engagement and anecdotal feedback rather than rigorous quantitative metrics. The findings indicate that all participating groups successfully met the learning objectives by completing the tasks within the allotted time. Observations noted high levels of enjoyment and engagement across all formats. The authors highlight significant logistical insights: initial design required approximately 12 hours, but subsequent iterations took only 3–5 hours due to reusable digital components. Furthermore, the study contrasts the effects of grading structures; optometry students graded on speed reported concerns about fairness, whereas pharmacy students receiving completion-only points experienced a more supportive environment that allowed facilitators to assist struggling individuals.

The authors conclude that escape rooms are a viable and flexible strategy for health sciences library instruction. They emphasize the importance of considering learner diversity, particularly for non-native English speakers who may face barriers with text-heavy clues, suggesting team-based formats as a potential mitigation strategy. The report serves as a practical guide for librarians, outlining cost considerations (minimal financial cost but significant time investment) and design best practices, such as starting with simple clues to build confidence and testing activities with colleagues before student implementation. This work contributes to the growing literature on game-based learning in academic libraries by providing concrete examples of adaptation during shifting educational landscapes, including the transition to remote instruction due to the COVID-19 pandemic.
