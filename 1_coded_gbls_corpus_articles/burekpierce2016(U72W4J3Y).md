  Contribution_Text: Today's game-based and multimedia library programs are not new; they continue a
    long-standing struggle to balance imagination and instruction, play and professionalism in
    library service. Burek Pierce (2016) shows that between 1876 and 1925, children's librarians
    used toys, games, pictures, maps, puzzles, scrapbook-making, lantern slides, and realia as
    child-centered service media informed by the child study and kindergarten movements. These
    resources did not displace books; they linked tactile, visual, social, and creative learning to
    reading, memory, curiosity, peer interaction, and a welcoming children's room. The later retreat
    from playful youth services came as library leaders sought efficiency, uniformity, dignity, and
    alignment with adult departments, treating the "experimental temptation" of nonbook media as a
    threat to professional seriousness. For GBLS, this history reframes games as a recurring
    public-library strategy rather than a digital-age novelty.

### Summary
# **Burek Pierce, J. (2016). The reign of children: The role of games and toys in American public libraries, 1876-1925. *Information & Culture, 51*(3), 373-398. https://doi.org/10.7560/IC51304**

Burek Pierce provides a historical argument that games, toys, pictures, puzzles, and other nonbook resources were not late twentieth- or twenty-first-century novelties in public libraries. In late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century children's rooms, librarians used games and tactile materials as part of serious youth services. The article is informed by the child study and kindergarten movements, both of which encouraged adults to understand children's developmental needs and to use sensory, visual, and creative materials to support learning. In that setting, children's librarians saw play, making, and handling objects as compatible with library service rather than as distractions from it.

The article revises book-centered histories of children's librarianship by examining professional discussions and material culture around nonbook resources. Burek Pierce shows that children were once barred or viewed cautiously in many public libraries, but the emergence of children's rooms created space for more child-centered professional practice. Librarians such as May Lowe imagined a "reign of the children" marked by colorful pictures, dissected maps, sliced animals, scrapbook-making, and other resources that would attract young users and transfer enthusiasm for pictures and playthings into enthusiasm for books, knowledge, and the library itself.

A major contribution is the detailed account of what early games and toys did pedagogically. Dissected maps required children to handle pieces, understand spatial relationships, master geography, and connect place names to visual form. Sliced Animals and related games combined image recognition, spelling, and assembly. Scrapbooks were both calming activities and creative productions, sometimes made by children for other children. Picture bulletins, stereoscopes, stereopticons, lantern slides, and realia brought visual and material learning into children's rooms. These examples show that early librarians were already designing multimodal learning environments, not simply adding amusements to book collections.

For GBLS, the article is especially useful because it frames playful library service as a long-running professional tension rather than a recent technology debate. Games and toys did not displace books in these early children's rooms; they often connected directly to books, stories, geography, history, art, and moral instruction. Burek Pierce also notes that when games moved from family settings into children's library rooms, they likely supported peer interaction among children as well as adult-guided learning. This history helps explain why contemporary libraries can treat games as part of access, learning, sociality, creativity, and public culture.

The article also explains why this early playful orientation receded. Some librarians questioned whether games and amusements belonged in libraries, and leaders such as Montrose J. Moses and Clara Whitehill Hunt pushed children's rooms toward the dignity, uniformity, efficiency, and book-centered seriousness associated with adult departments. Burek Pierce calls this opposition a banishing of "the experimental temptation": nonbook, colorful, playful services were increasingly treated as undisciplined or insufficiently professional. Later youth programs that used games tended to stress direct instruction, measurable improvement, and approved methods.

The application to the GBLS review is strong but historical. Burek Pierce does not assess modern game programs, circulation, or digital media. Instead, she supplies a professional genealogy: present-day arguments over apps, computer games, interactive books, toys, and atypical materials repeat older anxieties about whether playful, child-centered services support or dilute the library's mission. The article should be used in the introduction and historical frame to show that GBLS is not an abrupt break from librarianship but a recurring effort to align library service with children's agency, community needs, educational theory, and changing media forms.
