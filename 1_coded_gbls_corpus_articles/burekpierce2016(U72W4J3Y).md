## Findings
The analysis reveals that early children's librarians actively incorporated nonbook resources to create engaging, "homelike" environments. Key findings include:
1.  **Prevalence of Nonbook Media:** Dissected maps, sliced animals, scrapbooks, and picture bulletins were standard in children's rooms, intended to stimulate interest and facilitate learning through tactile engagement.
2.  **Educational Influence:** Librarians adopted Froebelian kindergarten principles, believing that visual and tactile aids improved comprehension and memory more effectively than text alone for young or illiterate patrons.
3.  **Professional Tension:** A significant conflict existed between youth services librarians, who advocated for playful, experimental methods, and senior library administrators/adult services professionals, who viewed these practices as undignified and contrary to the library's mission of serious self-improvement.
4.  **Shift in Practice:** By the 1920s, pressure for professional uniformity and efficiency led to the suppression of game-oriented programming. The "experimental temptation" was largely banished in favor of book-centered services aligned with adult library standards.
Evidence: "The belief that libraries ought to both teach and delight their young patrons by going beyond the book was increasingly central to youth services in this era, but this approach was not shared by all." (p. 389)

# Summary

Jennifer Burek Pierce’s 2016 article, "The Reign of Children: The Role of Games and Toys in American Public Libraries, 1876–1925," provides a historical analysis of the integration of nonbook media into early American public library youth services. The study challenges the traditional historiography that portrays libraries primarily as repositories for serious literature, arguing instead that games, toys, and visual aids were foundational to the development of children's librarianship during this period.

The methodology relies on an examination of professional library literature from the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, alongside archival artifacts such as dissected maps, sliced animals, scrapbooks, and stereopticon slides. Pierce situates these materials within the broader context of the child study movement and Froebelian kindergarten education, which emphasized tactile learning and the distinct developmental needs of children. Early librarians, influenced by figures like G. Stanley Hall and Friedrich Froebel, believed that young patrons required engaging, multimodal resources to foster interest and comprehension, particularly given the limited literacy skills of many children at the time.

Key findings indicate that children's rooms were designed as active, "homelike" spaces filled with colorful pictures, puzzles, and creative supplies. Dissected maps, for instance, were not merely novelties but educational tools that taught geography, morality, and national identity while requiring manual dexterity and cognitive engagement. Similarly, scrapbooks and sliced animal games promoted literacy and concentration. These resources were intended to attract children who might otherwise be intimidated by traditional library environments, thereby expanding the library's reach to diverse socioeconomic groups.

However, the article highlights a significant professional tension. While youth services librarians championed these experimental methods, senior administrators and adult services professionals often viewed them with suspicion. Critics argued that games and amusements detracted from the library's dignity and its core mission of providing "good books" for self-improvement. This conflict culminated in a shift toward standardization and efficiency in the 1920s. Influenced by calls for professional uniformity, the profession largely abandoned the playful, experimental approaches of the earlier era. The "reign of children," characterized by peer interaction and creative expression, was curtailed in favor of book-centered services that aligned with adult library norms.

Pierce concludes by drawing parallels between this historical period and contemporary debates over digital media and games in libraries. She suggests that current librarians facing skepticism about nonbook resources can find validation in the historical precedent of early youth services professionals who successfully advocated for the educational value of play. The article serves as a reminder that the tension between institutional tradition and innovative, user-centered services is a recurring theme in library history, urging modern practitioners to recognize the enduring relevance of engaging, multimodal approaches to youth engagement.
