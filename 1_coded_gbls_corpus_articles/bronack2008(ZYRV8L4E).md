# Summary

This article introduces and analyzes "Presence Pedagogy" (P2), a pedagogical framework developed for use within 3D immersive virtual worlds, specifically focusing on the Appalachian Educational Technology Zone (AET Zone) at Appalachian State University. Grounded in social constructivist theory, P2 aims to overcome the limitations of traditional asynchronous distance education tools by fostering a persistent, collaborative community of practice. The authors argue that while standard online platforms often fail to support social presence and serendipitous interaction, 3D virtual environments enable a more authentic social learning experience where students and faculty co-exist as avatars, facilitating continuous engagement across course and program boundaries.

The methodology is primarily descriptive and theoretical, detailing the design principles of AET Zone while incorporating empirical data from a survey of 121 current and former Instructional Technology students administered in Spring 2007. The student population typically consists of working K-12 educators attending graduate school part-time. The study highlights ten core principles of P2, including asking questions to correct misperceptions, stimulating background knowledge, capitalizing on the presence of others, facilitating interactions, supporting distributed cognition, sharing tools, delineating context, encouraging exploration, fostering reflective practice, and utilizing technology for dissemination. Each principle is mapped to specific virtual spaces within AET Zone, such as the "So What? Saloon" for inquiry, the "Information Gardens" (virtual library) for resource access, and the "Commons" for general interaction.

Key findings from the survey data indicate a strong sense of community among participants. Specifically, 71.9% of respondents strongly agreed and 21.5% agreed that they felt part of an effective and supportive learning community due to the program. Furthermore, 81.8% strongly agreed and 11.6% agreed that the program promotes continuous, collaborative, and active learning. The authors assert that these results demonstrate the efficacy of P2 in creating a flattened hierarchy where expertise is distributed among all participants—students and faculty alike—rather than being confined to traditional instructor-student dynamics. The virtual environment allows for "just-in-time" interactions and serendipitous encounters that are integral to the learning process, contrasting with the scheduled, isolated nature of face-to-face or standard online courses.

The implications for library and information science services are significant, particularly regarding the role of librarians in virtual educational spaces. The article describes scenarios where student avatars interact with reference librarian avatars at a "virtual reference desk" within the Information Gardens to locate resources on topics such as university accountability. This illustrates how P2 integrates information literacy and resource discovery directly into the social fabric of the learning community. Librarians and library services are positioned not merely as providers of static content but as active participants in the community of practice, facilitating access to tools and resources that support collaborative projects and reflective inquiry. The study suggests that for games-based or immersive library services to be effective, they must be embedded within a pedagogical framework that values social presence, peer collaboration, and the continuous negotiation of meaning among diverse learners. By leveraging the persistent nature of 3D worlds, educators and librarians can create environments where learning is an ongoing, socially constructed process rather than a discrete event.
