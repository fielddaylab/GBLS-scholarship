### Summary
# **Bronack, S., Sanders, R., Cheney, A., Riedl, R., Tashner, J., & Matzen, N. (2008). Presence pedagogy: Teaching and learning in a 3D virtual immersive world. *International Journal of Teaching and Learning in Higher Education, 20*(1), 59-69.**

Bronack, Sanders, Cheney, Riedl, Tashner, and Matzen describe Presence Pedagogy, or the P2 Model, through AET Zone, a 3D immersive virtual world used for more than seven years in Appalachian State University's Reich College of Education. The article is not about public libraries or game programming, but it is directly relevant to the GBLS strand on services inside game-like virtual worlds because it treats a persistent 3D environment as a social, instructional, and information-service space. Its opening example includes graduate students working in AET Zone, library science students posted at a virtual reference desk in front of a virtual library, and a higher education student being escorted by a reference librarian avatar into virtual stacks to locate resources.

The article argues that conventional distance education tools do not sufficiently support social presence, serendipitous interaction, informal learning, or communities of practice. AET Zone was built as an alternative: a persistent 3D environment where students, faculty, peers, experts, novices, tools, and resources are available across course, cohort, department, and program boundaries. The authors ground this model in social constructivist theory and describe learning as participation in a community of practice where knowledge is socially constructed, learners move from novice to expert with guidance, and shared tools, artifacts, dispositions, and repertoires accumulate over time.

Presence Pedagogy emphasizes that everyone in the virtual community can become a potential instructor, peer, expert, or novice. This matters for GBLS because many game-based or virtual-world services depend on co-presence rather than content delivery alone. In AET Zone, students can see others through avatars, approach them, use text and audio communication, ask questions, receive just-in-time support, and move through spaces designed to prompt conversation and discovery. The authors describe P2 as student powered and instructor navigated: learning emerges through feedback loops among learners, instructors, tools, tasks, spaces, and the community.

The article identifies ten core P2 principles: asking questions and correcting misperceptions; stimulating background knowledge and expertise; capitalizing on the presence of others; facilitating interactions and encouraging community; supporting distributed cognition; sharing tools and resources; encouraging exploration and discovery; delineating context and goals to act upon; fostering reflective practice; and using technology to achieve and disseminate results. These principles are connected to concrete virtual-world places, including the So What? Saloon, the Case Study Conference Center, the Commons, Information Gardens, the Training Shoppe, the Discussion Depot, Chit Chats, the Break Time Game House, Wiki World, blogs, wikis, databases, visual cues, and greeter bots.

The design details are especially useful for GBLS. The Commons brings participants into shared space at the start of each session, increasing the likelihood of cross-cohort and cross-program interaction. Information Gardens functions as a virtual library. Spaces such as coffee houses, saloons, depots, shops, newsstands, hypermazes, and game houses give abstract learning activities visible context and navigation. Resources persist in-world, so student work, case analyses, discussion artifacts, RSS feeds, blogs, wikis, journals, databases, and multimedia become part of a living curriculum. These are not game mechanics in the narrow sense, but they show how spatial metaphor, avatar presence, shared artifacts, and exploration can structure learning in ways that resemble game-world service design.

Bronack et al. also provide limited evidence of student response. In a spring 2007 survey of 121 current and former instructional technology students, 71.9% strongly agreed and 21.5% agreed that they felt part of an effective and supportive learning community; 81.8% strongly agreed and 11.6% agreed that the program promoted continuous, collaborative, and active learning. These survey results do not isolate the effect of the 3D world or compare it with other platforms, but they support the authors' claim that AET Zone helped create a durable learning community.

For the larger GBLS project, the article's value is conceptual and design-oriented. It shows why virtual game-like worlds should not be treated as decorative delivery channels. If a library builds services inside Minecraft, Roblox, a social VR world, a campus virtual world, or another persistent online environment, the service needs more than a static replica of a room. It needs visible community entry points, staff and peer presence, tools for synchronous and asynchronous interaction, places where resources can persist, activities that make use of the world, and opportunities for patrons to contribute artifacts back to the shared environment.

The limits are important. Bronack et al. study higher education, not public-library GBLS; the article does not analyze entertainment games, patron gaming programs, teen services, public access, collection development, or library outcomes. It also comes from an early 3D-world moment and describes an institutionally controlled platform rather than a public commercial game world. The source should therefore support the virtual-platform section as adjacent design evidence, not as direct evidence that libraries should run game-world programs.

Suggested review placement: Add to `# Programming Formats and Service Models` > `## Programs Inside Game Worlds and Virtual Platforms`, immediately after the Mon (2012) paragraph and before Chow et al. (2012). Proposed text: "Bronack et al. (2008) broaden the virtual-platform discussion with AET Zone, a 3D immersive learning world that included a virtual library, Information Gardens, a virtual reference desk, and library science students serving as reference librarian avatars. Their Presence Pedagogy model treats persistent virtual worlds as communities of practice built through co-presence, shared spaces, just-in-time peer and expert support, reusable artifacts, visual metaphors, and exploration; for GBLS, the article is adjacent rather than library-program evidence, but it clarifies why game-like service worlds need social infrastructure and activity design rather than static room replicas."

# Hobart, E. (2024). Describing games for special collections libraries. *RBM: A Journal of Rare Books, Manuscripts, and Cultural Heritage, 25*(1), 50-64. https://doi.org/10.5860/rbm.25.1.50

Unique identifier: `hobartDescribingGamesSpecial2024`; Zotero item key `CG9SZAXC`; attachment key `Y5ZH3PRJ`.

Hobart (2024) describes the process of cataloging games in the Eberly Family Special Collections Library at Pennsylvania State University. The article begins from a cataloging problem that is central to GBLS infrastructure: games appear in special collections, but catalogers are often trained around printed formats, and no single standard fully addresses games as rare or special-collections objects. RDA provides general instructions, OLAC's object best practices gather guidance for three-dimensional objects, and the DCRM manuals support artifact-level description, but special-collections game cataloging often requires drawing from all of them.

The Penn State game collection included sixty-four cataloged items as of February 2024, chiefly from the nineteenth century, with some twentieth- and twenty-first-century materials. Formats included toys, card games, board games, puzzles, blocks, toy books, and construction games. Hobart explains why Penn State chose item-level MARC 21 cataloging rather than only archival or group-level description: the games did not share one provenance, country, genre, date range, or access point, and researchers needed detailed information about printers, techniques, materials, formats, provenance, and copy-specific condition.

The article's two examples, *Bal d'enfants: travesti* and *Musikalische Zauber-Karten*, show how detailed catalog records can make games legible as both play objects and research artifacts. Records included transcribed title and publication/manufacture information, supplied dates and places when necessary, physical descriptions that enumerated components, RDA content/media/carrier types, source-of-title notes, summaries of gameplay and physical construction, citations to external references, local notes for provenance or imperfections, subject headings, genre/form terms, and added entries for printers, publishers, lithographers, and former owners.

Several technical points are directly useful for the GBLS review. Penn State cataloged games using the visual materials workform in OCLC; coded MARC leader/006 as "r" for three-dimensional artifact or naturally occurring object; coded MARC 008/33 as "g" for game or "w" for toy; and added a local Blacklight facet labeled "Games/Toys." Hobart notes that other catalogs used varied and less user-facing format terms such as three-dimensional object, image, other, kit, realia, object, manuscript/archive, non-print, or unknown. The Penn State facet made the game collection more discoverable to users.

For the larger GBLS project, Hobart strengthens the argument that game collections require metadata labor beyond acquisition. Circulating and special-collections game services depend on description that can support discovery, identification, condition tracking, provenance research, component inventories, publisher/printer research, and genre access. This is especially important for older, unique, incomplete, or materially complex games that cannot be adequately represented by title and broad format alone.

The article also identifies infrastructure gaps. DCRMR currently addresses books, DCRM(G) is closest but defines graphic material around two-dimensional representation, OLAC is strong for objects but oriented toward general object cataloging, LCGFT is limited for games and toys, and detailed reference sources for the bibliographic history of games are uncommon. Hobart notes that cataloging from one specimen inevitably flattens some manifestation-level history, but also argues that getting games into catalogs and enhancing those records increases the likelihood that users will find them.
