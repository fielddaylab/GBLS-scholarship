# Summary

*Dragons in the Stacks: A Teen Librarian’s Guide to Tabletop Role-Playing* by Steven A. Torres-Roman and Cason E. Snow is a professional guide designed to assist librarians in implementing tabletop role-playing games (RPGs) as programming for young adult patrons. Published in 2014 as part of the Libraries Unlimited Professional Guides series, the text functions as an instructional manual rather than an empirical study. Its primary objective is to demystify the hobby of tabletop RPGs, providing librarians with the necessary background knowledge, practical tools for collection development, and strategies for program execution.

The authors begin by defining tabletop RPGs, distinguishing them from live-action role-playing (LARP) and computer role-playing games (CRPGs). They emphasize that tabletop RPGs are collaborative storytelling experiences where players assume character roles within an imaginary setting, guided by a Game Master (GM) and structured by rule systems. The introduction outlines the historical origins of RPGs, tracing their roots to miniatures wargaming and fantasy literature, particularly noting the influence of *Dungeons & Dragons*. The authors argue that RPGs require literacy, socialization, and strategic thinking, making them beneficial for teen development.

A central theme of the text is the suitability of RPGs for library environments. The authors assert that libraries are ideal venues for such programming due to their role as community centers and safe spaces. They highlight the economic efficiency of RPG programs, noting that initial costs can range from free to $100, which is sustainable given shrinking library budgets. Space requirements are minimal, typically needing only a room for five to eight people. The text addresses potential barriers to implementation, including librarian unfamiliarity with the hobby and community objections, offering advice on how to respond to concerns about violence or inappropriate content.

The book is structured into five chapters. Chapter 1 provides a brief history of tabletop RPGs. Chapter 2 focuses on selection and collection development, guiding librarians on where to purchase games and how to choose appropriate titles for teen audiences. Chapter 3 addresses cataloging and classification issues specific to gaming materials. Chapter 4 covers programming logistics, including advertising, planning, and managing game groups. The authors emphasize the "Golden Rule" of RPGs: if all participants are enjoying themselves, the group is playing correctly, regardless of adherence to strict rules or genre conventions.

Chapter 5 presents a curated collection of RPG reviews. Each review follows a standardized format, providing publication details, genre tags, and a numerical evaluation on a scale of 1–5 across six criteria: Complexity (ease of learning), Popularity (community recognition), Support (availability of supplementary materials), Completeness (self-sufficiency of the core product), Versatility (adaptability to different genres), and Emulation (faithfulness to source material). This structured approach allows librarians to compare games based on practical implementation factors rather than subjective preference alone.

The authors acknowledge that their selections are somewhat idiosyncratic and do not cover every available RPG. They encourage librarians to explore beyond the provided list, directing readers to an accompanying website for additional resources. The text concludes by reinforcing the idea that RPGs can serve as a gateway to broader literary and cultural engagement for teens, leveraging existing interests in gaming to foster library participation. Overall, the work serves as a comprehensive resource for librarians seeking to integrate tabletop role-playing into their young adult services, balancing theoretical context with actionable practical advice.
