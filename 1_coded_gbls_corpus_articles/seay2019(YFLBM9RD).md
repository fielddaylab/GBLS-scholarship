# Summary

Jared Alexander Seay’s 2019 article, "Considering Games in Libraries and Such — The Ultimate Library Instruction," published in *Against the Grain*, presents a narrative reenactment of a gamified library instruction session. The primary objective of the piece is to illustrate the potential of scenario-based games to enhance student engagement and learning outcomes in information literacy education. Rather than presenting empirical data from a controlled study, the author employs a descriptive, qualitative approach, detailing a specific instructional event designed as a role-playing game (RPG) centered on a fictional nuclear meltdown crisis.

The methodology involves a simulated environment where students are placed in a library classroom transformed into a high-stakes scenario. The setting includes atmospheric elements such as low lighting, mechanical hums, and visual projections of a "Nuclear Core" with rising temperature gauges. The participants, identified as students attending a scheduled library class, are divided into five teams, each stationed at tables equipped with two computers. The narrative serves as the data source, relying on observed student behaviors and reactions throughout the session. While the exact number of participants is not explicitly stated, the structure implies approximately ten students based on the team and computer configuration.

The instructional activity requires teams to "hack" into the nuclear core to prevent a meltdown by solving a series of puzzles. These puzzles are directly tied to information literacy skills, specifically requiring students to perform searches in the Web of Science database to find answers that unlock subsequent levels. The game design integrates both digital and physical components; for instance, teams must locate printed articles hidden in a locked box to decipher codes, thereby reinforcing the relevance of analog resources alongside digital databases. As teams progress, their advancement is visually tracked on classroom screens, fostering a competitive atmosphere.

The findings reported in the narrative highlight a significant shift in student engagement. Initially, students exhibit confusion and skepticism, with some displaying "latent eye rolling" upon realizing the activity involves database searching. However, as the immersive elements take effect—particularly the visible competition between teams and the urgency of the countdown timer—the mood shifts dramatically. The text notes that "audible excitement filled the room," and students became deeply invested in the task. Despite challenges, such as the difficulty of interpreting clues from printed articles, the teams successfully navigated most levels, demonstrating proficiency in database searching under pressure. Although the narrative concludes with a fictional "meltdown" due to time constraints, the pedagogical success is underscored by a student’s comment: "That database thing was kind of cool. Can we do it again?"

The implications of this case study suggest that gamification can effectively mitigate student apathy in library instruction. By framing technical skills within a compelling narrative, librarians can transform routine tasks into engaging challenges. The article argues that such immersive experiences facilitate learning without the monotony often associated with traditional instruction ("nary an observation of drying paint"). This approach supports the broader integration of games-based library services as a viable strategy for improving information literacy education.
