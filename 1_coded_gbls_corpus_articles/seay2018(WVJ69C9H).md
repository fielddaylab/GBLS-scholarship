### Summary
# **Seay, J. A. (2018). Considering games and gamification in libraries & associated entities: How the longing for tabletops has revitalized games. *Against the Grain, 30*(3), 64-67.**

Seay's "How the Longing for Tabletops has Revitalized Games" is a practitioner essay about the resurgence of tabletop and analog games in a highly digital culture. The article is not research evidence, but it gives the GBLS review useful language for why board games, card games, escape rooms, LARP, and megagames should be treated as significant library formats rather than nostalgic curiosities. Seay frames the board game renaissance as a response to high-tech life and as a source of face-to-face community.

The column begins with an anecdote from a library classroom in which Seay discovered that many students had never played a board game. That moment becomes a contrast with the later resurgence of tabletop gaming: people gather in pubs, public libraries, board game cafes, hobby shops, and International Tabletop Day events to play games with boards, cards, pieces, and other tangible materials. Seay highlights board game cafes as social spaces where patrons value community and a chance to put down smartphones.

The main conceptual frame is John Naisbitt's "high tech high touch" idea. Seay argues that as communication becomes increasingly mediated by screens, email, chat, text, and social media, people may long for real human contact, tactile materials, synchronous conversation, and nonverbal cues. Tabletop games meet that longing because they gather people around a table in real time and make interaction visible, embodied, and social. The point for GBLS is not anti-technology; rather, analog play can answer needs that digital systems often intensify.

Seay also connects tabletop games to broader market and cultural shifts. He notes the growth of the hobby game market, the rise of board game versions of video games, and the reverse trend of digital versions of board games. This matters because it suggests that analog and digital game cultures are intertwined rather than separate. Libraries that collect or program tabletop games are engaging a live, evolving cultural field, not simply preserving an older form.

The column's library implications are broad. If board game cafes thrive by offering community, tables, game access, and social play, libraries are already well-positioned to support similar needs through public space, collections, programming, and facilitation. Seay also argues that libraries' interest in escape rooms may be only the beginning of a wider turn toward immersive group learning formats such as LARP and megagames. These formats extend the high-touch logic of tabletop games into larger interactive learning environments.

For the GBLS project, this source helps articulate why analog games remain central even in a literature review that includes digital games, VR, and gamification. Analog games can support social presence, communication, community-building, low-tech access, tactile engagement, and collaborative learning. The column pairs well with more concrete sources such as Copeland et al. (2013), Forsythe (2021), Robson et al. (2018), and Seay's later analog-space columns.

Its limitations are important. The article is playful, anecdotal, and speculative, with no systematic data about library patrons or program outcomes. It should therefore be used as practitioner discourse and conceptual framing rather than evidence that tabletop programs produce particular learning or social outcomes. Its best use is to support the review's claim that libraries should match game format to purpose: analog tabletop formats are especially strong where co-presence, social connection, and material interaction are desired.
