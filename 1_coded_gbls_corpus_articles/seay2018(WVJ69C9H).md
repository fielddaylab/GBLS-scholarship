# Summary

Jared Alexander Seay’s 2018 column, "Considering Games and Gamification in Libraries & Associated Entities — How the Longing for Tabletops has Revitalized Games," published in *Against the Grain*, explores the intersection of analog gaming trends and library services. The article posits that the recent resurgence of board games and tabletop activities is not merely a nostalgic revival but a significant cultural response to the isolating nature of pervasive digital technology. Seay argues that libraries are uniquely positioned to capitalize on this trend by providing spaces for "high touch" human interaction, thereby enhancing community engagement and educational outcomes.

The author begins with an anecdotal account from his professional experience as a library instructor. Approximately five years prior to publication, Seay attempted to introduce a board game to a class of 27 undergraduate students. He observed widespread confusion among the participants, culminating in one student admitting he had never played a board game before. When Seay asked if others shared this lack of experience, he discovered that none of the students in the room had ever played a board game. This incident served as a catalyst for the author’s reflection on the perceived decline of analog play among digital natives. However, Seay notes that his initial conclusion—that analog gaming was dead—was premature. He observes a concurrent global renaissance in tabletop games, evidenced by the growing popularity of board game cafes and increased market sales.

To explain this paradox, Seay invokes John Naisbitt’s concept of "High Tech High Touch." Naisbitt theorized that as society becomes more technologically saturated, individuals increasingly crave personal, human contact to counterbalance the sterility of digital communication. Seay supports this with personal observations, such as family members texting each other while sitting at the same table, and a self-reported anecdotal poll of roughly a dozen students who expressed a preference for physical books over digital screens due to their tactile qualities. The article cites market data indicating that the hobby game market exceeded $1.4 billion in 2016, growing by 21%, suggesting a robust commercial viability for analog games. Quotes from board game cafe managers highlight that customers value these spaces for fostering community and providing an opportunity to disconnect from smartphones.

Seay connects these broader societal trends directly to library services. He notes that libraries have already begun adopting immersive activities such as escape rooms, which he describes as "even more high touch" than traditional tabletop games. The author argues that these analog environments facilitate synchronous, face-to-face interaction with full non-verbal interpretation capabilities, offering a reprieve from the solitary nature of screen-based communication. He suggests that this trend augurs well for the adoption of other immersive group learning experiences, including Live Action Role-Playing (LARP) and megagames. The core implication is that libraries should view analog gaming not as a relic of the past, but as a vital tool for fostering human synergy and community connection in an increasingly digital world.

While the article does not present formal empirical data or controlled studies, it effectively synthesizes anecdotal evidence, market statistics, and sociological theory to argue for the integration of analog games into library programming. Seay concludes that while analog resurgence will not replace the digital world, it will significantly influence it by providing necessary spaces for tangible human interaction. The article serves as a persuasive case for librarians to embrace tabletop gaming as a means to enhance instruction, inspire learners, and fulfill the community’s desire for meaningful social engagement.
