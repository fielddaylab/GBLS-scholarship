# Summary

This source text is a professional review of the book *Let’s Roll: A Guide to Setting up Tabletop Role-Playing Games in Your School or Public Library* by Lucas Maxwell, published in *School Library Journal* in May 2023. The review serves as an evaluation of the book's utility for library professionals interested in implementing games-based services, specifically Tabletop Role-Playing Games (TTRPGs).

The primary purpose of Maxwell’s work, as described in the review, is to provide ample resources and support material for library staff who are either entirely new to TTRPGs or are familiar with gaming but concerned about specific aspects of launching an RPG programming initiative. The review notes that the book addresses several "specific hurdles" inherent in this process. These include finding institutional support, marketing the programs to patrons, and managing potentially chaotic program sessions. By targeting these practical challenges, the book aims to lower the barrier to entry for librarians who may feel intimidated by the complexity of TTRPGs.

Regarding methodology, the source is a qualitative professional review rather than an empirical study. Therefore, there is no experimental design, data collection, or statistical analysis presented. The reviewer assesses the book based on its content structure, tone, and practical applicability. The review highlights that the book is "well organized" and offers "very specific strategies for success." It also notes a "gentle, conversational tone," which contributes to an "earnest and encouraging authorial voice." This tone is presented as beneficial for alleviating the anxiety or trepidation librarians might feel when considering new programming initiatives.

The population addressed by the book, and consequently the review, consists of school and public library professionals. The review explicitly states that the book is recommended for "any school or public library professional who has felt trepidation about introducing an RPG program." Within the book itself, Maxwell includes case study statements from other librarians who are running TTRPGs in varied library settings and with a range of age groups. While the review mentions these case studies as evidence of the book's breadth, it does not provide specific details regarding the sample sizes, demographics, or outcomes of these case studies. The reviewer notes that Maxwell leans heavily on his own personal experiences as a high school librarian, but balances this with the aforementioned case studies from other practitioners.

In terms of findings and content evaluation, the review describes the book as "slim but comprehensive." It lists several supplemental materials included in the text that contribute to its utility: a glossary of common terms, a sample program proposal, case studies, recommended reading lists, and brief reviews of simple gateway RPGs. These gateway RPGs are noted as being learnable and playable by librarians and patrons with "minimal preparation or expertise," which is a key finding for practitioners seeking low-barrier entry points into games-based services. The review concludes with a verdict that the book is recommended, emphasizing its value as a supportive resource for those hesitant to engage in RPG programming.

The implications of this review for a literature on Games-Based Library Services (GBLS) are centered on practical implementation and professional development. The text suggests that TTRPGs are a viable form of library programming that can be successfully implemented with the right support materials. It highlights the importance of addressing institutional and logistical challenges, such as marketing and session management, alongside the pedagogical or recreational aspects of gaming. By providing specific strategies and case studies, the book aims to demystify TTRPGs for librarians, thereby encouraging broader adoption of games-based services in school and public library settings. The review underscores that successful implementation requires not just knowledge of the games themselves, but also skills in advocacy, marketing, and crowd management.
