# Summary

This article by Ramón Alberto Manso Rodríguez (2022) presents a descriptive bibliographic review analyzing trends, good practices, and recommendations for applying gamification in Media and Information Literacy (MIL) programs within library settings. The study aims to synthesize existing literature to reinforce the application of game-based strategies in libraries and provide actionable guidelines for implementation.

The methodology follows the PRISMA statement for systematic reviews. The author conducted a search in Scopus and Web of Science databases using the keywords “gamification,” “media and information literacy,” and “library” for publications between 2011 and 2021. Additionally, a snowball sampling technique was employed to identify further relevant sources. From an initial pool of 233 records, 49 documents were selected based on inclusion criteria, which required peer-reviewed articles or conference proceedings focusing on gamification in library-based MIL contexts. The final corpus consisted primarily of studies from academic libraries (89.7%), with minor representation from public and school libraries.

The findings reveal a stable trend in research output over the decade, with 2011 being the most productive year. The analysis categorizes the literature into theoretical characterizations and practical implementations. Key themes identified include "Information literacy" and "Library escape rooms." The review highlights that gamification serves as an effective tool to increase user motivation, engagement, and autonomy in learning. It facilitates the development of critical skills such as information evaluation, combating misinformation (fake news), and mastering database usage. Common gamification mechanics observed in successful implementations include role-playing, cooperation, rewards, points, levels, badges, and immediate feedback. The article distinguishes between two primary application areas: library orientation, which familiarizes users with institutional resources, and MIL instruction, which focuses on deeper skill acquisition related to accessing, using, and producing information.

Despite the benefits, the author notes challenges in implementation, such as the tendency of some librarians to use gamification merely as a vehicle for exposition or passive quizzes, lacking interactive elements. Successful practices often involve simple games, mini-games, or digital platforms like Moodle, Kahoot, Unity3D, and Minecraft. The review emphasizes that failure should be integrated as part of the learning process, and that co-creation with users and faculty enhances the effectiveness of these programs.

Based on the synthesis of literature and the author’s expertise, the article proposes a six-step framework for implementing gamified MIL programs: (1) defining the target audience and their informational needs; (2) establishing a competency tree; (3) evaluating existing projects to identify best practices; (4) developing a game prototype that integrates specific learning objectives with game mechanics; (5) implementing the program; and (6) conducting systematic evaluation and feedback. The conclusion asserts that gamification is a valuable strategy for promoting lifelong learning and deep understanding of complex concepts, provided that design considerations prioritize interaction, accessibility, and meaningful engagement over mere entertainment.
