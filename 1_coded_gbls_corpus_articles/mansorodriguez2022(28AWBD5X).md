### Summary
# **Manso Rodriguez, R. A. (2022). Gamificacion en bibliotecas para la alfabetizacion mediatica e informacional, tendencias, buenas practicas y recomendaciones. *Bibliotecas. Anales de Investigacion, 18*(3), 1-15.**

Manso Rodriguez offers a descriptive literature review of gamification in library-based media and information literacy (MIL) programs. The article works from UNESCO's integrated idea of media and information literacy: the capacity to locate, retrieve, evaluate, use, produce, and communicate information across formats and media. Its purpose is not to report a new game or assessment, but to synthesize trends, good practices, and recommendations for libraries that want to design gamified MIL instruction.

Methodologically, the study uses documentary analysis supported by bibliographic review. Manso Rodriguez searched Scopus and Web of Science for "gamification" AND "media and information literacy" AND "library," focused on publications from 2011 through 2021, removed duplicates, screened according to inclusion and exclusion criteria, and then added additional sources through chain searching. The final corpus contained 49 relevant studies: 42 from database searching and 7 from citation chaining. The review categorizes the literature into theoretical studies that characterize gamification in library MIL and practical studies that report implementation.

Several trend findings matter for GBLS. The literature is heavily academic-library centered: 89.7 percent of the authors worked in academic libraries, compared with 6.2 percent in public libraries and 4.1 percent in school libraries. Keywords cluster around information literacy, library escape rooms, instructional design, academic libraries, library instruction, video games, games in libraries, media information literacy, and game design. The review identifies two main gamified MIL service forms: library orientation, where games introduce users to catalogs, electronic resources, library spaces, and general services; and MIL instruction, where games teach access, use, production, diffusion, evaluation, communication, citation, database searching, and critical information skills.

The article also gathers the game elements most often used in library MIL settings. These include role play, cooperation, movement, scenes and story, collection or selection, question-and-answer structures, rewards and penalties, feedback, points, levels, exploration, time pressure, competition, strategy, resource management, and capture or elimination mechanics. Manso Rodriguez is careful to note that gamification is not simply points and rewards. Drawing on the literature, he emphasizes that users must recognize the meaning and value of the content, be able to experiment without fear of failure, interact socially, and solve problems in a context where game mechanics support learning rather than merely decorate a tutorial.

The article's clearest practical contribution is a six-step implementation guide. Libraries should first define the target audience and its information and training needs; second, create a "tree of competencies" that identifies the MIL skills users need; third, evaluate other library projects and identify good practices; fourth, build a game prototype by choosing an MIL model, defining specific learning objectives, mapping game mechanics and learning mechanics, creating supporting instructional materials, designing the game, testing it with collaborating users, and adjusting it; fifth, implement the training program; and sixth, evaluate and gather systematic feedback so the program can be revised.

For GBLS, Manso Rodriguez strengthens the design and implementation layer of the review. The article connects gamification to lifelong learning, active learning, critical thinking, fake-news evaluation, collaborative learning, information-search tasks, citation work, database use, and the development of advanced communication and information skills. It also reinforces several cross-cutting cautions: games should be simple enough for users to understand, learning objectives should remain primary, failure should be part of the learning process, games should balance information seeking and information use, and librarians should treat gamified instruction as both teaching and assessment.

The main limitation is that this is a review and recommendation article rather than a tested intervention. Some claims about positive effects are synthesized from the reviewed literature rather than independently measured here, and the study's database strategy may miss non-indexed practitioner work. Still, its value for the larger GBLS project is substantial because it consolidates a decade of library gamification/MIL discourse and converts it into an actionable design sequence that can guide future GBLS program planning.
