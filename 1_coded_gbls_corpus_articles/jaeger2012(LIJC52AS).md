### Summary
# **Jaeger, P. T., Bertot, J. C., Thompson, K. M., Katz, S. M., & DeCoster, E. J. (2012). The intersection of public policy and public access: Digital divides, digital literacy, digital inclusion, and public libraries. *Public Library Quarterly, 31*(1), 1-20. https://doi.org/10.1080/01616846.2012.654728**

Jaeger, Bertot, Thompson, Katz, and DeCoster examine how the concepts of digital divide, digital literacy, and digital inclusion have shaped Internet-related policy and public-library service. Their central concern is the gap between concepts and policies: public libraries are expected to provide Internet education, access, and assistance, but policy language often uses these terms inconsistently and does not reliably fund or include libraries in decision making. The article is therefore less a study of a particular library program than an information-policy argument about how public libraries became frontline institutions for public access to digital information.

The authors distinguish the three core terms. Digital divide names gaps in access to Internet technologies, broadband, hardware, and related infrastructure, often structured by socioeconomic status, education, geography, age, disability, language, and literacy. Digital literacy names the skills and abilities needed once access exists, including the ability to locate, evaluate, and use digital information. Digital inclusion is treated as the policy effort to combine high-speed Internet access with literacy, training, outreach, and service to unserved and underserved populations. The article stresses that these concepts shift as technology changes, so policy that focuses only on devices or broadband may miss participation, accessibility, affordability, training, and content-compatibility problems.

The policy history moves from the Communications Act of 1934 and the Telecommunications Act of 1996 to the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, the Broadband Technology Opportunities Program, the Broadband Initiatives Program, and the FCC's National Broadband Plan. Jaeger et al. argue that U.S. policy has often prioritized technical means of access more than social conditions of access and literacy. They also criticize contradictions in policy, especially proposals that rely on public libraries as broadband and digital-literacy infrastructure while threatening programs such as E-Rate that help fund school and library connectivity.

Public libraries occupy the article's practical center. The authors cite national data showing that nearly all public libraries offer public Internet access, many are the only free public computer and Internet provider in their communities, and large numbers provide formal or informal technology instruction. Libraries offer public-access computers, Wi-Fi, one-on-one help, online-search training, database instruction, and employment-related assistance. The article emphasizes that patrons often come to libraries not only because they lack home access, but because they need skilled assistance with forms, government services, employment resources, and other online tasks.

The article's strongest claim is that libraries are being asked to solve digital-inclusion problems without matching governance, funding, or policy authority. Demand for public-access technologies, training classes, and online resources increased while many public-library budgets declined. Federal policies and initiatives acknowledged libraries as central to digital inclusion, yet libraries were often excluded from policy design and did not always receive direct support from broadband or workforce programs. The authors call for three changes: do not reduce library funding while demanding more digital-inclusion work, include public libraries in policy discussions and design, and build better interagency partnerships that recognize libraries as community-based intermediaries.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful background for thinking about equity, access, public technology, and the social responsibilities of libraries. It helps frame why game-based services involving VR headsets, gaming labs, consoles, virtual worlds, or digital literacy cannot be evaluated only as entertainment or novelty; they also sit inside broader questions of public access, affordability, accessibility, training, and staff capacity. However, the article does not discuss games, play, gamification, game-based learning, game collections, library gaming programs, or playful service design. It is a public-library digital-inclusion policy source rather than a GBLS source.

Because the review already includes more directly relevant sources on VR access, game collections, information-literacy games, and inclusive tabletop programming, Jaeger et al. should not be added to `review.md`. It is best retained as explicitly excluded background that may be useful later if the project develops a broader policy appendix on public access, digital inclusion, and library technology infrastructure.
