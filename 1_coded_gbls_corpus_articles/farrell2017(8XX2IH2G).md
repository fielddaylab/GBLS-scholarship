## Purpose
To assess the information and technology needs of scholars (faculty, staff, and graduate students) at the University of Minnesota who use video games for research and teaching, with the goal of informing multidisciplinary library strategies for collection development and service provision.
Evidence: "The authors conducted 30 semistructured interviews to gather data about these needs and understand how the University of Minnesota Libraries can facilitate access to games and technology." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Method
Qualitative study utilizing semistructured narrative interviews. The researchers identified a population of 92 scholars through database searches (SciVal Experts), website reviews, librarian recommendations, and snowball sampling. Thirty participants were interviewed. Data was coded using a controlled vocabulary via Google Forms, with two reviewers coding the first 15 interviews to ensure consistency before one reviewer handled the remaining 15.
Evidence: "We conducted semistructured narrative interviews of faculty, staff, and graduate students who use games or gaming technology in their work... We used a Google form to code data from the audio... we reviewed the audio from the first 15 interviews in tandem and resolved disputes with the codes and analysis methodology." (Methods section)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The study population consisted of 92 identified scholars at the University of Minnesota Twin Cities across four disciplinary groups: Arts and Humanities, Social Sciences, STEM, and Health Sciences. The final sample included 30 interviewees (17 faculty/staff and 13 graduate students). The sample overrepresented STEM participants (52% response rate) and underrepresented Health Sciences (19% response rate).
Evidence: "We identified 92 people from four broad disciplinary groups... We interviewed 30 people from this population, an overall response rate of 33 percent. The interview sample overrepresented the STEM population... and underrepresented health sciences." (Results section)
Confidence: high

## Findings
1. **Usage Patterns:** 28/30 participants used games in research; 23/30 used them in teaching. Most (21/30) used games for both purposes.
2. **Disciplinary Trends:** Arts and Humanities scholars primarily used games as objects of study/texts. STEM scholars focused on game development, instrumentation, and software needs. Health Sciences utilized peripherals for instrumentation. Social Sciences used games as course material and web-based platforms.
3. **Information Needs:** Common sources included journals, Google Scholar, and colleagues. Arts and Humanities relied heavily on traditional library materials; STEM relied on colleagues and web sources. Non-traditional materials like game manuals and trade magazines were noted but difficult to access via libraries.
4. **Technology Needs:** PCs were the most common equipment need. PC games were more prevalent than console games in academic use. Software needs were highest in STEM and Health Sciences.
5. **Funding Barriers:** Graduate students, particularly in Arts and Humanities and Social Sciences, faced significant funding barriers, often paying out-of-pocket. Tenured faculty primarily used grants. Untenured faculty sought diverse funding sources.
Evidence: "Arts and humanities participants used the most information sources... STEM participants had the most software needs overall... Graduate students, especially those from arts and humanities, are at a major funding disadvantage compared to colleagues in the sciences." (Results and Conclusion sections)
Confidence: high

## Implications
Libraries should adopt a multidisciplinary approach to video game collections, moving beyond single-department focus. Recommendations include collecting PC games alongside consoles, providing peripherals for makerspaces, and creating guides for non-traditional materials. Libraries can mitigate funding barriers for graduate students by providing access to technology and assisting with grant-seeking skills.
Evidence: "We propose a strategic approach to video game services and collections focused on disciplinary needs... building a collection focused on PC games, a few console games, cutting-edge equipment with game design software, and a collection of peripherals." (Conclusion)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This qualitative study by Farrell, Neeser, and Bishoff addresses a gap in library literature regarding the specific information and technology needs of scholars using video games for academic purposes. While previous studies have focused on game design programs or anecdotal evidence, this research provides a multidisciplinary assessment at the University of Minnesota (UMN), a large doctoral-granting institution without a dedicated game design program. The authors aimed to understand how libraries can facilitate access to games and technology across diverse disciplines, including Arts and Humanities, Social Sciences, STEM, and Health Sciences.

The methodology involved identifying 92 potential participants through database searches, web reviews, and snowball sampling. Of these, 30 faculty, staff, and graduate students participated in hour-long semistructured interviews. The sample included 17 faculty/staff and 13 graduate students. The authors acknowledge limitations regarding generalizability due to the single-institution focus and potential sampling bias, noting that STEM was overrepresented while Health Sciences were underrepresented. Data was coded using a controlled vocabulary to analyze disciplinary trends in information sources, technology requirements, and funding mechanisms.

Key findings reveal that video games are utilized extensively across disciplines for both research (28/30 participants) and teaching (23/30 participants). Disciplinary differences were pronounced in how games were employed. In Arts and Humanities and Social Sciences, games were frequently treated as primary sources or texts for critical analysis. Conversely, STEM scholars predominantly engaged in game development, using games as instrumentation for data collection, or studying educational technology applications. Health Sciences participants uniquely utilized peripherals, such as Wii balance boards, for physiological research.

Regarding information needs, traditional library resources like journals and books were most valued by Arts and Humanities and Social Sciences scholars. However, STEM and Health Sciences researchers relied heavily on colleagues and web-based sources. The study highlighted a significant barrier in accessing non-traditional materials, such as game manuals and trade magazines, which are rarely held by academic libraries but are essential for certain research contexts.

Technology needs varied significantly by discipline. Personal computers (PCs) were the most universally required equipment, with PC games being more common in academic settings than console games. This contrasts with many library collections that prioritize consoles due to ease of circulation and lack of digital rights management (DRM) restrictions. STEM and Health Sciences participants reported higher needs for specialized software, including proprietary and open-source tools, while Arts and Humanities scholars had minimal software requirements.

A critical finding concerned funding disparities. Graduate students, particularly in the humanities and social sciences, faced substantial financial barriers, often paying out-of-pocket for games and technology required for their research. In contrast, tenured faculty primarily secured funding through grants. The authors argue that libraries can play a pivotal role in mitigating these inequities by providing access to technology and supporting grant-seeking initiatives for students.

The implications for library services are substantial. The authors recommend a strategic, multidisciplinary approach to collection development that reflects the diverse needs of the institution. This includes expanding collections to include PC games, providing peripherals for experimental use, and creating guides to help scholars locate ephemeral materials like game manuals. Furthermore, recognizing the high level of interdepartmental collaboration among game scholars, libraries should foster cross-disciplinary partnerships in service design. The study concludes that while faculty may have access to funding, the provision of library-held resources can reduce financial pressure on all users and support emerging pedagogical and research practices involving video games.
