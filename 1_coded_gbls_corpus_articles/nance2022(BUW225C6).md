# Summary

Megan Nance (2022) presents a comprehensive review of the intersection between academic library services, student mental health, and the therapeutic benefits of play. The article addresses the rising prevalence of mental health issues among college students, citing data from the Association for University and College Counselling Centre Directors (2019) which identifies anxiety, depression, and stress as the primary concerns affecting nearly half to sixty percent of the student population. Despite these high rates, Nance notes a significant gap in service utilization, with only 13% of students seeking help from campus counseling centers. This disparity highlights the need for accessible, low-stigma interventions that can reach students who may not engage with traditional clinical services.

The core argument of the article is that academic libraries are uniquely positioned to address this gap by integrating play-based resources into their collections and spaces. Nance synthesizes existing literature to demonstrate that playfulness in adulthood is not merely a leisure activity but a critical developmental tool that enhances coping skills, creativity, and cognitive function. Citing studies by Magnuson and Barnett (2013) and Proyer (2011), the article establishes a correlation between adult playfulness and positive outcomes such as reduced stress, higher self-esteem, and improved academic performance. The text distinguishes between "play" as an activity and "playfulness" as a personality trait characterized by flexibility, engagement, and creative problem-solving.

Nance categorizes library play initiatives into three main types: coloring, kinetic building materials (e.g., Legos, Play-Doh), and video games. For coloring, the article highlights its ability to induce a meditative state, reduce anxiety, and provide structure without the pressure of creation from scratch. The repetitive nature of mandala coloring is compared to mindfulness practices, offering students a "micro-break" that restores attention and reduces fatigue. Regarding kinetic materials, Nance references the Lego Group’s research on stress relief and the concept of "Serious Play," noting that hands-on building activities promote a sense of accomplishment and tangible progress, which can counteract feelings of burnout and isolation. Finally, the article defends the use of video games in libraries, countering negative stereotypes by citing research that links casual gameplay to stress reduction comparable to meditation, as well as improvements in mood, self-esteem, and social connection through multiplayer features.

The implications for library practice are significant. Nance argues that these resources should be available year-round rather than limited to high-stress periods like midterms and finals. By providing these materials, libraries can destigmatize mental health support by framing it as a routine part of academic life rather than a clinical intervention. The article emphasizes that while these play initiatives are not substitutes for professional therapy, they serve as vital preventative measures that encourage healthy coping mechanisms and self-care. Ultimately, Nance concludes that diversifying library holdings to include play-based materials supports the holistic mission of higher education by fostering an environment where students can maintain mental well-being alongside academic rigor.
