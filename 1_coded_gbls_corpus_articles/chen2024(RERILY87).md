## Purpose
The study aimed to evaluate the effectiveness of a knowledge check-based concept map-guided gaming (KC-CMG) strategy compared to a conventional concept map-guided gaming (CMG) strategy in secondary-level chemistry learning. Specifically, it sought to determine differences in learning achievement, problem-solving awareness, critical thinking tendency, germane cognitive load, and learning behaviors between the two groups.

## Method
A quasi-experimental design was employed with two groups: an experimental group using the KC-CMG strategy and a control group using the CMG strategy. The intervention lasted approximately five weeks (two weeks of formal course instruction, twice a week). Data collection included pre- and post-tests for learning achievement, pre- and post-questionnaires for problem-solving awareness, critical thinking tendency, and germane cognitive load, behavioral logs from the digital game, and semi-structured interviews with five randomly selected students from each group. Statistical analyses included ANCOVA for achievement and psychological measures, sequential analysis for behavior patterns, and grounded theory coding for interview data.
Evidence: "The experimental group employed the KC-CMG strategy, while the control group undertook the CMG strategy... The experiment was conducted for a total of 2 weeks, with classes twice a week." (Method section)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The participants were 48 seventh-grade students (24 in the experimental group: 12 males, 12 females; 24 in the control group: 11 males, 13 females). The setting was a secondary school chemistry class focusing on the "Classification of Substances" unit. The learning environment was a self-developed digital game built using RPG Maker, incorporating concept maps and a two-tier knowledge check mechanism (factual questions followed by conceptual reasoning questions).

Confidence: high

## Findings
1. **Learning Achievement:** The KC-CMG group showed significantly higher adjusted mean scores than the CMG group (F = 7.17, p < 0.01).
2. **Problem-Solving Awareness:** The KC-CMG group demonstrated significantly higher problem-solving awareness (F = 6.49, p < 0.01).
3. **Critical Thinking Tendency:** The KC-CMG group exhibited a significantly higher tendency toward critical thinking (F = 5.10, p < 0.05).
4. **Germane Cognitive Load:** The KC-CMG group reported significantly higher germane cognitive load (t = 2.73, p < 0.05), indicating deeper mental model construction.
5. **Behavioral Patterns:** Sequential analysis revealed that experimental group students who failed second-tier conceptual checks were more likely to revisit concept maps (F2→RE) compared to the control group. Experimental group students also showed higher success rates in subsequent learning challenges after knowledge checks.
6. **Interviews:** Experimental group students reported increased motivation, better conceptual clarity, and enhanced reflection due to the two-tier testing. Control group students noted engagement and efficiency but lacked the reflective depth seen in the experimental group.
Evidence: "The results indicated a significant difference between the groups (F = 7.17, p < 0.01)... KC-CMG effectively enhances learners’ chemistry learning achievement." (Results section)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors suggest that integrating two-tier knowledge checks into concept map-guided digital games can enhance conceptual understanding and critical thinking by forcing students to validate their reasoning rather than just recalling facts. They recommend that educators adjust scaffolding based on learner behaviors observed in game logs. Future research should address the time-consuming nature of game development by creating reusable templates for teachers.
Evidence: "Furthermore, this study implies that teachers can adjust their teaching scaffolds and provide strategies according to learners’ learning behaviors in digital game-based learning." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This quasi-experimental study investigates the efficacy of a knowledge check-based concept map-guided gaming (KC-CMG) strategy in secondary chemistry education. The primary objective was to compare this novel approach against a conventional concept map-guided gaming (CMG) strategy, assessing impacts on learning achievement, problem-solving awareness, critical thinking tendency, germane cognitive load, and specific learning behaviors.

The study involved 48 seventh-grade students divided into an experimental group (n=24) and a control group (n=24). Both groups utilized a self-developed digital game focused on the classification of substances. The control group engaged with concept maps and answered first-tier factual questions. The experimental group followed the same structure but additionally completed second-tier conceptual questions designed to probe reasoning and correct misconceptions, constituting the "knowledge check" mechanism. The intervention spanned approximately five weeks, including pre- and post-tests, questionnaires, behavioral logging, and interviews.

Quantitative results demonstrated that the KC-CMG strategy significantly outperformed the CMG strategy across all measured variables. ANCOVA analyses revealed significant differences in learning achievement (F = 7.17, p < 0.01), problem-solving awareness (F = 6.49, p < 0.01), and critical thinking tendency (F = 5.10, p < 0.05) favoring the experimental group. Furthermore, independent samples t-tests indicated that the KC-CMG group experienced significantly higher germane cognitive load (t = 2.73, p < 0.05), suggesting more effective mental model construction.

Behavioral analysis provided insight into these outcomes. Sequential analysis of game logs showed that students in the experimental group who failed second-tier conceptual checks were significantly more likely to revisit and re-read concept maps (F2→RE behavior) compared to the control group. This iterative process allowed learners to identify gaps in their understanding and reinforce conceptual connections. Consequently, the experimental group demonstrated higher success rates in subsequent learning challenges.

Qualitative data from interviews supported these findings. Experimental group students reported that the two-tier testing forced them to reflect on *why* answers were correct or incorrect, enhancing their motivation and conceptual clarity. In contrast, control group students appreciated the engagement and efficiency of the game but did not report the same depth of reflective practice. The authors conclude that the knowledge check mechanism serves as a critical scaffold, transforming passive consumption of concept maps into active validation of understanding.

The study acknowledges several limitations. The sample size was small (N=48), limiting generalizability. The findings are specific to chemistry and may not transfer to other subjects. The intervention duration was relatively short (approximately five weeks). Additionally, the development of such integrated game systems is resource-intensive. The authors recommend creating basic templates to facilitate broader adoption by educators. Overall, the study provides empirical evidence that combining concept maps with rigorous knowledge checks in digital games can significantly enhance cognitive engagement and learning outcomes in science education.
