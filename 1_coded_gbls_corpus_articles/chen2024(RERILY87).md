### Summary
# **Chen, K.-F., Hwang, G.-J., & Chen, M.-R. A. (2024). Knowledge check-based concept mapping in digital games: Impacts on students' learning performance and behaviors. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 72*(4), 2297-2324. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-024-10378-6**

Chen, Hwang, and Chen study how a knowledge check-based concept map-guided gaming strategy affects secondary students' chemistry learning. The article begins from a common problem in digital game-based learning: students may play through tasks, guess answers, or follow game prompts without connecting game activity to formal disciplinary concepts. Concept maps can help learners organize relationships among concepts, but concept maps alone may not ensure conceptual understanding. The authors therefore propose knowledge check-based concept map-guided gaming, or KC-CMG, as a scaffold that combines concept maps with two-tier knowledge checks inside a digital game.

The study was conducted in a chemistry unit on the classification and separation of substances. The researchers built a role-playing digital game in RPG Maker, aligned with a middle-school chemistry curriculum. Learners moved through game levels, received concept maps about mixtures, pure substances, compounds, elements, and principles of classification, and then completed in-game tasks. The experimental group used KC-CMG: after learners encountered a concept map, they answered a first-tier factual question and then a second-tier conceptual-understanding question. Incorrect answers led students back toward the material or concept map before they could continue. The control group used the same game and concept maps but completed only first-tier factual questions before moving on.

The quasi-experiment included 48 seventh-grade students, 24 in the experimental group and 24 in the control group. Both groups used the same game, teacher, learning content, class time, pretests, posttests, and questionnaires. Measures included chemistry learning achievement, problem-solving awareness, critical thinking tendency, germane cognitive load, game behavior logs, and interviews. ANCOVA and t-test results favored the KC-CMG group: students using the two-tier knowledge check strategy had significantly higher learning achievement, problem-solving awareness, critical thinking tendency, and germane cognitive load than students using ordinary concept map-guided gaming.

The learning-behavior analysis explains why the strategy mattered. When experimental-group learners missed second-tier conceptual questions, they often returned to read the concept map again. Their behavior suggested double-loop learning: instead of only repeating a task or guessing, they reconsidered the concept relationships behind the task. The experimental group also showed a stronger path from completing learning objectives to challenge success, while the control group did not show the same pattern. Interviews reinforced this interpretation. Experimental-group students said that two-tier questions helped them correct concepts, think about why a concept map was drawn a certain way, and look back at the concept map more carefully; control-group students reported that the game and concept maps made learning more engaging and efficient but were less reflective.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful as adjacent instructional-design evidence. It suggests that game-based learning services should not rely on play, points, narrative, or maps alone when the goal is conceptual understanding. Learners may need embedded checks, prompts to explain reasoning, feedback loops, and opportunities to revisit conceptual materials. This is potentially relevant to library escape rooms, information-literacy games, digital tutorials, and game-based workshops where the library wants transfer rather than simple task completion.

Even so, the source should remain outside the main GBLS review. It is a school chemistry study, not library literature, and its digital game was a researcher-developed curriculum tool rather than a library service. The sample was small, the intervention was short, and the authors acknowledge that developing such games is time-consuming and not easily generalizable across subjects. The article can inform design principles for future GBLS instruction, especially scaffolding and assessment inside games, but it should not be cited as evidence about library programs, collections, outreach, or patron outcomes.
