## Findings
The study found that the game and debrief supported players in three key areas: (1) **Infrastructural meaning-making**: Players gained awareness of how social media platform affordances and AI technologies facilitate misinformation spread, recognizing the difficulty of detecting deepfakes. (2) **Psychological reflection**: Players reflected on emotional vulnerabilities and the "third-person effect," acknowledging their own susceptibility to misinformation despite prior confidence in their discernment skills. (3) **Personal connection**: Players connected game experiences to real-world behaviors, though many reported they would not change their social media usage habits, citing existing caution or limited engagement. The collaborative nature of the escape room and the structured debrief were identified as critical components for facilitating these learning outcomes.
Evidence: "The mixed methods study finds the game and accompanying debrief supported players in reflecting upon social media platform infrastructures, the psychological and emotional dimensions of misinformation, and how their personal behaviors intersect with online misinformation."

## Implications
The authors argue that a sociocultural perspective enriches the understanding of games-based MIL interventions by highlighting the importance of narrative, collaboration, and post-game debriefing. They suggest that future designs should consider contextual targeting for specific communities and emphasize the role of facilitators (e.g., librarians) in guiding reflection to prevent cynicism. The study implies that moving beyond individual skill acquisition to address social and infrastructural contexts is essential for effective misinformation education.

# Summary

This research article examines the efficacy of *The Euphorigen Investigation*, a misinformation-themed escape room game, in supporting media and information literacy (MIL) education. Departing from autonomous models that treat literacy as a set of individual cognitive skills, the authors adopt a sociocultural perspective, which situates literacy within everyday practices, social contexts, and infrastructural arrangements. The study investigates how this collaborative, narrative-driven game facilitates learning about misinformation among public library patrons.

The methodology involved a nationwide mixed-methods field study conducted across 10 diverse U.S. public libraries between February and April 2022. A total of 53 game sessions were hosted, involving 211 players. Data collection included recorded post-game debrief discussions, online player surveys (completed by 127 participants), librarian session reports, and librarian focus groups. The analysis employed a hybrid inductive-deductive thematic coding approach for qualitative data and descriptive statistics for quantitative survey results.

Key findings indicate that the game effectively supported learning through three primary mechanisms. First, it fostered **infrastructural meaning-making**, helping players understand how social media algorithms and AI technologies (such as deepfakes) facilitate the spread of misinformation. Players expressed surprise at the sophistication of these tools and the difficulty of distinguishing authentic content from manipulated media. Second, the intervention prompted **psychological and emotional reflection**. Despite many participants identifying as skeptical or cynical, the game experience revealed their vulnerability to misinformation, challenging the "third-person effect" (the belief that others are more susceptible than oneself). Players reported feelings of shock and regret upon realizing they had shared a deepfake video during gameplay, leading to reflections on how emotions influence information processing. Third, players made **connections to personal behaviors**, recognizing their role in the broader information ecosystem. However, while many acknowledged the ease with which misinformation spreads, a significant portion reported no intention to change their social media habits, citing existing caution or limited political engagement online.

The authors emphasize that the collaborative nature of the escape room and the structured post-game debrief were critical to these outcomes. The debrief allowed players to process their experiences, share perspectives, and connect fictional scenarios to real-world events, facilitated by librarians who guided discussions to mitigate cynicism. The study concludes that a sociocultural lens offers valuable insights for designing MIL interventions, highlighting the importance of narrative immersion, peer collaboration, and facilitated reflection over isolated skill-building exercises. Limitations include the reliance on self-reported data, lack of longitudinal follow-up, and potential variability in facilitation quality across different library settings.
