### Summary
# **Wedlake, S., Coward, C., & Lee, J. H. (2024). How games can support misinformation education: A sociocultural perspective. *Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology, 75*(13), 1480-1497. https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.24954**

Wedlake, Coward, and Lee extend the public-library misinformation game work represented by *The Euphorigen Investigation*, but they frame the project through a sociocultural perspective on media and information literacy. The article argues that misinformation education should not be treated only as an "autonomous" set of individual evaluation skills. Instead, belief, sharing, and interpretation are situated in social identities, platform infrastructures, communities, emotions, and everyday information practices. From that perspective, games can matter because they create shared situations in which people experience misinformation dynamics together, talk about them, and reflect on how their own practices fit larger systems.

The study examines *The Euphorigen Investigation*, an online escape-room game about a fictional brain-enhancing supplement, across a much larger public-library implementation than the earlier pilot. The game places players in a narrative investigation where they encounter misleading headlines, bots, manipulated charts, deepfakes, influencer content, and other misinformation tactics. It also includes a designed failure: players are led to share a video that is later revealed to be a deepfake. The learning design depends on collaboration and a librarian-facilitated debrief, not on puzzles alone. The debrief helps players connect the fictional experience to real online environments, their own assumptions, and the social consequences of sharing.

Data came from 10 public libraries, 53 game sessions, and 211 players. The authors use multiple data sources, including debrief recordings, player surveys, librarian session reports, and librarian focus groups. They analyze how players and facilitators made meaning from the game and ask how game attributes supported misinformation education. The empirical scope makes the article especially useful for GBLS because it studies an actual library-hosted program across many sessions rather than presenting only a design concept or small demonstration.

Three game attributes are central to the findings: narrative, collaboration, and debriefing. Narrative gave misinformation a context that felt less abstract than a checklist or lecture. Players could see how emotional stakes, uncertainty, suspicion, and urgency affected their own judgments. Collaboration mattered because players brought different observations, assumptions, and online experiences to the table, turning misinformation evaluation into a social process. The debrief was the clearest bridge to transfer. It gave players a structured opportunity to name what happened, compare interpretations, revisit their vulnerability to confirmation bias, discuss platform features, and think about future behavior.

The findings show that the game supported reflection on several dimensions of misinformation. Players discussed platform and media infrastructures, including how bots, influencers, social media metrics, and manipulated media can shape attention and trust. They also discussed psychological and emotional dimensions, including confidence, surprise, suspicion, embarrassment, and concern about being fooled. Survey responses suggested that many players found the game helpful, and 73.23% reported being more concerned about misinformation in society after playing. Importantly, the authors do not claim that the game inoculated participants or produced durable behavioral change. The stronger claim is that it created a memorable, social, reflective learning environment aligned with the sociocultural character of misinformation.

For the GBLS project, this article is a key source because it makes the library role visible in a way that many game-based learning studies do not. Libraries are not merely venues for a game designed elsewhere. Librarians host sessions, prepare participants, manage group interaction, lead debriefs, and translate game experience into local information-literacy conversation. The article therefore supports a service model in which game-based library services require facilitation, staff training, program design, and assessment practices that look beyond attendance. It also helps connect escape rooms to civic information literacy, misinformation resilience, and public-library trust.

The article's limitations are useful for the review as well. The study relies heavily on self-report and immediate reflection, and it does not establish long-term learning or behavior change. The intervention also required a purpose-built game, librarian training, and coordinated implementation, which may be challenging for smaller libraries. Even so, Wedlake et al. provide one of the strongest empirical and theoretical accounts of how a library-based game can address a difficult public problem by combining narrative experience, social play, and reflective facilitation.
