## Purpose
The study aims to analyze the social, political, cultural, and economic meaning of the newly constructed Seattle Public Library (SPL) Central Library by applying two theoretical frameworks: Oldenburg’s "third place" concept and Cresswell’s five facets of place. The authors seek to determine if these frameworks adequately explain the library's role as a physical, social, and informational space, specifically investigating whether existing theories account for the concept of "information" within the broader notion of place.

## Method
The researchers conducted a qualitative field study using semi-structured interviews with library users and passers-by. Data collection occurred over three weeks (October 8–November 7, 2004). The methodology included a free association exercise where participants responded to eight stimulus terms (architecture, books, community, free speech, learning, librarians, reading, technology). Responses were transcribed and analyzed using QSR NUD*IST 5 software for content analysis to identify themes reflecting the theoretical frameworks.

## Findings
Participants viewed the SPL as a physical place characterized by modernity, civic pride, and spaciousness, though some found it "cold" or "intimidating." As a social place, the library functioned as a connector for diverse groups, fostering both bonding and bridging social capital. However, the data did not fully support Oldenburg’s "third place" criteria; specifically, conversation was not the main activity, and the building’s bold architecture contradicted the "low profile" criterion. As an informational place, users valued free access to resources for lifelong learning and equity. The study found that neither Oldenburg’s nor Cresswell’s frameworks adequately addressed the role of information seeking and consumption as a core aspect of place.
Evidence: "results from our study suggest that while it may be a third place in spirit, it fully meets few of Oldenburg’s criteria... Neither [framework] adequately addresses the concept of information as it figures in the broader notion of place." (Discussion section)

## Implications
The authors argue that existing theoretical frameworks for "place" in library science are insufficient because they omit the explicit role of information. They propose adding "information" to the repertoire of place, encompassing themes of information finding, lifelong learning, and learning environments. The study suggests that while central libraries may not fit the "third place" model due to their scale and design, branch libraries might better align with Oldenburg’s criteria. Future research should explore staff perspectives and apply this design to branch libraries.
Evidence: "Thus, this study contributes 'information' to the repertoire of place... In future research we will expand our study by interviewing SPL staff... we may also apply our study design to understanding the views of various stakeholders towards new SPL branch libraries." (Discussion section)

# Summary

This article presents a field study examining the Seattle Public Library (SPL) Central Library through the theoretical lenses of Ray Oldenburg’s "third place" concept and Tim Cresswell’s five facets of place. The primary objective was to understand how library users and passers-by perceive the library as a physical, social, and informational space, and to evaluate whether existing frameworks adequately capture the role of information within these perceptions.

The methodology involved semi-structured interviews with 226 participants over a three-week period in late 2004. The sample included 151 library users and 75 passers-by. Participants were predominantly White, English-speaking, and highly educated, with income levels skewed toward lower brackets. Data collection utilized both open-ended questions and a free association exercise involving eight stimulus terms. Qualitative content analysis was performed using NUD*IST 5 software to identify emergent themes.

The findings reveal that participants viewed the SPL as a significant physical landmark symbolizing modernity and civic pride. While many appreciated the building’s spaciousness and light, others described it as cold or intimidating. Socially, the library functioned as a connector, facilitating interactions across generations and socioeconomic groups, thereby fostering both bonding and bridging social capital. However, the study found that the SPL did not fully meet Oldenburg’s criteria for a "third place." Specifically, conversation was not the central activity, the building’s prominent architecture contradicted the requirement for a low profile, and visitors could not reliably expect to find acquaintances due to the facility's size.

Crucially, the authors argue that both Oldenburg’s and Cresswell’s frameworks fail to explicitly incorporate information seeking and consumption as core aspects of place. While users highly valued free access to resources for lifelong learning and equity, this informational dimension was not adequately addressed by the existing theories. Consequently, the study proposes expanding the concept of "place" in library science to include an "informational place" component, defined by themes of information finding, learning resources, and learning environments. The authors suggest that while central libraries may not fit the "third place" model, smaller branch libraries might align more closely with Oldenburg’s criteria, warranting further investigation.
