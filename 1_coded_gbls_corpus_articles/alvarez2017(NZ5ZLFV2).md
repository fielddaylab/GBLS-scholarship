## Purpose
The article aims to argue for the integration of modern tabletop games in library settings as a pedagogical tool to foster "student agency," defined as the initiative and capacity for students to construct possibilities for themselves. It seeks to demonstrate how analog gaming can develop critical soft skills—such as problem-solving, collaboration, and adaptability—that are transferable to academic success and career readiness, contrasting these benefits with the passive consumption often associated with digital gaming.
Evidence: "The goal is for students to adapt these learned skills and dispositions, and then apply them to their education... student agency is defined as the initiative and capacity for students 'to construct possibilities for themselves'" (pp. 41–42).

## Method
The article employs a qualitative, reflective case study approach based on the author’s professional experience implementing a tabletop gaming program in a public library media center that serves middle and high school students. The methodology involves observational assessment of student engagement and skill development over time, specifically tracking the progression from simple games to complex strategic ones. It includes an informal evaluation framework where the author assessed various games on a scale of 0–10 regarding skills reliance, luck mechanism, and reading frequency.

## Population and Data
The population consists of middle and high school students participating in drop-in programs at a public library media center in Chicago. The setting is described as an out-of-school learning environment serving schools that lack dedicated libraries. The data comprises anecdotal observations of student behavior, engagement levels, and strategic development during gaming sessions from spring 2015 onward. Specific games analyzed include *Agricola*, *Dominion*, *Splendor*, *Pandemic*, *Mansions of Madness*, and others, compared against traditional luck-based games like *Monopoly* and digital titles like *Candy Crush Saga*.
Evidence: "Our media center in a public library operates as a drop-in out-ofschool learning environment... providing resources that they lack" (p. 41); "In the spring of 2015 students at our school were introduced to Magic: The Gathering..." (p. 46).

## Findings
The author reports that modern tabletop games, which rely on strategy rather than luck, successfully engaged students who previously exhibited low interest in reading or complex rule sets. Initial attempts with highly text-dependent games (*Magic: The Gathering*) failed due to comprehension barriers and a preference for instant gratification found in digital media. However, introducing simpler, skill-based card games (*Splendor*, *Star Realms*) served as effective scaffolding. Over time, students progressed to more complex cooperative and competitive games (*Agricola*, *Mansions of Madness*), demonstrating improved strategic thinking, collaboration, and willingness to engage with text. The author observed a shift in student preference from luck-based outcomes to self-determined strategic success, indicating the development of agency.
Evidence: "Participation evolved into Dominion and Star Realms... Zombicide became the next hit... Students then began to express increased interest in other cooperative games" (p. 46); "where students once demanded games heavily reliant on luck, they now want to be the authors of their own destinies" (p. 47).
Confidence: high

## Implications
The article implies that libraries should curate collections of modern, skill-based tabletop games to support academic readiness and social-emotional learning. It suggests that educators must act as moderators to scaffold learning, helping students overcome initial resistance to complex rules. The findings support the use of analog play to counteract the "self-defeating habits" formed by digital instant gratification, fostering long-term stamina, critical thinking, and interpersonal skills essential for college and career success.
Evidence: "The goal of educators encouraging students to play these games is to plant a seed of stamina that is also applicable outside of the tabletop experience" (p. 47); "Inspiring students to become moderators is directly dependent on educators who are knowledgeable about the games’ rules" (p. 46).
Confidence: high

# Summary

Vivian G. Alvarez’s 2017 article, "Power to the Pupil: Student Agency in the School Library," presents a qualitative case study advocating for the use of modern tabletop games as educational tools in library settings. The primary objective is to demonstrate how analog gaming can cultivate "student agency"—defined as the capacity for students to construct possibilities for themselves and act toward desired goals. Alvarez argues that while digital media often promotes passive consumption and instant gratification through trial-and-error mechanics, modern tabletop games require active strategic thinking, collaboration, and resilience, thereby fostering skills transferable to academic and professional contexts.

The study is situated in a public library media center in Chicago that functions as an out-of-school learning environment for middle and high school students from schools lacking dedicated libraries. The methodology relies on the author’s observational data collected over several years, beginning in spring 2015. Alvarez implemented a progressive gaming curriculum, starting with simpler games to build confidence and gradually introducing more complex titles. She developed an informal assessment framework to evaluate games based on three criteria: reliance on skills (strategy), reliance on luck (chance), and reading frequency (text engagement). This framework allowed her to categorize games such as *Agricola*, *Dominion*, *Splendor*, and *Pandemic* against traditional luck-based games like *Monopoly*.

The findings indicate a significant evolution in student engagement and skill acquisition. Initially, students struggled with text-heavy games like *Magic: The Gathering*, exhibiting low comprehension and interest due to habits formed by digital gaming that minimized literacy requirements. However, by introducing scaffolded games like *Splendor* and *Star Realms*, which required less reading but emphasized strategy, Alvarez successfully engaged participants. Over time, students progressed to complex cooperative and competitive games, such as *Mansions of Madness* and *Agricola*. Observations revealed that these experiences improved students’ ability to interpret rules, collaborate with peers, manage resources, and reassess strategies after failure. Notably, student preferences shifted from seeking luck-based outcomes to valuing self-determined strategic success, indicating a growth in agency and confidence.

Alvarez contrasts this analog experience with digital gaming trends, noting that many popular video games rely on repetitive trial-and-error mechanics that do not require critical thinking or deep engagement with instructions. In contrast, tabletop games provide a "level playing field" where outcomes are determined by player decisions rather than chance. The article highlights specific skills aligned with Tony Wagner’s seven skills for success, including critical thinking, collaboration, and adaptability. For instance, *Agricola* requires players to manage limited resources and prioritize tasks, mirroring the time management and decision-making required in academic settings.

The implications of this work suggest that libraries should actively curate modern tabletop games to support student development. Alvarez emphasizes the role of the educator or librarian as a moderator who scaffolds learning, helping students navigate complex rules and fostering an inclusive environment. By engaging in these analog experiences, students develop stamina, improve their comprehension skills, and learn to view failure as a strategic opportunity rather than a discouragement. The article concludes that integrating such games into library services can effectively bridge the gap between play and academic readiness, empowering students to take ownership of their learning trajectories.
