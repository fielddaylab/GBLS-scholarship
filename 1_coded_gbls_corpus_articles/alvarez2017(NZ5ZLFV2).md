### Summary
# **Alvarez, V. G. (2017). Engaging students in the library through tabletop gaming. *Knowledge Quest, 45*(4), 40-48.**

Alvarez argues for modern tabletop games as analog learning experiences that can support student agency, interpersonal engagement, comprehension, strategic thinking, and college-and-career readiness. The article is written from a public-library media center that functions as a drop-in out-of-school learning environment and works with nearby middle and high schools that lack school libraries. Alvarez frames tabletop gaming as a deliberate response to young people's sometimes isolated or low-effort digital play habits: students may sit side by side with online games while remaining socially disengaged, or rely on trial and error rather than close reading, strategic planning, and sustained attention.

The article's core concept is student agency, defined through Toshalis and Nakkula as students' initiative and capacity to construct possibilities for themselves and act toward desired goals. Alvarez argues that modern tabletop games can help students practice agency because they require players to interpret rules, make choices, test strategies, adapt to failure, and see how their own knowledge and decisions influence outcomes. This is contrasted with older luck-heavy games such as Monopoly and Risk and with digital games whose mechanics may remain nearly unchanged beneath different themes. Modern games are described as more likely to involve player agency, reduced downtime, multiple paths to victory, cooperation, decision-making, reading, and strategic reassessment.

Alvarez connects tabletop play to soft skills and academic readiness. Drawing on Wagner's seven skills, the article names critical thinking, problem solving, collaboration, leadership by influence, agility, adaptability, initiative, communication, information analysis, curiosity, and imagination as skills practiced through games. Agricola is used as a detailed example: players have limited turns, limited workers, competing priorities, food pressures, family growth choices, farm development decisions, and end-game scoring. These mechanics require players to assess priorities, plan under constraints, interpret rules together, and reflect afterward on whether their strategy worked. Alvarez sees a direct analogy to school tasks that require students to manage time, plan across constraints, and revise approaches.

The program examples are concrete. Games used successfully in the library included Agricola, Descent: Journeys in the Dark, Dominion, Mansions of Madness, Mysterium, Pandemic, Pathfinder, Splendor, Star Realms, and Zombicide. Alvarez compares games by skill reliance, luck reliance, reading frequency, cooperation, character cards, and dice. Cooperative games were especially useful for students who preferred not to compete directly and for building camaraderie, leadership, and storytelling. The article also describes an intentional progression: after students found Magic: The Gathering unfamiliar and complicated, staff and a teen advisory committee introduced less reading-intensive games first, then moved from Splendor to Dominion and Star Realms, then to cooperative and more complex games such as Zombicide, Descent, Mysterium, Pandemic, Pathfinder, Agricola, and Mansions of Madness.

For GBLS, Alvarez is important because it treats game selection and facilitation as developmental scaffolding. Students initially resisted reading rules or struggled with comprehension, but with staff knowledge, welcoming facilitation, teen advisory input, and carefully sequenced games, they became more willing to engage with complexity and to act as authors of their own outcomes. The educator's role is to know the rules, animate the game, include students with different skill sets, identify leadership skills, and gradually help students become moderators themselves. This makes the article relevant not only to tabletop clubs but also to advisory, collection development, youth leadership, and facilitation infrastructure.

The article is practitioner advocacy rather than formal assessment. Its evidence is observational and local, and it sometimes overgeneralizes digital games as encouraging shallow trial and error. Even so, it is valuable because it offers a detailed rationale for why modern tabletop games, especially skill-based and cooperative games, may support agency, reading comprehension, social interaction, and strategic self-confidence in library youth services. It also shows that analog and digital games need not be opposed; the stated aim is to help students notice the habits formed by both and to develop stamina, reflection, and agency through shared play.
