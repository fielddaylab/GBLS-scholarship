# Summary

This article details the creation and evaluation of "Mystery at the Library," an Alternate Reality Game (ARG) style orientation activity designed for freshman engineering students at James Madison University (JMU). The primary objective was to familiarize students with the Rose Library’s physical space, resources, and services in a manner that was engaging and cost-effective, addressing challenges associated with traditional library tours and scavenger hunts. The author, Kelly Giles, notes that engineering freshmen often lack awareness of discipline-specific resources housed in the science library, despite receiving general information literacy instruction.

The methodology involved developing a narrative-driven game where students solved the mystery of a stolen fictional notebook belonging to Nikola Tesla. The game required players to search the library building and online databases for clues, thereby practicing essential skills such as using the online catalog, locating books by call number, accessing reserve items, finding reference materials, and utilizing RefWorks for citation management. The development process was iterative; initial pilot versions included complex narrative elements like a detective character via email, which proved confusing and time-consuming. Feedback from library staff and students led to simplifications, removing non-instructional puzzles and streamlining the narrative to focus on learning objectives. The final version used minimal materials—primarily photocopies in red folders—costing less than a dollar per participant.

The study employed a quasi-experimental design during the fall of 2012. Two sections of a required engineering course (N=44) participated in the game, while a third section (N=24) served as a control group. An online survey was administered to all groups after the experimental group completed the activity but prior to any formal library instruction. The survey measured self-reported familiarity with six specific library tasks using a 5-point Likert scale and assessed student perceptions of the game’s enjoyment and utility.

Key findings revealed that the experimental group reported higher familiarity with all listed library tasks compared to the control group. The most significant difference was observed in familiarity with RefWorks, a tool many students had not previously encountered. Control group responses indicated a strong preference for hands-on learning styles, supporting the rationale for an experiential game-based approach. Among the experimental group, 88% agreed or strongly agreed that the activity was fun, and 74% found it helpful. Additionally, 79% recommended the activity for future classes. Qualitative comments highlighted improved understanding of library layout, catalog usage, and Library of Congress call numbers.

The author acknowledges several limitations. Primarily, the study relied on self-assessment of familiarity rather than objective measures of learning or skill retention. Consequently, the results do not definitively prove educational efficacy but suggest increased confidence and awareness. The sample size was limited to one specific cohort of engineering students at a single institution. Despite these constraints, the article demonstrates that effective library instruction games can be developed without significant budgets or technical expertise. The narrative structure provided a scaffold for subsequent in-class instruction, allowing librarians to connect game experiences to broader information literacy concepts such as source evaluation and ethical use of information. The author concludes that while the specific theme was tailored to engineering students, the framework is adaptable for other disciplines by modifying the thematic content and resource targets.
