# **Walton, J. L. (Ed.). (2024). *Utopia on the tabletop*. Ping Press. https://drive.google.com/file/d/142T45GkcP4KAKxF_6s91klH2ulWDwSKd/view**

Walton's edited volume brings together utopian studies and tabletop roleplaying game (TTRPG) studies. The collection includes essays, dialogues, two playable games, and an afterword. Its central premise is that the contemporary tabletop roleplaying renaissance is resonant with utopian themes: tabletop games can stage solarpunk imaginaries, ecological harmony, dystopian collapse, postcapitalist speculation, queer and neurodiverse futures, labor organizing, decolonial design, collaborative worldbuilding, and the question of whether serious play can touch real-world issues.

The introduction frames TTRPGs as cultural forms that train players to think from within rules. Walton treats tabletop games as a medium where little systems can make players feel, test, or question big social systems. That argument runs across the volume. Several chapters analyze specific games or actual-play texts, including *The Book of Cairn*, *Arium: Create*, *Paranoia*, *Dialect*, *Masks*, *Dimension 20's The Unsleeping City*, *Dungeons & Dragons*, and *Dungeons & Dragons, Ancestry & Culture*. Other contributions focus on larp, irrigation gaming, art practice, fictioning, labor organizing, and original game design. The through-line is not a single theory of play but a recurring interest in utopia as method, critique, social dreaming, blueprint, failure, repair, and world-making.

The middle of the book is especially relevant to TTRPG studies because it examines how fantasy roleplaying systems carry political assumptions. Chapters on *Dungeons & Dragons* and related homebrew discourse ask how ancestry, culture, race, commodified neutrality, diversity, and actual-play communities shape what players imagine as possible. Other chapters move away from mainstream fantasy and toward indie or critical games, where mechanics may foreground care, social reproduction, language loss, community maintenance, organizing, or alternative institutional forms. In that sense, the volume gives a broad view of tabletop play as more than entertainment: it can be a site for procedural rhetoric, speculative sociology, collective design, and critical reflection.

For the GBLS project, the book is intellectually adjacent but not directly evidentiary. It can help explain why TTRPG programs might matter beyond recreation, especially when libraries use roleplaying to support imagination, collaborative storytelling, identity exploration, civic reflection, or community-building. The volume also offers vocabulary for thinking about design tensions that matter in library programs: whether a game invites repair or critique, whether its worldbuilding is genuinely collaborative, whether inclusion is only surface-level, and whether play helps participants imagine alternatives to the status quo.

However, the book does not substantially address libraries, librarianship, library programs, library collections, school libraries, public libraries, academic libraries, cataloging, access, facilitation in library settings, or library outcomes. Searches of the text show only incidental references to archives, libraries, and related memory institutions, not a sustained treatment of library service. Its primary audiences are scholars and designers in utopian studies, TTRPG studies, speculative fiction, game design, cultural studies, and activist or artistic play communities.
