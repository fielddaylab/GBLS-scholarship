## Findings
The contributors argue that TTRPGs are not merely escapist but serve as diagnostic and performative practices that reflect and contest real-world political economies. Key findings include:
1. *Dungeons & Dragons* often reproduces "capitalist realism" and racial capitalism through its mechanics (e.g., commodified magic items, colonialist adventure structures), though homebrew supplements attempt to address these issues.
2. Games like *The Book of Cairn* use procedural rhetoric to model utopian maintenance; mechanics such as "Town Statistics" and "Harmony" incentivize prosocial behavior, collective care, and community resilience over individualistic violence.
3. TTRPGs can facilitate "reparative" rather than just "paranoid" readings of society, allowing players to experiment with alternative social relations, neurodivergent identities (as in *Masks*), and labor organizing (as in *Beat the Boss*).
4. Indie game design scenes offer spaces for anti-capitalist and queer utopian imaginaries, though tensions exist between radical ideals and commercial realities.
Evidence: "Cairn’s combination of unique rules and use of a pastoralist utopian setting function as a method of critique... producing through play of the game, both a sense of what would be necessary to maintain... a small pastoralist utopian society." # Summary

Jo Lindsay Walton’s edited volume *Utopia on the Tabletop* (2024) bridges Utopian Studies and Tabletop Roleplaying Game (TTRPG) Studies to explore how tabletop games function as sites for imagining and enacting alternative social realities. The central objective is to defend "utopia" as a viable conceptual framework despite its pejorative associations, arguing that TTRPGs offer unique procedural and narrative affordances for critiquing capitalist realism and modeling postcapitalist futures.

The collection employs qualitative literary and cultural analysis, drawing on theoretical frameworks such as Ruth Levitas’s "Imaginary Reconstruction of Society" (IROS), Ian Bogost’s "procedural rhetoric," and Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick’s distinction between paranoid and reparative reading. The text is structured into essays analyzing specific games, interviews with designers, and playable game texts.

A significant portion of the analysis focuses on *Dungeons & Dragons* (D&D). Contributors argue that mainstream D&D often reproduces capitalist and colonialist ideologies through its mechanics, such as the commodification of magic items and adventure structures centered on resource extraction and violence. However, the volume also highlights efforts within the homebrew community and official publications to address these issues, suggesting that while superficial diversity efforts are insufficient, the game’s popularity provides a platform for critical engagement with race, ancestry, and representation.

In contrast, several chapters examine indie TTRPGs as more explicit utopian experiments. Tyler Brunette’s analysis of *The Book of Cairn* demonstrates how procedural rhetoric can model utopian maintenance. The game’s "Town Statistics" and "Harmony" mechanics incentivize prosocial behavior, collective care, and community resilience, positioning players as caretakers rather than transient adventurers. This procedural design critiques the individualistic violence typical of fantasy RPGs and offers a tangible experience of egalitarian mutual aid. Other games discussed include *Paranoia*, which serves as an anti-utopian satire; *Masks: A New Generation*, which explores neurodivergence through superhero allegory; and *Beat the Boss*, which simulates labor organizing to educate players on union strategies.

The volume also features interviews with designers like Gabriel Caetano Barbosa and Kellynn Wee, who discuss the ethical implications of game design. Caetano Barbosa critiques the colonialist premises of mainstream TTRPGs and advocates for designs centered on uncertainty and hope rather than conflict. Wee provides an anthropological perspective on TTRPG play in Singapore, noting how commercial play studios shape participation and how players navigate Eurocentric discourses.

Overall, *Utopia on the Tabletop* argues that TTRPGs are not merely escapist entertainment but are diagnostic and performative practices that reflect and contest real-world political economies. By engaging with game mechanics and narratives, players can experiment with alternative social relations, challenge capitalist realism, and cultivate utopian desires for more just and care-centered futures. The work concludes that utopian thinking, facilitated by the interactive nature of tabletop gaming, remains a crucial tool for imagining and building better worlds.
