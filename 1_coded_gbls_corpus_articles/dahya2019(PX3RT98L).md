# Summary

This report details a mixed-methods case study conducted by researchers from the University of Washington Information School, funded by Washington State Libraries and Oculus, to evaluate the role of Virtual Reality (VR) in public libraries. The primary objective was to assess the impact of VR on community engagement and informal learning within seven diverse library sites in Washington State during spring 2018. The study employed qualitative interviews with 23 librarians and 39 patrons, alongside a survey of 185 patrons and direct site observations.

The demographic data from the survey revealed that participants were predominantly young (60% were teens or in their 20s), White (69%), and male (53%). Notably, 81.6% of respondents identified as video gamers, suggesting a strong correlation between existing gaming habits and VR participation. The findings highlight a distinct gap between patrons' and librarians' perceptions of VR's potential and their actual experiences. While participants held strong beliefs that VR could foster empathy, support social equity, and serve as a therapeutic or educational tool, the short-term, drop-in nature of the library programs did not provide evidence to support these claims. Specifically, the study found no clear evidence of empathy building through the available experiences.

In terms of user experience, 99% of surveyed patrons reported enjoying their VR session, with many describing a profound sense of immersion and interactivity. However, this immersion was sometimes accompanied by disorientation, dizziness, or nausea, particularly among first-time users. Learning outcomes were primarily technical; 97% of respondents felt they learned how to use the technology, compared to 78% who felt they learned about the content of the experiences. The study suggests that while VR inspires curiosity, it functions more as a gateway to technological literacy than as a standalone educational tool in its current library implementation.

Significant equity concerns emerged regarding participation demographics. White males and individuals with prior video gaming experience were the most frequent users, reinforcing existing stereotypes about gamer identities. The authors note that non-White communities, including immigrants and refugees, were underrepresented, partly due to barriers such as legal waivers and a lack of targeted outreach. Furthermore, while VR was intended to bridge access gaps for those who could not afford the technology, the study observed that users often accepted VR as a substitute for real-world experiences (e.g., travel) without critically examining the corporate curation of these virtual environments.

From an operational standpoint, librarians reported that successful VR programming requires substantial preparation time, careful consideration of physical space for safety and privacy, and strategic promotion beyond simple flyers. The mandatory age restriction of 13+ for Oculus devices was identified as a frustration for both staff and younger patrons ("tweens"), necessitating alternative activities to maintain engagement. The report concludes that while VR is a popular and engaging addition to library services, its long-term value remains unclear. Future initiatives should focus on expanding access to diverse populations, exploring VR creation tools rather than just consumption, and developing structured pedagogical approaches to maximize educational benefits.
