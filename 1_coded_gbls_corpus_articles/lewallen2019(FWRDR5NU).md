# Summary

This article by Kate Lewallen, published in *Knowledge Quest* (2019), serves as a professional feature advocating for the adoption of breakout boxes within school library services. The primary objective is to align the use of these gamified educational tools with the American Association of School Librarians (AASL) Standards Framework for Learners, specifically highlighting their capacity to foster inquiry, collaboration, and resilience. Lewallen argues that while traditional academic merits are important, soft skills such as those outlined in the AASL Shared Foundations are critical for student success but often difficult to teach in conventional classroom settings. Breakout boxes, described as portable versions of escape rooms, offer a mechanism to gamify curriculum, thereby increasing student engagement and investment in learning outcomes.

The methodology of the article is descriptive and anecdotal rather than empirical. Lewallen draws upon her professional experience as the head librarian at the Webb School of Knoxville to illustrate practical applications. There is no formal data collection, statistical analysis, or controlled study design. Instead, the text functions as a guide, combining theoretical justification with practical implementation strategies. The population referenced includes students (notably sixth and ninth graders during library orientations), classroom teachers, and school staff. The "data" consists of the author’s observations regarding teacher adoption rates and student responses to breakout activities in various contexts, including classrooms, the library media center, and summer day camps.

Key findings reported by the author indicate that breakout boxes are highly adaptable resources. Physically, they consist of reprogrammable locks, boxes, and puzzle materials that can be customized for any subject area or grade level. Lewallen notes that while initial teacher adoption was slow, usage has increased over time as educators recognized the utility of these kits for introducing new units, reviewing content, or building team cohesion among faculty. In the library setting, breakout games proved effective for orientation, transforming a potentially passive introduction into an interactive exploration of the space. Furthermore, the author highlights the accessibility of digital breakout options via platforms like BreakoutEDU and Google Sites, which allow libraries with limited budgets to participate in gamified learning without purchasing physical kits.

The implications for library practice are significant. Lewallen posits that curating breakout boxes supports the librarian’s role as a teacher leader and collaborator. By providing resources that require students to work in teams, communicate effectively, and think critically under time pressure, librarians can directly support AASL standards related to inquiry and collaboration. The article provides actionable recommendations for other librarians, including creating free accounts on BreakoutEDU to access pre-made games, joining online professional communities for peer support, and starting with simple games to acclimate students to the format. Lewallen emphasizes that the success of these activities relies on a student-centered approach where the librarian acts as a "guide on the side," allowing students to take control of the problem-solving process. Ultimately, the article concludes that breakout boxes are a valuable addition to school library collections, offering a versatile, engaging method to develop essential soft skills and prepare learners for future challenges in college, career, and life.
