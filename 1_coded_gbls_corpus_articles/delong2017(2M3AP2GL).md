### Summary
# **De Long, K., Garrison, J., & Ryan, M. (2017). All in: A public services philosophy for the twenty-first century academic library. *portal: Libraries and the Academy, 17*(2), 201-204.**

De Long, Garrison, and Ryan introduce a special issue of *portal: Libraries and the Academy* on public services in twenty-first-century academic libraries. The editorial argues that academic libraries are no longer merely support units but institutional partners in teaching and learning, faculty scholarship, student success, informed citizenship, global awareness, and knowledge creation. The authors describe public services as a broad philosophy of user engagement rather than a single department or desk-based function.

The editorial's central claim is that contemporary academic libraries need an organization-wide public services philosophy. Access services, cataloging, facilities, information technology, liaison work, scholarly communication, instruction, collections, assessment, and learning spaces should all be oriented toward users' needs. The article frames this as "the library in the life of the user," emphasizing outward-facing relationships, user-centered language, flexible learning spaces, innovative technologies, collections as service, space as service, assessment, and engagement across the organization.

For the GBLS project, the article has only indirect value. Its user-centered service philosophy is compatible with game-based library services, especially where GBLS work involves collections, spaces, technologies, outreach, liaison relationships, and assessment. The editorial also briefly mentions another article in the special issue about lending video game consoles to support a game-design curriculum. That referenced article may be directly relevant if it appears elsewhere in the Zotero database.

This editorial itself, however, should be explicitly excluded from the main GBLS literature review. It does not analyze games, game-based learning, gamification, game collections, TTRPGs, escape rooms, esports, videogame preservation, or game-based programming. Its contribution is broad public-services framing rather than game-specific evidence or practice. Including it in the review would dilute the GBLS scope when more directly relevant public-service and game-collection sources are available.
