## Findings
The editorial identifies several key themes emerging from the special issue articles:
1.  **Organizational Philosophy:** Success depends on a user-centric vision ("the library in the life of the user") rather than specific reporting structures. All staff, including back-end operations, must understand constituent needs.
2.  **Language and Marketing:** Terminology matters; outdated terms like "reference desk" may alienate users. User-centered language is critical for effective marketing (Benedetti).
3.  **Instruction and Literacy:** Information literacy is being broadened through micro-teaching, social learning, and integration into curricula (Rutledge and LeMire).
4.  **Relationships:** Successful liaison models rely on personal relationships, mutual benefit, and shared responsibility with faculty (Church-Duran; Díaz and Mandernach).
5.  **Collections as Service:** This includes transitioning monographic practices to user-centric models (Way), lending non-traditional items like video game consoles for curriculum support (Buller), and managing data collections and faculty profile systems (Givens et al.; Hogenboom and Hayslett).
6.  **Space as Service:** Libraries are developing learning spaces, family areas to support student retention, and makerspaces (iSpaces) to foster innovation and digital humanities (Spencer and Watstein; Godfrey et al.; Nichols et al.).
7.  **Assessment:** Evaluation occurs at multiple levels, presenting both challenges and opportunities for future service improvement (Savage et al.).

# Summary

This editorial by De Long, Garrison, and Ryan introduces a special issue of *portal: Libraries and the Academy* dedicated to redefining public services in academic libraries for the twenty-first century. The authors argue that while the term "public services" may be evolving or becoming obsolete due to new organizational structures like Digital Initiatives or Scholarly Communication, the underlying philosophy must remain unified. The core argument is that it is less important which department reports to whom than it is for the entire library organization—including catalogers, facilities managers, and IT staff—to embrace a common user-centric vision. The editors emphasize the shift from "the user in the life of the library" to "the library in the life of the user," asserting that this organizational understanding is critical for success in a competitive information market.

The editorial summarizes fifteen articles that collectively serve as a "playbook" for implementing this philosophy. Key themes include the importance of language and marketing, with Allison Benedetti’s work highlighting the need to drop outdated terminology like "reference desk" to better resonate with faculty and students. In instruction, Lorelei Rutledge and Sarah LeMire discuss broadening information literacy through micro-teaching and participatory learning. The role of liaison librarians is examined by Jennifer Church-Duran and others, who stress that engaged librarianship must be connected to organizational strategy and that successful faculty-librarian relationships depend on personal connections and shared responsibility.

Significantly for games-based library services, the editorial highlights "collections as service" and "space as service." Ryan Buller’s contribution is noted for challenging traditional collection boundaries by describing a project to lend video game consoles to support a game-design curriculum. This example illustrates the shift toward user-centric models where libraries provide access to tools and technologies directly relevant to academic programs, rather than merely holding static collections. Additionally, the editorial mentions the creation of makerspaces (iSpaces) designed to support digital humanities, innovation, and entrepreneurship, further expanding the library’s role in hands-on, creative learning environments.

Other contributions address the transition of monographic collections to user-centric models, the management of data collections, and the development of faculty profile systems. Space is redefined not just as square footage but as a service tool for student retention and learning, with examples including family areas and collaborative spaces. Finally, the editorial notes the importance of assessment in defining success, acknowledging that evaluation occurs at multiple levels. The authors conclude that embracing this holistic philosophy allows libraries to influence institutional strategic directions and demonstrate that user engagement is their primary priority. The text serves as a conceptual framework for understanding how modern academic libraries can integrate diverse services under a unified commitment to user needs.
