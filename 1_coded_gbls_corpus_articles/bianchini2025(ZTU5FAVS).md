## Purpose
The article aims to present and evaluate ClaG, a novel classification system designed specifically for organizing plays and games in toy libraries and general library collections. The primary objective is to create a practical tool that facilitates the selection, research, identification, and delivery of games to players by highlighting relevant characteristics such as space, materials, setting, outcome, and genre. The authors seek to address the inadequacies of existing bibliographic classifications (like DDC) and specialized systems (like ESAR) for physical game collections.

## Findings
The ClaG system is structured around five fundamental facets: Space, Materials, Setting, Game Outcome, and Genre, with a sixth facet (Age) added based on expert feedback. The evaluation revealed that the resulting classes are very small, with an average ratio of 1.45 games per class for table and board games (51 games across 35 classes) and 1.37 overall (59 games across 43 classes). This indicates high granularity and effective differentiation between items. Expert testing showed mostly overlapping results when the same games were classified independently, suggesting reliability. However, the current sample is biased toward table and board games (84% of examples), with traditional children’s games, sports, mimicry games, and video games less thoroughly addressed in this version.

# Summary

This article introduces ClaG, a new classification system developed for the Archivio Italiano del Gioco (AIG) in Udine, Italy, aimed at organizing plays and games within toy libraries and general library collections. The authors, Carlo Bianchini and Paolo Munini, argue that existing bibliographic classifications, such as the Dewey Decimal Classification (DDC), are inadequate for game collections due to limited class availability and lack of mutual exclusiveness. Similarly, specialized systems like ESAR are criticized for focusing on psychological development rather than practical collection organization and for lacking unique notation rules. The primary objective of ClaG is to provide a practical, player-centric tool that aids in the selection, identification, and delivery of games by highlighting key characteristics relevant to user choice.

The methodology employed a twofold approach. A top-down strategy identified core concepts from established literature on game theory and design, drawing on works by Huizinga, Caillois, Salen and Zimmerman, and others. A bottom-up strategy involved applying these concepts to a sample of over 200 games, primarily table and board games, to refine the classification structure iteratively. The system was tested by a panel of experts, including librarians and educators, and presented in two public seminars to gather feedback.

The resulting ClaG system is built on five fundamental facets: Space (e.g., real-scale vs. reduced-scale play grounds), Materials (e.g., cards, tiles, toys), Setting (thematic, non-thematic, or simulation), Game Outcome (e.g., solitaire, cooperative, single winner), and Genre (based on Caillois’ categories: agon, alea, mimicry, vertigo). Based on expert feedback regarding the importance of age in game selection, a sixth facet for Age was added. The system adheres to Ranganathan’s principles of library classification, particularly exclusiveness and consistency, ensuring that classes do not overlap and are derived from single characteristics.

Empirical evaluation of the system used a subset of 59 classified examples. The analysis revealed that the classification yields very small, granular classes. For table and board games, which constituted 84% of the sample, the ratio was 1.45 games per class (51 games across 35 classes). The overall average ratio for all 59 examples was 1.37 games per class (across 43 classes). Independent classification tests by experts showed mostly overlapping results, indicating consistency in application. However, the authors acknowledge a significant limitation: the current sample is heavily biased toward table and board games. Traditional children’s games, sports, mimicry games, and video games are not yet thoroughly classified in this version, with only main general classes provided for these categories.

The implications of this work suggest that ClaG is a viable candidate for improving the organization of physical game collections in libraries. The authors plan to expand the system to cover underrepresented genres and conduct field tests in institutional settings. To support adoption and collaboration, a Wikibase Cloud instance has been created to share classification data among participating institutions. The study concludes that ClaG effectively addresses the practical needs of game librarians by providing a structured, exclusive, and user-oriented framework for organizing diverse ludic materials.
