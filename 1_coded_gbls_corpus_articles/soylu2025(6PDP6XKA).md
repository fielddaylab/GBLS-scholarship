# Summary

The article "Design of an Escape Room Game: Practical Clues and Ideas for Librarians" by Demet Soylu and Tunç Durmuş Medeni (2025) serves as a practical guide for integrating gamification into library services. The primary objective is to equip librarians with the conceptual frameworks and concrete examples necessary to design escape room games that function as interactive educational tools. The authors posit that escape rooms, characterized by themes, puzzles, time constraints, and collaborative problem-solving, are effective in triggering emotional engagement—such as excitement and curiosity—which facilitates deeper learning experiences in information centers like libraries and museums.

The methodology is descriptive and prescriptive, focusing on the structural elements of game design rather than empirical testing. Drawing on established gamification literature (e.g., Kapp, 2012; Claunch et al., 2023), the authors outline essential components for successful escape room creation: clear goals, rules, conflict, cooperation, time limits, feedback mechanisms, and a compelling narrative. They emphasize that the storyline must be simple yet engaging to maintain participant focus. The article provides specific design strategies, such as incorporating mystery, using physical props (e.g., magnetic pendulums, UV lights), and creating immersive sensory experiences (e.g., scent-based puzzles).

The core of the text is a detailed case study of a proposed escape room titled "ALPHABET DETECTIVE: Saving the Alphabet in the Ancient Library." This scenario places players in an ancient library on an alien planet where they must recover a lost alphabet to survive. The game consists of 11 distinct levels, each designed to test specific library and information literacy skills. These levels include:
1.  **Card Matching:** Testing knowledge of library concepts like information literacy and classification systems.
2.  **Book Face:** A social media integration task requiring creative interaction with book covers.
3.  **Librarian Identification:** Assessing users' familiarity with staff roles and services.
4.  **Catalog Search:** Practical application of finding books on specific topics (Greece, Egyptian History) using library catalogs.
5.  **Information Verification:** Critical evaluation of fake news, suspicious quotes, and edited media using verification tools.
6.  **Treasure Hunt:** Locating items via call numbers and answering general knowledge questions.
7.  **Dewey Challenge:** Identifying books based on Dewey Decimal System clues.
8.  **Puzzle Piece Hunting:** Reconstructing a map or quote hidden across different library genres.
9.  **Photo Hunt:** Navigating the physical library space to locate specific landmarks.
10. **Classification Task:** Applying LC classification rules to new books.
11. **Plagiarism Detection:** Identifying correct citations and plagiarism in text samples.

The authors claim that this structured approach transforms passive learning into active, experiential engagement. They argue that by embedding information literacy tasks within a narrative framework, users are motivated to engage in critical thinking and collaboration without perceiving the activity as traditional instruction. The article concludes that escape rooms are versatile, learner-centered designs that can be adapted to various library contexts, special days, or specific educational goals. While the text does not provide empirical data on user performance or satisfaction from a pilot implementation, it offers a comprehensive blueprint for librarians seeking to enhance user inclusion and promote library services through gamified experiences. The study underscores the importance of careful planning, including flowcharting puzzles and defining clear rules, to ensure the educational objectives are met within an entertaining format.
