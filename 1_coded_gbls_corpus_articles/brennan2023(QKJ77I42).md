# Summary

This feature article by Rachael Brennan, published in *School Librarian* (Volume 71, Number 2, Summer 2023), provides a reflective account of the author’s transition into school librarianship and her subsequent efforts to reimagine the library at Stonyhurst College as a hub for student wellbeing. The text is not an empirical study but rather a professional narrative detailing personal career development and practical library management interventions.

Brennan begins by contextualizing her entry into the profession, noting that the COVID-19 pandemic inspired her to pursue a lifelong ambition of becoming a school librarian. She enrolled in a master’s degree programme in Library and Information Services Management at Sheffield University after previously working as a primary school teaching assistant. Following this academic preparation and experience under an inspirational mentor at Stockport Grammar School, she assumed the role of Librarian at Stonyhurst College in September 2022.

The article describes the initial state of the More Library at Stonyhurst. While the building itself is impressive—a Grade I listed structure with traditional decorative features—the library had not employed a dedicated librarian for several years. Brennan observed that the space functioned primarily as a silent study area. She acknowledges the importance of this function, particularly in a boarding school environment where many pupils reside on campus and require a calm, quiet environment away from lively boarding houses, especially during examination periods. However, she argues that the library had untapped potential to serve broader purposes beyond academic study.

Drawing on research conducted during her master’s studies, Brennan posits that libraries play a critical role in supporting students’ mental health and wellbeing. She identifies the library as a potential "third place"—a welcoming and inclusive space distinct from home and school—that can address the social and emotional needs of pupils. This is particularly relevant in boarding settings where the boundaries between home and school life are often blurred.

To realize this vision, Brennan implemented a series of small but intentional environmental changes aimed at creating a more relaxed and inviting atmosphere. These interventions included streamlining and reorganizing cluttered areas, revamping displays and signage to ensure they felt modern and relevant to current pupils, and adding aesthetic elements such as plants, motivational posters, and artwork. The article notes a specific physical limitation: the library is not directly visible from the school corridor, which may affect its ability to project an inviting presence to students passing by.

The article concludes by highlighting Brennan’s ongoing professional development, noting her sponsorship by the School Library Association (SLA) for a Professional Doctorate in Creative Media Education at Bournemouth University. This suggests a continued commitment to integrating creative and educational practices within library services. The overarching implication is that school librarians can actively shape library environments to support holistic student wellbeing, transforming traditional study spaces into dynamic community hubs. While the article does not provide quantitative data on the impact of these changes, it offers a qualitative perspective on the importance of intentional space design in fostering a supportive educational environment.
