## Purpose
The study aims to systematically review academic publications from 2009 to 2020 to investigate how self-regulated learning (SRL) strategies are interwoven with game-based learning (GBL) in higher education. It seeks to map trends regarding primary research purposes, SRL strategies employed, game types and genres, application domains, research methods, study durations, and specific research issues addressed.
Evidence: "To this end, this study investigated how SRL strategies are interwoven with gamebased learning in higher education by searching the web of science database to systematically review the papers published between 2009 and 2020..." (Abstract)

## Method
The authors conducted a systematic literature review and content analysis following protocols suggested by Moher et al. (2009) and Wu et al. (2012b). They searched the Web of Science (WOS) database using specific keywords related to GBL, SRL, and higher education. The initial search yielded 668 papers, which were filtered down to 55 final articles based on inclusion/exclusion criteria (e.g., peer-reviewed articles, higher education context, empirical focus on SRL in GBL). Two experienced coders analyzed the data using a coding scheme derived from Hwang and Tu (2021) and Lai (2020), achieving a kappa score of 0.81.
Evidence: "This systematic review and content analysis were conducted according to the rigorous protocols suggested by Moher et al. (2009)... The final number of articles analyzed was 55... The coding was done by two experienced coders with a kappa score of 0.81..." (Research methods section)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The population consists of 55 academic journal articles published between 2009 and 2020 in the Web of Science database. These articles represent empirical studies involving undergraduate, graduate, or post-secondary students engaged in game-based learning environments that incorporate self-regulated learning strategies. The data extracted from these articles includes coded dimensions such as research purpose, SRL strategy type, game genre/type, application domain, methodology, and duration.

## Findings
The review identified several key trends. From 2015 onward, research purposes diversified, with skills acquisition becoming the dominant goal (50.91% of articles), followed by knowledge acquisition and behavior change. SRL strategies were increasingly integrated, with mixed strategies being most common; within these, goal orientation, peer learning, and regulating were predominant. These strategies positively impacted learning performance, self-efficacy, attitudes, satisfaction, and learning behavior. Problem-solving (27.27%) and simulation (21.82%) games were the most popular types, often featuring real-life-related storylines (45.45%). Engineering was the leading application domain (21.82%), followed by social studies and language. Experimental designs (43.64%) and mixed methods (36.36%) were the primary research approaches. Most studies lasted less than 8 weeks. Research issues focused heavily on affective outcomes (attitudes, self-efficacy) and cognitive performance, with fewer studies addressing anxiety, collaboration, or specific skill acquisition metrics.
Evidence: "It was found that since 2015, the research purposes have become increasingly diverse, with skills acquisition in game-based learning being regarded as the most important goal... Such games took goal orientation, peer learning, and regulating as the main SRL strategies... problem-solving, simulation, multi-type, and RPG game types against the setting of the real-life-related storyline..." ## Implications
The authors suggest that future research should address gaps in the current literature. Recommendations include: (1) conducting more studies on behavior change and the effects of SRL on learning behavior; (2) exploring the comprehensive application of all SRL strategies rather than isolated tactics; (3) investigating underutilized game types like action, competitive, and strategy games, as well as genres like space exploration or fantasy; (4) expanding application domains to include science, mathematics, arts, and STEM; (5) employing qualitative methods and longer study durations (up to 12 months) to better understand learning processes; and (6) focusing on under-researched issues such as cognitive load, anxiety, technology acceptance, higher-order thinking skills, and collaboration.
Evidence: "Hence, this study proposed corresponding suggestions for future research issues... (1). There are large openings for future studies to conduct behavior change verification... # Summary

Zheng et al. (2024) present a systematic literature review examining the intersection of self-regulated learning (SRL) and game-based learning (GBL) in higher education settings. The study addresses a gap in existing literature, noting that while GBL is recognized for enhancing motivation and engagement, its effectiveness is mediated by learners' SRL competencies. Prior reviews often focused on general GBL effectiveness or theoretical bases without specifically analyzing how SRL strategies are embedded within game mechanics and narratives.

The researchers conducted a systematic search of the Web of Science database for articles published between 2009 and 2020. Using strict inclusion criteria, they filtered an initial pool of 668 records down to 55 empirical studies relevant to higher education students engaging in GBL with SRL components. Two coders analyzed these articles using a structured coding scheme covering research purposes, SRL strategies, game types/genres, application domains, methodologies, and study durations, achieving high inter-rater reliability (kappa = 0.81).

The findings reveal a significant growth in publication volume after 2015. The primary research purpose shifted toward skills acquisition, which accounted for over half of the studies, surpassing knowledge acquisition and behavior change. In terms of SRL strategies, "mixed" approaches involving multiple tactics were most prevalent. Among specific strategies, goal orientation, peer learning, and regulating were most frequently employed, showing positive correlations with learning performance, self-efficacy, satisfaction, and engagement. Conversely, strategies such as task value, elaboration, organization, and help-seeking were underrepresented.

Regarding game design, problem-solving and simulation games were the dominant types, often utilizing real-life-related storylines to contextualize learning. Engineering emerged as the leading application domain, followed by social studies and language arts. Methodologically, experimental designs and mixed methods were preferred, with most interventions lasting less than eight weeks. The research issues predominantly focused on affective outcomes (e.g., attitudes, self-efficacy) and cognitive performance, while areas like anxiety, collaboration, and higher-order thinking skills received less attention.

The authors identify several limitations, including the reliance on a single database (WOS), the exclusion of socially shared regulation models, and the inability to capture rapidly evolving game genres. Based on these findings, they propose six directions for future research: investigating behavior change more rigorously; integrating comprehensive sets of SRL strategies rather than isolated ones; exploring underutilized game types (e.g., action, strategy) and domains (e.g., STEM, arts); adopting qualitative methods to capture fine-grained learning processes; extending study durations to observe long-term growth; and addressing neglected research issues such as cognitive load and collaboration. This review provides a structured framework for educators and developers to better integrate SRL principles into GBL environments in higher education.
