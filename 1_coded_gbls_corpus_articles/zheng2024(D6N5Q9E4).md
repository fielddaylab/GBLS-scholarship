### Summary
# **Zheng, X.-L., Tu, Y.-F., Hwang, G.-J., Yu, J., & Huang, Y.-B. (2024). Interweaving of self-regulated learning and game-based learning in higher education: A review of academic publications from 2009 to 2020. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 72*(6), 3185-3216. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-024-10393-7**

Zheng, Tu, Hwang, Yu, and Huang review how self-regulated learning, or SRL, has been interwoven with game-based learning in higher education. Their starting point is that playful environments can motivate and engage students, but games can also draw learners toward entertainment rather than learning if students lack SRL competence. The authors therefore treat SRL as crucial for effective game-based learning: learners need goal direction, monitoring, regulation, and context-sensitive learning strategies if game activity is to produce academic gains rather than only participation.

The review focuses on academic journal articles published from 2009 to 2020 and indexed in Web of Science. The authors searched for combinations of game-based learning, gamification, self-regulated learning, metacognition, learning strategies, and higher-education terms, then screened records by title, abstract, and full text. From an initial set of 668 records, they retained 55 studies. Their coding scheme drew from technology-based learning review models and SRL literature, with dimensions for primary research purpose, SRL strategies, game type, game genre, application domain, research method, study duration, and research issues. Two experienced coders analyzed the studies and resolved disagreements through discussion.

The findings show a marked increase in SRL-supported game-based learning after 2015. The most common purpose was skills acquisition, followed by knowledge acquisition, mixed purposes, and behavior change. The reviewed studies most often used mixed SRL strategies, with especially frequent attention to goal orientation, peer learning, regulating, planning, seeking information, critical thinking, self-efficacy, monitoring, and task value. By contrast, organization, elaboration, time and study environment, rehearsal, and help-seeking appeared less often. Game types were dominated by problem-solving games, simulation games, and multi-type games, and real-life-related storylines were the most common genre. Application domains included engineering and computing, social studies, language, health and medical education, business and management, science, STEM, and mixed domains.

The discussion is useful because it treats SRL as a design problem rather than a learner trait alone. Zheng et al. argue that game-based learning environments need to make room for dynamic, cyclic SRL processes: task understanding, goal setting, planning, engagement in learning strategies, adaptation, monitoring, and regulation can happen simultaneously and iteratively during play. They also identify gaps in the higher-education literature, including limited attention to all SRL strategies together, limited use of qualitative methods to examine fine-grained learning processes, short intervention durations, and underdeveloped research on cognitive load, anxiety, technology acceptance, higher-order thinking, collaboration, and communication.

For the larger GBLS project, this article offers adjacent design vocabulary for instruction-oriented game services. It supports a caution that game-based library learning should not depend on engagement alone. If a library escape room, tutorial, workshop, or digital game is meant to teach research, information literacy, orientation, or disciplinary practices, the design likely needs explicit goals, prompts, feedback, opportunities for monitoring, social learning, and chances to revisit strategies. The review also helps name a possible assessment agenda: GBLS research can study not only whether patrons enjoyed a game, but whether the game supported planning, confidence, strategy use, collaboration, and learning behavior.

Even so, this source should remain outside the main GBLS review. It is a systematic review of higher-education educational technology studies, not library literature, and it does not examine library collections, library programs, library instruction, patron services, or cultural institutions. It is relevant as background theory for instructional game design, but citing it in the main review would risk shifting the evidence base away from libraries and toward classroom educational technology. It is best retained as excluded adjacent literature that may inform future design or assessment language if the project later builds a separate theoretical appendix.
