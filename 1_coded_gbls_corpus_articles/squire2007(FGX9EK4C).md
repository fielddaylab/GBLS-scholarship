## Findings
The article identifies several key conceptual findings regarding the state of game-based learning research:
1. Traditional educational technology research often ignores out-of-school digital practices and relies on outdated information processing models that treat learning as a commodity to be "increased" via standardized tests.
2. "Serious Games" represent an industry capacity-building sector rather than the academic majority of games and learning research.
3. An emerging paradigm integrates basic theory, critical analysis of game mechanics, naturalistic research, design-based research, and collaborative studies.
4. Effective game-based learning is predicated on situated cognition, where learners take on expert identities and develop fluency in semiotic domains through "designed experiences" that offer hybrid guidance (neither wholly open-ended nor wholly directed).

# Summary

This article by Kurt Squire (2007) serves as a theoretical critique of traditional educational technology research and a proposal for establishing "Games, Learning, and Society" as a distinct academic field. The author argues that current research paradigms are largely positivist and neo-Taylorist, focusing on efficient instruction within controlled circumstances and treating learning as a commodity to be measured by standardized tests. Squire contends that this approach ignores the significant impact of popular digital media and out-of-school gaming practices on cognition and social interaction. He specifically critiques the "Serious Games" movement as an industry-driven capacity-building exercise that does not fully represent the broader academic work emerging in games and learning.

The article posits that educational technologists have been "standing on the sidelines" while scholars in literacy, media theory, and learning sciences have advanced the understanding of digital media. Squire outlines an emerging paradigm for game-based learning grounded in situated cognition theories. This perspective suggests that learning is most effective when students engage in goal-directed activities that allow them to adopt expert identities and develop fluency within specific semiotic domains. Unlike traditional instruction, games offer "designed experiences" that provide hybrid guidance through mechanisms such as differentiated roles, visualization of performance, just-in-time feedback, and seductive failure states. These features support learning without being wholly open-ended or overly directed.

A central finding of the article is the necessity for new assessment methodologies. Squire argues that traditional pencil-and-paper tests are inadequate for measuring the complex skills developed through gaming, such as problem identification, conceptual understanding, and communication within digital environments. Instead, he advocates for portfolio-based assessments and leveraging in-game feedback systems like charts, social network displays, and peer assessments to evaluate student achievement. The article emphasizes that games should not be viewed in isolation but as part of broader learning systems that include instructional supports and scaffolding.

Squire highlights the importance of understanding digital media culture on its own terms. He notes that contemporary digital cultures are deeply participatory, with users actively creating content and interacting in virtual worlds. Research should therefore focus on how these participatory mechanisms can be integrated into educational design. Examples cited include cognitive apprenticeships observed in online gaming communities like Apolyton University, which have inspired after-school programs for children. The author also references work on fan fiction communities for second-language learning and massively multiplayer games as sources of inspiration for designing effective multiplayer learning environments.

The article concludes with a call for interdisciplinary collaboration among media scholars, educational technologists, literacy scholars, learning scientists, and commercial developers. Squire warns against applying outdated information processing models and cookie-cutter research methodologies to new technologies. He argues that the shift toward digital media represents a move toward understanding complex systems and identifying leverage points within them. By embracing this new paradigm, researchers can develop theories that explain how specific game-based approaches work within particular contexts, rather than seeking universal blanket statements about whether games "work." The ultimate implication is that educational technology must evolve to prepare students for an interactive age characterized by participatory media cultures, or risk becoming obsolete.
