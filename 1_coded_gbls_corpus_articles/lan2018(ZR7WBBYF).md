# Summary

This guest editorial, authored by Lan, Botha, Shang, and Jong (2018), introduces a special issue of the *Journal of Educational Technology & Society* focused on technology-enhanced contextual game-based language learning (GBLL). The authors argue that games are significant for human language development because they allow learners to explore alternative decisions and actions without the risk of real-world failure. However, they emphasize that only games involving language input and output at linguistic, semantic, and pragmatic levels contribute effectively to learning. The editorial posits that contextual learning is crucial for second language (L2) acquisition, as it helps learners forge direct links between L2 forms and underlying concepts, providing essential scaffolding in low-stress environments.

The authors highlight that advanced technologies now allow for the creation of authentic L2 learning contexts beyond the conventional classroom. They identify four key technological avenues: computer-mediated communication (CMC) for virtual community engagement; mobile devices for presenting real-world situations; augmented reality (AR) for bridging real and virtual worlds; and 3D created environments for immersive experiences. The special issue aims to provide insights into the application, evaluation, and comparative outcomes of these technologies in various contexts, as well as their impact on knowledge transfer.

The editorial summarizes ten empirical studies included in the issue, detailing their methodologies and findings:
1.  **Rusman et al.** conducted a three-cycled design research study using a mobile game ("ELENA goes shopping") with children and adults. Data from questionnaires, interviews, and language tests confirmed the game aided children in gaining knowledge of neighboring languages.
2.  **Sun and Hsieh** introduced a gamified interactive response system (IRS) to 144 Taiwanese junior high school students. Qualitative and quantitative data indicated benefits in intrinsic/extrinsic motivation, engagement, and attention.
3.  **Lin et al.** examined flipped contextual game-based learning for English business writing with 68 participants (35 experimental, 33 control). Mixed methods analysis showed the experimental group had better writing performance, increased motivation, and fewer errors.
4.  **Wen** designed an AR-based Chinese character composition game ("ARC") for CSL beginners in Singapore. Evaluations of character knowledge and cooperative learning suggested the system benefited natural interactions and ameliorated learning outcomes.
5.  **Wei et al.** evaluated a competitive English vocabulary game with personalized assistance among 120 college students. Results indicated improved vocabulary acquisition, performance, and reduced anxiety.
6.  **Yükseltürk et al.** investigated Kinect technology in game-based learning with 62 college students (experimental vs. control). Findings confirmed benefits for self-efficacy and attitude toward English learning.
7.  **Yang and Quadir** studied an MMORPG-based educational game with 55 sixth-grade pupils, examining relationships between prior knowledge, proficiency, performance, and anxiety. They provided suggestions for digital GBLL application based on these variables.
8.  **Chen et al.** developed "PlanetAdventure," a contextual game-based system with narrative elements, tested on 61 first-year college students. Analysis of English results, perceptions, and behavior indicators indicated benefits to English studies.
9.  **Vazquez-Calvo** used a case study method to explore an online gamer’s language learning through game translation. Data from interviews, observations, and screencasts identified features of gamers' literacy practices and workflow.
10. **Lan et al.** elucidated design principles for 3D virtual language learning environments for special education students. Using a two-cycled design-based research approach with four pupils comorbid with language delay, they proposed principles for effective 3D environment design.

The authors conclude that these studies collectively demonstrate the potential of combining digital technology and context for language learning. The variables explored were multi-faceted, covering performance, attitudes, and behaviors. The editorial asserts that these papers provide a deep understanding of contextual games in language learning and identify areas for future research, particularly regarding the transfer of knowledge from formal to real-life contexts.
