# Summary

This article presents a professional book review of *Everyone Plays at the Library: Creating Great Gaming Experiences for All Ages* by Scott Nicholson, published in 2010. The reviewer, Dale Storie, evaluates the text’s relevance to library science, specifically focusing on its utility for integrating games into library services. The review highlights Nicholson’s standing as a prominent figure in the field of library gaming, noting his academic and professional background which lends credibility to the work.

The central argument of Nicholson’s book, as identified by Storie, is that gaming should be viewed primarily as a social experience rather than merely a recreational activity. The text emphasizes how games can be strategically integrated into library programs to fulfill broader institutional missions, such as community engagement and patron outreach. Storie notes that while the book is applicable to various library types, it holds particular value for public and school libraries, where gaming is often used to reach underserved populations and serve as a community hub.

Structurally, the review outlines the book’s three-part organization. The first part introduces the concept of gaming in libraries and aligns it with standard mission statements. The second part functions as a "reader’s advisory," categorizing games into five distinct archetypes: knowledge, strategy, action, narrative, and social gaming. This categorization allows librarians to select games based on specific programmatic goals. The third part addresses the practical logistics of implementation, including facilitation techniques, marketing strategies, and evaluation methods.

A significant finding in the review is the book’s inclusivity regarding game formats. Storie praises Nicholson for moving beyond the dominant narrative of digital video gaming, which often carries high costs and technical requirements. Instead, the book reviews a wide spectrum of games, including board games, card games, word games, and live-action role-playing games. The reviewer argues that this approach debunks the "marketing hype" of the video game industry, asserting that successful library gaming programs depend more on staff enthusiasm and the quality of gameplay than on purchasing the latest, most expensive digital titles.

The review also highlights the depth of the individual game recommendations provided in the text. Each game is analyzed with specific metrics relevant to librarians, such as cost, number of players, complexity level, recommended age group, play duration, and the specific skills developed (e.g., teamwork, strategic planning). This detailed advisory content is described as a major strength, distinguishing it from other literature that may focus more on theory than practical selection.

In conclusion, Storie recommends the book for librarians and educators seeking to incorporate gaming into their programming. The review positions the text as a cost-effective and durable resource, noting that its physical format and broad applicability give it a longer shelf-life than rapidly evolving digital media. The overall tone is positive, emphasizing the book’s practical utility, comprehensive scope, and alignment with library goals of inclusivity and community engagement.
