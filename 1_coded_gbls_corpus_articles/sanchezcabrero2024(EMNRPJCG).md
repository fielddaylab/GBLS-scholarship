### Summary
# **Sánchez-Cabrero, R. (2024). Conexiones en las nuevas metodologías educativas del Lore como recurso educativo [Connections in the new educational methodologies of Lore as an educational resource]. *Educación y Humanismo, 26*(46), 126-152. https://doi.org/10.17081/eduhum.26.46.6668**

Sánchez-Cabrero asks whether lore, now a major narrative form in video games, can be converted into a useful resource for formal education. The article is written in Spanish and located in educational innovation rather than library studies. It defines lore as a body of cultural and social knowledge, then narrows the concept for digital games: optional or structural secondary stories, world rules, objects, histories, wikis, and background systems that deepen player immersion and allow players to choose how much of a fictional world they want to understand.

The article is a theoretical review with three aims. First, it reviews the existing academic literature on educational use of video-game lore. Second, it compares lore with common game-derived educational approaches, especially gamification, game-based learning, and serious games. Third, it examines educational paradigms that share lore's emphasis on personalization, interactivity, autonomy, optional exploration, and distributed structure, including connectivism, universal design for learning, multi-level teaching, student-centered learning, personal learning environments, learning landscapes, Design Thinking, problem-based learning, challenge-based learning, and the Maker Movement.

For the first aim, the author followed a PRISMA-style search in Web of Science and Scopus during October 2023. The search combined lore terms with video game/gaming terms and education/learning terms. It retrieved nine records from Web of Science and 23 from Scopus; after removing three duplicates, 29 publications were screened. Nineteen were excluded because they did not address video-game lore, and four more were excluded because they did not address learning or educational use. Six publications, published between 2016 and 2023, were included as directly relevant to educational use of video-game lore.

The six included studies are treated as an emerging but still small evidence base. Sánchez-Cabrero highlights work on *League of Legends* lore and motivation to read, *Dota 2* cosmetic goods and collective value-building, a gamified city/public-institution application, *Valiant Hearts: The Great War* as an interactive museum for history learning, the relationship among video games, history, and gender, and archaeology as represented in *League of Legends* lore. The author concludes that lore has shown potential for motivating reading, cultural learning, historical and archaeological interest, prosocial values, and curiosity-driven learning, but that the literature is early and limited.

The discussion argues that lore sits between free and guided play. Like free play, it is open, optional, and personally driven; like guided play, it is structured by a designer through a prebuilt world, narrative, and rule system. Compared with gamification, lore offers more freedom and personalization but much less explicit progress structure. Compared with serious games, lore is less directly tied to performance improvement or real-world problem response. The article therefore imagines lore as a complementary educational design resource: it can enrich gamification, serious games, learning landscapes, problem-based learning, challenge-based learning, maker learning, and student-centered digital environments by providing optional depth, narrative coherence, and meaningful context.

The conclusion is cautiously optimistic. Sánchez-Cabrero argues that educational use of lore is feasible and may positively affect students' performance by supporting intrinsic motivation and reducing resistance to learning situations. At the same time, lore-based formal education is difficult to design because it requires a complex, open, optional, reusable knowledge world. The initial production cost may be higher than other active or ludic methods, but once built, lore can operate with less direct teacher intervention and can serve multiple learners simultaneously. The author expects future technological advances, especially artificial intelligence, to make educational lore easier to produce and adapt.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful as indirect design background for narrative and world-building approaches. It suggests that library game design workshops, local-history games, fandom/lore discussions, digital storytelling programs, and TTRPG world-building sessions could use lore to support curiosity, self-directed exploration, cultural learning, and reflective engagement. However, the article does not study libraries, librarians, patrons, collections, information literacy, public programming, or library outcomes. It should therefore remain outside the main review except as broader educational-game theory.
