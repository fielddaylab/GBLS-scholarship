## Purpose
The article aims to conduct a theory-based meta-analysis examining the effects of Digital Game-Based Learning (DGBL) interventions on cognitive, metacognitive, and affective-motivational learning outcomes specifically within school contexts. It seeks to address limitations in prior meta-analyses by focusing on recent studies (2015–2020), a specific target population (pupils aged 6–18), and distinct learning outcome categories derived from the Integrated Design Framework for Playful Learning.
Evidence: "Therefore, the aim of this meta-analysis is a theory-based examination of DGBL interventions' effects on different learning outcomes (cognitive, metacognitive, affective-motivational) in the school context, using studies published between 2015 and 2020..." (Abstract, p. 2)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employs a quantitative meta-analysis methodology. It utilizes random-effects models to estimate average effect sizes (Hedges’ g) and meta-regression models to analyze moderator variables. The literature search covered multiple databases (PubMed, ProQuest, IEEE, ACM, Web of Science, EBSCOhost, Google Scholar) for studies published between 2015 and 2020. Eligibility criteria required quasi-experimental pre-post-control designs, digital game or simulation interventions, and quantitative data allowing effect size calculation. Inter-rater reliability was assessed using Cohen’s Kappa.
Evidence: "The random-effects meta-analysis and meta-regression models to analyze the impact of the moderator variables were carried out using R... and the metafor package..." (Method, p. 28); "Eligible studies had to be articles published in peer-reviewed journals or conference proceedings." (Method, p. 24)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The final sample consisted of N = 35 studies (one outlier excluded from an initial 36), representing a total of N = 7,139 participants. The participants were pupils aged 6 to 18 years (mean age = 10.89 years; SD = 2.41), primarily from grades 1–13. The studies were conducted globally: 16 in Asia, 10 in Europe, 6 in America, 2 in Australia, and 1 in Africa. Data included cognitive (n=57 outcomes), metacognitive (n=5 outcomes), and affective-motivational (n=27 outcomes) measures.
Evidence: "The n = 35 studies represent N = 7,139 participants in total. Participants’ average age was 10.89 years... Most of the studies came from Taiwan..." (Results, p. 29); "In total n = 57 cognitive learning outcomes, n = 5 metacognitive learning outcomes and n = 27 affective-motivational learning outcomes were observed." (Method, p. 27)
Confidence: high

## Findings
Random-effects models revealed a significant medium overall effect for DGBL compared to traditional instruction (g = .54). Specifically, there was a significant medium effect for cognitive learning outcomes (g = .67) and a significant small effect for affective-motivational outcomes (g = .32). No significant effect was found for metacognitive outcomes (g = .32, p = .276). Moderator analyses indicated no significant influence from personal factors (age, gender), environmental factors (number of sessions, playing mode, competition, dimensionality, visual realism, narrative, avatar, digital agent), or confounding factors (publication year, country). No evidence of publication bias was detected.
Evidence: "Results from random-effects models revealed a significant medium effect for overall learning (g = .54) and cognitive learning outcomes (g = .67). Also found were a small effect for affective-motivational learning outcomes (g = .32) and no significant effect for metacognitive learning outcomes." (Abstract, p. 2); "Further meta-regression models did not reveal evidence of moderating personal, environmental, or confounding factors." (Abstract, p. 2)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The findings partially support the positive impact of DGBL in schools, particularly for cognitive and affective-motivational outcomes. The lack of significant moderator effects suggests that DGBL is generally effective across various design features and contexts within the studied parameters. The authors imply that while DGBL is superior to traditional instruction for knowledge acquisition and motivation, its impact on metacognitive skills remains unproven in this dataset. Recommendations for practice are derived from these general effectiveness trends rather than specific design optimizations.
Evidence: "The findings partially support the positive impact of DGBL interventions in school, and the study addresses its practical implications." (Abstract, p. 2); "...provide recommendations for the design of future digital learning environments." (Objectives, p. 19)
Confidence: medium

# Summary

This meta-analysis by Barz et al. (2023) investigates the efficacy of Digital Game-Based Learning (DGBL) interventions in school settings, specifically targeting pupils aged 6 to 18. The study addresses gaps in previous literature by focusing on a recent publication window (2015–2020) and categorizing learning outcomes into cognitive, metacognitive, and affective-motivational domains based on the Integrated Design Framework for Playful Learning.

The methodology involved a systematic search of seven major academic databases, resulting in an initial pool of 7,013 studies. After rigorous screening based on eligibility criteria—including quasi-experimental design, digital game or simulation intervention, and quantitative reporting—36 studies were selected. One study was excluded as an outlier due to an unrealistically high effect size, leaving a final sample of 35 studies comprising 7,139 participants. The coding process utilized two independent raters with inter-rater reliability assessed via Cohen’s Kappa, achieving moderate to very good agreement across variables.

The primary findings indicate that DGBL interventions yield a significant medium overall effect size (g = .54) compared to traditional instruction methods. When disaggregated by outcome type, cognitive learning outcomes demonstrated a significant medium effect (g = .67), suggesting that digital games are effective for knowledge acquisition and retention. Affective-motivational outcomes showed a significant small positive effect (g = .32), indicating improved attitudes, emotions, or motivation associated with game-based learning. However, metacognitive outcomes did not show a statistically significant effect (g = .32, p = .276), likely due to the limited number of studies measuring this variable (n=5).

Extensive moderator analyses were conducted to determine if personal factors (age, gender), environmental factors (session count, playing mode, competition, dimensionality, visual realism, narrative, avatars, digital agents), or confounding factors (publication year, country) influenced these effects. The results revealed no significant moderating effects for any of the tested variables. This suggests that within the constraints of the included studies, the effectiveness of DGBL is robust across different design features and demographic contexts. Additionally, tests for publication bias (Fail-safe N, funnel plots, Egger’s regression) indicated no evidence of bias, strengthening the validity of the reported effects.

The study concludes that DGBL is generally superior to traditional instruction for cognitive and affective-motivational learning in schools. However, the lack of significant moderator effects implies that specific design elements (such as narrative or competition) do not consistently enhance learning outcomes more than others in this dataset. The authors note that while DGBL supports learner-centered pedagogy and engagement, its impact on higher-order metacognitive skills remains inconclusive due to insufficient data. These findings provide a nuanced evidence base for educators and researchers, highlighting the general utility of digital games in education while identifying areas requiring further empirical investigation, particularly regarding metacognition and specific design optimizations.
