## Purpose
The study aimed to measure burnout levels among health sciences librarians at the Spencer S. Eccles Health Sciences Library and evaluate whether a serious game intervention could improve personal well-being, workplace collegiality, and reduce burnout symptoms.
Evidence: "The purpose of this study was to investigate health sciences librarians’ burnout before and after a game intervention that was designed to decrease burnout and increase a sense of community." (Introduction)
Confidence: high

## Method
The researchers employed a quasi-experimental design involving pre- and post-intervention surveys. A modified American Medical Association (AMA) Mini-Z burnout survey was administered in 2016 (faculty only), 2017 (faculty and staff), and 2018 (faculty and staff). Between the 2017 and 2018 surveys, a three-month team-based "Wellness Game" was implemented. The game utilized design box methodology to target personal resilience and social connection through four categories: physical, mental, social, and appreciation. Data analysis included statistical comparisons using Fisher’s exact tests and Barnard’s exact tests for risk differences, with Cohen’s h reported for effect sizes due to small sample sizes. Qualitative data from post-game surveys were thematically coded using Strauss and Corbin’s method.
Evidence: "A modified American Medical Association Mini-Z burnout survey was administered... A three-month team-based game was implemented and assessed as an intervention... Statistical analysis of survey data... Qualitative analysis of game evaluation survey" (Methods)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The population consisted of employees at the Spencer S. Eccles Health Sciences Library at the University of Utah. The sample included library faculty and staff. In 2016, 15 faculty members were eligible for the baseline survey (80% response rate). In 2017, 14 faculty and 39 staff were eligible (50% and 33% response rates, respectively). In 2018, post-intervention surveys were administered to faculty and staff (93% and 54% response rates, respectively). For the post-game evaluation survey, 30 out of 59 employees (50.85%) completed the instrument. Demographic data indicated no significant differences in sex, age, or race between faculty and staff, though a significant difference was found regarding Latino/Hispanic origin reporting.
Evidence: "Eligibility to participate in the survey expanded from library faculty members in 2016 to both library faculty and staff in 2017... Thirty out of the 59 employees (50.85%) completed the post-game survey." (Methods; Results)
Confidence: high

## Findings
Quantitative results indicated that library faculty reported high levels of burnout indicators, with 38%–73% reporting emotional exhaustion and 54%–91% reporting job-related stress. In 2017, 62% of staff reported experiencing burnout. Post-intervention data showed that while there were trends toward decreased burnout symptoms and increased control over workload for both faculty and staff between survey years, these changes were not statistically significant. However, effect sizes for some variables (e.g., effective teamwork) were large. Qualitative findings revealed that 70% of post-game respondents reported the game encouraged socialization with colleagues. Thematic coding identified four positive aspects: sociability, motivation, gameplay, and fun. Negative aspects included tedious record-keeping, low team engagement, and excessive game duration. The intervention did not significantly reduce burnout or increase job satisfaction but improved collegiality and recognition.
Evidence: "Although the game intervention did not significantly reduce burnout or increase job satisfaction, it improved collegiality and recognition... 70% of post-game survey respondents reported that the game encouraged them to socialize with colleagues." (Abstract; Results)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that while serious games can foster team building and a more social workplace culture, they may not be sufficient to address the root causes of burnout, such as organizational stressors or workload issues. They recommend a multifactorial approach to well-being, including addressing organizational-level predictors of burnout as described by Maslach. The study suggests that future interventions should consider broader systemic changes alongside individual-focused activities like games.
Evidence: "Therefore, a workplace well-being game can encourage team building but may not sufficiently address the root causes of health sciences librarian burnout... LIS professionals and researchers should investigate Maslach’s organizational level predictors of burnout" (Conclusions; Discussion)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This study investigates the prevalence of burnout among health sciences librarians at the Spencer S. Eccles Health Sciences Library and evaluates the efficacy of a serious game intervention designed to enhance workplace well-being and collegiality. The authors define burnout as a psychological syndrome characterized by emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment, noting that while burnout is well-documented in healthcare, it has been less frequently studied specifically within health sciences librarianship.

The methodology involved a longitudinal survey design using a modified American Medical Association (AMA) Mini-Z burnout instrument. Surveys were administered to library faculty in 2016 and to both faculty and staff in 2017 and 2018. Between the 2017 and 2018 surveys, the researchers implemented a three-month "Wellness Game." The game was designed using design box methodology to target personal resilience and social connection, focusing on four dimensions: physical, mental, social, and appreciation. Participants were organized into teams and earned points for wellness activities, with bonus points for collaborative efforts. The intervention aimed to give employees control over their participation while fostering a sense of community.

The population consisted of library faculty and staff. Response rates varied across the three survey periods, ranging from 33% to 93%. Demographic analysis revealed no significant differences in sex, age, or race between faculty and staff groups, although reporting on Latino/Hispanic origin differed significantly. The sample size was small, necessitating the use of exact tests for statistical comparisons and Cohen’s h for effect size estimation.

Key findings indicate that burnout levels were initially high, particularly among faculty, with 38%–73% reporting emotional exhaustion and 54%–91% reporting job-related stress. Staff also reported significant burnout (62% in 2017). Post-intervention analysis showed that while there were non-significant trends toward reduced burnout symptoms and increased perceived control over workload, the game intervention did not produce statistically significant improvements in overall burnout or job satisfaction. However, qualitative data from post-game surveys highlighted positive impacts on social dynamics. Seventy percent of respondents indicated the game encouraged socialization with colleagues. Thematic analysis identified sociability, motivation, gameplay, and fun as the most enjoyable aspects of the intervention. Conversely, participants cited tedious record-keeping, low team engagement, and excessive duration as drawbacks.

The authors conclude that while the serious game successfully improved collegiality and recognition among employees, it failed to significantly mitigate burnout or enhance job satisfaction. They argue that games alone cannot address the root organizational causes of burnout, such as workload demands and leadership alignment. The study suggests that effective well-being strategies require a multifactorial approach, combining individual-focused interventions like games with broader organizational changes. This research contributes to the literature on Games-Based Library Services by demonstrating the potential of gamification for team building while highlighting its limitations in addressing deep-seated professional stressors.
