### Summary
# **Casucci, T., Locke, A. B., Henson, A., & Qeadan, F. (2020). A workplace well-being game intervention for health sciences librarians to address burnout. *Journal of the Medical Library Association, 108*(4), 605-617. https://doi.org/10.5195/jmla.2020.742**

Casucci, Locke, Henson, and Qeadan study whether a serious game could improve personal and workplace well-being among employees at the University of Utah's Spencer S. Eccles Health Sciences Library. The article begins with burnout as a workplace syndrome involving emotional exhaustion, depersonalization or cynicism, and reduced personal accomplishment, and then connects burnout to the opposite condition of engagement, described through vigor, dedication, and absorption. The authors note that LIS has a burnout literature, but that health sciences librarians had received little specific attention and that LIS burnout-prevention interventions had not been created and assessed in the field. This makes the study unusual in the GBLS corpus: the game is not for patrons or students, but for library employees themselves.

The project used a modified American Medical Association Mini-Z burnout survey, administered to library faculty in 2016, faculty and staff in 2017, and again in abbreviated form in 2018. The survey included items on job satisfaction, burnout symptoms, job-related stress, teamwork, values alignment with leadership, work atmosphere, and workload control. Because 2017 results showed high burnout, the library and the University of Utah Resiliency Center developed a three-month team-based "Wellness Game" as an intervention. The game was designed to target personal resilience, build a culture of well-being, and give employees control over participation because control over work and environment had appeared as a concern in baseline results.

The game design combined wellness categories, social structure, visible progress, and recognition. Drawing on wellness dimensions, the team selected physical, mental, social, and appreciation as point categories. Appreciation was added because employees reported that they often heard complaints rather than thanks. Library employees were assigned to six cross-departmental teams with captains; participants earned points for any activity related to the categories, with bonus points for doing activities with colleagues. Each team used a large physical game board in the employee break room, and employees could nominate colleagues for serious or funny awards. The game ended with a potluck lunch, awards ceremony, and trophy. This low-tech design matters: the intervention relied on social nudges, shared visibility, team identity, and optional participation rather than on a digital platform.

The quantitative findings were sobering. Library faculty and staff reported substantial burnout and job-related stress, but the game did not significantly reduce burnout or improve job satisfaction. Faculty burnout symptoms were 41.67% in 2016 and 38.46% in 2018; staff burnout symptoms were 61.54% in 2017 and 42.86% in 2018, but the changes were not statistically significant. Several organizational indicators also moved in mixed directions, and the authors argue that broader factors such as workload, leadership, turnover, work atmosphere, and organizational culture likely shaped well-being in ways a single game could not address.

The qualitative findings are more positive, especially around collegiality. Thirty of 59 employees completed the post-game evaluation survey. Seventy percent said the game encouraged them to socialize with others, and more than half reported encouragement in appreciation, physical, and social categories. Free-text responses about what employees enjoyed most were coded into social, motivation, gameplay, and fun; the largest category was social, with comments about team building and increased socializing. Employees reported meeting new people, being invited into conversation in the staff lounge, joining walks or hikes, and eating lunch together. Most respondents said they would play the game again. At the same time, disliked elements included tedious point tracking, uneven team engagement, and excessive duration.

For the GBLS project, Casucci et al. provide a careful caution about wellness-oriented game services. The article supports the idea that games can create low-stakes social contact, recognition, and participation in wellness activities inside library workplaces. It also warns against overstating what a game can do. A playful intervention may build collegiality and make well-being visible, but it may not address root causes of burnout such as workload, leadership, turnover, control, and organizational culture. The study therefore belongs in the review's emerging wellness and care section and in its assessment lessons: success cannot be reduced to enjoyment, participation, or even social connection when the stated problem is structural burnout.

The study's limitations are important but useful. It took place in one health sciences library, used small samples, had uneven response rates, and could not isolate the game from other Resiliency Center and human-resources interventions occurring around the same time. Still, it is one of the clearer empirical examples of a game-based library intervention that reports mixed results rather than a simple success story. It helps GBLS distinguish between games as social and motivational infrastructure and games as insufficient substitutes for organizational change.
