The article aims to demonstrate how scenario planning can serve as a strategic tool for public libraries navigating turbulence, uncertainty, novelty, and ambiguity (TUNA conditions). It specifically examines the Reading Public Library’s (RPL) use of the Oxford Scenario Planning Approach to develop a flexible, long-term strategy that addresses future contextual uncertainties rather than relying on traditional predictive methods.

## Findings
The strategic process resulted in three distinct scenarios for 2040 based on two key uncertainties: the degree of social cooperation/fragmentation and the mode of civic life (digital vs. physical). The scenarios were labeled "Life in the Clouds" (high digital, high cooperation), "The Wild Frontier" (high fragmentation, mixed digital/physical retreat), and "Paris on the Schuylkill" (high physical, high cooperation, cultural flourishing). From these scenarios, four strategic "tracks" were developed: Co-designed Programs, Intentional Partnerships, Staff Reflects the Community, and Create Digital Opportunities. The authors found that this approach allowed the library to make durable decisions relevant across all potential futures, such as creating a Maker Space informed by teen input (addressing multiple tracks) and developing an Art Display Policy to anticipate potential fragmentation and book challenges. The process also facilitated a smooth leadership transition between executive directors.
Evidence: "The committee members met in person twice to facilitate animated discussions and distilled the mural map and three scenarios into four tracks... For each track, the committee developed tasks and metrics." (The outgoing director’s story)

## Implications
The authors argue that scenario planning transforms strategic planning from a rigid, predictive exercise into a flexible, imaginative "playground" that enhances organizational adaptability. They suggest that libraries should view strategy as "serious play," allowing them to suspend current assumptions and explore alternative identities without risking their current operational stability. The article implies that this method is particularly valuable for institutions facing rapid environmental changes, as it prepares leaders to pivot quickly when unexpected shifts occur. It also highlights the potential for libraries to act as transitional spaces for community dialogue and future-oriented decision-making.

# Summary

This article presents a reflective case study on the application of scenario planning within the Reading Public Library (RPL) in Pennsylvania, aiming to navigate strategic uncertainty during the early 2020s. The authors, including RPL’s outgoing and incoming Executive Directors and a scenario planning consultant, argue that traditional strategic planning methods are often insufficient for addressing "TUNA" conditions—Turbulence, Uncertainty, Novelty, and Ambiguity. Instead, they advocate for the Oxford Scenario Planning Approach, which uses storytelling and systems mapping to create plausible alternative futures, thereby enhancing organizational flexibility and adaptability.

The methodology involved a collaborative process with RPL’s strategic planning committee, comprising key staff and board members. Participants engaged in virtual workshops and created an online mural map of the library’s transactional environment, identifying flows of value and external uncertainties. Through iterative discussions, the committee identified two critical axes of uncertainty: the degree of social cooperation versus fragmentation, and the extent to which civic life would be conducted in digital spaces versus physical presence. Using these axes, they developed three scenarios for the year 2040: "Life in the Clouds," depicting a highly digital, cooperative society; "The Wild Frontier," characterized by social fragmentation and retreat into isolated groups; and "Paris on the Schuylkill," envisioning a culturally rich, cooperative community with strong physical civic engagement.

From these scenarios, the library derived four strategic "tracks" designed to remain relevant regardless of which future materializes: Co-designed Programs, Intentional Partnerships, Staff Reflects the Community, and Create Digital Opportunities. The article details how these tracks informed specific operational decisions. For instance, the decision to convert an empty workspace into a Maker Space was justified by its alignment with multiple scenarios: it fostered community co-design (addressing fragmentation risks), provided digital access tools (supporting the digital future), and served as an innovation hub (aligning with the cultural flourishing scenario). Similarly, the development of an Art Display Policy was driven by the need to anticipate potential challenges arising from social polarization, as depicted in "The Wild Frontier" scenario.

The authors emphasize that this process facilitated a seamless leadership transition, as the scenarios provided a shared strategic language and framework that persisted beyond individual tenures. The article concludes by framing scenario planning as a form of "serious play," allowing library professionals to experiment with new identities and strategies in a low-risk environment. This approach not only aids in internal strategic resilience but also positions libraries as proactive partners in community future-making, capable of adapting to unpredictable shifts in the social and technological landscape. The study underscores the value of imagination and narrative in strategic planning, suggesting that libraries can leverage these tools to maintain relevance and agility in an increasingly complex world.
