### Summary
# **Werner, K. (2013). Bringing them in: Developing a gaming program for the library. *Library Trends, 61*(4), 790-801.**

Werner offers a practitioner guide to starting and sustaining a youth gaming program, grounded in the Benton Harbor Public Library's experience. The program began when a parent asked whether her son could use the library as a place to play *Yu-Gi-Oh!* after a local gaming store closed. Staff opened the auditorium once a month for a two-hour supervised gaming afternoon with light refreshments, in-house flyers, and bookmarks. Initial attendance was about fifteen children and teens; after the library added game equipment and collections, attendance doubled within a year. The program eventually split into a children's gaming afternoon for ages twelve and younger and a teen program for ages thirteen and older, with steady attendance over several years.

The article is useful because it traces a complete service arc from one patron request to a durable youth-services program. Werner describes adding a library-owned *Yu-Gi-Oh!* deck for patrons without cards, consulting teen patrons and a local GameStop manager when purchasing systems and games, and connecting gaming afternoons to other library structures. Teen Advisory Board meetings were scheduled before teen gaming, children's book group meetings before children's gaming, and Summer Reading Program rewards were redesigned to include program attendance. Teens who first came for gaming became Teen Advisory Board and Teen Book Club participants, children's-program volunteers, and regular library users. Teen summer reading participation rose from about fifty participants five years earlier to over one hundred in 2011.

Werner's format guidance covers card games, board games, role-playing games, video games, and tournaments. Traditional card games are easy to teach, while collectible card games such as *Yu-Gi-Oh!* and *Magic: The Gathering* can draw dedicated players who need meeting space. Board games should be quick enough for library sessions and staff should play them first so they can teach patrons. Role-playing games require more time, continuity, and a knowledgeable facilitator. Video games require decisions about consoles, controllers, Internet access, content ratings, multiplayer modes, and whether online play should be muted or avoided. Recommended core purchases include approachable multiplayer games, sports games, racing games, dance games, and fighting games that avoid Mature-rated content.

The tournament discussion is especially concrete. Werner recommends agreeing on rules before play begins, using library-owned games and systems to keep the playing field even, choosing games with clear winners, and using brackets, random pairings, byes, double elimination, or points depending on the game. Prizes can be small, and local gaming stores can help with flyers, prizes, advice, and visibility. Staff must be ready to act as referees and adjudicators because competition can heighten conflict if rules feel unclear or shifting.

Operational challenges are treated honestly. Gaming programs can be loud and need appropriate rooms, lock-ins, or quieter tabletop formats. Theft affected games and personal cards, leading the library to check bags, cancel programs temporarily after thefts, store unused games behind locked doors, and consider locking equipment or RFID tags. Downloadable content and online codes made the future of used-game purchasing uncertain. Parent challenges were possible, especially around violent games, so Werner recommends a clear game-selection policy, age-rating restrictions, reviews, and alignment with existing policies for R-rated movies.

For GBLS, Werner is valuable as an implementation manual for youth game programming. It foregrounds flexibility: the most successful programs change with the patrons who attend them. It also shows that gaming can operate as a pipeline into broader library relationships, including advisory boards, book clubs, volunteering, reading programs, collections, and regular use. The evidence is local and mostly descriptive, but the article gives concrete service-design details that remain useful: start from community demand, involve the target audience, consult local expertise, select short multiplayer games, build policies before challenges arise, plan for noise and theft, assess circulation/library-card/program data, and let patrons help shape the program over time.
