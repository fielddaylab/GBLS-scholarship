The implementation of gaming programs led to a significant increase in teen engagement, particularly among adolescent males. Attendance stabilized after initial growth. Libraries should view gaming as a strategic tool for outreach and retention, particularly for hard-to-reach demographics like teen males. # Summary

Kat Werner’s 2013 article, "Bringing Them In: Developing a Gaming Program for the Library," presents a practical guide for librarians seeking to implement gaming initiatives to enhance library relevance and patron engagement, particularly among children and adolescents. The article is grounded in the author’s experience developing the "Game On" program at the Benton Harbor Public Library. The primary objective is to share operational strategies, resource recommendations, and lessons learned from establishing a sustainable gaming environment within a public library setting.

The methodology relies on a descriptive case study of the Benton Harbor Public Library’s gaming program. Werner details the program's evolution from a simple Yu-Gi-Oh! card game session to a comprehensive offering including video games, board games, and tournaments. The population served includes children (aged 12 and under) and teens (aged 13 and older). Data presented in the text indicates that initial attendance was approximately fifteen patrons. By the program’s first anniversary, attendance had doubled to at least thirty participants monthly. Over a four-year period, attendance stabilized, with teen participation ranging from twenty to thirty per month and children’s attendance between twelve and twenty. Additionally, the article reports a significant increase in Teen Summer Reading Program participation, rising from fifty participants five years prior to over one hundred in 2011.

Key findings highlight the program's success in engaging adolescent males, a demographic often difficult to attract to library services. Werner notes that many Teen Advisory Board (T.A.B.) and book club members originated from the gaming program, indicating successful cross-over into other library activities. The article provides detailed recommendations for game selection, distinguishing between traditional card games, collectible card games (e.g., Yu-Gi-Oh!, Magic: The Gathering), board games, role-playing games (e.g., Dungeons & Dragons), and video games. For video games, Werner compares consoles available at the time (Nintendo Wii, Xbox 360, PlayStation 3), noting the Wii’s accessibility and lower cost, while cautioning about online safety issues with Xbox Live. She suggests purchasing used games to maximize budget efficiency and emphasizes selecting games with short play rounds to accommodate multiple participants.

The article also addresses significant operational challenges. Theft of equipment and personal items was identified as a major issue, leading to the implementation of security measures such as locking unused games behind doors, using locked cabinets for consoles, and tagging items with RFID systems where possible. Werner advises establishing clear collection development policies to preempt parental challenges regarding game content, specifically recommending adherence to age ratings (e.g., avoiding M-rated games) and utilizing professional review sources like *School Library Journal* and IGN for selection justification.

In terms of implications, Werner argues that gaming programs serve as a vital outreach tool that can transform transient visitors into regular library patrons. She emphasizes the importance of flexibility and patron involvement in shaping program content. The article concludes that while the gaming landscape is shifting toward digital downloads and handheld devices, traditional console-based programming remains a viable strategy for fostering community engagement and promoting literacy through increased circulation and program participation.
