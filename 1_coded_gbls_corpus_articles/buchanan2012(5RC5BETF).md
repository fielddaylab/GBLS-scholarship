# Summary

This article by Kym Buchanan and Angela M. Vanden Elzen argues that video games are a significant medium worthy of inclusion in 21st-century library services. The authors contend that libraries must evolve to remain relevant, particularly in the face of budget cuts and changing media landscapes. They propose that libraries can fulfill their core missions of providing access, motivation, and guidance by integrating video games into their collections and programming.

To illustrate the communicative power of video games, the authors analyze two specific titles: *September 12th* and *Beyond Good & Evil*. *September 12th*, a browser-based game released in 2003, simulates missile strikes on a Middle Eastern city to demonstrate the collateral damage and cyclical nature of violence in the "war on terror." The authors use this example to highlight how interactivity allows players to experience consequences firsthand, creating a more impactful message than passive media. *Beyond Good & Evil*, a console game from 2003, is presented as a sophisticated narrative tool that explores themes of patriotism, propaganda, and dissent through the role of an investigative journalist. These examples serve to demonstrate that games can encode complex ideas and provoke critical thinking, thereby honoring the spirit of the First Amendment.

The authors define video games by three distinguishing traits: they are visual, interactive, and based on simulations. They argue that these traits demand a mix of traditional literacies (such as interpreting metaphor and author intent) and new literacies (such as understanding simulation mechanics and systemic consequences). For instance, players must develop mental models of the simulated world through trial and error, while also critically assessing the designer's assumptions and biases.

The article identifies three primary motivations for playing video games: immersion, challenge, and connection. Immersion refers to the semi-voluntary experience of being transported into a new context, which can be more intense in games due to player agency. Challenge relates to the concept of "flow," where optimal difficulty leads to engagement and learning; the authors note that the low stakes of the "magic circle" of play encourage risk-taking and mistake-making, which are beneficial for learning. Connection encompasses self-discovery through identity exploration, social bonding with other players, and broader societal dialogue about values and ethics.

Practically, the authors provide guidelines for librarians to implement these concepts. For selection, they advise considering the target audience (general public, educators, or scholars), age appropriateness via ESRB ratings, and genre diversity. They recommend waiting 8–12 months after a new console release before purchasing hardware to ensure market stability. For access, libraries should catalog games effectively, consider circulating consoles, and address security concerns with protective cases. To motivate patrons, the authors suggest organizing gaming tournaments, forming interest groups for multiplayer online games (MMOs), and using games as gateways to related books or non-fiction materials. They also emphasize the importance of staff training to help librarians understand gaming culture and facilitate critical discussions.

The authors acknowledge potential harms, such as sedentary behavior and exposure to inappropriate content, but argue that libraries can mitigate these through guidance and by promoting active gaming systems like the Nintendo Wii. Ultimately, the article posits that by embracing video games, libraries can reach new audiences, foster critical literacy, and support the evolving cultural landscape of media consumption.
