## Purpose
The primary objective of this systematic review was to identify the specific drivers (facilitators) and barriers that influence university educators' adoption and utilization of gamification and game-based learning (GBL). The authors aimed to categorize these factors to provide actionable insights for universities to better support educators in implementing these pedagogical approaches.
Evidence: "Therefore, the primary goal of this systematic review was to identify the drivers and barriers that influence the adoption of gamification and GBL by educators in the university sector." (Introduction)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employed a systematic review methodology adhering to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines. The search strategy covered multiple databases (Web of Science, Scopus, and EBSCO) using specific keywords related to higher education settings, educator populations, gamification/GBL concepts, and adoption outcomes. Eligibility criteria restricted inclusion to peer-reviewed English-language journal articles published from 2011 onwards that explicitly reported university educators' perceptions regarding the use of gamification or GBL. The Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT) was used for critical appraisal of included studies. Data extraction was performed independently by researchers using predefined tables tailored to quantitative, mixed-methods, and qualitative study designs.
Evidence: "This systematic review... followed the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines... The Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT, 2018 version)... was used to critically appraise the methods of the included studies." (Methods)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The review analyzed data from 17 eligible studies (11 quantitative, 2 mixed-methods, 4 qualitative). The primary population across these studies consisted of university educators, including professors, lecturers, and teachers. Sample sizes in the included studies varied significantly, ranging from 6 to 375 participants for quantitative studies, 8 to 41 for mixed-methods studies, and 4 to 16 for qualitative studies. The data extracted focused on educators' self-reported perceptions, attitudes, and experiences regarding the implementation of gamification and GBL.
Evidence: "Seventeen articles (11 quantitative, two mixed-methods and four qualitative) were included in the systematic review... These 11 studies had sample sizes ranging from 6–375 participants..." (Abstract; Results)
Confidence: high

## Findings
The review identified three primary categories of drivers and barriers: attitudinal, design-related, and administrative. 
**Drivers:** Educators were primarily motivated by the belief that gamification/GBL encourages student interaction and collaborative learning, provides fun, improves engagement, and is easy for students to use. Attitudinal factors, particularly perceived usefulness and ease of use, were strong predictors of intention to use these methods.
**Barriers:** Major barriers included a lack of time to develop gamified approaches, skepticism regarding proven benefits (particularly for GBL), classroom setting issues, and administrative constraints such as lack of institutional support or resources. Design-related challenges involved difficulties in linking learning outcomes to game mechanics and finding capable designers.
Evidence: "The primary drivers described by the educators... were their beliefs that it encourages student interactions and collaborative learning; provides fun and improves engagement; and can easily be used by students. Alternatively, the university educators' major barriers included a lack of time to develop gamification approaches, lack of proven benefits and classroom setting issues." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors recommend that universities address attitudinal barriers by sharing high-quality evidence (e.g., meta-analyses) demonstrating the benefits of gamification/GBL. To overcome design-related and administrative barriers, they suggest developing flexible training programs and providing support from teaching staff and administrators. The review implies that educators might benefit from starting with gamification before progressing to more complex GBL implementations due to the higher number of barriers associated with GBL.
Evidence: "Universities may, therefore, need to consider how to provide greater flexibility with the resources and programs they develop to up-skill educators... interested educators may wish to start with gamification before considering whether GBL will be suitable for their students." (Discussion; Conclusion)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This systematic review by Lester et al. (2023) investigates the factors influencing university educators' adoption of gamification and game-based learning (GBL). Despite evidence suggesting educational benefits, many educators do not routinely use these approaches. The study aims to identify specific drivers and barriers to inform institutional support strategies.

The authors conducted a systematic search of Web of Science, Scopus, and EBSCO databases, identifying 1,330 articles. After removing duplicates and screening titles, abstracts, and full texts against strict eligibility criteria (peer-reviewed, English, published post-2011, focusing on educator perceptions), 17 studies were included for analysis: 11 quantitative, 2 mixed-methods, and 4 qualitative. The Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool was used to assess the quality of these studies.

The findings reveal that drivers and barriers can be categorized into attitudinal, design-related, and administrative domains. Key drivers include educators' beliefs that gamification/GBL fosters student interaction, collaboration, fun, and engagement. Quantitative data indicated that perceived usefulness and ease of use significantly predict an educator's intention to adopt these methods. Conversely, significant barriers were identified. Administrative challenges, such as lack of time, insufficient institutional support, and financial constraints, were prominent. Design-related barriers included difficulties in aligning game mechanics with learning outcomes and a lack of technical expertise. Attitudinal barriers involved skepticism about the proven benefits of GBL and concerns that games might be perceived as childish or distracting.

The review notes that while gamification is often viewed as easier to implement and more readily accepted by students, GBL faces greater resistance due to higher development costs and complexity. The authors acknowledge limitations, including potential bias in the included studies (e.g., over-representation of existing gamification users) and variability in sampling methods across different countries and disciplines.

In conclusion, Lester et al. argue that universities must adopt a multi-faceted approach to support educators. This includes disseminating robust evidence to shift attitudes, providing flexible training programs to address design skills gaps, and fostering administrative collaboration to remove structural barriers. The study suggests a phased implementation strategy, encouraging educators to begin with gamification before attempting more resource-intensive GBL initiatives.
