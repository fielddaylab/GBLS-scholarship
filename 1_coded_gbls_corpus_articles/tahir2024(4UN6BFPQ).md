## Purpose
The article aims to propose an integrated evaluation approach (LEAGUÊ-GQM) for planning and executing Game-Based Learning (GBL) studies and to apply this framework to evaluate the effectiveness of a specific Arabic language learning game ("Feed the Monster") for migrant refugee children in an informal setting. Specifically, it seeks to determine how user characteristics—age group, learning modality preference, and prior mobile experience—affect learning outcomes, usability, and gameplay performance.
Evidence: "This paper proposes an integrated approach for planning and executing GBL evaluation studies and presents its application by evaluating the effectiveness of a GBL approach to improve the Arabic reading skills of migrant refugee children in an informal learning setup." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employs a quasi-experimental design with a one-group pretest-posttest structure. The authors developed and applied the integrated LEAGUÊ-GQM approach, which combines the LEAGUÊ framework (covering Learning, Environment, Affective-cognitive reactions, Game factors, Usability, and User) with the Goal-Question-Metric (GQM) model to guide evaluation planning. Data collection involved a one-week intervention comprising an initial playtest session (pre-test, gameplay, interview) followed by daily home use of the game on smartphones, concluding with a post-test. Quantitative data were analyzed using Wilcoxon Matched Pairs Test, Mann-Whitney test, and Spearman correlation. Qualitative data from observations and interviews were analyzed using grounded theory.
Evidence: "A quasi-experiment with a one-group pretest-posttest design was conducted... The study used mixed methods including pre/posttest, questionnaire, interview, game logs, observation checklist, and notes..." (Abstract; Section 3.2.4)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 30 children (14 girls, 16 boys) aged 5–10 years from migrant refugee backgrounds who spoke Arabic but lacked formal reading or writing skills in the language. Participants were recruited through a weekend class program at the Muslim Society Trondheim (MST) in Norway. Data sources included Early Grade Reading Assessment (EGRA) scores for learning gain, game log data for performance metrics, observation checklists for usability, demographic questionnaires, and VARK questionnaires for learning modality preferences.
Evidence: "A quasi-experiment with a one-group pretest-posttest design was conducted with 30 children (5–10 years old) from migrant refugee backgrounds." (Abstract); "The sample comprised 14 girls... and 16 boys..." (Section 3.2.3)
Confidence: high

## Findings
The study found a statistically significant improvement in Arabic reading assessment scores following the GBL intervention (large effect size). Age group significantly influenced learning gain, usability scores, and total levels played; younger children (5–6 years) demonstrated higher learning gains but lower usability scores compared to older children (7–10 years), who played more levels. Learning modality preference and prior mobile experience had statistically significant effects on usability and gameplay performance parameters but no significant effect on learning gain. Qualitative analysis identified specific usability issues faced by children during initial gameplay.
Evidence: "The results show a statistically significant improvement in their reading assessment score... clear impact of children’s age groups on their learning gain, usability score, and total levels played... learning modality preference and prior mobile experience both had a statistically significant effect related to usability and gameplay performance parameters. However, no effect was found on learning gain." (Abstract; Section 4)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors suggest design recommendations for more inclusive GBL development, emphasizing the need to account for user characteristics such as age and prior digital experience. The study highlights the potential of mobile-based GBL for supporting literacy in refugee contexts where formal education is inaccessible. It also validates the LEAGUÊ-GQM approach as a practical tool for structuring GBL evaluations by linking evaluation goals to specific metrics and data sources.
Evidence: "Based on the findings, some design recommendations are suggested for more inclusive design focusing on user characteristics." (Abstract); "The contribution of this paper is two-fold. # Summary

This article by Tahir and Wang (2024) addresses the lack of empirical evidence regarding the effectiveness of Game-Based Learning (GBL) and the absence of standardized evaluation frameworks for educational games. The authors propose an integrated LEAGUÊ-GQM approach, combining the LEAGUÊ framework—which categorizes GBL criteria into dimensions such as Learning, Usability, and User characteristics—with the Goal-Question-Metric (GQM) model to guide systematic evaluation planning. To demonstrate the utility of this framework, the authors conducted a quasi-experimental study evaluating "Feed the Monster" (FTM), an Arabic language learning game designed for Syrian refugee children.

The study was conducted in an informal learning setting involving 30 migrant refugee children aged 5–10 years from Trondheim, Norway. Participants were recruited through a community weekend school and had no prior formal Arabic literacy skills or experience with the FTM game. The methodology employed a one-group pretest-posttest design over a one-week period. Data collection was multimodal, utilizing Early Grade Reading Assessment (EGRA) tests to measure learning outcomes, game logs for performance metrics, observation checklists for usability, and questionnaires to capture demographic data, mobile usage experience, and learning modality preferences (via the VARK instrument).

The primary finding was a statistically significant improvement in Arabic reading assessment scores after the intervention, indicating that the GBL approach was effective in enhancing literacy skills in this context. The analysis further revealed that user characteristics significantly moderated the learning experience. Age group emerged as a critical factor: younger children (5–6 years) achieved higher learning gains but reported lower usability scores and struggled with interface interactions, whereas older children (7–10 years) demonstrated better usability scores and completed more game levels but perceived the content as too easy. Additionally, while learning modality preferences and prior mobile experience significantly influenced usability perceptions and gameplay performance metrics (such as time played and error rates), neither factor had a significant impact on actual learning gains.

Qualitative data from observations and interviews highlighted specific usability challenges, particularly for younger users who found certain gestures and icons difficult to navigate. The authors argue that these findings underscore the necessity of inclusive design practices in GBL development, specifically tailoring interfaces and difficulty levels to accommodate diverse age groups and digital literacy backgrounds. By applying the LEAGUÊ-GQM framework, the study demonstrates a structured method for aligning evaluation goals with measurable outcomes, offering a replicable model for future research in educational game effectiveness, particularly within underserved populations such as refugee children.
