## Findings
Key findings include that patrons enjoyed playful activities, citing "fun" as a primary theme. Activities requiring low concentration and easy start/stop capabilities (e.g., coloring pages, LEGO, puzzles) were more successful than those requiring higher cognitive load or prior skill (e.g., origami, collaborative story-building). Patrons frequently moved portable games to other areas, such as the coffee shop, indicating comfort in breaching liminal spaces. Writing center employees reported using games effectively with international student conversation partners to facilitate language practice. Feedback indicated a desire for more variety and multiplayer games.

## Implications
The authors recommend that libraries start with simple, low-stakes playful activities in liminal spaces to encourage patron comfort and engagement. They suggest observing patron behavior to adapt and grow these offerings, rather than over-planning. # Summary

This article by Ruth Monnier and Lora Winters explores the integration of play into the liminal spaces of an academic library and writing center at Pittsburg State University (PSU). The authors define liminal spaces as thresholds or areas of ambiguity where different functions overlap, such as the boundary between a quiet study environment and a service-oriented writing center. The primary objective is to demonstrate how playful activities can promote student engagement with these physical spaces, particularly in the context of post-pandemic challenges where institutions must justify their physical presence and attract students back to campus facilities.

The methodology involves an exploratory qualitative approach. The authors implemented a series of playful interventions in liminal areas outside the writing center and near library reading zones. These interventions included jigsaw puzzles, coloring pages, LEGO bricks, origami, board games (e.g., Connect 4, Scrabble), and card games. Data was collected through visual observation of patron interactions with these materials during International Games Week (IGW) 2021 and subsequent weeks, followed by informal semi-structured interviews with patrons who engaged in the activities and with writing center staff.

The findings reveal distinct preferences among adult learners regarding play. Activities that required minimal cognitive load and allowed for easy entry and exit were most successful. Coloring pages and LEGO bricks were highly popular; coloring provided a low-stress "brain break," while LEGO served as both a fidget tool and a creative outlet, with some students using blocks to model chemical structures for lab reports. Conversely, activities requiring higher concentration or specific skills, such as origami and collaborative story-building on an easel pad, saw lower participation. The authors hypothesize that adult patrons in these spaces are often seeking relaxation rather than rigorous mental engagement, and may experience performance anxiety regarding creative tasks like writing stories.

Observational data indicated that portable games were frequently moved by patrons to other areas, such as the library’s coffee shop ("Axe Grind"), suggesting that access to play materials helps students feel comfortable navigating and utilizing various parts of the library. Interviews with writing center employees highlighted the utility of games in facilitating conversation with international students learning English, where games like card games helped maintain dialogue flow without the pressure of formal instruction.

The authors conclude that playful activities in liminal spaces effectively reduce library anxiety and foster a sense of community. They recommend that other libraries adopt a "start simple" approach, utilizing passive programming to invite interaction without demanding commitment. Future plans for PSU include expanding the variety of multiplayer games and placing them closer to social hubs to further integrate play into the library experience. The study underscores the value of play not just as recreation, but as a strategic tool for spatial engagement and student well-being in higher education settings.
