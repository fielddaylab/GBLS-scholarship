### Summary
# **Noorda, R., & Inman Berens, K. (2021). Immersive media and books 2020: New insights about book pirates, libraries and discovery, millennials, and cross-media engagement: Before and during COVID. *Publishing Research Quarterly, 37*, 227-240. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12109-021-09810-z**

Noorda and Inman Berens summarize findings from *Immersive Media & Books 2020*, a large consumer-behavior survey about books, video games, TV/movies, discovery, libraries, and cross-media engagement before and during COVID-19. The study uses the term "book engagement" rather than simply reading or buying, defining it broadly as buying, borrowing, subscribing to, reading, or gifting a printed book, ebook, or audiobook in whole or in part. That definitional move is important because it treats media use as a wider ecology of practices: people discover, sample, collect, borrow, multitask, gift, display, and move across formats rather than simply consuming one medium in isolation.

The article is especially useful for GBLS because it refuses to separate books, games, and TV/film into discrete cultural silos. It describes "immersive" media as deep involvement in a topic or storyworld rather than as a state of uninterrupted attention. In this model, books, games, TV, and movies are all longform media through which audiences pursue worlds, genres, authors, franchises, and social recommendations. The survey included 4,314 respondents recruited through Qualtrics, with quotas for age, race, gender, and region aligned with U.S. Census data. Respondents had to have engaged with at least one book in the previous twelve months, and the authors note that the broader concept of engagement likely captures more activity than narrow reading measures.

Several findings matter for libraries. Book discovery is highly distributed, with recommendations from friends, favorite authors, family, and social media all playing roles, but no single method dominating. Author brand and genre are major drivers of discovery and purchase. Black and Latinx millennials appear as particularly avid book engagers, complicating industry assumptions about who constitutes the core reading public. The study also finds that book discovery is context-agnostic: people discover books in one setting and acquire or borrow them in another. Libraries are central to that ecosystem. About one-third of respondents bought a book online that they first found in a library, and about one-third bought a book in a bookstore that they first found in a library. Millennials showed even stronger cross-channel movement from library discovery to online or bookstore purchase.

The cross-media findings are the clearest bridge to GBLS. Noorda and Inman Berens report that 60.3% of respondents discover TV/movies or games through books, 61.2% discover books or games through TV/movies, and 33.1% discover books or TV/movies through games. Cross-media discovery is highest among millennials: 72.4% discover TV/movies and games through books, 72.9% discover books or games through TV/movies, and 48.1% discover books or TV/movies through games. The article therefore gives empirical backing to a claim often made in library gaming discourse: patrons do not necessarily experience books and games as separate service worlds. They move across narrative, genre, fandom, platform, and format.

For the larger GBLS project, this source supports collection development, readers' advisory, media advisory, display work, and programming that treats games as part of a connected cultural media environment. It suggests that gaming collections and programs can support book discovery and that book-centered services can also lead patrons toward games, films, and other media. It also reinforces the value of browsing and in-person discovery spaces: public library shelves remain one of the preferred discovery contexts, and library borrowing can encourage rather than cannibalize sales. For libraries, this matters because games-based services need not be justified as a departure from traditional book culture. They can be framed as an extension of how contemporary patrons already follow stories, genres, creators, and communities across media.

The article has limits for GBLS. It is not a study of game collections or game programs specifically, and it does not assess library gaming outcomes. Its emphasis is on consumer behavior around books in a larger media ecosystem. Still, that perspective is valuable because it gives GBLS a stronger audience rationale. If avid book engagers are also active across TV/film and games, then game-based library services can be understood as part of integrated cultural advisory, discovery, and access work rather than as a niche recreational add-on.
