# Summary

This article summarizes the findings of the "Immersive Media & Books 2020" report, a study conducted by Rachel Noorda and Kathi Inman Berens from Portland State University. The research investigates consumer behavior regarding books, video games, and TV/movies, specifically analyzing shifts before and during the COVID-19 pandemic. The primary objective was to situate books within a broader media ecosystem and evaluate the role of libraries in this network.

The methodology relied on a survey of 4,314 U.S. respondents collected in September and November 2020 via Qualtrics. To ensure demographic representativeness, quotas were enforced for age (millennials, Gen X, baby boomers), race/ethnicity, gender, and region, aligning with U.S. Census data. Participants were screened based on having engaged with at least one book in the preceding twelve months. The authors define "book engagement" broadly to include buying, borrowing, subscribing, reading, or gifting books in any format, aiming to capture behaviors beyond traditional reading metrics.

Key findings reveal that book discovery is highly distributed through word-of-mouth channels, including friends, family, and social media. While genre is the most significant factor driving purchase decisions, author brand plays a crucial role in discovery, particularly for men, millennials, and Black and Latinx respondents. The data challenge traditional assumptions about immersion, showing that multitasking is prevalent; 61% of ebook readers and 70% of audiobook listeners engage with content while performing other tasks.

Demographically, the study highlights that Black and Latinx millennials are avid book engagers, with higher participation rates than white respondents. Specifically, 67% of Black/African American respondents and 59% of Latina/Latino/Latinx respondents engaged with four or more books per month, compared to 50.1% of European American/white respondents. This suggests the publishing industry has historically underestimated these audiences.

The report emphasizes cross-media engagement, noting that consumers frequently traverse between books, games, and TV/movies. Millennials exhibit the highest rates of cross-media discovery. Regarding piracy, the authors present a nuanced view: book pirates are not merely thieves but often avid readers, library patrons, and buyers who pirate when value propositions are unclear. They are significantly more likely to engage with fanfiction, indicating deep investment in storyworlds.

Crucially for library services, the data demonstrate that libraries function as discovery tools that reinforce sales rather than cannibalize them. Approximately one-third of respondents purchased books online or in bookstores after discovering them in libraries. Library card holders were more likely to increase their book buying during COVID-19 compared to the general population. When desired titles were unavailable at libraries, patrons frequently purchased them elsewhere, indicating that library access drives demand across channels. The authors conclude that these insights necessitate a reevaluation of industry strategies regarding diverse audiences, cross-media collaboration, and the economic value of library lending.
