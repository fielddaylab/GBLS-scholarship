## Findings
The study identified four primary categories of complex relationships that challenge current bibliographic standards:
1. **Edition Relationships:** Video games often exist in multiple formats (e.g., arcade, physical disc, digital download) and editions (e.g., "FES" versions). Traditional print-centric definitions of "edition" are inadequate because game variations may differ in source code, visual imagery, and gameplay experience, leading some users to consider them distinct games.
2. **Series Relationships:** Game series are sprawling aggregate entities with intricate sub-series and spin-offs. For example, the *Persona* series is a sub-series of *Shin Megami Tensei*, containing unrelated stories in early entries but shared settings and characters in later ones, alongside various anime and media adaptations.
3. **Relationships with Additional Content:** Digital distribution allows for downloadable content (DLC), expansions (e.g., *Civilization V: Gods & Kings*), and patches. These range from significant gameplay additions to security fixes, creating a need to distinguish between content that alters gameplay versus content that maintains it.
4. **Other Relationships:** Many games are "inspired by" or "based on" predecessors (e.g., *Candy Crush Saga* based on *Bejeweled*, which was inspired by *Shariki*). Tracing these lineage relationships is important for scholars and designers but lacks clear representation in current metadata.
Evidence: Sections 4.1 through 4.4 detail these specific challenges regarding editions, series, additional content, and inspirational lineages.
Confidence: high

## Implications
The findings suggest that existing conceptual models like FRBR are insufficient for describing video games due to their print-centric origins. The authors argue that these complex relationships must be clearly defined and incorporated into a new data model to support robust, user-centered game description. # Summary

This poster presentation by Clarke, Lee, Jett, and Sacchi (2014) addresses the inadequacy of current bibliographic conceptual models, specifically Functional Requirements for Bibliographic Records (FRBR), in describing the complex relationships inherent to video games. The authors argue that as video games gain cultural and scholarly significance, the challenges of describing them—particularly regarding digital distribution and non-physical components—require a user-centered approach to metadata. The primary objective was to explore these relationship types to inform the development of a robust metadata schema for the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) and broader digital library contexts.

The methodology combined qualitative consultation with video game experts and enthusiasts alongside a direct examination of specific game sets and their existing metadata structures. The sample population was selected to ensure diversity in genre, platform, creation date, and distribution method. Key case studies included the *Shin Megami Tensei: Persona*, *Disgaea*, *Final Fantasy*, *Bejeweled*, and *Civilization V* series. These "seed games" were chosen specifically because they exhibit complicated relational structures with other titles, editions, and additional content.

The analysis revealed four distinct categories of relationships that current standards fail to capture effectively. First, **edition relationships** are problematic because traditional bibliographic definitions rely on print-centric concepts (e.g., copies from the same typographic image). In video games, "editions" may differ significantly in source code, visual presentation, and gameplay mechanics across platforms (arcade, console, digital network), leading users to perceive them as distinct entities rather than mere variations. Second, **series relationships** are characterized by sprawling, aggregate structures. The *Persona* series, for instance, functions as a sub-series of *Shin Megami Tensei*, containing entries with unrelated narratives, shared settings, and numerous spin-offs, anime adaptations, and merchandise. This complexity extends to other major franchises like *Final Fantasy* and *Civilization*.

Third, the study highlighted **relationships between games and additional content**. The rise of digital distribution has introduced downloadable content (DLC), expansions, and patches that vary in impact. Some additions, such as the *Gods & Kings* expansion for *Civilization V*, introduce substantial new gameplay features, while others merely fix security issues or update software. Current metadata lacks the granularity to distinguish between these types of additions, which is critical for fulfilling user information needs. Fourth, the authors identified **inspirational and lineage relationships**. Many games are explicitly "based on" or "inspired by" predecessors, such as *Candy Crush Saga* deriving from *Bejeweled*, which itself was inspired by the 1984 game *Shariki*. Tracing these genealogical links is valuable for scholars and designers but remains unrepresented in standard cataloging.

The implications of this work are significant for library science and digital preservation. The authors conclude that FRBR’s Group I entities (work, expression, manifestation, item) are insufficient for alternative media like video games. To improve user access and description, future work must clearly define these complex relationships and integrate them into a new conceptual model and metadata schema. This research serves as a preliminary step toward creating a standardized, user-centered framework for describing interactive media in digital libraries.
