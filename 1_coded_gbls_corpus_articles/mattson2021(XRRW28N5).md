# Summary

This article is a professional book review of *Games and Gamification in Academic Libraries*, edited by Stephanie Crowe and Eva Sclippa, published in the Association of College and Research Libraries series. Reviewed by Janna L. Mattson from George Mason University, the text evaluates the volume’s relevance to academic librarians aiming to integrate gaming into library services. The review situates the book within the context of a "cultural renaissance" in traditional board games and the broader educational shift toward active and experiential learning.

The reviewed book is structured into four primary sections. Section I provides an overview of games and gamification, featuring case studies on hosting game nights and training student workers through rewards-based systems like "library flair." The reviewer notes that while these chapters offer best practices for collaboration with campus partners and local businesses, some content regarding escape rooms feels repetitive when compared to later sections. Section II addresses collection development, detailing strategies for acquiring and maintaining both electronic and tabletop game collections. Contributors from institutions such as the University of North Texas and Lycoming College provide practical advice on accessibility, circulation policies, and using databases like BoardGameGeek.com for assessment.

Section III focuses on information literacy instruction. The review highlights chapters that utilize games to address challenges in high-volume one-shot sessions and for-credit courses. Notable examples include the use of *Pandemic Legacy* to teach disinformation identification and escape rooms to foster problem-solving skills. Mattson observes that while these pedagogical approaches are innovative, not all chapters explicitly connect their methods to the ACRL Framework for Information Literacy, a linkage that is present in Chapters 11 and 12 but absent elsewhere. Section IV, the largest section, covers programming and outreach. It includes case studies on transforming library fairs into mystery games, which reportedly tripled attendance at Texas Tech University, and initiatives targeting casual gamers to reduce library anxiety and build community.

Critically, Mattson identifies several limitations in the edited volume. The primary critique concerns structural inconsistency; some chapters include helpful features like best practices and key takeaways, while others do not, leading to a lack of cohesion. Additionally, there is significant repetition regarding specific resources and game types, particularly escape rooms. The absence of a concluding chapter is noted as a missed opportunity to reflect on the future trajectory of gaming in libraries.

Despite these flaws, the review concludes that the book’s greatest strength is its wide appeal and practical utility for librarians across various roles. It serves as a comprehensive guide for implementing game-based services, offering adaptable models for institutions of different sizes. The reviewer affirms that games and gamification are effective tools for enhancing student engagement and learning, recommending the volume as a worthy addition to contemporary professional development literature in library science.
