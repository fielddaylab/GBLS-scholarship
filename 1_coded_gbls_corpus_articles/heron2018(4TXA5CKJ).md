## Purpose
The article aims to introduce and discuss the "Meeple Centred Design" heuristic toolkit, a framework developed by the Meeple Like Us project to evaluate and map the accessibility landscape of tabletop games. The authors seek to address the lack of comprehensive tools for assessing accessibility in tabletop gaming, which varies significantly from video game environments due to diverse interaction regimes (verbal, non-verbal, tactile, visual). The toolkit is intended to serve as a preliminary evaluative exercise for game designers and developers to identify barriers before formal user testing.

We argue that there should be greater inclusion of diversity when play-testing game titles, but hope this framework serves as a useful preliminary exercise for game designers and developers." ## Method
The study employs a heuristic evaluation approach rather than empirical user testing with impaired participants. The authors developed a six-part heuristic toolkit covering visual, cognitive, physical, communication, socioeconomic, and intersectional impairments. This toolkit was applied by qualified researchers to analyze specific game titles. The method involves contemplative analysis of game components, rules, and mechanics against the heuristic criteria to produce "accessibility teardowns." The authors explicitly state that this framework does not replace traditional user testing but offers a lower-cost preliminary assessment.

## Findings
The authors found that tabletop gaming lags significantly behind video gaming in accessibility. Key findings include:
1. **Visual Accessibility:** Color blindness is frequently ignored; contrast and font choices often hinder readability; tactile differentiation of tokens is rare.
2. **Cognitive Accessibility:** Complex rules, state-dependent conditions, and obfuscated scoring systems create high cognitive burdens. Memory requirements for game state can exclude players with cognitive impairments.
3. **Physical Accessibility:** Many games require fine motor skills for manipulating small components (meeple, cards, dice). Paper money is identified as a significant barrier due to lack of tactile differentiation.
4. **Communication & Emotional Factors:** Games requiring bluffing or complex strategy communication can exclude those with communication impairments. "Take-that" mechanics and player elimination can cause emotional distress.
5. **Socioeconomic Barriers:** Representation in artwork and themes often excludes marginalized groups; high costs limit accessibility for budget-conscious players.
6. **Intersectionality:** Compounding impairments (e.g., physical and cognitive) create unique barriers not addressed by single-axis assessments.
Evidence: "Board gaming lags considerably behind even video gaming when it comes to the accessibility of the products that are on the market... Accessibility is a multi-channel challenge where subtle and nuanced interactions of impairments may create complications as a result of intersectionality." (Section 3)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors imply that current industry practices rely too heavily on "do-it-yourself" hacks or partial solutions (e.g., braille dice) rather than inclusive design. They recommend that the heuristic toolkit be used by designers to identify accessibility issues early in development. The study suggests that future research should explore external compensatory technologies (digital apps, cognitive support tools) and that the industry must recognize accessibility as a market opportunity. The framework aims to raise awareness and provide a standardized language for discussing tabletop accessibility.
Evidence: "A real, lasting solution must involve raising everyone to the same standard rather than attempting to create different, incompatible categories of accessible games... Adoption of the framework by designers and publishers of board-games would offer an important step forward in addressing accessibility and inclusion." (Section 3; Section 12)
Confidence: high

# Summary

Heron et al. (2018) present "Meeple Centred Design," a heuristic toolkit designed to evaluate the accessibility of tabletop games. The authors argue that while video game accessibility has gained attention, the modern renaissance of tabletop board games has outpaced efforts to make them inclusive. Tabletop games present unique challenges due to their reliance on physical components, varied interaction regimes (verbal, tactile, visual), and complex rule systems. The study aims to provide a structured framework for identifying barriers that exclude players with visual, cognitive, physical, communicative, or socioeconomic impairments.

The methodology involves the application of a six-part heuristic toolkit to approximately 120 high-profile tabletop games. Unlike empirical studies involving user testing with impaired participants, this approach relies on expert analysis by researchers familiar with accessibility issues. The toolkit categorizes accessibility concerns into visual, cognitive, physical, communication, socioeconomic, and intersectional domains. This method allows for a comprehensive "teardown" of each game, identifying specific design elements that may hinder participation.

Key findings highlight pervasive inaccessibility across multiple dimensions. Visually, games often fail to accommodate color blindness or provide sufficient contrast and tactile differentiation for tokens. Cognitively, complex rules, hidden information, and obfuscated scoring systems impose heavy burdens on working memory and fluid intelligence. Physically, the manipulation of small components like cards, dice, and meeples excludes players with motor impairments; notably, paper money is cited as a frequent barrier due to its lack of tactile distinction. Communication barriers arise in games requiring bluffing or complex strategic dialogue, while emotional accessibility is compromised by mechanics such as player elimination or "take-that" interactions that can cause distress. Socioeconomic factors, including cost and non-inclusive thematic representation, further limit access. The authors emphasize intersectionality, noting that compounded impairments create unique barriers not captured by single-axis assessments.

The implications of this work suggest that the tabletop industry must move beyond partial solutions like braille adaptations toward universal design principles. The heuristic toolkit is proposed as a preliminary screening tool for designers to identify accessibility issues before costly user testing. The authors call for greater diversity in playtesting and advocate for future research into external compensatory technologies, such as digital aids, to support inclusive gameplay. This study contributes a foundational framework for assessing tabletop accessibility, addressing a significant gap in game studies literature.
