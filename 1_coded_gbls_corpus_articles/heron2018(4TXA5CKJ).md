### Summary
# **Heron, M. J., Belford, P. H., Reid, H., & Crabb, M. (2018). Meeple centred design: A heuristic toolkit for evaluating the accessibility of tabletop games. *The Computer Games Journal, 7*(2), 97-114. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40869-018-0057-8**

Heron, Belford, Reid, and Crabb develop the Meeple Centred Design heuristic toolkit for evaluating the accessibility of tabletop games. The article is not about libraries, but it is highly relevant to GBLS because tabletop collections, clubs, game nights, school-library lessons, and public-library programs can unintentionally exclude players when games are selected only for popularity, cost, theme, or age range. The authors argue that tabletop accessibility is more complicated than video game accessibility because board and card games vary widely in game systems, game mechanisms, interaction regimes, sensory demands, social rules, component design, and table logistics.

The article begins from the sociocultural importance of board games. Tabletop gaming is framed as a way to build social capital, participate in popular culture, and share cultural experiences with friends and family. This makes accessibility a cultural inclusion issue, not only a functional design issue. If people cannot participate in popular tabletop games because components, rules, time demands, communication systems, or economic conditions exclude them, they are also blocked from the social and conversational benefits of play.

The authors identify a wide range of accessibility barriers. Physical barriers include small components, spatially complex game states, cards held privately, tile flipping, cube movement, board size, fine motor demands, target zones, shuffling, dice rolling, and repeated physical manipulation. Visual barriers include color coding, poor contrast, small fonts, visually busy boards, component similarity, orientation-dependent symbols, and paper money. Cognitive barriers include complex rules, state-dependent exceptions, large decision spaces, memory burdens, hidden information, randomness, scoring complexity, and rule mutability. Communicative barriers include language demands, hearing requirements, secret communication, negotiation, bluffing, and body-language interpretation.

A key strength of the article is that it also treats emotional and socioeconomic accessibility as part of the design problem. Emotional barriers may arise from high randomness, betrayal, ganging up on a player, harsh loss conditions, take-that mechanics, player elimination, or situations where a game makes one person feel targeted or unable to recover. Socioeconomic barriers include cost, cultural familiarity, hobby knowledge, language, required expansions, and the assumptions games make about who has access to time, money, space, and a game-playing community. This broader framing is especially useful for libraries, which often use games to reduce access barriers but may reproduce them if collection and program decisions ignore who can actually play.

The Meeple Like Us project organizes the heuristic lens around six areas: visual impairments, cognitive impairments, physical impairments, communication impairments, socioeconomic impairments, and intersectional issues where combinations of impairments create new barriers. The authors emphasize that accessibility cannot be solved one impairment at a time. A symbol may work for some players with visual impairments but become difficult when visual and cognitive impairments intersect. A card hand may be manageable for someone with a physical impairment until visual inspection also requires movement or magnification. Dice may create both cognitive burden and physical interaction problems. Time limits may become unfair when compensatory regimes are needed.

The article is careful not to overclaim the heuristic. The authors state that the toolkit does not replace traditional user testing with impaired players. Direct playtesting produces richer feedback, but it also requires recruiting multiple players with varied impairments, teaching games, observing play, interpreting feedback, compensating participants, and adapting to schedule changes. The toolkit is therefore positioned as a preliminary evaluative pass by an informed analyst, helping designers, publishers, or evaluators identify common accessibility issues before deeper user testing. The Meeple Like Us project had applied the framework to more than one hundred games and published accessibility teardowns for the tabletop community.

For the larger GBLS project, Heron et al. offer a practical lens for inclusive tabletop service. Libraries that circulate board games, run tabletop clubs, host D&D or board-game cafes, teach through games, or advise families and educators need a way to talk about more than age recommendations and player counts. The heuristic can support collection development, catalog notes, advisory, program planning, facilitation, accessibility accommodations, staff training, and post-program reflection. It also reminds GBLS practitioners that accessible play may require flexible rules, multiple roles, component supports, alternative communication, larger tables, drop-in/drop-out options, session-length choices, and explicit attention to emotional safety.

The source should still be cited as adjacent rather than library-specific evidence. It does not assess library programs, patron outcomes, or library collection use. It is a game accessibility framework developed for tabletop games broadly. Its value for the review is translational: it gives libraries a vocabulary for evaluating whether tabletop game services are inclusive in practice. Because the GBLS review already includes adjacent audience and culture sources in the inclusion/advisory section, Heron et al. belongs there as the strongest accessibility-focused tabletop design source in this batch.
