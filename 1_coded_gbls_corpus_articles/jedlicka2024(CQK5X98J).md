## Purpose
To document the development, implementation, and assessment of a homemade mobile augmented reality (MAR) game created by librarians at Kingsborough Community College (KCC) using open-source JavaScript libraries (AR.js and A-Frame). The project aimed to orient new students to library spaces and services while demonstrating that librarians can build such tools in-house without commercial applications.
Evidence: "This case study will describe the process by which the KCC librarians created a homemade AR application... To the authors’ knowledge, the approach described below – building an AR game in-house using open source JavaScript libraries – is a novel contribution to the literature of librarianship." (p. 3)

## Method
The study employs a qualitative case study design involving the iterative development and deployment of a Web-based AR scavenger hunt. The methodology includes technical implementation using AR.js and A-Frame hosted on LibGuides, followed by two phases of beta-testing: first as a standalone outreach activity at the reference desk, and second as an integrated component of library instruction sessions. Data collection was informal, relying on direct observation and qualitative verbal feedback recorded in a Google document.

## Population and Data
The participants were undergraduate students at Kingsborough Community College, a public community college in Brooklyn, USA. The sample consisted of two groups: 12 students who completed the activity during standalone reference desk shifts (with an additional unspecified number who started but did not finish), and 34 students from three library instruction sessions who all completed the activity. Data comprised observational notes on student engagement and technical performance, along with informal verbal feedback regarding enjoyment and usability.
Evidence: "Over the course of two shifts, 12 students successfully completed the activity." (p. 24); "All 34 students, from three library sessions, completed the activity." (p. 25)
Confidence: high

## Findings
Technically, the open-source Web AR solution functioned effectively on student smartphones and library tablets, requiring only brief initial Wi-Fi connectivity. However, challenges included marker recognition issues (false positives with 3x3 markers, false negatives with 4x4 markers) and camera selection errors on approximately 5% of devices. Pedagogically, the standalone activity had low participation rates due to student busyness, whereas integration into library instruction sessions resulted in 100% completion and higher engagement. Student feedback was overwhelmingly positive, describing the game as "fun" and "engaging," with some noting increased awareness of previously unknown library resources like study rooms.
Evidence: "The AR activity was very successful as a class-based activity... All 34 students... completed the activity." (p. 25); "Students’ comments were nearly all positive and included that the activity was 'fun', 'cool'..." (p. 25)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors argue that open-source Web AR is a viable, cost-effective alternative to commercial apps for academic libraries, particularly when librarians possess basic JavaScript skills. They recommend integrating AR activities into formal instruction rather than relying on standalone outreach to maximize participation. The study highlights the importance of accessibility considerations (e.g., providing paper checklists) and notes that while current tools like AR.js are functional, long-term software maintainability remains a concern due to the rapid evolution of JavaScript libraries.
Evidence: "Ultimately, the librarians found that the AR activity worked best as an in-class exercise." (p. 26); "Building this game in JavaScript was a useful, constructive exercise... This is a project that can be built by librarians for their libraries." (p. 30)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This case study details the creation and implementation of a homemade mobile augmented reality (MAR) game at Kingsborough Community College (KCC), developed by librarians using open-source JavaScript libraries AR.js and A-Frame. The primary objective was to create an engaging, low-cost orientation tool for new students that would familiarize them with library locations and services while mitigating "library anxiety." Unlike many academic libraries that utilize proprietary commercial applications, KCC librarians opted for an in-house, open-source solution to align with budget constraints and institutional values, demonstrating that such projects are feasible without professional developers.

The methodology involved a planning committee led by the Web Librarian, who programmed the game using AR.js for marker recognition and A-Frame for visual interactivity. The application was hosted on LibGuides, utilizing its group homepage feature as a static web server. The game functioned as a scavenger hunt where students scanned physical markers placed at eight key library locations to trigger virtual shapes on their mobile devices. Development faced technical hurdles, including the need for high-contrast marker printing and issues with camera selection on certain smartphones. Despite these challenges, the final product required only minimal Wi-Fi connectivity after initial loading, enhancing accessibility in a library with unreliable internet infrastructure.

Data collection was informal due to time constraints, relying on a convenience sample of student participants and qualitative feedback. The study evaluated two deployment models. Initially, the game was offered as a standalone activity at the reference desk during the first week of the fall semester. Participation was low; only 12 students completed the activity out of those who engaged, with many others starting but not finishing due to time constraints or lack of interest. However, when the librarians pivoted to integrating the AR game into formal library instruction sessions, results improved significantly. All 34 students across three classes completed the activity, reporting high levels of engagement. Student feedback was consistently positive, with participants describing the experience as "fun" and "cool," and some noting that they discovered previously unknown resources, such as private study rooms.

The findings suggest that while open-source Web AR is a technically viable and cost-effective tool for library outreach, its success depends heavily on context. Standalone activities struggled to attract busy students, whereas embedded instruction ensured participation and completion. The authors also highlight the importance of accessibility, noting that while a paper checklist was provided as an alternative, most students preferred the digital interface. Furthermore, the study acknowledges limitations regarding software maintainability, as JavaScript libraries evolve rapidly, potentially requiring ongoing updates. Ultimately, the project serves as a proof-of-concept that librarians with modest programming skills can successfully develop and deploy innovative AR tools to enhance student orientation and engagement.
