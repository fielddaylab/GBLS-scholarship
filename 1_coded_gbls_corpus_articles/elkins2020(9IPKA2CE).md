### Summary
# **Elkins, A. J., & Hollister, J. M. (2020). Power up: Games and gaming in Library and Information Science curricula in the United States. *Journal of Education for Library and Information Science, 61*(2), 229-252. https://doi.org/10.3138/jelis.2019-0038.r1**

Elkins and Hollister examine whether U.S. Library and Information Science education prepares future librarians to support games and gaming in libraries. The article starts from a tension that is central to the GBLS project: games are widespread in American society and already present in public, school, and academic libraries, but LIS curricula rarely offer sustained preparation for game collections, programming, literacies, or services. The authors connect games to digital literacy, problem solving, communication, critical thinking, information literacy, media literacy, social learning, and access to digital media, arguing that games belong in professional education because library workers increasingly encounter them in practice.

The study follows an earlier curriculum scan by the authors that found only a small number of ALA-accredited U.S. programs offering games- or gaming-related courses. In this article, they survey LIS educators who teach or have taught graduate-level courses in U.S. LIS programs. The survey asked about demographics, professional background, personal and professional experience with games, whether respondents used or discussed games in their teaching, barriers to inclusion, and resources that might help them integrate game-related content. The survey was distributed through professional listservs, social media, ALISE channels, ALA GameRT spaces, and related networks between February and June 2018.

The completed sample was small: 24 valid responses. That limitation matters, and the authors explicitly state that the findings are not generalizable. Even so, the results are useful because they name the kinds of professional education barriers that appear downstream in library practice. Of the respondents, 41.7% said they neither used nor discussed gaming in their courses, while 58.3% did. Among gaming-exclusive educators, half said they had no interest in adding gaming, half said students had not expressed interest, and half were unsure how gaming related to their coursework. Other barriers included not knowing how to integrate gaming, not feeling knowledgeable enough, seeing gaming as unsuitable for their courses, difficulty changing course content, and difficulty setting up special topics courses.

For educators who did include gaming, personal experience mattered most. A majority of gaming-inclusive respondents identified personal experience with gaming as an influence, and some also cited conference sessions, professional development, textbooks, or best-practices guidebooks. The authors find that gaming-inclusive educators tended to have more teaching experience, position titles more often associated with tenure, more experience or identity connection with gaming, and higher preferences across a wider range of digital and analog game genres. Gaming-exclusive educators were not necessarily nonplayers; some reported frequent play and enjoyment, but their preferences clustered more narrowly around casual forms such as matching, card, puzzle, and word games. The authors interpret broader game exposure as one possible reason some educators see more curricular applications.

The discussion is especially valuable for GBLS because it links professional preparation to institutional conditions. Many respondents, both gaming-inclusive and gaming-exclusive, reported that they had not encountered game-related content in their own MLS education. Most with prior library or information-professional experience also had not used games professionally. This lack of exposure may limit educators' sense of what games can do in library contexts. The authors also suggest that tenure status, curricular flexibility, and administrative support may shape whether educators feel able to add game content or develop special topics courses. In other words, the absence of games from LIS curricula is not only a matter of individual preference; it may reflect structural constraints in professional education.

The article's practical recommendations map directly onto a GBLS research and training agenda. Educators who do not currently include games but are open to doing so named relevant course modules, best-practices guidebooks, conference sessions, assignment materials, textbooks, and professional development as supports that might encourage integration. Elkins and Hollister therefore call for evidence-based professional development materials and experiences that clearly convey the relevance of games to library practice and build on adult learners' existing knowledge. They also point toward future interviews with gaming-inclusive LIS educators to identify best practices.

For the GBLS project, this source supports the professional-practice side of the literature review. It helps explain why many libraries may be interested in games but unevenly prepared to design, assess, catalog, facilitate, or defend game-based services. It also gives the project a concrete training mandate: GBLS work should not only describe successful programs, but also create reusable modules, assignments, examples, and professional development resources for librarians and LIS educators. The study is small and survey-based, so it should be used as exploratory evidence rather than a national estimate. Its value lies in naming a preparation gap and identifying the supports that could make game-based library services more teachable and sustainable.
