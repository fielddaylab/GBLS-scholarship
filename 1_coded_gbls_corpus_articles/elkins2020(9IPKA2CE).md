## Method
The researchers employed a quantitative survey design distributed via professional listservs, social media, and networking sites between February 2018 and June 2018. The survey utilized purposive sampling of LIS educators at ALA-accredited programs in the United States. Data were collected using Survey Monkey, with participation incentivized by a lottery drawing for an Amazon gift card. The instrument was pre-tested by five academics and approved by the Institutional Review Board of Texas Woman’s University.

## Population and Data
The final sample consisted of 24 complete and valid questionnaire submissions out of 38 individuals who initiated the survey. Respondents were LIS educators (including teaching assistants, lecturers, adjuncts, and tenured faculty) at ALA-accredited institutions in the United States. Demographically, 66.67% identified as female, 66.67% as white, with a mean age of 46.27 years. Seventy-five percent held or were working toward an MLS/MLIS degree.

## Findings
The sample was divided into gaming-exclusive (41.7%, n=10) and gaming-inclusive (58.3%, n=14) educators. Gaming-inclusive educators tended to have more teaching experience (mean 11.57 years vs. 7.95 years), higher tenure status (42.8% tenured vs. 30%), and broader preferences for various game genres compared to gaming-exclusive educators. Gaming-exclusive educators cited lack of knowledge, student disinterest, or difficulty changing course content as barriers. Those interested in inclusion but currently excluding games indicated that professional development materials, conference workshops, and best-practice guidebooks would encourage integration.
Evidence: "LIS educators who included gaming-related content tended to have had more experience with gaming, higher preferences for multiple genres of games, more teaching experience, and a position title indicating tenure." (Abstract)

The authors suggest that administrative support, particularly for pre-tenured faculty, and access to professional development resources are critical for increasing the inclusion of gaming in LIS curricula. They recommend creating evidence-based materials such as course modules and guidebooks to help educators understand the educational value of games. The study implies that current LIS programs may be underpreparing librarians to support gaming services due to this curricular gap.

# Summary

This article investigates the integration of games and gaming within Library and Information Science (LIS) curricula in the United States. The authors posit that while games offer significant social and educational benefits and are prevalent in library services, LIS programs largely fail to prepare students for these roles. The study’s primary objective was to identify demographic and experiential factors that influence whether LIS educators include gaming-related content in their courses.

The methodology involved a quantitative survey distributed via professional listservs and social media platforms between February and June 2018. The target population comprised LIS educators at ALA-accredited programs in the United States. The final dataset included 24 complete and valid responses. The sample was predominantly female (66.67%) and white (66.67%), with a mean age of 46.27 years. Most respondents held or were pursuing MLS/MLIS degrees, and many had prior work experience as librarians or information professionals.

The findings categorize respondents into "gaming-inclusive" (58.3%) and "gaming-exclusive" (41.7%) educators. The analysis revealed distinct differences between these groups. Gaming-inclusive educators reported significantly more teaching experience (mean 11.57 years) compared to gaming-exclusive educators (mean 7.95 years). Furthermore, a higher proportion of gaming-inclusive educators held tenured positions (42.8% vs. 30%), suggesting that the tenure process and associated job security may facilitate curricular experimentation. In terms of gaming preferences, gaming-inclusive educators demonstrated higher enjoyment ratings across a wider variety of digital and analog game genres, whereas gaming-exclusive educators showed preferences primarily for casual games like puzzles and card games.

Regarding barriers to inclusion, gaming-exclusive educators who expressed interest in integrating games cited a lack of knowledge, uncertainty about how to relate gaming to coursework, and difficulties in modifying existing course content or establishing special topics courses. Conversely, those already including gaming content attributed their practices to personal experience with gaming and professional development opportunities such as conference sessions. The study highlights that most LIS educators, regardless of their current curricular stance, did not encounter gaming-specific content during their own graduate studies, indicating a systemic gap in pre-service training.

The authors conclude that administrative support and professional development are essential for overcoming barriers to gaming integration. They specifically note that pre-tenured faculty may face constraints that limit their ability to innovate curriculum. To address these challenges, the authors recommend the development of evidence-based resources, including course modules, best-practice guidebooks, and conference workshops. These resources aim to demonstrate the educational value of games and provide practical strategies for implementation, thereby better preparing future librarians to support gaming services in library settings. The study acknowledges limitations related to the small sample size and low response rate, noting that results are not generalizable but offer preliminary insights into the factors influencing curricular decisions in LIS education.
