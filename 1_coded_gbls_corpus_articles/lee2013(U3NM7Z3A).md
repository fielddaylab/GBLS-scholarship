## Findings
The study identified a core set of 16 metadata elements deemed essential for video game description: Title, Edition, Platform, Format, Developer, Publisher, Retail Release Date, Number of Players, Online Capabilities, Special Hardware, Genre, Series/Franchise, Region, Rating, Language, and UPC. The authors found that traditional FRBR models are ill-suited for video games, particularly regarding "born-digital" items lacking physical packaging. They concluded that a faceted approach to genre description is necessary to capture the complexity of gameplay, style, theme, and audience.

## Implications
The authors argue that existing library standards and commercial web metadata are insufficient for organizing video games due to their unique nature as interactive cultural artifacts. They propose that future work must include the development of controlled vocabularies, particularly for genre and publisher names, and the expansion of the core schema into a larger "recommended" set. # Summary

This article reports on a collaborative project between researchers at the University of Washington Information School and the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) to develop a formal metadata schema for video games. The authors argue that traditional library organization standards, such as the Functional Requirements for Bibliographic Records (FRBR), are inadequate for describing video games because they were designed primarily for books and physical artifacts. Video games present unique challenges due to their interactive nature, digital existence, complex production teams, and lack of standardized descriptive vocabularies in both academic and commercial contexts.

The study utilized a user-centered design methodology conducted within a graduate course setting. The researchers first developed six user personas—Player, Parent, Collector, Academic, Game Developer/Designer, and Curator/Librarian—to identify the information needs of diverse stakeholders. Based on these personas and a domain analysis of existing commercial and hobbyist websites (e.g., MobyGames, Amazon, Wikipedia), the team compiled a list of potential metadata elements. Through iterative evaluation, they established a core set of 16 essential metadata elements: Title, Edition, Platform, Format, Developer, Publisher, Retail Release Date, Number of Players, Online Capabilities, Special Hardware, Genre, Series/Franchise, Region, Rating, Language, and UPC.

To test the usability of this schema, the researchers cataloged a sample of 30 video games selected for their diversity in genre, platform, and packaging. This empirical exercise revealed significant practical challenges. First, sourcing reliable information was difficult; release dates often conflicted across websites, and copyright dates were frequently inaccurate indicators of retail availability. Second, distinguishing between developers and publishers was complicated by the fluid nature of game companies and inconsistent branding on physical media. Third, genre classification proved particularly problematic due to the lack of controlled vocabularies and the subjective, broad nature of existing labels. The authors found that current genre terms fail to capture the multidimensional nature of games, prompting a recommendation for a faceted classification system that separates gameplay, style, theme, audience, and other attributes.

The study also highlighted issues with "born-digital" games, which lack physical packaging (the traditional chief source of information in library cataloging), making metadata extraction difficult without playing the game or relying on inconsistent secondary sources. The authors concluded that a new, domain-specific conceptual model is necessary to accurately represent video games as cultural artifacts. Future work outlined in the paper includes expanding the core schema into a larger recommended set, developing controlled vocabularies for key elements like genre and publisher names, and conducting further user studies to validate the schema’s effectiveness. The findings are relevant to Games-Based Library Services (GBLS) as they provide a foundational framework for organizing game collections, addressing the specific metadata gaps that hinder effective access and preservation in library and museum settings.
