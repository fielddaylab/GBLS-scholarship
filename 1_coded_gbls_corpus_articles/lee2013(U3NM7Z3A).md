### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Tennis, J. T., Clarke, R. I., & Carpenter, M. (2013). Developing a video game metadata schema for the Seattle Interactive Media Museum. *International Journal on Digital Libraries, 13*(2), 105-117. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00799-013-0103-x**

Lee, Tennis, Clarke, and Carpenter present the Seattle Interactive Media Museum metadata-schema project as a user-centered response to inadequate video game description. The article argues that games are economically, culturally, academically, and educationally significant, but that traditional library standards and commercial game sites do not provide consistent, interoperable, or user-centered access. Book-centered rules struggle with interactive media because games lack title-page-like chief sources, have unstable digital/physical boundaries, and require description of play experience, context, similarity, mood, platform, version, and access conditions.

The study design grew out of a University of Washington course conducted with SIMM. The team developed personas and use cases for five user groups: Player, Parent, Collector, Academic, and Game Developer/Designer. They then gathered 61 candidate information features from game websites, catalogs, and organizational systems, evaluated those features against persona needs, and selected a CORE16 set of elements: Title, Edition, Platform, Format, Developer, Retail Release Date, Number of Players, Online, Special Hardware, Genre, Series/Franchise, Region, Rating, Language, UPC, and other basic access elements as represented in the paper's table.

The cataloging exercise used thirty games chosen to vary by genre, platform, creator, edition, and descriptive difficulty. That exercise made several problems visible. Many elements lack clear chief sources of information because boxes, manuals, publisher sites, commercial databases, and fan sites disagree or omit key facts. Retail release date is important for users and preservation but may differ across sources and regions. Genre is one of the most useful elements for browsing, yet commercial genre vocabularies are inconsistent, broad, undefined, and not crosswalkable. Feature descriptions are valuable but often marketing-oriented, making them hard to transcribe objectively without either controlled vocabularies or a deliberately open notes field.

Region, language, developer, and publisher created further difficulties. Region matters because console games may be hardware-locked, while smartphone or digital games may target language communities without region locks. Language description becomes complicated when imported games, subtitles, voice acting, and later localizations cross markets. Developer and publisher roles are also hard to identify from packaging alone because boxes carry multiple company logos, companies change names, merge, disappear, or act as both developer and publisher. The article also notes title variations, regional renumbering, editions such as special/classic/limited releases, and series identification as persistent game-specific cataloging problems.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful because it makes game discovery infrastructure concrete. It shows that useful game access requires more than title, platform, publisher, and broad format. Libraries and museums need media-specific element definitions, description rules, controlled vocabularies, and eventually hierarchical or faceted structures for elements such as genre, plot, and visual style. The source also explains why local game collections can become hard to browse or recommend without standardized treatment of platform, number of players, online play, region, language, special hardware, edition, and series/franchise relationships.

The source overlaps substantially with the existing Lee, Cho, Fox, and Perti (2013) metadata-schema summary and the Lee, Tennis, and Clarke (2012) precursor. It should be retained in `summaries.md` as the fuller IJDL companion to the precursor, but it does not require a separate review paragraph unless the metadata section is later revised to distinguish the project's stages more carefully.
