## Purpose
The article aims to explore and document three distinct approaches to integrating tabletop games (board, card, and role-playing games) into school library settings. It seeks to demonstrate how these non-digital games can serve both educational and recreational goals, facilitating social engagement, curriculum integration, and student learning across various grade levels.

## Method
The study employs a qualitative, descriptive case-study approach. It presents first-person narratives from three school library professionals detailing their specific implementations of tabletop gaming programs. The authors describe the design, execution, and observed outcomes of these programs without employing quantitative metrics or controlled experimental designs.

## Population and Data
The data consists of anecdotal evidence and observational reports from three distinct educational contexts:
1. Tesseract School (Paradise Valley, Arizona, USA): A PK-12 independent school where games are integrated into the curriculum across all grade levels.
2. Trinity High School Learning Resources Centre (Redditch, UK): A secondary school (ages 13–18) hosting an after-school board game club called "Unplugged," with a regular attendance of six to twelve students.
3. Genesee Valley Educational Partnership (LeRoy, New York, USA): A regional support system for twenty-two school districts, featuring a collection of over 200 games aligned with learning standards and used in collaborative classroom projects.

## Findings
The authors report several key findings across the three cases:
1. Curriculum Integration: At Tesseract School, role-playing games like "Trial of Socrates" significantly increased student engagement with historical texts (e.g., Plato, The Iliad). Students demonstrated deeper investment in character research and critical thinking compared to traditional assignments. Games were also used for vocabulary building (Apples to Apples) and online safety education.
2. Social and Recreational Benefits: At Trinity High School, the "Unplugged" club provided a safe social space for students who did not participate in traditional sports. It fostered cross-year friendships, improved social skills (turn-taking, handling loss), and encouraged peer-to-peer teaching of rules and strategies. An informal survey of 13 students indicated low participation in other after-school activities.
3. Pedagogical Effectiveness: At Genesee Valley, games aligned with state standards helped struggling students connect with concepts through play. The "authenticity" of the game experience was crucial for engagement; students transitioned from viewing tasks as chores to challenges within about 20 minutes of play. Game design workshops allowed students to demonstrate understanding by creating their own games, leveraging diverse learning styles within groups.
Evidence: "The enthusiasm the game brings to the study of the ancient past is unmatched by any other project." ## Implications
The authors argue that tabletop games are valuable library resources because they are inexpensive, portable, and adaptable. They recommend that libraries can serve as hubs for both recreational gaming and curriculum-aligned educational play. Key implications include the need for librarian facilitation to manage rules and social dynamics, the importance of selecting games that are genuinely engaging rather than purely didactic, and the potential for game design projects to assess student learning. The article suggests that tabletop gaming can reach students who may be disengaged from traditional academic or extracurricular activities.
Evidence: "Any school library could benefit from a board games club... It provides another reason for students to use the library." (Henderson section); "Without the authenticity of the game experience, the engagement and connection with the students are often lost." (Mayer section)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This article, titled "Three Different Paths for Tabletop Gaming in School Libraries," presents a qualitative examination of how three school library professionals have implemented tabletop gaming programs in diverse educational settings. The authors—Teresa Copeland, Brenda Henderson, and Brian Nicholson (with contributions from Scott Nicholson)—argue that nondigital games, including board, card, and role-playing games, offer unique advantages over digital counterparts, such as lower cost, portability, and the ability for players to manually manipulate game mechanics, thereby enhancing understanding. The article is structured around three case studies that illustrate distinct approaches: curriculum integration, recreational club formation, and systemic support for game-based learning.

The first case study, presented by Teresa Copeland from Tesseract School in Arizona, focuses on integrating games into the academic curriculum across PK-12 grades. Copeland describes using card games like *Apples to Apples* for vocabulary building and role-playing games (RPGs) for deep historical engagement. A primary example is "Trial of Socrates," an adaptation of a college-level RPG where students assume roles in ancient Athens, requiring them to study primary sources like Plato’s works to argue their character’s case. Copeland reports that this method generated unprecedented enthusiasm and critical thinking, with students investing significant effort into research and speechwriting. The narrative highlights the flexibility of live-action RPGs, allowing facilitators to adjust scenarios on the fly to encourage creativity. Additionally, simpler games are used for foundational skills, such as alphabetical order and online safety, demonstrating that gaming can be tailored to various developmental stages.

The second case study, by Brenda Henderson from Trinity High School in the UK, details the establishment of "Unplugged," an after-school board game club. This program targets students aged 13–18, many of whom do not participate in traditional sports or other extracurriculars. Henderson notes that the club provides a safe, social environment where students develop soft skills such as turn-taking, conflict resolution, and handling winning and losing gracefully. An informal survey of 13 regular attendees revealed that most did not participate in other clubs, suggesting the library fills a gap in social engagement for these students. The club also fosters peer-to-peer learning, with older students teaching rules to younger ones, thereby reinforcing their own understanding. Henderson emphasizes the importance of the librarian’s role in facilitating these interactions and maintaining game quality, noting that the program has helped expand social circles for isolated students.

The third case study, from Brian Mayer at the Genesee Valley Educational Partnership in New York, describes a regional initiative supporting 22 school districts with a collection of over 200 games aligned with state and national learning standards. Mayer outlines a selection process based on engagement, curricular connection, time efficiency, and return on investment. He reports that games serve as a "nonthreatening environment" for students to explore complex ideas, particularly benefiting those who struggle with traditional instruction. A key finding is the "20-minute drop point," where students transition from viewing the activity as a chore to engaging with it as a challenge, provided the game is authentically fun rather than overtly didactic. Mayer also highlights the expansion into game design workshops, where students create their own games to demonstrate mastery of curricular concepts. This approach leverages diverse learning styles within groups, promoting collaboration and practical application of skills.

Collectively, these cases suggest that tabletop gaming in school libraries can effectively bridge education and recreation. The authors conclude that such programs enhance student engagement, support social development, and provide alternative pathways for learning, particularly for students who may be disengaged from conventional academic or extracurricular structures. The article underscores the librarian’s critical role in curating collections, facilitating play, and integrating games into broader educational goals.
