### Summary
# **Copeland, T., Henderson, B., Mayer, B., & Nicholson, S. (2013). Three different paths for tabletop gaming in school libraries. *Library Trends, 61*(4), 825-835. https://doi.org/10.1353/lib.2013.0018**

Copeland, Henderson, Mayer, and Nicholson present three practitioner accounts of tabletop gaming in school libraries. The article begins from the observation that school-library gaming has two primary goals: education and recreation. Tabletop games, if chosen well, can serve both goals because they are inexpensive, portable, durable, adaptable, social, and easier to integrate into classrooms than many digital games. The authors focus on nondigital games: board games, card games, and role-playing games played around tables rather than on screens.

The first account, from Teresa Copeland at Tesseract School in Arizona, describes a school where games are integrated across grades and curricular areas. Younger students use games for vocabulary, dictionary skills, online safety, word recognition, physics, logic, pattern matching, and mathematics. Older students participate in role-playing games tied to history and literature, including a Reacting to the Past-style "Trial of Socrates" and a teacher-written Salem Witch Trials game. These role-playing activities require students to research time periods, read primary and literary sources, write speeches in character, avoid anachronisms, negotiate with peers, and reflect after play. The account is especially useful for GBLS because it shows how role-playing games can turn historical and civic questions into participatory inquiry.

Copeland's examples also emphasize the importance of flexible facilitation and debriefing. In tabletop role-play, teachers and librarians can adjust events on the fly, reward student research, respond to creative suggestions, and let outcomes vary across iterations. Socrates may be acquitted, imprisoned, or commit suicide; accused witches may escape, be convicted, or accuse others. The games are memorable because students take control while adults facilitate from the edges. Post-game discussions about free speech, treason, democracy, accusation, and group deception turn game experience into reflective learning.

The second account, from Brenda Henderson at Trinity High School Learning Resources Centre in the United Kingdom, describes an after-school board game club called "Unplugged." The club attracted students who were not drawn to other after-school activities and gave them a safe, sociable library space for play. Games had to fit a one-hour time limit, and the library selected accessible, engaging modern board games such as *Zombies*, *Marrakech*, *Ticket to Ride*, and *Forbidden Island*. Henderson emphasizes that board game clubs support reading rules, mental arithmetic, turn-taking, graceful winning and losing, consensus-building, cross-year friendships, and social connection. The club also filled an access gap for students who lacked people to play with, could not afford designer board games, or mostly played single-player video games at home.

The third account, from Brian Mayer at the Genesee Valley Educational Partnership in rural western New York, describes a shared school-library game collection of more than 200 modern board and card games aligned to state and national learning standards. Mayer uses four selection criteria: the game should provide an engaging play experience; connect to curriculum through mechanics, theme, or strategy; fit reasonable time constraints; and offer a good return on investment in relation to student learning. The account is valuable because it insists that educational games must first be good games. Students respond when they realize they are playing something fun and challenging rather than a thinly disguised worksheet.

Mayer also describes two expansions: life-skills work and tabletop game design. For life skills, librarians and special education teachers used games to support gross and fine motor skills, social skills, cooperative play, team play, and problem solving. Language-independent games helped students participate without reading ability becoming a barrier. For game design, students studied modern tabletop games as models and then built their own games to represent curricular concepts. Because tabletop prototypes can be made with paper, cards, poster board, and index cards, students can focus on representing systems, rules, and concepts rather than learning complex digital creation tools. The design process also lets students with different strengths collaborate.

For the GBLS project, this article is a strong service-model source. It shows that tabletop gaming in libraries can be curricular, recreational, social, inclusive, standards-aligned, and design-oriented. It connects several review themes: clubs and recurring play groups, TTRPG and role-play programs, instruction and information literacy, game design workshops, facilitation, reflection, and access. It also helps explain why tabletop games remain important even in a digital-games literature review: they are affordable, adaptable, socially rich, and transparent enough for players to understand and modify game systems.

The article is practitioner-based rather than a formal assessment study, so its evidence is anecdotal and local. Still, the three cases give concrete implementation models and vocabulary for school-library GBLS work. They also show that the library role can range from collection provision to club facilitation, curriculum collaboration, game mastering, professional development for teachers, and design coaching.
