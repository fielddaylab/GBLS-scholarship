## Purpose
The article aims to investigate the prevalence, forms, and supporting or constraining factors of videogame programming in U.S. libraries, specifically analyzing how these programs support youth videogame literacies. The authors seek to understand why such programming is not more widespread despite its potential benefits for literacy development.

## Method
The study employs an integrated mixed-methods design, drawing from a larger survey of over 1,000 U.S. librarians. Data were collected via an online Qualtrics survey administered between August 2017 and May 2018. The instrument included 26 open-ended questions and 21 multiple-choice/Likert-type questions. Quantitative data were analyzed using SPSS (frequencies, descriptives, Pearson correlations), while qualitative data were coded using ATLAS.ti to identify themes related to barriers and literacy practices. The study utilized Ertmer’s framework of first-order (external/resource) and second-order (internal/belief) barriers to technology integration.

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 1,016 U.S. librarians from all 50 states, recruited via snowball sampling through the American Library Association, email listservs, and library organizations. The demographic breakdown included 79.3% women, 17.8% men, and 2.9% non-identifying. Settings were distributed as 26% urban, 38% suburban, 28% rural, and 3% other. Most respondents (71.5%) held Library Science graduate degrees and had approximately 12 years of experience. The data represent self-reported practices, beliefs, and barriers regarding videogame programming.

## Findings
**Prevalence and Form:** Only 48% of respondents reported offering videogame programming; 52% did not. Among those with programs, 63% had existed for less than five years. Scheduled access was the most common format (48%), followed by open play (22%). 
**Barriers:** Librarians identified significant first-order barriers, particularly regarding funding and resources (mean difficulty rating of 3.71/5). Training was largely informal (independent searches, peer feedback), with less than 12% citing Library Science degrees as a source. Second-order barriers included low personal confidence in facilitating gaming (mean 3.76/5) despite high device ownership (69%). 
**Literacy Perceptions:** Librarians who supported programming valued social interaction, community building, and multimodal meaning-making. Conversely, dislikes centered on management challenges, noise, hygiene issues, and lack of administrative buy-in.
Evidence: "384 (48%) indicated that they had one and 420 (52%) indicated that they did not... Librarians tended to 'agree' (3.71 out of 5) that the difficulty of supporting videogames was a significant barrier... Librarians tended to 'agree' that they were not confident in their own abilities... (3.76 on a 5-point scale)" (pp. 230–232)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors argue that addressing both first-order (resources, training) and second-order (beliefs, confidence) barriers is essential for expanding videogame literacies in libraries. They recommend integrating videogame literacy into Library Science curricula and accreditation standards, providing professional development, and reorganizing programming to better support youth interests and reduce management burdens. The study suggests that videogame programming can serve as a catalyst for community engagement and literacy development if structural and attitudinal barriers are mitigated.
Evidence: "To overcome these barriers, we recommend greater focus on librarian/educator professional development activities... suggest library accreditation bodies modify their standards... Modifications to library science and teacher education programs... could support a shift in practice" (pp. 234–235)

# Summary

This article by Abrams and Van Eck (2021) examines the state of videogame programming in U.S. libraries, focusing on its prevalence, structural forms, and the barriers influencing its adoption. The study is grounded in the premise that videogaming constitutes a form of literacy involving multimodal meaning-making, social interaction, and iterative learning. Despite the recognized benefits of gaming for engagement and problem-solving, the authors note a gap in understanding why such programming is not more ubiquitous in library settings.

The research utilizes an integrated mixed-methods approach, analyzing data from a survey of 1,016 librarians across all 50 U.S. states. The sample was predominantly female (79.3%) and experienced (average 12 years), with representation from urban, suburban, and rural settings. Data collection occurred between August 2017 and May 2018 via an online survey containing both quantitative Likert-scale items and qualitative open-ended questions. The analytical framework distinguishes between first-order barriers (external constraints like resources and training) and second-order barriers (internal factors like beliefs and confidence), adapted from Ertmer’s model of technology integration.

Key findings reveal that videogame programming is not yet standard practice: only 48% of respondents reported offering such programs, while 52% did not. Among libraries with gaming initiatives, the majority (63%) had established them within the last five years, indicating a recent trend rather than an entrenched practice. The most common format was scheduled access (48%), which the authors critique as potentially restrictive compared to open play, which better supports the iterative nature of videogame literacies.

The study identifies significant barriers to adoption. First-order barriers were prominent, with librarians reporting high difficulty in securing funding and resources (mean score 3.71/5). Training was largely informal, derived from independent research or peer advice, rather than formal Library Science education (less than 12%). Second-order barriers included low self-efficacy among librarians regarding their ability to facilitate gaming (mean confidence barrier score 3.76/5), despite high rates of personal device ownership (69%). Qualitative data highlighted that librarians who valued programming appreciated the social bonding and community building it fostered, whereas those opposed cited management challenges, noise, hygiene concerns, and lack of administrative support.

The authors conclude that expanding videogame literacies in libraries requires addressing both resource deficits and attitudinal barriers. They recommend integrating gaming literacy into Library Science curricula and accreditation standards, enhancing professional development, and restructuring programming to align with youth interests while reducing managerial burdens. The study underscores the potential of libraries as informal learning spaces where videogames can support complex literacy practices, provided that systemic and individual obstacles are effectively mitigated.
