## Findings
The article finds that no single standard adequately covers the cataloging of three-dimensional game objects in special collections. RDA provides general guidance but is dispersed and primarily intended for textual materials; OLAC Best Practices offer workflow-based guidance for objects but focus on circulating collections; DCRM manuals provide artifact-level detail but lack instructions for three-dimensional objects. Consequently, a hybrid approach was necessary. Key findings include:
1. Item-level cataloging was preferred over archival description because the games lacked shared provenance or collective access points, requiring detailed differentiation of each item.
2. Consistent MARC coding (Leader/006 set to "r" for three-dimensional artifact; 008/33 coded as "g" for game or "w" for toy) enabled effective faceting in the library's Blacklight discovery layer, improving user search capabilities compared to inconsistent terminology found in other institutions.
3. Detailed physical descriptions, including enumeration of subunits (e.g., specific game pieces), served as essential inventories for rare items.
4. Local notes and citations were critical for capturing provenance, condition, and external bibliographic data when information was missing from the item itself.

# Summary

Elizabeth Hobart’s 2024 article, "Describing Games for Special Collections Libraries," addresses the challenges librarians face when cataloging three-dimensional game objects within special collections. The primary objective is to demonstrate a practical methodology for describing these complex bibliographic entities by synthesizing instructions from multiple standards: Resource Description and Access (RDA), the Online Audiovisual Catalogers (OLAC) Best Practices, and the Descriptive Cataloging of Rare Materials (DCRM) manuals. The article argues that no single standard currently provides comprehensive guidance for cataloging games at the item level in special collections contexts.

The study is grounded in the experience of cataloging a collection of sixty-four historic games at the Eberly Family Special Collections Library at Pennsylvania State University. The collection, primarily consisting of nineteenth-century items such as toys, card games, board games, and puzzles, presented significant descriptive challenges due to the lack of existing catalog records (only three of the sixty-four items had prior records) and the absence of a unified standard. Hobart details the decision-making process that led to choosing item-level bibliographic cataloging over archival description or group-level cataloging. This choice was driven by the heterogeneity of the collection; the games lacked shared provenance, collective access points, or uniform formats, making high-level summarization insufficient for researcher needs. Item-level cataloging allowed for precise identification and detailed physical description, which is crucial for rare materials.

The methodology involves creating original MARC 21 records for two illustrative items: *Bal d’enfants*, a mid-nineteenth-century French paper toy, and *Musikalische Zauber-Karten*, a German musical card game from the same period. Hobart demonstrates how RDA provides general structural guidance, while OLAC Best Practices offer specific workflows for three-dimensional objects. However, because OLAC focuses on circulating collections, DCRM manuals were consulted to capture copy-specific details such as provenance, condition, and printing techniques. For instance, detailed physical descriptions enumerated all subunits (e.g., individual game pieces), serving as an inventory for preservation purposes. Local notes were used extensively to document imperfections and ownership history, while citations linked to external reference sources when bibliographic data was missing from the items themselves.

A key finding relates to discovery and access. Hobart highlights that inconsistent terminology across institutions hinders user search effectiveness. By examining catalogs from twenty-eight other libraries, she found wide variation in how games were classified (e.g., "three-dimensional object," "image," "realia"). At Penn State, consistent MARC coding—specifically setting the Leader/006 field to "r" for three-dimensional artifacts and the 008/33 field to "g" for game or "w" for toy—enabled the creation of a dedicated "Games/Toys" facet in the Blacklight discovery layer. This technical implementation significantly improved the collocation and discoverability of these resources for users.

The article concludes by emphasizing the limitations of current cataloging frameworks. While RDA is designed to be format-neutral, its instructions for three-dimensional objects are dispersed and less developed than those for textual materials. Furthermore, the bibliographic history of games lacks the depth of reference sources available for rare books, forcing catalogers to rely on single specimens and external dealer descriptions. Hobart asserts that while these hybrid cataloging practices improve access, they also underscore a broader need for specialized bibliographic research and standardized guidelines tailored to non-print special collections materials. The work serves as a practical guide for other libraries seeking to enhance the description and discovery of their own game holdings.
