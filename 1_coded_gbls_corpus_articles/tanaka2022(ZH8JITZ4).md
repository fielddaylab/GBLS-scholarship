# Summary

Candie Tanaka’s 2022 article, "Indigenous Video Games in Libraries," addresses the significant gap in public library collections regarding video games created by Indigenous peoples or featuring authentic Indigenous narratives. Situated within the broader context of "Indigenous Futurisms"—a movement examining Indigenous perspectives in technology—the paper argues that while video games are a dominant form of modern media and education, libraries have largely failed to include Indigenous voices in their gaming holdings. This omission perpetuates historical stereotypes and excludes valuable educational resources that could support decolonization efforts.

The author begins by critiquing the historical representation of Indigenous peoples in mainstream video games. Titles such as *The Oregon Trail* and 4X strategy games (e.g., *Civilization*, *Europa Universalis*) are cited as examples of settler-colonial narratives that prioritize resource extraction, conquest, and Eurocentric worldviews. These games often portray Indigenous characters as obstacles or stereotypes, erasing their sovereignty and relational connections to the land. Tanaka contrasts this with recent developments in the industry, highlighting a shift toward Indigenous-led creation. Games such as Elizabeth LaPensée’s *When Rivers Were Trails*, *Never Alone* (developed by E-Line Media and Upper One Game), and Maize Longboat’s *Terra Nova* are presented as counter-narratives. These titles utilize non-linear storytelling, traditional languages, and gameplay mechanics that emphasize generosity, reciprocity, and community rather than exploitation. For instance, *When Rivers Were Trails* incorporates Ojibwe language and focuses on emotional and spiritual wellbeing alongside physical survival, directly challenging the extractive logic of earlier games.

Despite the educational potential of these titles, Tanaka notes that they are largely absent from library collections. Several barriers contribute to this exclusion. Many Indigenous games are produced by independent studios and are available only as digital downloads, complicating traditional library acquisition models. Additionally, limited marketing budgets mean these games rarely appear in mainstream gaming reviews, leading to low awareness among librarians. The author emphasizes that video games are interactive media that require player agency, making them powerful tools for digital literacy, critical thinking, and cultural education. Citing research by Batchelor et al. (2021), the article suggests that interactive fiction can enhance connections to print-based texts and support multimedia skills.

To remedy this exclusion, Tanaka proposes eight specific strategies for libraries to integrate Indigenous video games into their services. First, libraries should improve accessibility by setting up computer or console stations with pre-downloaded games, allowing patrons to play in reserved time slots. Second, institutions should pursue community funding and partnerships with larger gaming companies to support accessible gaming spaces. Third, libraries can host cultural teachings led by Elders to provide context for the Indigenous knowledges presented in the games. Fourth, cooperative gaming sessions can be organized, where groups play together and discuss narrative choices, fostering communal learning. Fifth, programming should include events featuring Indigenous creators and industry professionals. Sixth, libraries can offer workshops that teach technical skills such as coding, design, and storytelling, empowering Indigenous youth to create their own content. Seventh, physical and online spaces should be provided for Indigenous creators to produce and share work. Finally, library staff must increase their awareness of Indigenous video games through professional development and conference participation, recognizing these titles as essential components of decolonizing collections.

The article concludes by framing the inclusion of Indigenous video games as a matter of equity and historical preservation. By supporting Indigenous creators and making these games accessible, libraries can challenge settler-colonial narratives and provide safer, more inclusive spaces for learning. Tanaka asserts that "true self-determination in games must happen from the code up," urging librarians to take proactive steps to ensure that Indigenous stories are represented and preserved in digital archives.
