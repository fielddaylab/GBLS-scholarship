# Summary

Christopher J. Baker’s 2014 Master of Fine Arts thesis, *Video Games: Their Effect on Society and How We Must Modernize Our Pedagogy for Students of the Digital Age*, argues that video games are a transformative medium with significant benefits across education, medicine, economics, and the arts. The primary objective is to advocate for a modernization of pedagogical methods to align with the interactive learning styles fostered by digital gaming.

The methodology consists of a comprehensive literature review and synthesis of existing data. Baker utilizes demographic statistics from the Entertainment Software Association (ESA) to establish the prevalence of gaming, noting that 59% of Americans play video games, thereby debunking the stereotype of gamers as a "fringe minority." The text aggregates findings from various scientific studies to demonstrate the tangible benefits of gaming. For instance, it cites research indicating that high school gamers outperformed medical residents in virtual surgery tasks, and that regular gaming can increase gray matter in brain regions associated with memory and spatial navigation. Additionally, the thesis highlights clinical applications, such as the SPARX video game, which showed a 44% recovery rate for depression in teenagers compared to 26% in traditional counseling groups, and studies showing improved reading skills in dyslexic children after playing action games.

Baker further explores the economic and artistic validity of video games. He discusses how virtual economies in games like *Eve Online* provide complex data for real-world economic modeling, suggesting that game environments allow for experiments impossible in physical societies. In the realm of art, he notes institutional recognition by the Smithsonian and the Museum of Modern Art, framing games as a sophisticated form of interactive design and expression. The thesis also examines the intersection of gaming and theatre, describing how immersive theatre companies like Punchdrunk utilize game-like interactivity to engage audiences.

The core argument centers on education. Baker contends that traditional K-12 education is failing due to passive learning models. He proposes adopting "game-based learning" principles, such as clear goals, immediate feedback, and exploration, to enhance student motivation and retention. The thesis concludes that ignoring the influence of video games on digital-native students is detrimental, urging educators to integrate these interactive methodologies to create more effective and engaging learning environments. While the text acknowledges controversies regarding violence and addiction, it primarily focuses on the positive psychological state of "flow" induced by well-designed games as a counter-narrative to negative perceptions.
