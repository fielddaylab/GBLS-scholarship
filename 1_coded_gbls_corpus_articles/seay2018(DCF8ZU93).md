# Summary

Jared Alexander Seay’s 2018 commentary, "Considering Games and Gamification in Libraries & Associated Entities — In Praise of Real People," published in *Against the Grain*, argues for the pedagogical superiority of immersive, analog games over digital alternatives in library and educational contexts. The article is not an empirical study but a synthesis of psychological theory, educational trends, and library practice observations. Its primary objective is to demonstrate how collaborative, face-to-face gaming formats can mitigate the cognitive challenges of learning, specifically short attention spans and rapid memory decay.

Seay begins by referencing Hermann Ebbinghaus’s "forgetting curve," noting that learners forget approximately 50% of new information within an hour and 90% within a month. He couples this with estimates that average attention spans in lecture settings last only seven to ten minutes. Given these constraints, Seay posits that traditional instruction is inherently limited. While acknowledging the popularity of digital games, he critiques them for often isolating learners ("everyone being together in the same room alone") and focusing primarily on rote memorization or dexterity rather than deep social engagement. He argues that digital interfaces fail to capture the "visceral difference" provided by real-time, non-verbal, and verbal cues inherent in physical proximity.

To address these limitations, Seay introduces the term "collaborative multi-player immersive games" to describe analog formats that place groups of players into shared problem-solving scenarios. He identifies three primary examples relevant to library services: escape rooms, Educational Live Action Role-Playing Games (Edu-LARPs), and megagames. Escape rooms are described as a form of problem-based learning that enhances engagement and subject recall. Edu-LARPs involve participants physically role-playing characters within historical or theoretical scenarios (e.g., the French Revolution or Salem Witch Trials), engaging cognitive, affective, and behavioral levels of learning through experiential methods similar to drama pedagogy. Megagames are characterized as large-scale hybrid formats involving hundreds of players divided into factions, combining elements of board games, LARPs, and escape rooms to simulate complex social dynamics.

The core argument rests on the premise that human beings are fundamentally social creatures whose learning potential is maximized through collaborative interaction. Seay cites secondary literature suggesting that analog game players expect a "slower-paced and chatty group experience," which fosters communication skills that digital games cannot codify. By leveraging these social dynamics, libraries can create learning environments that are not only more engaging but also more effective at retaining information. The article highlights specific library innovations, such as those at the University of Wollongong Library, as evidence of this growing trend.

Seay concludes by advocating for a "high tech high touch" approach, where libraries embrace analog interactivity to complement digital resources. He suggests that the future of library instruction lies in immersive, socially rich activities that allow patrons and students to collaborate creatively. While the article does not provide quantitative data on learning outcomes, it offers a strong theoretical framework for why analog games may outperform digital ones in fostering deep, retained understanding through social engagement. The piece serves as a persuasive call for librarians to integrate these collaborative formats into their service models to enhance educational impact.
