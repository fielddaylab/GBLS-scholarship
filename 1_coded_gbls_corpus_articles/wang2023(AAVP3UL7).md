## Implications
The authors suggest that game designers should implement simple, clear competition mechanisms to stimulate engagement without causing excessive stress or distracting from learning goals. They recommend linking victory conditions directly to learning outcomes. For practitioners, the study implies that while enjoyment drives initial adoption of educational games, educators must explicitly highlight the usefulness of these tools to foster long-term acceptance and correct attitudes toward them as serious learning instruments rather than mere entertainment.

# Summary

This study by Wang and Huang (2023) investigates the complex interplay between competition, collaboration, and learning performance within digital game-based learning (DGBL) environments. Recognizing that competition can act as a "double-edged sword"—enhancing motivation but potentially causing stress—the authors developed a research model to determine how perceived competition and perceived collaboration jointly influence student outcomes. The study addresses a literature gap where previous research often examined these variables in isolation or failed to elucidate their interactive mechanisms.

The methodology involved the creation of a custom digital game termed "IC2," which integrated Tetris mechanics with a question bank system for database principles. The game featured an intergroup competition structure (2v2 teams) requiring intragroup collaboration, where one player answered questions while the other managed game tasks, with roles swapping periodically. The study population consisted of 108 sophomore students from the Department of Information Management at a Taiwanese university. Participants engaged with the game during a two-week voluntary practice period following a 15-week course. Data were collected via a structured questionnaire measuring seven constructs: perceived competition, perceived collaboration, perceived enjoyment, perceived usefulness, attitude toward using, behavioral intention, and learning performance. The authors utilized Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM) to analyze the causal relationships among these variables.

The empirical results revealed that perceived competition is a more significant driver of learning performance than perceived collaboration. Specifically, perceived competition directly influenced perceived collaboration, supporting the notion that intergroup rivalry can foster intragroup teamwork and interdependence. Furthermore, perceived competition affected behavioral intention both directly and indirectly through perceived enjoyment (intrinsic motivation). In contrast, perceived collaboration influenced behavioral intention only indirectly via perceived usefulness (extrinsic motivation). Behavioral intention was found to be a direct and strong predictor of learning performance. A notable finding was that perceived enjoyment had a stronger effect on behavioral intention than perceived usefulness, suggesting that students are primarily motivated by the hedonic aspects of educational games rather than their utilitarian value.

The authors discuss these findings in the context of technology acceptance models and behavioral decision theory. They argue that because educational games are often viewed as entertainment extensions, students adopt emotional views (enjoyment) rather than rational views (usefulness) when deciding to engage with them. Consequently, competition, which generates excitement and a sense of achievement, is more effective at prompting engagement than collaboration alone. However, the study acknowledges several limitations. The sample was drawn from a single department in Taiwan, a culture with high credentialism pressure, which may limit generalizability to other cultural contexts. Additionally, learning performance was measured via self-report rather than objective exam scores, and the specific game mechanics (Tetris-based) may not represent all DGBL types. The authors recommend future research include diverse game genres, larger samples, and objective performance metrics to validate these findings.
