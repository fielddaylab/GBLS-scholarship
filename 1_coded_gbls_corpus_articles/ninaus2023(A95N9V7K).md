### Summary
# **Ninaus, M., Kiili, K., Cortez, R., Hazin, I., Wortha, S. M., Klein, E., Weiss, E. M., & Moeller, K. (2023). The added value of game elements: Better training performance but comparable learning gains. *Educational Technology Research and Development*. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-023-10263-8**

Ninaus and colleagues test whether intrinsically integrated game elements improve learning in a digital fraction magnitude training task. The study is not library-based, but it is useful for GBLS because it gives empirical support for a caution that recurs across library gamification: game elements can change engagement and in-task behavior without necessarily improving durable learning outcomes. The authors situate their work between two competing claims. On one hand, game-based learning may increase cognitive engagement, effort, motivation, generative processing, and persistence. On the other hand, added game elements can become extraneous cognitive load or "seductive details" if they distract from the instructional goal.

The study uses a pre-post randomized training design with 85 adult participants. Participants trained fraction magnitude understanding over five consecutive days, about 15-20 minutes per day, using digital number-line estimation tasks. One group used a game-based version with narrative, visual game aesthetics, an avatar, virtual incentives, feedback, and level structure. The comparison group used a matched non-game version stripped of those game elements but preserving the core number-line estimation task. Learning was measured with paper-based number-line estimation before and after training.

The key finding is deliberately mixed. Both the game-based and non-game-based conditions improved from pretest to posttest, confirming that the number-line training itself supported fraction magnitude learning. However, the two groups did not differ significantly in learning gains. During training, participants in the game-based condition responded more accurately than those in the non-game-based condition, but they also responded more slowly. The authors interpret this pattern as possible evidence that game elements increased cognitive engagement and shifted participants' priorities toward accuracy over speed during the training process.

For GBLS, this distinction between training performance and learning outcome is important. A library escape room, gamified orientation, reading challenge, or badge system may produce better in-session behavior, more careful task completion, more time on task, or stronger visible engagement without automatically producing stronger transfer, retention, information literacy, reading, or wellness outcomes. Ninaus et al. therefore support a review argument that game-based library services need assessment designs that look beyond attendance, completion, enjoyment, and in-game success.

The article is also useful for design vocabulary. The authors emphasize "intrinsic integration," meaning that game mechanics and learning mechanics should be tightly connected rather than pasted together. They also argue that researchers need well-matched control conditions when testing the specific contribution of game elements. Without such controls, positive outcomes may be due to novelty, real-time instruction, the base task, collaboration, or extra time rather than to the game elements themselves.

The limitation for GBLS is scope. This is an adult math-learning experiment, not a study of libraries, librarians, patrons, collections, library instruction, or library programming. It should not be used as evidence that library game services work. Its proper role is adjacent methodological and conceptual support: it helps explain why GBLS assessment should separate engagement, performance during play, transfer, and longer-term outcomes, and why gamification should be evaluated as a specific design intervention rather than assumed to add value by default.
