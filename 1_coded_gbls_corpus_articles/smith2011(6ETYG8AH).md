### Summary
# **Smith, A.-L., & Baker, L. (2011). Getting a clue: Creating student detectives and dragon slayers in your library. *Reference Services Review, 39*(4), 628-642. https://doi.org/10.1108/00907321111186659**

Smith and Baker describe two self-paced academic-library orientation games created at Utah Valley University Library after focus groups showed that students were intimidated by the size of the new 190,000-square-foot library and lacked knowledge of library services. The article is a case study rather than a controlled experiment, but it is especially useful for GBLS because it reports concrete design choices, assessment data, and implementation lessons from a library that needed to reach large numbers of new students without adding a heavy staff burden.

The first game, *Get a Clue*, was a physical mystery-tour game launched at the beginning of the Fall 2009 semester. The library's promotions committee adapted the familiar premise of *Clue*: students moved through library spaces, read clues, eliminated suspects, and learned about services, collections, floor locations, and circulation processes. The game was designed to take about 30 minutes, long enough to introduce the building but short enough to hold attention. Staff used floor maps, clue posters, a short video introduction, prize drawings, and faculty promotion to encourage participation. The first version drew 311 entries, but only 55 percent correctly identified the villain, leading the committee to simplify the second version by removing some elimination mechanics, dropping the registration requirement, and giving students a stamped game piece for proof of completion.

The second game, *LibraryCraft*, was an online medieval quest modeled loosely on popular computer-game conventions such as avatars, chapters, points, inventory, and fantasy artwork. Instead of moving students through the physical building, *LibraryCraft* moved them through the library website. Students looked up books and articles, answered questions about services such as interlibrary loan and media checkout, and unlocked new chapters after correct answers. The design used HTML and JavaScript, pop-up help, task hints, and a reference-desk cheat sheet so that students could keep moving even when they were confused. The committee also gathered feedback from student workers on artwork, story, tasks, and wording, trying to make the experience entertaining without letting the game drift away from library-learning objectives.

The assessment findings are modest but directly relevant to GBLS. In the Fall 2010 version of *Get a Clue*, 338 students completed a post-game survey. Most participated for a class assignment or extra credit, and 63 percent had visited the library fewer than five times, meaning the game reached its intended audience. More than 91 percent rated the game easy or very easy, nearly 90 percent reported being more comfortable navigating the library, 92 percent described it as a good use of time, and 92.3 percent would recommend it to other students. For *LibraryCraft*, 52 students responded to the first survey question, and 84.5 percent agreed or strongly agreed that the game taught them a new library skill; 87 percent agreed or strongly agreed that they would recommend it. Comments suggest that students valued the story, graphics, and the way the game required them to search rather than simply receive answers.

The article's implementation lessons are as important as its outcomes. Both games were revised because complexity interfered with learning. The authors note that a game can fail when students do not understand the rules, when vocabulary is too difficult, when tasks require too many unrelated steps, or when digital clues depend on unstable URLs and e-book records. They argue that orientation games need a level of difficulty that is "doable" but not boring, and that designers must accommodate varied reading levels and varied familiarity with library resources. They also emphasize faculty buy-in, advertising, prizes, proof of completion, launch timing, and student feedback as practical infrastructure. These details align strongly with the GBLS principle that facilitation and operations are part of the service, not afterthoughts.

For the larger GBLS project, Smith and Baker provide one of the clearest examples of games as orientation infrastructure. The case shows how a library can turn wayfinding, service awareness, catalog/database use, and research support into playable tasks, and how asynchronous games can scale orientation when staff cannot provide tours or workshops for every incoming student. The article also illustrates the difference between superficial gamification and purposeful game-based service design: the games worked best when story, challenge, feedback, and progression were aligned with concrete library behaviors. The evidence is limited to short post-game surveys and self-reported comfort or skill gains, with heavy dependence on course credit, but the case remains strong practitioner evidence for game-based orientation, especially in academic libraries.
