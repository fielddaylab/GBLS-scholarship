# Summary

This case study by Smith and Baker (2011) details the creation and evaluation of two games-based orientation initiatives at Utah Valley University (UVU) Library. Facing challenges related to a large, commuter-heavy student body and limited staff capacity for traditional tours, the library developed "Get a Clue" and "LibraryCraft" to asynchronously introduce students to physical spaces and online resources.

The first initiative, "Get a Clue," was a physical mystery game modeled after the board game *Clue*. Launched in Fall 2009, the initial version required students to eliminate suspects and locations while navigating the new 190,000-square-foot library building. However, the authors found that the elimination mechanic confused many students who were unfamiliar with the original game's rules, leading to lower success rates in identifying the correct solution (55% of 311 entries). Consequently, the game was revised for Fall 2010. The revised version removed the elimination requirement and registration hurdles, simplifying the task to following clues and recording locations. Post-game survey data from 338 students indicated high satisfaction: 91.7% found the game easy to complete, 89.6% reported increased comfort navigating the library, and 92% viewed it as a worthwhile use of time. Notably, 63% of respondents had visited the library fewer than five times prior to playing, confirming the game reached its target audience of novice users.

The second initiative, "LibraryCraft," was an online browser-based game launched in Spring 2010. Designed to teach digital literacy and familiarize students with the library website, it featured a medieval quest narrative where students completed tasks such as searching catalogs and locating e-books. The development process took three and a half months due to the need for custom graphics and JavaScript programming. Survey data from 52 respondents showed that 84.5% agreed the game taught them a new skill. While 56% of respondents noted difficulties with specific tasks or confusion, 94% still rated the game as easy to follow, which the authors interpret as evidence of an optimal difficulty balance—challenging enough to engage but not so difficult as to frustrate users. Qualitative feedback praised the graphics and narrative structure.

The authors identify several critical factors for success. First, faculty buy-in was essential; many instructors incorporated the games into course requirements or offered extra credit, which drove participation. Second, iterative design based on student feedback improved usability and learning outcomes. Third, the games required minimal ongoing staff intervention once deployed, making them scalable for large institutions. The study concludes that well-designed, self-paced games can effectively supplement traditional library instruction by providing an engaging, low-barrier entry point for students to learn about library services.
