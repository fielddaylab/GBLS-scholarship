The authors conclude that current literature does not support evidence-based decisions regarding the efficacy of scavenger hunts. # Summary

Stark et al. (2021) present a systematic review titled "Scavenging for evidence," which investigates the implementation, assessment, and effectiveness of scavenger hunts in academic libraries. The authors define scavenger hunts as outreach or instruction activities where participants physically travel through library spaces to encounter new information. Given the popularity of these gamified activities for student engagement and orientation, the review seeks to address a lack of systematic investigation into their efficacy.

The methodology adhered to PRISMA-P guidelines and utilized SPICE and PICO frameworks to guide the search strategy. Searches were conducted across multiple databases, grey literature sources, and Google Scholar in June 2019, with an update in November 2020. Inclusion criteria required studies to involve physical components within academic library settings (including hybrid models) and include some form of assessment. Non-English publications and purely virtual hunts were excluded. Two researchers independently screened titles, abstracts, and full texts, resolving discrepancies through consensus or a third reviewer. Data extraction focused on study population, design, goals, execution, and limitations.

The final analysis included 13 case studies. The authors highlight significant methodological weaknesses in the existing literature. Only four of the 13 articles explicitly stated the number of participants, and only one provided a clear timeframe for the intervention. Demographic data was sparse, with most studies failing to report details beyond the students' year of enrollment. Furthermore, assessment tools were predominantly in-house creations; none utilized validated measures, and only three articles indicated that their instruments were pretested for logic and usability.

Regarding outcomes, the most commonly stated objectives were student fun/engagement, space orientation, and familiarity with materials/resources/technologies. However, a notable disconnect existed between stated objectives and assessed findings. For instance, while "fun" was a desired outcome in eight studies, it was recorded as a finding in nine, indicating inconsistent alignment. Findings generally reported that students became more familiar with library spaces and resources and felt more confident using the library. However, the authors assert that these anecdotal and informal assessments do not constitute sufficient evidence to determine if scavenger hunts are effective for teaching information literacy or orienting students in a rigorous sense.

The review also identified operational challenges from the librarian perspective, including high staff demands, difficulties in coordination, and concerns regarding scalability. Some studies noted benefits from partnering with other campus departments to share resources.

In conclusion, Stark et al. argue that the current body of literature is insufficient for making evidence-based decisions about implementing scavenger hunts. The authors call for future research to elevate methodological standards by collecting meaningful demographic data, reporting precise sample sizes and study durations, utilizing validated assessment tools, and clearly separating data from different interventions. They emphasize the need for replicable research designs that can genuinely assess the pedagogical value of gamified library instruction.
