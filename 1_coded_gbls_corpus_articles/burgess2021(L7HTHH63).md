# Summary

Burgess and Wallace (2021) address a critical pedagogical challenge in Library and Information Science (LIS) education: how to effectively teach "clinical intuition" and metacognition for reference transactions, particularly within the growing context of online Master of Library and Information Science (MLIS) programs. The authors argue that traditional reference instruction relies heavily on real-time interaction and immediate feedback, elements that are often diminished in asynchronous online environments. To address this, the article employs a concept analysis method to define clinical intuition in reference work and proposes investigative video games as a viable pedagogical tool for developing these skills.

The authors begin by defining clinical intuition not as a vague "gut feeling," but as a form of professional judgment refined through experience, metacognition, and the iterative testing of ad hoc rules. Drawing parallels with nursing practice, they posit that reference workers must possess self-awareness and situational awareness to adapt their approaches during fluid, unscripted patron interactions. This intuition is developed through metacognitive reflection—thinking about one’s own thinking processes—and requires opportunities to test hypotheses and receive feedback on decision-making outcomes.

The study identifies a specific limitation in current online LIS education: while asynchronous courses can effectively deliver domain knowledge (e.g., database features, search syntax), they often fail to provide the dynamic, interactive practice necessary for refining clinical judgment. Without live, real-time feedback, students may struggle to internalize the complex social, cognitive, and behavioral skills required for effective reference interviews. The authors contend that this gap risks leaving new librarians to learn these critical skills on the job, potentially compromising service quality.

To bridge this gap, Burgess and Wallace introduce a conceptual model linking three types of reasoning—abductive, deductive, and inductive—to specific phases of the reference transaction. Abductive reasoning involves forming hypotheses about patron needs; deductive reasoning applies known principles to select resources; and inductive reasoning evaluates evidence from search results. The authors then analyze three investigative video games—*Her Story*, *Return of the Obra Dinn*, and *Subserial Network*—as case studies demonstrating how game mechanics naturally engage these reasoning skills. For instance, *Her Story* requires players to use inductive trial-and-error with keyword searches to uncover narrative clues, mirroring the iterative process of refining search strategies. *Return of the Obra Dinn* demands abductive inference to determine character fates from fragmented evidence, paralleling the need to infer tacit patron questions.

The authors argue that these games offer three key advantages for LIS pedagogy: they allow for autonomous, self-paced learning; they provide immediate, tangible feedback on hypothesis testing; and they deliver this feedback in the same mode as the activity, reinforcing the connection between action and outcome. This aligns with Experiential Learning Theory (ELT), which emphasizes the dialectic of action/reflection and experience/abstraction. By engaging with these games, students can practice rule-testing and metacognitive reflection in a low-stakes environment.

However, the authors acknowledge several limitations. Investigative games are not educational tools designed for LIS; thus, instructors must explicitly connect game mechanics to reference competencies. Additionally, some games contain violent or unsettling content, suggesting they should be offered as optional supplements rather than mandatory assignments. The article concludes that while the current research is conceptual, it highlights a promising avenue for enhancing online reference instruction. The authors call for future empirical studies to validate whether using investigative video games significantly improves students’ reasoning skills and clinical intuition in professional practice.
