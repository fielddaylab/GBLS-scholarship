### Summary
# **Chapman, A. (2021). Trials of metadata: Emerging schemas for videogame cataloguing. *Journal of Library Metadata, 21*(3-4), 63-103. https://doi.org/10.1080/19386389.2021.2007729**

Chapman examines emerging videogame metadata schemas at a moment when games are increasingly entering museums, archives, schools, and libraries, but widely accepted cataloging guidance remains unsettled. The article is valuable for GBLS because it compares schemas created by information professionals, fan communities, and commercial platforms rather than treating library cataloging in isolation. Chapman asks how videogames challenge and expand traditional metadata standards, especially Resource Description and Access (RDA), Library of Congress vocabularies, and genre/form practice.

The literature review situates videogame cataloging between two older problem areas: three-dimensional objects and electronic resources. Physical games and peripherals can resemble realia, toys, or board games; digital games can resemble software, machine-readable data files, or networked electronic resources. Yet videogames often combine both. A cartridge, disk, manual, controller, accessory, online dependency, patch, downloadable add-on, and executable code may all matter. Chapman uses examples such as *Stack-Up*, *Colossal Cave Adventure*, *Undertale*, *Doom*, and *The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time/Master Quest* to show how titles, editions, versions, platforms, carriers, add-ons, and sources of information become difficult in game cataloging.

The method is comparative analysis. Chapman analyzes five schemas or schema-like systems: the Core Metadata Schema for Cataloguing Video Games (CMS) developed by GAMECIP; the University of Washington GAMER Group's Video Game Metadata Schema (VGMS); MobyGames; Steam; and Itch.io. The first two are academic/library-oriented schemas. MobyGames represents a long-running fan/community database with archival ambitions and documented standards. Steam and Itch.io represent commercial digital distribution platforms whose descriptive elements and user-generated tags reveal what game marketplaces treat as useful for discovery. Chapman compares these systems to core RDA elements as interpreted by OLAC's *Best Practices for Cataloguing Video Games Using RDA and MARC21*, and then analyzes their treatment of genre.

The findings show both compatibility and tension with RDA. Many videogame elements can be crosswalked to RDA, but games require more medium-specific granularity. Title can be difficult because packaging, disk labels, menus, title screens, regional releases, alternate titles, abbreviations, colloquial names, and series/franchise naming may conflict. Statements of responsibility do not map cleanly because games are produced by teams, studios, publishers, distributors, voice actors, testers, and other contributors rather than a single authorial figure. Publication is complicated by retail release dates, region, copyright dates, digital updates, developer self-publishing, and changing company names. Edition and version are complicated by commercial editions, software versions, patches, and downloadable content.

Chapman's analysis strongly supports platform as a distinct metadata element. RDA and OLAC can treat platform information through edition and system details, but all of the videogame schemas provide a platform element because platform can define compatibility, gameplay, historical context, and expression-level difference. The article also highlights the need for system requirements, developer, publisher, summary or game description, media format, release/update information, and carrier descriptions that are more precise than generic computer-carrier terms. For GBLS, this matters because patrons and staff need to know not only what a game is called but whether it can be played, on what hardware, with what dependencies, and in what version.

The genre discussion is especially important. Chapman shows that Library of Congress subject headings and genre/form vocabularies remain thin for games, while game websites and fan databases use genre as a major discovery facet. The article separates user-generated genres, narrative genres, visual styles, and gameplay genres. Steam and Itch.io tags can be rich but subjective and unstable. MobyGames and VGMS provide more structured vocabularies that distinguish gameplay from narrative theme, setting, visual presentation, art style, perspective, mood, and related facets. Chapman's main point is that game genre is overloaded: a single label may mix mechanics, narrative, visual style, audience expectations, marketing terms, and player culture. Faceted vocabularies help reduce that overload.

For the larger GBLS project, Chapman extends the metadata strand by showing that game cataloging is both interoperable and distinctive. Libraries can use RDA and MARC structures, but games require enhanced description if catalogs are to support scholarly research, preservation, circulation, discovery, and player advisory. The article also usefully draws attention to gaps. Because many schemas focus on manifestations or expressions, they may underdescribe material-culture elements such as peripheral hardware, manuals, packaging, sound, video, digital file characteristics, color, number of players, connectivity, and ratings. Those details are not decorative; they affect access, compatibility, preservation, and patron decision-making.

Chapman should be used as a synthesis and comparison source rather than as a user study. Its value lies in clarifying how several schema communities are solving similar problems and where standards still need development. For GBLS, the takeaway is practical: game collections need metadata that can communicate across library systems while remaining faithful to games as interactive, technical, visual, narrative, commercial, and community-described objects. That work is ongoing because game genres, platforms, distribution models, and user vocabularies keep changing.
