## Findings
The analysis reveals that while RDA lacks medium-specific guidance, emerging schemas address critical gaps. Key findings include:
- **Title/Series:** Videogame titles are complex due to multiple sources (packaging vs. title screen). VGMS and CMS adapt RDA by allowing variant/colloquial titles. VGMS uniquely distinguishes between "series" and "franchise."
- **Responsibility/Publisher:** Traditional "statement of responsibility" is inadequate for games with multiple contributors. Schemas prioritize distinct elements for developers and publishers, with MobyGames offering granular role-based credits.
- **Edition/Platform:** A distinct "platform" element is universal across all analyzed schemas, separate from edition or system requirements, reflecting the technical necessity of platform identification.
- **Carrier/Content Type:** RDA’s generic carrier terms are insufficient. Schemas employ detailed controlled vocabularies for media types (e.g., specific cartridge brands).
- **Genre:** Genre is multifaceted. Commercial platforms (Steam/Itch.io) use user-generated tags which offer high granularity but lack consistency for library use. Academic schemas (VGMS/MobyGames) decompose genre into gameplay, narrative, and visual style facets to improve precision.
Evidence: "These schemas highlight many of the same medium-specific elements absent from RDA, for example, the value of a platform element distinct from system requirements or edition; the equal emphasis on developer and publisher; or the multifaceted nature of videogame genre." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The article argues that these emerging schemas provide necessary granularity for scholarly research and preservation. They demonstrate that while RDA can accommodate videogames, it requires significant supplementation via medium-specific elements (platform, developer, detailed genre). The study suggests that controlled vocabularies developed by fan communities and academic groups should inform future Library of Congress Genre/Form Terms (LCFGT) to improve discoverability and bibliographic integrity.
Evidence: "Through their schemas and vocabulary sets, GAMECIP, GAMER Group, and the contributors to MobyGames have all helped alleviate that confusion... What is needed is a commitment to providing an enhanced level of description necessary to support scholarly work." (Discussion)

# Summary

Abigail Chapman’s 2021 article, "Trials of Metadata: Emerging Schemas for Videogame Cataloguing," addresses the pressing need for standardized metadata practices as videogames enter library and museum collections. The study conducts a comparative analysis of five distinct metadata schemas to evaluate how they handle the unique complexities of the medium compared to traditional standards like Resource Description and Access (RDA). The selected schemas include two academic initiatives—the Core Metadata Schema (CMS) by GAMECIP and the Video Game Metadata Schema (VGMS) by the GAMER Group—one fan-community database with formal standards (MobyGames), and two commercial distribution platforms (Steam and Itch.io).

The methodology involves comparing these schemas against eleven core RDA elements identified by Online Audiovisual Cataloguers (OLAC) and analyzing their approaches to genre classification. The study finds that traditional cataloguing rules struggle with the hybrid physical-digital nature of games, particularly regarding title sources, edition definitions, and carrier types. For instance, determining the primary source for a title is complicated by discrepancies between packaging, menus, and digital storefronts. While RDA prioritizes the title screen, videogame-specific schemas often allow for broader sourcing to capture variant or colloquial titles used by players.

A significant finding is the universal inclusion of a distinct "platform" element across all analyzed schemas, separate from edition or system requirements. This reflects the critical role hardware and software environments play in defining the game experience. Similarly, the concept of "publisher" is often decoupled from "developer," with schemas emphasizing the developer’s creative role, which is frequently obscured in traditional bibliographic records. MobyGames demonstrates high granularity by offering controlled vocabularies for nearly forty contributor roles, whereas academic schemas like VGMS use broader agent elements but provide detailed faceted descriptions for other attributes.

The analysis of genre reveals that single-term classification is inadequate for videogames. Commercial platforms utilize user-generated tags, which offer rich, emergent terminology regarding gameplay mechanics and visual style but lack the consistency required for formal library cataloguing. In contrast, academic schemas decompose genre into multiple facets: gameplay (ludic elements), narrative (plot/themes), and visual style (artistic presentation). VGMS, for example, employs separate vocabularies for mood, dimension, and point of view, acknowledging that these visual aspects are integral to the user experience.

Chapman concludes that while RDA provides a foundational structure, it lacks the medium-specific granularity necessary for effective videogame cataloguing. The emerging schemas from academic and fan communities offer valuable models for enhancing bibliographic records, particularly through detailed controlled vocabularies for platforms, media types, and multifaceted genre terms. These developments are essential for supporting scholarly research, digital preservation, and improved user discovery in library environments.
