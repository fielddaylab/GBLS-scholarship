# Summary

Kathleen A. Langan’s 2021 perspective article, "The Library Language Game: Information Literacy through the Lens of Wittgenstein's Language Games," explores the philosophical underpinnings of information labeling and its impact on knowledge construction within library settings. The author utilizes Ludwig Wittgenstein’s concept of "language games" to argue that labeling is not a neutral act of categorization but a powerful epistemological process that shapes how users understand and interact with information. The article serves as an invitation for librarians, particularly those involved in information literacy instruction, to critically reflect on the biases, power dynamics, and rules inherent in cataloging practices.

Langan begins by illustrating the power of labeling through the example of a *New York Post* headline change from "ban assault weapons" to "ban weapons of war." This shift demonstrates how relabeling can forcibly alter the meaning and context of an object, guiding readers toward specific interpretations. Drawing on Wittgenstein’s *Philosophical Investigations*, Langan explains that language games rely on shared rules and contexts for meaning to be successfully constructed. When these rules are misunderstood or when labels are biased, "language goes on holiday," leading to philosophical problems and hindering knowledge building. In the library context, cataloging acts as a primary language game where call numbers and subject headings serve as the rules that structure reality for information seekers.

The article integrates the philosophy of information, specifically Luciano Floridi’s concept of the "infosphere," to describe the ecosystem where information retrieval and organization occur. Within this infosphere, labels carry meaning specific to individual items and their relationships to broader groups. Langan argues that cataloging practices often privilege the labeler (the librarian or algorithm) over the information seeker. This power dynamic is exacerbated by biases inherent in naming conventions. For instance, the Library of Congress subject heading "Illegal Aliens" is cited as an example of a label that imposes a colonialist and pejorative reality on a group of people, despite efforts to change it due to political pressures. Langan notes that cataloging standards often prioritize consistency over correctness or ethical inclusivity, which can perpetuate systemic biases related to race, gender, and social status.

Furthermore, the article discusses the role of critical librarianship and critical race theory in understanding how labeling can fail marginalized users. The "insider/outsider" dynamic is highlighted, where those trained in library systems (insiders) navigate language games more successfully than those who are not (outsiders). This creates inequitable barriers to information access. Langan suggests that information literacy instruction must address these disparities by teaching students to recognize the constructed nature of labels and the biases embedded within them. Rather than focusing solely on mastery of search techniques, instruction should foster critical thinking about how language shapes reality and knowledge.

In conclusion, Langan posits that the praxis of information literacy is deeply dependent on how information is labeled. Librarians are encouraged to engage in continuous reflection and re-evaluation of cataloging practices to ensure they are ethically deployed and responsive to user needs. The ultimate goal is to empower students to be mindful participants in the "library language game," capable of questioning labels and understanding their impact on knowledge building. By acknowledging the limitations and biases of current labeling systems, librarians can better support users in navigating the complex infosphere and constructing meaningful knowledge.
