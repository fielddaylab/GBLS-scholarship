# Summary

This article presents a bibliometric analysis of scientific literature concerning gamification from 2013 to 2023, with a specific focus on its application within library and information science (LIS). The primary objective was to map the growth, key contributors, and thematic trends of gamification research using data from the Web of Science database.

The methodology involved extracting 4,885 records initially, which were filtered to 4,778 English-language documents for final analysis. The authors utilized R Software with the Biblioshiny package to perform scientometric mapping, analyzing indicators such as author productivity, institutional affiliation, country output, and keyword co-occurrence.

Key findings indicate a substantial increase in research output over the decade, peaking in 2021 with 754 publications. The United States emerged as the leading nation in scientific production (1,976 documents), followed by Spain and China. Among institutions, the University of Pennsylvania was the most prolific, while Hamari was identified as the most productive individual author. In terms of citation impact, a 2015 study by Seaborn et al. received the highest number of global citations. Keyword analysis revealed that "gamification" is the central term, strongly associated with "motivation," "education," and "behavior."

Specifically regarding LIS, the study isolated approximately 200 documents relevant to the field. It noted a publication peak in 2017 within LIS journals, particularly the *International Journal of Information Management*. The authors argue that gamification serves as an effective strategy for enhancing patron engagement, improving library orientation experiences, and promoting information literacy through tools such as virtual escape rooms, interactive quizzes, and digital badges.

The implications drawn by the authors emphasize the ethical considerations of gamification and its role in making learning more engaging. They highlight a disparity in adoption, noting that while developed nations have extensively integrated these game-like elements into library services, developing nations have yet to fully explore these opportunities. The study concludes that gamification is not merely a trend but a significant interdisciplinary approach that can transform library culture by motivating users and enhancing the overall user experience through interactive and immersive digital humanities tools.
