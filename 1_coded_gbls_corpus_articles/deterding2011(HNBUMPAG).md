### Summary
# **Deterding, S., Dixon, D., Khaled, R., & Nacke, L. (2011). From game design elements to gamefulness: Defining "gamification." In *Proceedings of the 15th International Academic MindTrek Conference: Envisioning Future Media Environments* (pp. 9-15). ACM. https://doi.org/10.1145/2181037.2181040**

Deterding, Dixon, Khaled, and Nacke provide one of the canonical early academic definitions of gamification. The paper responds to the rapid spread of mass-market software that borrowed visible features from video games, especially productivity, finance, health, education, sustainability, news, entertainment, and marketing systems that used points, badges, levels, leaderboards, rewards, reputation systems, and progress feedback to motivate user activity and retention. The authors' concern is conceptual: the term gamification was spreading faster than scholars had clarified what it meant, how it differed from existing HCI and game-studies concepts, and whether it named a genuinely distinct phenomenon.

The paper first situates gamification historically. It notes that the term came from the digital media industry, with documented use by 2008 and wider uptake by 2010, while related terms such as funware, productivity games, behavioral games, game layer, applied gaming, playful design, and exploitationware circulated in parallel. The authors treat the term as contested, especially because critics saw many commercial implementations as shallow, manipulative, or limited to reward systems. They also show that gamification did not appear from nowhere: HCI already had traditions of enjoyable interfaces, funology, hedonic product qualities, motivational affordances, playful design, and game-inspired interface design, while game studies had serious games, serious gaming, pervasive games, alternate reality games, and broader claims about the ludification of culture.

Against that backdrop, the authors propose their compact definition: gamification is the use of game design elements in non-game contexts. Much of the article is devoted to unpacking each part of that definition. Gamification relates to games rather than play in general. Drawing on the paidia/ludus distinction, they separate open, improvisational playfulness from rule-bound, goal-oriented gamefulness. This lets them name gamefulness, gameful interaction, and gameful design as complements to playfulness, playful interaction, and playful design. For libraries, this distinction is useful because a playful children's space, a TTRPG campaign, a summer reading badge system, and a database-search quest may all be game-adjacent without operating in the same way.

The authors also distinguish elements from full games. Serious games are full-fledged games made for purposes beyond entertainment; gamified systems use only game elements. That boundary is not always clean, because whether users experience a system as a game can depend on context, intention, social interpretation, and local rules. Still, the authors suggest that game elements should be understood as design features characteristic of games rather than features unique to games. They organize these across levels: interface patterns such as badges and leaderboards; game mechanics or design patterns such as time constraints, limited resources, and turns; design principles such as clear goals or enduring play; models such as MDA or challenge, fantasy, and curiosity; and methods such as playtesting or playcentric design.

The paper further narrows gamification to game design rather than game technologies or broader game-related practices. A non-game use of a game controller, game engine, or machinima practice is not automatically gamification; the relevant issue is the use of game design elements. The authors also argue that non-game contexts should not be limited to a particular field, purpose, or medium. Just as serious games may be designed for training, health, news, or education, gamification can appear in many application areas. Its distinctive quality is often a flicker among gameful, playful, and instrumental modes of engagement.

For the larger GBLS project, Deterding et al. give the review a precise vocabulary for separating game-based library services from gamified library services. Their framework helps prevent points, badges, leaderboards, summer reading rewards, orientation quests, or progress dashboards from being treated as equivalent to board games, video games, TTRPGs, escape rooms, or serious games. It also helps library designers ask better questions: What game design elements are being used? Are they characteristic of games or merely decorative? Are they aligned with the learning, access, community, or advisory goal? Do patrons experience the activity as gameful, playful, instrumental, or some mix of these? This makes the article foundational for the conceptual terrain of GBLS, even though it is not a library study.
