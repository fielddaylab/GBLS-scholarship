The sample consisted of eight librarians/library staff representing three public library sites: Seattle Public Library, DC Public Library, and Fayette Public Library in La Grange, Texas. The data comprised 16 semi-structured interviews (pre- and post-program) and reflections from a Community of Practice discussion. The program itself involved teens aged 14–18 and undergraduate interns, but the study's empirical focus was on the librarians' experiences.

## Findings
Librarians initially expressed apprehension regarding mental health topics, co-design facilitation, and VR technology. Post-program, perceptions shifted toward viewing mental health as a spectrum rather than a crisis, increasing confidence in supporting patron well-being. Co-design was viewed as highly valuable for engagement, though librarians desired more structure to manage uncertainty. VR reactions were mixed; while some found it engaging and immersive, others noted technical barriers, privacy concerns (e.g., Facebook account requirements), and potential exclusion of non-"techie" teens. The study proposes a "recipe-based" model to balance core co-design principles with flexible implementation details.
Evidence: "Librarians’ attitudes notably shifted when comparing pre- to post- interviews... Co-design was perceived as the key enabler... Overall, the VR aspect garnered mixed reactions from the librarians." (pp. 141–148)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors propose a "recipe-based co-design model" to help librarians navigate the tension between structure and flexibility. This model distinguishes between non-negotiable core elements ("main ingredients") and adaptable components ("substitutes"), allowing libraries to tailor programs to local contexts while preserving the integrity of the co-design process. The study suggests that libraries are well-positioned to support youth mental health through normalized, everyday interactions rather than crisis intervention.

# Summary

This qualitative study investigates the experiences of librarians participating in a multi-site initiative to co-design virtual reality (VR) experiences aimed at supporting teen mental health. Conducted across three public libraries—Seattle Public Library, DC Public Library, and Fayette Public Library in Texas—the project involved eight librarians who were interviewed before and after the program. The research addresses two primary questions: what opportunities and challenges librarians perceive in offering such programs, and what tensions emerge during implementation that inform future practices.

The study context is framed by the exacerbation of youth mental health issues during the COVID-19 pandemic and the growing role of libraries as community hubs for well-being. The program utilized a co-design approach where teens aged 14–18 collaborated with librarians and undergraduate interns to create VR environments focused on stress reduction and mindfulness.

Findings indicate a significant shift in librarian attitudes regarding mental health support. Initially, many participants expressed anxiety about "saying something wrong" or causing harm due to a lack of formal training in mental health. However, through the program, librarians reframed mental health from a binary crisis state to a spectrum of everyday well-being. This perspective change increased their confidence in facilitating discussions and creating safe spaces for youth expression. The co-design process itself was identified as a critical enabler of engagement, fostering rapport and allowing teens to feel valued. Librarians noted that the collaborative nature of design helped normalize mental health conversations.

However, the implementation revealed tensions regarding structure versus flexibility. Librarians with little prior co-design experience initially desired highly detailed lesson plans to mitigate uncertainty. As the program progressed, some found rigid structures restrictive when adapting to local contexts or technical issues. To address this, the authors propose a "recipe-based co-design model." This framework analogizes co-design to cooking: it identifies "main ingredients" (core principles like partnership and inquiry) that must be preserved, "substitutes" (tools or activities that can be swapped), and "local ingredients" (community-specific strengths). This model aims to provide enough structure for novice facilitators while allowing necessary improvisation.

The role of VR technology elicited mixed reactions. While some librarians found VR immersive and effective for social engagement, others highlighted significant barriers. These included technical setup times, the potential for motion sickness, and privacy concerns related to platform requirements (e.g., Facebook accounts for Oculus Quest). Crucially, some librarians observed that VR might exclude teens who do not identify as "techies" or those from marginalized groups less familiar with gaming culture. One site reported that VR was not the primary draw for their community, suggesting that the technology itself may not be universally suitable for all library contexts.

The study concludes that while co-design is a robust method for engaging youth and supporting mental health, the integration of VR requires careful consideration of accessibility and inclusivity. The proposed recipe-based model offers a practical tool for libraries to adapt co-design programs to their specific resources and community needs without compromising the core benefits of participatory design. Future work will involve testing this model across additional library sites to assess its effectiveness in building librarian confidence and program quality.
