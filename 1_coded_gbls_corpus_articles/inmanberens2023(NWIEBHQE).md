## Purpose
To examine the media consumption habits, library usage patterns, and identity formations of Gen Z (ages 13–25) and Millennials (ages 26–40) to inform library service strategies. The report aims to explain how these generations identify through media use, discover books, and engage with physical and digital library resources, while highlighting challenges such as exclusive licensing and equitable access.
Evidence: "This report will explain some implications of that development. It will aid librarians and other stakeholders in examining how libraries currently serve Gen Z and millennials..." (Page 1)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employs a mixed-methods approach combining quantitative survey data and qualitative ethnographic research. The quantitative component involved a nationally representative online survey conducted via Qualtrics in March 2022, with quotas for race, gender, and region to ensure representativeness. The qualitative component included site visits and informal interviews at two branches of the Cuyahoga County Public Library (Garfield and South Euclid), approved by an Institutional Review Board for minor participation.
Evidence: "This report... is informed by two primary datasets, one quantitative and the other qualitative... survey of 2075 Gen Z and millennials... ethnographic research at two branches of Cuyahoga County (OH) Public Library" (Page 17)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The quantitative sample consisted of 2,075 U.S. residents aged 13–40, comprising 919 Gen Z respondents (44.3%) and 1,156 Millennial respondents (55.7%). The sample was stratified by race, gender, region, and income levels. Qualitative data were drawn from observations and interactions with teens and staff at two specific public library branches in Ohio.
Evidence: "The quantitative data comes from a survey of 2075 Gen Z and millennials (aged 13–40)... Gen Z represents 44.3%... millennials 55.7%... qualitative data comes from ethnographic research at two branches" (Page 17)
Confidence: high

## Findings
Key findings include:
1. **Library Usage:** 54% of Gen Z and Millennials visited a physical library in the past year. Libraries serve as "book showrooms," with 27% discovering books by browsing shelves. Notably, 54% of those who do not identify as readers still visit libraries, utilizing them for socialization, Wi-Fi, and safe spaces.
2. **Digital vs. Physical:** Despite high mobile media consumption (92% check social media daily), print remains the preferred book format. However, only 37% borrow from digital collections. Long wait times for digital books are a significant barrier, with 75% considering a one-week wait "long."
3. **Equity Issues:** Black (58%) and Latinx (57%) patrons use digital collections more than the general population (52%) but face greater disadvantages due to long hold times, framing fair digital access as a racial equity issue.
4. **Media Identities:** Participants hold fluid identities as readers (57%), gamers (53%), and fans (52%). Avid media consumers often engage across multiple formats; gaming and fandom are strongly linked to community building, whereas reading is often solitary.
5. **Discovery Channels:** Social media influencers and streaming adaptations are major discovery drivers. 34% of Gen Z discover books via influencers. Amazon-exclusive content and subscription apps (e.g., Webtoon, Audible) compete with library offerings, as many titles are unavailable for library licensing.
Evidence: "54% of Gen Z and millennials visited a physical library... 75%... believe a library wait of one week or less is 'long'... Black (58%) and Latinx (57%) physical library patrons use digital collections more than the general survey population" (Pages 2, 7)
Confidence: high

## Implications
Libraries must adapt to remain relevant by:
1. **Enhancing Visibility:** Integrating library links into digital borrowing apps to stay in users' consideration sets amidst social media saturation.
2. **Communicating Values:** Promoting libraries as free, fun, privacy-protecting, and environmentally sustainable ("green") spaces.
3. **Addressing Access Barriers:** Advocating for better licensing terms to reduce wait times and expand access to exclusive content, particularly to support BIPOC patrons who rely heavily on digital collections.
4. **Supporting Hybrid Identities:** Creating physical spaces that accommodate socializing, gaming, and making, recognizing that these activities coexist with reading rather than replacing it.
Evidence: "Recommendations for how libraries can connect with Gen Z and millennials... Get in Gen Z and Millennials’ Consideration Set... Communicate Libraries’ Unique Qualities" (Pages 16-17)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This report by Berens and Noorda (2023) investigates the library usage and media consumption habits of Generation Z (ages 13–25) and Millennials (ages 26–40). Based on a nationally representative survey of 2,075 individuals and qualitative ethnographic observations at two Ohio public libraries, the study challenges assumptions that younger generations are disengaged from reading or libraries. Instead, it reveals complex media identities where reading coexists with gaming, fandom, and digital content creation.

The quantitative data indicates that 54% of Gen Z and Millennials visited a physical library within the twelve months prior to the survey. Contrary to the notion that digital natives prefer exclusively digital formats, print books remain the preferred format for these groups. However, digital library usage is lower, with only 37% borrowing from digital collections. A significant barrier identified is wait times; 75% of respondents consider a wait of one week or less for a digital title to be "long." This issue disproportionately affects Black and Latinx patrons, who use digital collections at higher rates than the general population (58% and 57%, respectively, versus 52%) but face greater deterrence from long hold lists. The authors frame equitable access to digital materials as a critical racial equity issue.

The report highlights that libraries function as "book showrooms" and safe social spaces. Notably, 54% of respondents who do not identify as readers still visit physical libraries, utilizing them for Wi-Fi, study spaces, programming, and socialization. Ethnographic observations confirmed that teens use library teen lounges to "vibe," craft, and game, with books present but not always the primary focus. This suggests that libraries support mental well-being and community building beyond traditional lending services.

Media discovery is heavily influenced by social media and streaming. 92% of respondents check social media daily, with TikTok and Instagram being key platforms for book recommendations. Gen Z relies more on influencers (34%) compared to Millennials (23%). Furthermore, exclusive licensing models from publishers like Amazon (e.g., Audible exclusives) limit library access to popular content, driving users toward paid subscriptions or unlicensed sources; 32% of respondents admitted to downloading books from unlicensed sources.

The authors argue that Gen Z and Millennials hold fluid identities as readers, gamers, and fans. Avid engagement in one media type often correlates with avid engagement in others, indicating a non-zero-sum relationship between gaming/fandom and reading. To remain relevant, the report recommends that libraries improve their visibility within digital ecosystems, communicate their unique values (privacy, sustainability, free access), and advocate for licensing reforms that reduce wait times and expand content availability, particularly to support underserved communities.
