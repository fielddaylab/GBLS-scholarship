### Summary
# **Hwang, G.-J., & Zhang, D. (2024). Effects of an adaptive computer agent-based digital game on EFL students' English learning outcomes. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 72*(6), 3271-3294. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-024-10396-4**

Hwang and Zhang report the design and evaluation of an adaptive computer agent-based digital game for English as a Foreign Language (EFL) vocabulary learning. The study is located in educational technology and language learning rather than library services. Its central premise is that game-based language learning can increase engagement and reduce anxiety, but that fixed, single-role pedagogical agents may not respond flexibly enough to learners' changing cognitive, behavioral, and emotional needs.

The authors developed an adaptive computer agent-based digital game (ACA-DG) in which the agent could switch among three roles: teacher, peer, and student. Teacher agents provided confident, correct guidance and real-time content support when players repeatedly failed or appeared frustrated. Peer agents offered more optional interaction and mostly correct information, inviting players to explore and request help. Student agents appeared less confident, asked players questions, and encouraged learners to take on a teaching or advising role. A decision-tree role-switching strategy, built from two experienced English teachers' judgments across 30 cases, used behavior features such as cognitive status, help-seeking, non-task interaction, and knowledge review to determine which agent role should appear next.

The game was developed in RPG Maker MV and taught junior-high English prepositions through tutorial dialogue, pictures, videos, treasure hunts, food collection, and battle tasks. Non-player characters introduced vocabulary, while the agent followed the player as a partner who could prompt, advise, or ask for help depending on role. The control condition used a conventional computer agent-based digital game (CCA-DG) in which the agent stayed in a teacher role throughout the game, without monitoring or adaptive role switching.

The study used a quasi-experimental design with 56 junior-high students in northern Taiwan. Two classes used the adaptive agent game (n = 30), and two classes used the conventional teacher-agent game (n = 26). The experiment lasted five weeks: two weeks of prior instruction and computer-skills preparation, pretests and questionnaires in week three, game-based learning in week four, and posttests/questionnaires in week five. Measures included English vocabulary achievement tests on time and place prepositions, learning motivation, self-efficacy, English anxiety, and logged behavior features.

Results favored the adaptive agent condition on several outcomes. Students in the ACA-DG group had significantly higher English learning achievement than the CCA-DG group, with adjusted means of 61.87 and 52.01. They also reported significantly higher self-efficacy and significantly lower English anxiety. Learning motivation increased significantly from pretest to posttest in both groups, but there was no significant difference between groups. Behavior logs showed that ACA-DG students had fewer incorrect answers, sought help more often, and engaged in more non-task interactions with the game environment or virtual characters, suggesting that the adaptive agent increased both guidance and social exploration.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is relevant only as design-adjacent background. It suggests that games used for learning may benefit from adaptive support roles, differentiated feedback, affective scaffolding, and agent personalities that shift between expert guidance, peer collaboration, and learner-led explanation. Those ideas could inform library instruction games or youth learning experiences if a library had the capacity to build adaptive digital tools. However, the study does not involve libraries, librarians, information literacy, public programming, collections, access, or community learning. Its evidence concerns classroom EFL vocabulary acquisition in a school setting. It should therefore remain outside the main GBLS review except as broader educational-game context.
