# Structured Extraction

## Purpose
The study aimed to investigate the effects of an adaptive computer agent-based digital game (ACA-DG) on English as a Foreign Language (EFL) students' learning achievement, motivation, self-efficacy, and English anxiety, compared to a conventional computer agent-based digital game (CCA-DG). It sought to determine if dynamic role-switching of computer agents (teacher, peer, student) based on learner behavior improves educational outcomes.
Evidence: "This study aimed to investigate the effects of adaptive computer agents on EFL students’ learning performance, cognition, and behavior in the game..." (Introduction, p. 3273)
Confidence: high

## Method
A quasi-experimental design was employed over a five-week period. The intervention involved a digital game developed using RPG Maker MV. The experimental group used an ACA-DG where the computer agent dynamically switched roles (teacher, peer, student) based on a decision tree model analyzing player behavior (errors, help-seeking, non-task interactions, knowledge review). The control group used a CCA-DG with a static teacher-role agent. Data were collected via pre- and post-tests for achievement, and questionnaires for motivation, self-efficacy, and anxiety. Statistical analyses included one-way ANCOVA and independent/paired sample t-tests.
Evidence: "A quasi-experiment was designed... Two classes (n = 30) were arranged to be the experimental group... while two classes (n = 26) were arranged to be the control group..." (Abstract; Experiment design, p. 3283)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The participants were 56 junior high school students (average age 14) from four classes in a school in northern Taiwan. The experimental group consisted of 30 students, and the control group consisted of 26 students. All participants had similar computer skills training and were taught by the same English teacher. Data included test scores (pre/post), questionnaire responses (5-point Likert scales), and behavioral logs from the game system (incorrect answers, help-seeking frequency, non-task interactions, knowledge review instances).
Evidence: "The participants were 56 junior high school students from four classes... The ACA-DG group had 30 students... the CCA-DG group had 26 students..." (Participants, p. 3283)
Confidence: high

## Findings
Students in the experimental group (ACA-DG) demonstrated significantly higher learning achievement and self-efficacy, and significantly lower English anxiety compared to the control group (CCA-DG). There was no significant difference between groups regarding learning motivation, although both groups showed increased motivation from pre- to post-test. Behaviorally, the ACA-DG group had significantly fewer incorrect answers, sought help more frequently, and engaged in more non-task interactions than the CCA-DG group. No significant difference was found in knowledge review frequency. Role-switching frequency did not differ significantly between high- and low-achieving students within the experimental group, though descriptive trends suggested higher achievers switched roles more often.
Evidence: "The results showed that students in the experimental group had significantly higher learning achievement and self-efficacy than those in the control group... significantly lower English anxiety... no significant difference... regarding learning motivation." (Abstract; Experimental results, pp. 3284–3287)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The study suggests that adaptive computer agents that switch roles based on learner needs can enhance vocabulary learning outcomes, boost self-efficacy, and reduce language anxiety in EFL contexts. The findings support the integration of adaptive learning strategies into digital games to provide personalized scaffolding. The authors recommend future research with larger sample sizes, longer experimental durations, and machine learning-based adaptation models to improve the precision of agent role-switching decisions.
Evidence: "In summary... the adaptive computer agent-based digital game developed in this study helped learners gain better learning achievement, higher self-efficacy, and lower English learning anxiety." (Discussion and conclusions, p. 3290)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This quasi-experimental study by Hwang and Zhang (2024) investigates the impact of an adaptive computer agent-based digital game (ACA-DG) on English as a Foreign Language (EFL) learning outcomes among junior high school students. The research addresses limitations in traditional digital games where computer agents perform static roles, often failing to adapt to individual learner needs. The authors developed an ACA-DG system featuring a computer agent that dynamically switches between three roles—teacher, peer, and student—based on real-time analysis of player behavior, including cognitive status (error rates), help-seeking frequency, non-task interactions, and knowledge review choices. This adaptive mechanism was governed by a decision tree model constructed from expert teacher judgments.

The study involved 56 students (average age 14) from four classes in northern Taiwan, divided into an experimental group (n=30) using the ACA-DG and a control group (n=26) using a conventional computer agent-based digital game (CCA-DG) with a static teacher-role agent. The intervention lasted five weeks, focusing on English prepositions. Data were collected through pre- and post-tests for academic achievement and validated questionnaires measuring learning motivation, self-efficacy, and English anxiety. Behavioral data were logged directly from the game system.

Statistical analysis using one-way ANCOVA revealed that students in the experimental group achieved significantly higher learning outcomes than those in the control group (adjusted means: 61.87 vs. 52.01; p < 0.05). Furthermore, the ACA-DG group reported significantly higher self-efficacy and significantly lower English anxiety compared to the control group. However, there was no significant difference between the two groups in terms of learning motivation, although paired sample t-tests indicated that both groups experienced increased motivation from pre- to post-intervention. Behavioral analysis showed that the experimental group made fewer errors, sought help more frequently, and engaged in more non-task interactions than the control group, suggesting greater engagement and exploratory behavior facilitated by the adaptive agent.

The authors attribute these positive outcomes to the personalized scaffolding provided by the adaptive agents. By switching roles, the system offered appropriate support levels: teacher agents for struggling students needing direct guidance, and peer/student agents for higher-performing students benefiting from social interaction and reciprocal teaching. This adaptability helped reduce cognitive load and anxiety while fostering self-regulation.

The study acknowledges several limitations. The sample size was relatively small due to constraints imposed by the coronavirus epidemic, which may limit the generalizability of the behavioral analysis findings. Additionally, the role-switching strategy relied on expert decision-making rather than machine learning algorithms, and the experimental duration was limited to one week of active gaming. The authors recommend future studies with larger samples, longer durations, and more sophisticated adaptive mechanisms to further validate these findings. Overall, the research supports the efficacy of adaptive computer agents in enhancing digital game-based language learning environments.
