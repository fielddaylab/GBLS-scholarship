## Findings
The authors identified 11 specific relationship types prevalent in video games: *isPortOf*, *isRemakeOf*, *isRebootOf*, *isPrequelOf*, *isSequelOf*, *isSidestoryOf*, *isSpinoffOf*, *isCrossoverOf*, *isSpiritualSuccessorOf*, *isInspiredBy*, and *complements* (for expansions). The authors concluded that current standards are insufficient for the granular distinctions required in video game metadata.

# Summary

This article addresses the inadequacy of existing bibliographic and cultural heritage metadata standards in describing the complex relationships among video games. As video games become increasingly prevalent in cultural heritage institutions, there is a need for robust, media-specific metadata that reflects how users perceive and interact with these artifacts. The authors argue that standards such as Dublin Core, Functional Requirements for Bibliographic Records (FRBR), Resource Description and Access (RDA), CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model (CRM), and Cataloging Cultural Objects (CCO) offer limited utility due to issues of inaccuracy, inconsistency, and lack of granularity.

To address this gap, the researchers conducted a study involving the review of existing standards, consultation with domain experts from the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) and local game companies, and an examination of diverse sample game sets including *Final Fantasy*, *Kingdom Hearts*, *Pokémon*, and *Civilization V*. Through this iterative process, they identified and defined 11 distinct relationship types and 4 grouping entities specific to the video game domain.

The four grouping entities are Series (related games with consecutive numbering or continuing narratives), Franchise (intellectual property shared across media), Universe (a broader aggregation of themes and settings, such as the Final Fantasy universe), and Collection (anthologies of local releases). The 11 relationship types include *isPortOf* (conversion to a different platform), *isRemakeOf* (re-release with enhanced features), *isRebootOf* (restart of a series disregarding previous history), *isPrequelOf* and *isSequelOf* (narrative sequencing), *isSidestoryOf* (parallel narratives), *isSpinoffOf* (divergent stories featuring original characters), *isCrossoverOf* (appearance of characters from different IPs), *isSpiritualSuccessorOf* (similar games by the same developers under a new IP due to licensing), *isInspiredBy* (artistic or commercial influence), and *complements* (additional content/expansions).

The authors crosswalked these proposed relationships against existing standards to evaluate their applicability. They found that while mappings are possible, they result in significant information loss. For example, Dublin Core’s "relation" property is too broad to distinguish between a port and a remake. CIDOC CRM, while thorough for physical artifacts, restricts temporal relationships like "occurs before" to event entities, making it difficult to describe prequels and sequels as abstract game objects rather than fictional events. Furthermore, concepts like "Universe" have no equivalent in existing standards.

The study concludes that video games require specialized data modeling that captures the nuances of their development, distribution, and narrative structures. The proposed relationships and entities provide a foundation for developing a standardized metadata schema tailored to video games, which can improve description, organization, and access in library and museum collections. These definitions may also offer insights for modeling relationships in other complex media domains.
