### Summary
# **Bishoff, C., Farrell, S. L., & Neeser, A. E. (2015). Outreach, collaboration, collegiality: Evolving approaches to library video game services. *Journal of Library Innovation, 6*(1), 92-109.**

Bishoff, Farrell, and Neeser describe how the University of Minnesota - Twin Cities Libraries moved from a familiar academic-library video game service model toward a more relational, campus-specific strategy. Their starting point was the same set of services that appear repeatedly in the early academic gaming literature: a video game collection, undergraduate gaming events, and possible instruction modules. The authors were interested in games because video games were already being used, studied, and developed across many departments, including computer science, design, journalism, engineering, medicine, humanities, and the arts. Yet because the university had no single game studies department or natural selector home, a collection immediately raised questions about acquisition, preservation, replacement, cataloging, and long-term stewardship.

The Libraries chose a low-cost pilot instead of a collection. For less than $600, they bought a Nintendo Wii, extra controllers, and twenty multiplayer party games and hosted two finals-week game nights. The goals were modest: provide a study break, test whether students were receptive to gaming in the library, and gather feedback for future programming. The first event, held in a media viewing room, drew only three players, one of whom was a security guard who did not know the room existed. The second event moved to a computer lab adjacent to a busy study area, added snacks, Wii games, and retro computer games, and drew 33 students, but only 15 came to play. The authors emphasize that these results were disappointing not because no one on campus played games, but because the event form did not fit how students were using the library during finals week.

The failed pilots pushed the Libraries to assess demand rather than assume it. A first-year orientation survey produced 579 responses and found that only 54 percent of respondents said they would attend a video gaming event at the Libraries during finals week, and 56 percent said they would check out video games from the Libraries. Those numbers represented a small majority, not the strong evidence the team had expected. Informal event feedback also suggested that students wanted more than familiar Wii titles: some wanted access to newer systems such as the Wii U, Xbox One, PlayStation 4, or Oculus Rift, while others wanted LAN parties or graphically intensive PC gaming. The authors interpret this as a lesson about audience and environment. A large, urban, commuter campus is not the same service environment as a residential campus, a small college, or a public library youth program, even if all of those settings contain game players.

The article's central contribution is the pivot from stand-alone events and collections to partnerships, networking, game creation, education, and community outreach. The Libraries recognized that student groups were already doing game work on campus. Glitch hosted weekly gaming lounges and supported student developers; the Society for Physics Students borrowed the Libraries' Oculus Rift for an event that quickly became a collaborative learning environment; and the Video Game Development Club had built its own reference collection of game development, programming, and technology books. Rather than duplicating those activities, librarians began using their liaison relationships, spaces, technologies, collections, and convening power to support them.

Several later initiatives illustrate the new approach. The Libraries used an Xbox Kinect and Skanect software in a city-wide art festival to create 3D scans for 156 participants, showing how gaming hardware could be repurposed for creative public engagement. They partnered with Glitch on Co-Op Talk, a monthly discussion group in which a participant proposed a topic, Glitch identified a game, and a librarian selected related readings. They also collaborated on a campus event about gaming, gender, and identity that attracted more than 100 people. In another initiative, the Libraries hosted a panel of researchers using video games in different fields, drawing more than 40 attendees and prompting follow-up conversations among faculty, students, and researchers.

For the larger GBLS project, Bishoff et al. are especially useful because they resist a simple success-story template. The article shows that games are not automatically good outreach merely because students play games, and that a poorly matched program can underperform even when it is inexpensive, well promoted, and held in a busy library. It also shows that failure can be diagnostically productive when staff treat it as evidence about local needs. The UMN case supports a GBLS design principle: begin with the actual service ecology, not with a generic program type. On some campuses, a collection or game night may be the right starting point; on this campus, partnerships with existing student groups, researchers, makerspaces, and community organizations produced more meaningful service opportunities.

The article also broadens what counts as library video game service. Bishoff et al. are not arguing against collections or recreational events in general. They are arguing that academic libraries should develop services that can evolve with students' and researchers' work. In their conclusion, game services are most effective when they support networking, game creation, education, and community outreach. That makes the source a strong bridge between collection literature, outreach literature, and research-support literature. It should be cited when the review discusses matching game form to service goal, building partnerships, and evaluating outcomes beyond attendance.

The limitations are straightforward. This is a single institutional case, and the evidence includes event attendance, a brief informal survey, observations, and narrative accounts of subsequent collaborations rather than a controlled assessment. Still, its value lies precisely in the candid account of underperformance and redesign. GBLS needs such cases because they reveal the planning assumptions, environmental constraints, and partnership work that are often hidden behind successful program descriptions.
