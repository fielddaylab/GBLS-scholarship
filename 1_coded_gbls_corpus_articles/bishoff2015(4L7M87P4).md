# Summary

This article by Bishoff, Farrell, and Neeser (2015) examines the evolution of video game services at the University of Minnesota – Twin Cities Libraries. The authors describe an initial attempt to implement standard academic library gaming models—specifically a small collection and recreational undergraduate events—which ultimately failed to generate significant engagement. Through this failure, the libraries pivoted toward a strategy centered on collaboration, partnership, and supporting research and creation rather than mere consumption.

The study begins by contextualizing video games in higher education, noting their integration into research and curriculum across various disciplines. The University of Minnesota is characterized as a large, urban, commuter campus with over 52,000 students, many of whom live off-campus. Recognizing that traditional library gaming models often target residential campuses or public libraries, the authors initiated a low-cost pilot program to test student interest. They hosted two gaming events during finals week, providing Nintendo Wii consoles and multiplayer games. The first event, held in a media viewing room, attracted only three participants. The second event, held in a high-traffic computer lab with free food, drew 33 attendees, but only 15 engaged in gaming. The authors noted that the staff time required (approximately 22 hours per event) was disproportionate to the reach and impact of these activities.

To better understand student preferences, the authors surveyed 579 first-year students during orientation. Contrary to expectations based on national statistics regarding teen gaming habits, only 54% of respondents indicated they would attend a library gaming event, and 56% expressed interest in checking out video games. However, informal feedback revealed that students were interested in accessing newer, expensive technologies (such as the Oculus Rift or Wii U) and engaging in game development or LAN parties, rather than playing familiar party games.

Based on these findings, the libraries shifted their strategy to leverage existing community resources and student organizations. They partnered with "Glitch," a student gaming group, to co-host discussion series and events, and collaborated with the Society for Physics Students to provide access to an Oculus Rift for educational demonstrations. These partnerships resulted in higher engagement and fostered interdisciplinary connections among students, faculty, and staff. The authors also engaged in community outreach, such as using Xbox Kinect technology for a public art festival, demonstrating non-traditional uses of gaming hardware.

The article concludes that successful library video game services depend heavily on the institutional context. For commuter campuses, open recreational events may not align with student priorities. Instead, libraries should focus on facilitating networking, supporting game creation, and partnering with existing groups to provide access to emerging technologies. The authors argue that libraries need not maintain large collections or host standalone events to be effective; rather, they can serve as collaborative hubs that support the educational and research needs of their specific communities.
