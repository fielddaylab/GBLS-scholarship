## Findings
The authors report that SL facilitates immersive learning experiences, such as conducting research interviews with global residents and creating interactive exhibits. Students benefited from access to diverse interviewees, enhanced understanding of information literacy concepts through 3D modeling, and opportunities for international collaboration. Qualitative feedback indicated that virtual reference interviews were effective and comparable to real-life interactions, though communication nuances differed (e.g., text chat reactions). Professional mentors provided structured support for student-led events, fostering rapport and career development. The authors found that SL reduced barriers to CPD by eliminating travel costs and enabling participation in global conferences and workshops.
Evidence: "Particular benefits to this cohort were access to a wider range of interviewees... encouraging students to think more deeply about the requirements of a good interview." # Summary

This article by Webber and Nahl (2011) examines the application of the virtual world Second Life (SL) in Library and Information Science (LIS) education and Continuing Professional Development (CPD). The authors argue that SL serves as a sustainable "laboratory" for innovation, offering significant advantages in terms of resource efficiency, international collaboration, and pedagogical engagement. Drawing on their experiences teaching at the University of Sheffield and the University of Hawaii between 2007 and 2010, they describe specific implementations of SL in undergraduate and postgraduate curricula, as well as its role in professional networking and development.

The methodological approach is descriptive and reflective, relying on the authors' direct involvement in course design, student supervision, and CPD event organization. The population studied includes 25–35 first-year undergraduate students at Sheffield participating in an Information Literacy module, and 10–20 Masters students annually in Educational Informatics modules. At the University of Hawaii, Nahl taught four Masters courses with average enrollments of 10 students per elective and 15 in a core course. The data presented consists of student assignments, qualitative feedback from learners and mentors, and observations of virtual events.

Key findings indicate that SL enhances learning through immersive experiences. At Sheffield, undergraduate students conducted research interviews with SL residents globally, analyzing information behavior in a virtual context. This approach provided access to diverse participants and encouraged deeper reflection on interview techniques compared to face-to-face interactions. Masters students engaged with 3D models of educational concepts, such as Inquiry Based Learning, which aided in understanding abstract ideas. At Hawaii, students participated in reference shadowing at the Community Virtual Library (CVL) and organized professional events for organizations like the American Library Association (ALA). Qualitative feedback from students suggested that virtual reference interviews were effective and comparable to real-life interactions, although communication dynamics differed due to the mediated nature of text chat. Mentors reported that structured support for student-led projects fostered professional rapport and provided valuable career advice.

The article highlights several implications for LIS education and practice. First, SL supports sustainable learning by reducing the need for physical travel, thereby saving time, funds, and environmental resources while enabling global participation in CPD events. Second, it facilitates international collaboration, allowing students and professionals to interact with peers across different cultures and institutions. Third, it provides a platform for developing technical and pedagogical skills relevant to modern library services, such as virtual reference, event management, and content creation. The authors emphasize the importance of preparing LIS professionals to serve "virtual learners" and to create innovative, accessible library services in digital environments.

Limitations acknowledged include the requirement for good broadband connections and capable hardware, as well as the initial learning curve associated with navigating a 3D virtual world. However, the authors argue that these barriers are comparable to those of other CPD activities and are outweighed by the benefits of enriched professional engagement and global connectivity. The article concludes that engaging with virtual worlds offers significant opportunities for enhancing LIS education and professional development, fostering creativity, and building international networks.
