# Summary

Al-Hafdi and Alhalafawy (2024) present a comprehensive bibliometric analysis and systematic review of gamification-based learning (GBL) literature spanning ten years (2012–2021). The study addresses a gap in existing research by monitoring the developments, trends, and effects of gamification in educational environments. The primary objective is to provide scholars with a roadmap of key areas of emphasis, including prevalent terminology, geographic contributions, influential authors, and empirical findings regarding game elements and learning outcomes.

The methodology consists of two distinct phases. First, a bibliometric analysis was conducted using the Web of Science database. Search strings included terms such as "gamification," "gameful," and "gaming elements" combined with "education." This phase identified 3,196 records. Second, a systematic literature review was performed following PRISMA guidelines. Strict inclusion criteria required studies to be from Q1 or Q2 quartile journals, contain empirical evidence (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods), and explicitly detail the gamification components and their impact on learning outcomes. After excluding 3,043 records for various reasons (e.g., lack of empirical data, abstracts only, missing impact factors), a final sample of 52 studies was selected for detailed coding and analysis.

The bibliometric results reveal significant growth in the field, with publications increasing from seven in 2012 to 917 in 2021. The United States emerged as the leading contributor with 660 papers, followed by Spain (408) and Britain (276). *Computers in Human Behavior* and *Computers & Education* were identified as the most influential journals. Juho Hamari was cited as the most prolific and influential author in the domain. Keyword analysis highlighted "gamification," "motivation," "engagement," and "game-based learning" as central themes, alongside digital platforms like Moodle and Kahoot.

The systematic review of the 52 selected studies provided insights into practical applications. Undergraduate education was the most frequent setting for GBL implementation (35 studies), followed by primary education (14 studies). Secondary and intermediate levels were significantly less represented. Regarding game mechanics, points, leaderboards, and badges were the most commonly utilized elements. These elements are linked to educational theories such as goal-setting, self-efficacy, social comparison, and operant conditioning. The learning outcomes most frequently measured were achievement (24 studies), performance (16), engagement (14), and motivation (10). Other outcomes like satisfaction, attitude, and cognitive load were less commonly addressed.

The authors conclude that gamification is a rapidly developing field with substantial potential to enhance learner engagement and academic performance. They recommend that educators and designers prioritize points, leaderboards, and badges in digital platforms. Furthermore, they call for faculty training to align with gamification mechanisms and suggest future research expand into qualitative analyses of teacher and student perspectives, mixed-methods studies, and specific subject areas like science and mathematics. The study acknowledges limitations, including the exclusion of non-English publications and a heavy reliance on quasi-experimental designs in the analyzed literature.
