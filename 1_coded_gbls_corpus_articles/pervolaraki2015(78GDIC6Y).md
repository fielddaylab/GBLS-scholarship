## Findings
The meta-analysis identified three distinct patterns of information use: evolutionary, interactive, and sequential. It distinguished between analytic/professional strategies and intuitive/everyday strategies, noting that intuitive strategies (e.g., browsing, serendipity) offer more space for creativity. The study found that creative information strategies are tied to context and involve a balance of logical and intuitive processing. A three-dimensional conceptual model was proposed, comprising problem-oriented, knowledge-oriented, and interactive dimensions. Key supportive strategies identified include conceptual structuring, conceptual exploration, and conceptual navigation. The findings suggest that ecological information literacy involves awareness of the information environment, re-use of information objects, and adaptation to information overload.

# Summary

This article, titled "Looking for Creative Information Strategies and Ecological Literacy," presents a theoretical framework for understanding creativity within the domain of information literacy. Authored by Jela Steinerová and published in the proceedings of the Third European Conference on Information Literacy (ECIL 2015), the work addresses the gap in information science research regarding the role of creativity in information behavior. The primary objective is to identify creative information strategies and define "ecological information literacy" as a holistic approach to information use that considers the environment and context.

The methodology employed is a meta-analysis of three existing empirical studies, combined with a review of phenomenographic literature on information use patterns. The first dataset analyzed involves semi-structured interviews with 19 doctoral students from diverse disciplines, revealing variations in information styles such as monitoring key sources in sciences versus deeper categorization in social sciences. The second dataset comprises interviews with 17 information managers at Slovak universities, highlighting the significance of values, people, and creativity as knowledge assets. The third dataset is a survey of 255 scholars, artists, and information professionals in Slovakia, which compared analytical processing strategies used by professionals with holistic creative strategies employed by artists.

The findings synthesize these sources to propose that information strategies can be divided into traditional analytic strategies and intuitive strategies, with the latter providing greater scope for human creativity through uncertainty, heuristics, and serendipity. The author identifies three main patterns of information use: evolutionary, interactive, and sequential. Based on this analysis, a conceptual model of creative information strategies is developed, structured around three dimensions: problem-oriented, knowledge-oriented, and interactive. Within these dimensions, the article outlines specific supportive strategies, including conceptual structuring, conceptual exploration, and conceptual navigation. The model posits that creative information behavior relies on sources such as metaphors, analogies, intuition, and empathy, rather than solely on logical structures.

A significant portion of the article is dedicated to defining "ecological information literacy." This concept is described at two levels: a macro level involving environmental awareness and sustainability in library operations (e.g., green libraries), and a micro level concerning individual information behavior. At the micro level, ecological information literacy involves cultivating the information environment, assessing relevance, making informed decisions, and developing resilience against information overload. The article argues that users build their own "ecological information spaces" through mixed strategies of orientation and creative exploration.

The implications of this research suggest a shift in information literacy instruction and service design. The author recommends that digital libraries and information services should be designed to support implicit knowledge discovery, visualization, and collaboration. By integrating intuitive and synthetic thinking into information strategies, institutions can foster more creative and innovative information use. The proposed conceptual model serves as a guide for developing digital spaces that enhance information literacy by accommodating the complex, contextual, and creative nature of human information behavior. The article concludes by emphasizing the need for further research to better understand how communities of practice can be supported in becoming more creative in their information use.
