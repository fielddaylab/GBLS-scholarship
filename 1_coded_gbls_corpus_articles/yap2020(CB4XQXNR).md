## Findings
The authors report that game-based approaches successfully engage students who might otherwise be disinterested in traditional instruction. In Kazakhstan, the integration of online GBL tools like Mentimeter and Socrative enhanced participation, particularly among shy students, by allowing anonymous competition and immediate feedback. The InfoLit Race challenges were designed to align with MIL components such as searching for reliable information, evaluating sources, and ethical citation. In the Philippines, the Amazing Race format required teams to navigate physical library spaces and complete tasks related to resource location, fake news identification, and citation formatting. Observations indicated that participants who regularly used library resources completed tasks faster than those who did not. The activities were perceived as enjoyable, combining physical and intellectual challenges, and effectively taught MIL skills through experiential learning.
Evidence: "GBL enhances student participation and engagement, especially if they are shy to speak... It is observed that participants who regularly visit the library and use the libraries’ resources were able to finish most of the tasks faster than those who don’t." (Sections 4.1.1 and 4.2.2)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This project report by Yap and Peñaflor (2020) examines the implementation of game-based learning (GBL) and gamification strategies in two Asian academic libraries—Nazarbayev University in Kazakhstan and De La Salle University in the Philippines—to enhance students' Media and Information Literacy (MIL) skills. The authors argue that in an era characterized by disinformation and changing learner behaviors, traditional library instruction methods are insufficient for engaging post-millennial students. Consequently, libraries must adopt creative, interactive approaches to teach essential competencies such as information retrieval, critical evaluation, and ethical use of media.

The study utilizes an illustrative case study methodology to describe two specific initiatives: the "InfoLit Race" at Nazarbayev University and the "IamInfoSMART Amazing Race" at De La Salle University. Both programs are grounded in UNESCO’s Global Media and Information Literacy Assessment Framework, which categorizes MIL into three components: recognizing/searching for information, understanding/evaluating information, and creating/utilizing information ethically.

In Kazakhstan, Nazarbayev University integrated free online GBL tools, such as Mentimeter and Socrative, into its Information Literacy sessions starting in July 2018. These student-response systems allowed for anonymous participation, which the authors note was particularly beneficial for shy students who might otherwise hesitate to speak up. The InfoLit Race, introduced in 2018, involved a series of "pit stop" challenges designed to test specific MIL competencies. Tasks included arranging books by classification number, identifying plagiarism scenarios, and using library portals to find research information. The authors observed that these activities successfully increased student engagement and provided immediate feedback on knowledge consolidation.

In the Philippines, De La Salle University Libraries launched the IamInfoSMART campaign in 2013, culminating in an annual Amazing Race event during Library and Information Services Month. This event involved approximately eight teams of three students each (24 participants annually). The race required teams to navigate different sections of the library, completing tasks that mirrored real-world information challenges. These included locating specific media formats (DVDs, magazines, theses), conducting online scavenger hunts to identify library services and databases, and distinguishing between trustworthy sources and fake news. A notable challenge involved identifying satirical or biased articles, directly addressing the contemporary issue of misinformation. Another task required students to format citations correctly using APA or MLA styles, reinforcing ethical information use.

The findings from both cases suggest that GBL effectively bridges the gap between theoretical MIL concepts and practical application. Observations indicated that students who frequently utilized library resources completed tasks more efficiently than those who did not, suggesting that familiarity with library services is a key determinant of success in these games. The authors emphasize that while the games are competitive, they also foster collaboration and reduce the fear of failure associated with traditional assessments.

The implications for library practice are significant. Yap and Peñaflor conclude that gamification and problem-based learning offer a viable pathway to make MIL instruction more dynamic and relevant. By transforming passive learning into active participation, libraries can better equip students with the critical thinking skills necessary to navigate the complex digital information landscape. The report serves as a practical guide for other institutions looking to implement similar strategies, highlighting the importance of aligning game mechanics with specific learning outcomes to ensure educational value beyond mere entertainment.
