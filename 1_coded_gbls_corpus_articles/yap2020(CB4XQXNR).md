### Summary
# **Yap, J. M., & Peñaflor, J. (2020). The amazing library race: Developing students' media and information literacy skills through games. *Journal of Information Literacy, 14*(1), 66-82. https://doi.org/10.11645/14.1.2708**

Yap and Peñaflor present an illustrative case-study project report on how two academic libraries used game-based strategies to teach media and information literacy (MIL). The article begins from the problem of disinformation, misinformation, changing student learning behaviors, and the need for libraries to remain active champions of reliable information and lifelong learning. Their central claim is practical: games can be repurposed as interactive approaches that deliver information literacy instruction in a collaborative environment while also attracting students to library resources and services.

The authors distinguish gamification, game-based learning (GBL), serious games, and problem-based learning, while acknowledging that academic libraries often combine these approaches in orientation and instruction. They draw on the Amazing Library Race model as an upgraded scavenger-hunt format in which teams cannot proceed until they complete tasks tied to library spaces, resources, or competencies. Instead of treating play as decoration, the article maps race challenges onto UNESCO's three MIL components: recognizing information needs and accessing/retrieving information and media content; understanding, assessing, and evaluating information and media; and creating, using, and communicating information and media ethically.

The first case is Nazarbayev University Library in Kazakhstan, where the Library InfoLit Race was introduced in 2018. The race used pit-stop challenges to increase students' competencies in searching for reliable information, evaluating scholarly resources, citing and referencing ethically, and using library services. Sample tasks included arranging books by classification, identifying disciplines by class number, deciding when paraphrased text requires citation, assembling APA references, and using chat/reference services to locate research information through the library portal. The case also situates the race within a broader national and institutional MIL landscape that included UNESCO initiatives, IREX training, and Nazarbayev University's information literacy programming.

The second case is De La Salle University Libraries in the Philippines, where the IamInfoSMART campaign had developed since 2013. SMART stands for seeking the right information, managing retrieved information effectively, assessing information critically, referencing sources correctly, and treating/applying information ethically. The Amazing Race component led teams through library sections and pit-stop challenges around resource types, online library information, reference services, electronic databases, fake-news identification, citation games, and creative use of audiovisual collections. The article emphasizes that each task was designed to demonstrate a particular skill, not merely to entertain.

For the larger GBLS project, Yap and Peñaflor are useful because they show how relatively familiar library-game formats can be aligned with explicit MIL competencies across different national contexts. The race format combines physical movement, intellectual challenge, teamwork, clues, staff facilitation, and immediate task validation. It also demonstrates how library games can integrate orientation, database/resource discovery, classification, reference services, citation ethics, anti-plagiarism instruction, and misinformation evaluation in a single event structure. The cases therefore connect GBLS not only to engagement but to the public-service role of libraries as institutions that teach source evaluation and ethical information use.

The article's limits are also important. It is descriptive rather than an assessment study; evidence comes largely through practical examples and observations rather than pre/post learning data, comparison groups, or longitudinal transfer measures. Even so, it contributes implementation detail that is often missing from higher-level discussions of gamification. It names tasks, competencies, event structures, staff roles, and local adaptations, making it useful for GBLS design guidance. In the literature review, it should be treated as a concrete international academic-library example of problem-based, race-style MIL instruction rather than as proof that library races reliably improve learning outcomes.
