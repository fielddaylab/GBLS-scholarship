# Summary

This article presents a case study of "Nomad," an Alternate Reality Game (ARG) designed to teach and exercise twenty-first century literacies, particularly information and library literacies, among university students. The authors argue that traditional compulsory information literacy modules often fail to engage students due to a lack of perceived relevance. By leveraging the immersive, collaborative, and authentic nature of ARGs, the study aims to create an "authentic learning environment" where skills are exercised through gameplay rather than direct instruction.

The study was conducted at the University of Pretoria, targeting approximately 300 students across three modules. While 90 accounts were registered, only about 20 were active participants, with 11 participating in focus groups and questionnaires. The ARG involved players solving puzzles that required locating physical books in the library, decoding ciphers hidden within texts, solving riddles requiring critical thinking and internet search skills, and formatting academic references. These tasks were embedded in a narrative involving a time traveler named "The Nomad."

Data collection included non-participant observation, web analytics, document analysis, questionnaires, and focus groups. The findings indicate that players were highly engaged initially, rapidly completing the first puzzle, which suggested a baseline of existing library literacy. However, engagement varied across different puzzle types. Players struggled with the complexity of cipher decoding, leading to frustration and temporary abandonment of the task, though they did exercise library lending skills by borrowing books. The final puzzle, involving academic referencing, was criticized for being too obvious as an educational tool and lacking narrative integration, which reduced its motivational impact. Consequently, the game ended prematurely after six weeks, with only 65% of the planned content completed.

Despite these implementation challenges, qualitative data revealed that players found the experience enjoyable and effective. Participants reported that the ludic framing made traditional library tasks more engaging and helped contextualize information literacy skills. Players self-organized meetings at the library, utilized various technological platforms, and collaborated extensively to solve problems. The study concludes that ARGs hold significant potential for enhancing student engagement with library services and twenty-first century literacies. However, the authors highlight several design considerations for future implementations: diversifying tasks to prevent boredom, ensuring technical reliability of web-based components, providing transparency regarding deadlines and game structure, and designing for simplicity to avoid information overload. The article serves as a practical model for integrating game-based learning into library instruction, emphasizing the balance between educational objectives and player enjoyment.
