### Summary
# **Jerrett, A., Bothma, T. J. D., & de Beer, K. (2017). Exercising library and information literacies through alternate reality gaming. *Aslib Journal of Information Management, 69*(2), 230-254. https://doi.org/10.1108/AJIM-11-2016-0185**

Jerrett, Bothma, and de Beer report the design and case-study assessment of *Nomad*, an alternate reality game (ARG) created at the University of Pretoria to teach and exercise library, information, and twenty-first-century literacies. The article begins from a familiar instructional challenge: information literacy is required for academic success, but students may resist compulsory information literacy modules when they do not yet understand their relevance. The authors argue that games, and ARGs in particular, can turn abstract literacies into practical, situated activity.

The theoretical frame combines constructivist learning, game-based learning, and ARG theory. Because ARGs unfold across real-world spaces, multiple media, fragmented narratives, collaborative puzzle solving, and player-created meaning, the authors argue that the genre is especially well suited to information literacy. They draw on the Unified Metaliteracies Framework to describe ARG play as involving gathering, making sense, managing, solving, creating, respecting, and collaborating. In this view, an ARG does not merely deliver library facts; its form asks players to search, evaluate, organize, communicate, and solve problems together.

The target audience included about 300 students across two first-year modules and one third-year module at the University of Pretoria. The modules were selected because they already involved relevant skills: HTML, introductory information science and literacies, and game design/analysis. The game registered about 90 accounts, about 20 of which were active on forums. Eleven players participated in focus groups and questionnaires, divided by degree of activity. The authors are explicit that this was a qualitative case study, not a generalizable experiment. Data came from non-participant observation, document analysis, log-file analysis, questionnaires, focus groups, web analytics, and constant comparative analysis.

The narrative of *Nomad* centers on a mysterious time traveler and a missing character, with players helping through clues that interrupt classes, appear online, and lead to the university library. Major puzzles mapped game progress to concrete literacy tasks. The first puzzle required players to locate physical books through the library portal, collection numbers, QR codes, and notes hidden in books. The second required players to match ISBNs to books, borrow books, and decode ciphers embedded in texts. A riddle puzzle required critical thinking, search query construction, information dissemination, and collaboration. A later puzzle asked players to search academic journal databases and create references for the Nomad's research using the university's Harvard style.

The findings are mixed and therefore useful. Players completed the first book-location puzzle quickly, suggesting that they already had a strong baseline in using the library and library portal. The cipher puzzle slowed play and was eventually abandoned, although it did prompt students to borrow books and work with library materials. The riddle puzzle worked better, while the reference puzzle stalled because players found it too obvious, confusing, or weakly integrated into the story. The six-week game ended prematurely with about 65% of designed content completed, and the authors wrote an alternate ending to provide narrative closure.

Even with those problems, players stayed engaged. They self-organized meetings in the library, used forums and instant messaging, created private communication spaces, shared findings in a cloud folder, and reported that the game made traditional library tasks more meaningful. Players did not always consciously connect *Nomad* to information literacy modules, but they recognized the educational purpose when they noticed the repeated library setting and book-finding tasks. Some saw the hidden educational content as a strength: the story made library-skills practice feel like play rather than work.

The article's Table II is especially valuable for GBLS because it maps game tasks to literacies: finding books, decoding ciphers, academic referencing, constructing internet queries, searching the internet, using academic journal databases, using the library portal, moving across mobile and desktop platforms, communicating through social media, using blogs and forums, group problem solving, evaluating information relevance, rearranging information for context, and exploratory or tangential learning. This gives the review a concrete example of how ARG design can make information and library literacies operational.

For the GBLS project, Jerrett et al. contribute both promise and caution. The promise is that ARGs can make library space, catalog searching, source evaluation, collaboration, and referencing feel like meaningful actions inside a story, and can turn the library into a meeting point for peer learning. The caution is that educational ARGs are fragile. Designers should diversify locations and tasks, avoid excessive repetition, host web content reliably, make timelines and instructions transparent, keep the experience relatively simple and linear, support community recruitment without requiring a large player base, consider action-research designs with stronger outcome measures, and match running time to content. The study's limits include voluntary participation, a small active-player group, self-reported learning, lack of objective post-testing, incomplete gameplay, and local specificity. Still, it is one of the stronger GBLS sources on ARGs because it provides actual design details, literacy mappings, implementation problems, and player response.
