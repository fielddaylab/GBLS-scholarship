### Summary
# **Chen, C.-M., & Yang, Y.-C. (2024). A game-based augmented reality navigation system to support makerspace user education in a university library. *The Electronic Library, 42*(1), 78-101. https://doi.org/10.1108/EL-05-2023-0107**

Chen and Yang develop a game-based augmented reality navigation system (GARNS) for makerspace user education in a university library. The article begins from a service problem that is increasingly relevant to GBLS: library makerspaces can support creativity, interdisciplinary learning, practical skills, entrepreneurship, and hands-on collaboration, but their varied equipment and workflows can make user education labor-intensive for librarians. If every learner needs a full staff-guided tour or one-on-one explanation, makerspace services can become difficult to scale under ordinary staffing constraints.

The proposed GARNS combines augmented reality tools with game-based learning, the Octalysis gamification framework, and scaffolding theory. Its goal is to help learners navigate makerspace equipment, understand tasks, and build procedural knowledge through an immersive, autonomous learning environment. The study's framing is important for GBLS because the game layer is not simply decorative. It is meant to structure attention, sequence tasks, motivate exploration, and provide guidance inside a physical library makerspace where users need to find, evaluate, choose, and complete educational tasks.

The study used a true experimental research design with 24 grade 11 students from a high school in Keelung City, Taiwan. Ten students were randomly assigned to the GARNS experimental group, seven to a control group using a web navigation system, and seven to a control group using a narrative guided tour with a librarian. The comparison is useful because it sets the AR/game system against both a lower-tech self-guided tool and a familiar human-guided tour model. The article reports learning effectiveness and learning motivation outcomes, including value and expectation dimensions of motivation.

The reported findings are cautiously positive. Learners achieved significant learning effectiveness with all three approaches: GARNS, web navigation, and librarian-guided tour. There were no significant differences in learning effectiveness or motivation between GARNS and the librarian-guided tour, nor between the web navigation system and the librarian-guided tour. However, GARNS performed significantly better than the web navigation system on learning effectiveness and on value and expectation dimensions of learning motivation. The practical implication is not that AR should replace librarians, but that a carefully designed AR/game system may provide a scalable autonomous option that performs comparably to a guided tour and better than a flat web guide for some outcomes.

For the GBLS project, Chen and Yang extend the review's AR and orientation literature into makerspace user education. Their work differs from general library orientation because the learning object is not only where services are located but how to understand equipment, tasks, and procedural possibilities in a creative production environment. This makes the source relevant to library makerspaces, research/creative production, and emerging technology service models. It also supports the principle that game form should match service goal: AR navigation is most persuasive when users need situated, in-place guidance tied to physical objects and spaces.

The source also adds a staffing and sustainability argument. If makerspace services depend heavily on repeated tours or individualized explanations, staff workload can limit access. A GARNS-style system may help standardize introductory instruction, reduce repetitive explanation, and give users a more flexible way to revisit guidance. At the same time, the study's small sample and the lack of clean full-text extraction here require caution. The article should be used as promising evidence for AR-supported makerspace education, not as definitive proof that AR/game navigation is broadly superior to librarian instruction.
