## Purpose
To develop and evaluate a game-based augmented reality (AR) navigation system (Mini-Universe) for makerspace user education in a university library, comparing its learning effectiveness against a web-based navigation system and a traditional librarian-guided tour. The study aims to determine if gamified AR tools can enhance student engagement, motivation, and knowledge retention regarding makerspace equipment usage.
Evidence: "Therefore the purpose of this study is to develop a game based augmented reality navigation system (ARNS) that combines an alternative reality puzzle game and AR tools to support makerspace user education in a university library... and to compare the difference in learning effectiveness of learners among the use of ARNS web navigation system and narrative guided tour with librarian for makerspace user education"
Confidence: high

## Method
A quasi-experimental design involving three groups of participants randomly assigned to different educational interventions. The experimental group used a custom-developed AR puzzle game ("Mini-Universe") created using Mini-Universe software, which utilized cognitive scaffolding and real-time feedback mechanisms. Control Group 1 used a web-based navigation system designed on the Google platform. Control Group 2 participated in a traditional narrative guided tour led by a librarian. Learning effectiveness was measured using pre-tests and post-tests consisting of knowledge-based, combination, and sorting questions about four types of makerspace machines (3D printer, stereolithography, thermal transfer printer, laser cutter machine). Statistical analysis included Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for within-group improvements and Kruskal-Wallis tests with Conover’s post-hoc multiple comparison tests for between-group differences. Semi-structured interviews were conducted to gather qualitative feedback.
Evidence: "In this study a total of 36 research participants were randomly assigned to either the experimental group supported by ARNS, the control group 1 supported by a web navigation system or the control group 2 supported by a librarian with a narrated tour... The test content is presented in a problem solving manner... This study conducted pretests and posttests using this test paper before and after the experimental activities to evaluate learners’ prior knowledge and learning effectiveness."

## Population and Data
The participants were 36 first-year graduate students from a high school in Jeelung City, Taiwan. All participants were first-time users of the library’s makerspace. The sample was divided into three groups: Experimental Group (n=12), Control Group 1 (Web Navigation, n=12), and Control Group 2 (Librarian Tour, n=12). Data included quantitative test scores (pre-test and post-test) and qualitative interview responses regarding user experience, engagement, and perceived learning outcomes.

## Findings
Quantitative results indicated that all three groups showed significant improvement from pre-test to post-test scores. However, when comparing between groups, there was no significant difference in learning effectiveness between the ARNS group and the traditional librarian-guided tour group (p > 0.05). Similarly, there was no significant difference between the web navigation group and the librarian-guided tour group. Crucially, a significant difference was found between the ARNS group and the web navigation group (p < 0.05), with the ARNS group demonstrating significantly better learning outcomes than the web-based system. Qualitative interviews revealed that participants in the ARNS group appreciated the continuous judgment of task selection, the challenge elements, and the peer competition inherent in the game design, which improved focus and memory retention. Participants in the web navigation group found the content convenient but noted excessive text led to impatience and easy forgetting. The librarian tour group valued the direct explanation but noted limitations in understanding detailed machine functions due to reliance on oral explanation alone.
Evidence: "The test results showed that there were significant differences in learning effectiveness among the three groups of learners... Further analysis with Conover’s post hoc multiple comparison test showed that there was no significant difference between the ARNS group and the narrative guided tour with librarian group... and between the web navigation system group and the narrative guided tour with librarian group... However there was a significant difference between the ARNS group and the web navigation system group... The ARNS group was significantly better than the web navigation system group."

# Summary

This study investigates the efficacy of game-based augmented reality (AR) tools in facilitating user education within library makerspaces. As libraries evolve into creative hubs providing access to advanced technologies like 3D printers and laser cutters, traditional instructional methods often struggle to engage users or ensure retention of complex operational knowledge. The authors argue that while web-based tutorials and librarian-led tours are common, they may lack the interactivity required for deep learning. Consequently, this research develops and evaluates a "Game-Based AR Navigation System" (ARNS) called Mini-Universe, designed to teach makerspace equipment usage through an alternative reality puzzle game format.

The methodology employed a quasi-experimental design with 36 first-year graduate students from a high school in Taiwan, all of whom were novice users of the library’s makerspace. Participants were randomly assigned to one of three groups: (1) an experimental group using the ARNS via handheld mobile devices; (2) a control group using a web-based navigation system; and (3) a control group participating in a traditional narrative guided tour led by a librarian. The ARNS was developed using Mini-Universe software, leveraging cognitive scaffolding theory. It required users to scan physical "stickers" placed on makerspace equipment to collect clues, solve puzzles, and complete missions related to machine operation. This gamified approach included elements of challenge, peer competition, and immediate feedback via an "energy stone" mechanic that prevented random guessing and encouraged careful analysis of information.

Learning effectiveness was assessed using pre-tests and post-tests comprising knowledge-based, combination, and sorting questions regarding four specific machines: 3D printers, stereolithography units, thermal transfer printers, and laser cutter machines. Statistical analyses included Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for within-group changes and Kruskal-Wallis tests with Conover’s post-hoc comparisons for between-group differences.

The quantitative findings revealed that all three groups demonstrated significant improvement in learning outcomes from pre-test to post-test, indicating that each method was effective in transmitting basic knowledge. However, comparative analysis showed no statistically significant difference in learning effectiveness between the ARNS group and the traditional librarian-guided tour group, nor between the web navigation group and the librarian group. Crucially, the ARNS group performed significantly better than the web navigation group (p < 0.05). This suggests that while gamified AR is not superior to direct human instruction in this context, it is markedly more effective than passive, text-heavy digital tutorials.

Qualitative interviews supplemented these findings. Participants in the ARNS group reported higher engagement due to the game’s challenge elements and the necessity of actively analyzing clues rather than passively reading text. They noted that the process of combining stickers and solving puzzles helped them retain information about machine functions more effectively. In contrast, users of the web-based system found the content convenient but criticized the excessive amount of text, which led to impatience and poor retention. The librarian tour group appreciated the direct interaction but acknowledged that oral explanations alone made it difficult to grasp detailed technical specifications of each machine.

The study concludes that game-based AR is a valuable tool for makerspace education, particularly in overcoming the limitations of web-based tutorials. The authors recommend a blended learning approach: using web resources or librarian tours for initial orientation and basic familiarity, followed by the ARNS for interactive, deep-dive learning of equipment operations. This hybrid model aims to balance convenience with engagement, ensuring users are both informed and motivated to utilize makerspace resources effectively. Future research directions include enhancing the AR system with 3D object modeling rather than static images to further improve immersion and detail retention.
