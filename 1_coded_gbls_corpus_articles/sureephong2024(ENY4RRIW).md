## Purpose
The study aims to investigate the potential of a Gamified Metaverse platform for promoting library services by comparing its effectiveness against traditional library programs in terms of knowledge acquisition and reduction of library anxiety. It also seeks to understand student perceptions regarding the usefulness of gamification within this virtual environment.

## Method
The researchers employed an explanatory sequential mixed-methods design, specifically a randomized controlled trial. Participants were randomly assigned to either a Metaverse-based group or a traditional library program group. Quantitative data were collected via pre- and post-tests measuring library knowledge and anxiety levels, analyzed using paired sample t-tests and Cohen’s d for effect size. Qualitative data were gathered through open-ended questionnaires administered only to the Metaverse group to assess perceptions of usefulness and engagement.

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 80 first-year university students from Chiang Mai University who had no prior experience using the university library. The participants included 31 males (38.75%) and 49 females (61.25%). They were divided into two groups of 40: a control group participating in a traditional library orientation led by staff, and an experimental group engaging with a digital twin of the library in a Unity-based Metaverse environment using Meta Quest 2 VR headsets. The Metaverse platform featured gamified elements such as leaderboards, points, levels, and badges across four services: Library Orientation, Research Support, Book Borrowing, and Technology Services.

## Findings
Quantitative results indicated that both the traditional and Metaverse-based programs significantly improved participants' knowledge acquisition, with no statistically significant difference between the two groups (p = .677; Cohen’s d = .244). Regarding library anxiety, the traditional program showed significant reductions in four out of five anxiety factors, including comfort asking librarians for help. The Metaverse program only significantly reduced anxiety related to feeling overwhelmed and utilizing resources; it did not significantly reduce anxiety regarding interaction with staff or comfort levels. Qualitative data revealed that students found the Gamified Metaverse engaging and motivating due to competitive elements like leaderboards. However, participants noted that the virtual librarians were non-player characters (NPCs) with predefined responses, which failed to provide the empathy and personalized support of human staff, thereby limiting the platform's ability to reduce social anxiety related to library personnel. Some users also reported physical side effects from VR use, such as nausea and dizziness.
Evidence: "However, the t-test conducted to compare the pre–post differences between the two groups was not significant (p = .677)... In contrast, the Metaverse-based library program group demonstrated significant improvements in anxiety levels specifically related to feeling overwhelmed in libraries and utilizing library services and resources... Participants expressed that the virtual librarian, being just an NPC... was unable to effectively reduce barriers with the library staff." (Data Analysis and Results; Discussion)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that while the Gamified Metaverse is effective for knowledge acquisition and engaging students through gamification, it is less effective than traditional methods for reducing overall library anxiety, particularly concerning interpersonal interactions with staff. Due to high development costs, technical requirements, and potential VR side effects, the platform is recommended as a supplementary tool rather than a replacement for traditional services, potentially benefiting specific student populations or larger libraries with sufficient resources. Future research should explore long-term impacts and the integration of AI-powered assistants to improve virtual librarian interactions.
Evidence: "It should be noted that the platform may have limitations such as high costs and potential side effects of virtual reality, making it more suitable as an additional tool for promoting library services... exploring the potential integration of AI-powered chatbots or virtual assistants to enhance librarian-student interaction is an interesting avenue for future research." (Abstract; Future Research and Limitations)

# Summary

This study by Sureephong et al. (2024) evaluates the efficacy of a Gamified Metaverse platform in enhancing library services, specifically focusing on knowledge acquisition and the mitigation of library anxiety among university students. The research addresses the growing need for libraries to adapt to digital engagement strategies while addressing student apprehensions related to library use.

The methodology employed an explanatory sequential mixed-methods design involving a randomized controlled trial with 80 first-year students at Chiang Mai University who had no prior library experience. Participants were randomly assigned to either a traditional library orientation group (n=40) or a Metaverse-based experimental group (n=40). The experimental group utilized Meta Quest 2 VR headsets to interact with a digital twin of the university’s humanities library, developed using Unity and photogrammetry. This virtual environment incorporated gamification elements such as points, leaderboards, levels, and badges across four service areas: orientation, research support, book borrowing, and technology services. The control group participated in a standard physical tour led by librarians. Data were collected via pre- and post-tests measuring library knowledge and anxiety (using a modified Bostick scale), followed by open-ended qualitative surveys for the Metaverse group.

Quantitative findings revealed that both interventions significantly improved participants' knowledge of library resources and services. However, statistical analysis showed no significant difference in knowledge acquisition between the traditional and Metaverse groups (p = .677; Cohen’s d = .244), indicating that the immersive virtual environment did not offer a superior advantage over traditional instruction for learning factual library information.

Regarding library anxiety, the results diverged significantly. The traditional program demonstrated significant reductions in four of five anxiety dimensions, including comfort in asking librarians for help and feeling welcome in the library space. In contrast, the Metaverse program only significantly reduced anxiety related to feeling overwhelmed by the physical space and utilizing resources. It failed to significantly reduce anxiety associated with interacting with staff or feeling comfortable in the library environment. Qualitative data clarified this discrepancy: students reported that while the gamified elements (leaderboards, quests) were engaging and motivating, the virtual librains were non-player characters (NPCs) with limited, predefined responses. Participants felt these avatars lacked empathy and personal touch, failing to alleviate social anxiety or build confidence in seeking human assistance. Additionally, some users experienced physical discomfort, such as nausea and dizziness, attributed to VR usage.

The authors conclude that while the Gamified Metaverse is a viable tool for engaging students and facilitating knowledge transfer, it is not a substitute for traditional library orientations when the goal is reducing interpersonal anxiety. The high costs of development and hardware, combined with potential health side effects, suggest that such platforms are best utilized as supplementary tools for specific demographics or larger institutions. The study highlights the critical role of human interaction in alleviating library anxiety, suggesting that future iterations should integrate AI-driven assistants capable of more dynamic and empathetic communication to bridge the gap between virtual immersion and social support.
