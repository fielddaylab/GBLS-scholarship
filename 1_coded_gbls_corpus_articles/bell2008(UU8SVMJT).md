### Summary
# **Bell, L., & Trueman, R. B. (Eds.). (2008). *Virtual worlds, real libraries: Librarians and educators in Second Life and other multi-user virtual environments*. Information Today.**

Bell and Trueman's edited volume is an early snapshot of library and education projects in Second Life and other multi-user virtual environments (MUVEs). The available Zotero PDF is a two-page published book review, not the full 272-page volume, and the Zotero notes provide the table of contents. This entry therefore treats the source as a map of an important early professional conversation rather than as chapter-level evidence. Even in that bounded form, the book is directly relevant to GBLS because it documents librarians experimenting with services inside game-like networked environments where avatars, place, presence, simulation, and community shaped what library work could look like.

The review frames the volume as the first text aimed at helping librarians participate in Second Life and other MUVEs. Its audience included school, public, academic, research-library, and library-school contexts. The book was organized into three broad sections: virtual worlds and libraries; the Alliance Virtual Library Project; and virtual worlds and education. The table of contents points to chapters on library, education, and museum applications for children, tweens, and teens; massively multiplayer online worlds; edutainment before Second Life; the Alliance Virtual Library Project; management in virtual environments; reference services in a virtual world; collections in virtual environments; virtual branch libraries; Teen Second Life; HealthInfo Island; speculative fiction and mystery programming; historical environments such as Renaissance Island and Land of Lincoln; and instructional uses of Second Life for distance learning, research help, and MLIS teaching.

For GBLS, the strongest contribution is the way the volume broadens "game-based library service" beyond discrete games or programs. The review describes virtual-world library projects as service environments: librarians created branch libraries, reference desks, collections, author programs, book discussions, displays, health-information islands, historical simulations, teen spaces, and classroom extensions inside virtual worlds. This matters because the game-like world is not merely a wrapper around a lesson. It becomes the service site itself, with spatial design, avatar etiquette, in-world objects, collaboration, and a felt sense of presence shaping how patrons encounter librarians, collections, and programs.

The review also surfaces operational tensions that remain relevant for immersive GBLS. Virtual worlds can reduce some barriers of traditional virtual reference, such as depersonalization and channel loss, by giving users embodied presence and shared space. At the same time, they introduce social challenges, including the perception that the environment is "only a game," institutional challenges such as funding and staffing, and personal or behavioral challenges such as etiquette and disruption. The reviewer also notes hardware and server limitations for youth programs, the steep learning curve for novice librarians, and the difficulty of documenting fast-changing virtual platforms in a static book.

Several service areas named in the review connect directly to the current lit review. HealthInfo Island shows virtual worlds as a platform for consumer-health outreach and partnership with medical librarians and health professionals. Active Worlds and Appalachian State University's Information Gardens show virtual worlds as distance-learning library infrastructure. Teen Second Life and library, education, and museum projects show how youth services can experiment with virtual worlds, while also needing attention to platform suitability, technical requirements, and safety. The book's education chapters suggest immersive learning scenarios, such as replicas of historic sites, where three-dimensional virtual presence may support instruction in ways a flat webpage cannot.

The source should be cited carefully. Because the attached PDF is a review by Elisheba Muturi rather than the full edited book, it does not provide enough evidence to evaluate individual chapters or outcomes. Its best use in the GBLS review is to mark an early professional moment when librarians treated Second Life and related MUVEs as real service venues, not just recreational curiosities. It also helps connect later VR, AR, metaverse, and virtual-platform sources to older library experiments with avatar-based reference, in-world collections, virtual branches, health outreach, youth environments, and distance-learning support.
