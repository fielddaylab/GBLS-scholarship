# Summary

This document is a book review of *Virtual Worlds, Real Libraries: Librarians and Educators in Second Life and Other Multi-User Virtual Environments*, edited by Lori Bell and Rhonda B. Trueman (2008). The review, authored by Elisheba Muturi, evaluates the volume’s utility for librarians considering engagement with multi-user virtual environments (MUVEs), particularly Second Life. The reviewed text is positioned as the first resource aimed at helping librarians navigate these spaces, targeting a broad audience including school, public, university, and library school professionals.

The review outlines the book’s structure, which comprises three main sections: "Virtual Worlds and Libraries," "Alliance Virtual Library Project," and "Virtual Worlds and Education." The reviewer notes that the book assumes no prior technical knowledge, providing a glossary and lay-term descriptions of virtual environments, though it is not intended as a technical primer. A significant strength identified is the inclusion of 24 contributors who share personal experiences and lessons learned, offering concrete examples of library projects in virtual spaces.

Key findings from the review highlight specific content within the book. The first section examines MUVEs for "edutainment," with particular attention to youth services. Marty Grover’s chapter is cited for critically reviewing platforms suitable for Library, Education, and Museum (LEM) applications, while Kelly Czarnecki’s work supplements this by discussing activities for teens. The reviewer points out important technological constraints mentioned in the text, such as high video-resolution requirements and server limitations for multiple avatars, which are relevant for public library planning.

The second section focuses on the Alliance Virtual Library Project in Second Life. The reviewer finds these chapters practically significant for core library functions like reference, management, and collection development. Specific challenges in virtual reference services are detailed, categorized into social barriers (the perception that virtual worlds are merely games), institutional barriers (lack of funding and staff support), and personal barriers (disruptive patron behavior). Additionally, the review highlights Guus van Den Brekel and Carol Perryman’s account of developing clinical medical libraries on HealthInfo Island, funded by the National Library of Medicine, demonstrating innovative health outreach.

The third section addresses educational applications, focusing on immersive learning. The review notes Barbara Galik’s guidance on justifying virtual world presence in academic contexts and Geraldine Purpur and Louise Ochoa’s discussion of usability studies for distance learning. S. Thompson’s chapter is highlighted for identifying learning scenarios, such as historic site replicas, that benefit from 3D simulation.

The review identifies three overarching themes in the book: first, that virtual world initiatives were largely driven by individual librarians acting on their own time rather than through institutional mandates; second, that virtual worlds effectively promote traditional services like book discussions and author programs; and third, that these platforms facilitate unique collaborations among librarians, faculty, and external partners.

Critically, the reviewer questions the efficacy of a static print text for documenting rapidly evolving technologies, suggesting that companion websites and blogs may be more suitable for ongoing knowledge sharing. Despite this limitation, the review concludes that *Virtual Worlds, Real Libraries* is "required reading" for those considering virtual presence, offering a balanced perspective on both the opportunities and challenges inherent in MUVEs. The text serves as an inspirational resource, showcasing the creativity and commitment of early adopters in the field of games-based library services.
