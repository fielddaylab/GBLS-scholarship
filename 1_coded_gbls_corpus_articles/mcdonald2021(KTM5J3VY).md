## Findings
The study found that game development artifacts are highly variable and lack standardization, leading to "organizational entropy." Key challenges identified include: (1) Creators face issues with copyright, proprietary technology, lack of consistent naming conventions, and prioritizing storage space over organization; (2) Information professionals struggle with a lack of contextual information, media obsolescence, legal hurdles regarding intellectual property, and siloed organizational practices; (3) Game researchers encounter difficulties in searching databases due to poor finding aids and restricted access. The authors developed a taxonomy containing 123 terms organized into three main sections: Development, Organization-Related Materials, and Marketing.

## Implications
The authors recommend implementing the developed taxonomy and adopting consistent naming conventions and controlled vocabularies to streamline description and access. They advocate for stronger collaborative relationships between game companies and cultural heritage institutions, including "participatory archiving" where creators assist in organizing collections. Best practices include addressing copyright concerns through onsite-only access terminals and improving interoperability between institutional catalogs to reduce siloed practices.

# Summary

This research article addresses the critical need for better organization, description, and preservation of video game development artifacts within cultural heritage institutions. While final published games have increasingly been archived, the associated development materials—such as design documents, source code, test builds, and marketing assets—remain poorly understood and organized. The authors argue that these artifacts provide essential context for understanding the history and creative intent behind video games, yet they face significant risks of loss due to negligence, media obsolescence, and a lack of standardized descriptive practices.

To address this gap, the study employed a multimethod approach involving 29 semi-structured interviews with stakeholders (12 creators, 11 information professionals, and 6 game researchers) and an examination of five distinct video game artifact collections held by institutions such as The Strong National Museum of Play and Living Computers: Museum + Labs. The interview data were inductively coded to identify stakeholder needs, challenges, and values, while the collection examinations provided empirical grounding for artifact classification.

The findings reveal widespread disorganization across the game development lifecycle. Creators often prioritize rapid production over documentation, leading to inconsistent naming conventions and a reliance on cheap storage solutions rather than robust organizational structures. Legal concerns regarding copyright, proprietary technology, and nondisclosure agreements further complicate preservation efforts. Information professionals face significant hurdles in processing these collections due to a lack of contextual metadata, the complexity of mixed physical and digital formats, and siloed institutional practices that hinder interoperability. Game researchers reported difficulties in locating relevant materials due to inadequate finding aids and restricted access policies.

In response to these challenges, the authors developed a comprehensive taxonomy of video game development artifacts comprising 123 terms. The taxonomy is organized into three primary categories: Development (including design, engineering, quality assurance, art, audio, writing, and production), Organization-Related Materials, and Marketing. This framework was iteratively refined through collaborative review with stakeholders and tested in a graduate-level metadata course. The study also outlines best practices for describing and maintaining these artifacts, emphasizing the importance of controlled vocabularies, consistent file naming, and participatory archiving processes where creators assist information professionals in contextualizing materials.

The authors conclude that while their taxonomy offers a significant step forward, broader systemic changes are required within the game industry to prioritize documentation. They highlight limitations in the study, noting that the sample was drawn exclusively from North American contexts and may not fully represent all roles in game development or more recent digital-native collections. Future work will involve further evaluation of the taxonomy with diverse stakeholders and exploration of similar preservation challenges in other digital media domains. This research contributes directly to Games-Based Library Services by providing a structured framework for libraries and archives to better ingest, describe, and provide access to the complex materials that underpin video game history and education.
