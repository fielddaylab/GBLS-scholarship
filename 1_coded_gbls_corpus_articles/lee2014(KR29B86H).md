## Purpose
The article aims to develop a robust, multi-dimensional faceted classification scheme for video game genres to improve intellectual access and organization. It seeks to address the limitations of current library and commercial systems, which often rely on unstandardized, undefined, or overly simplistic genre labels that fail to capture the complexity of video games. The authors specifically investigate two research questions: (1) What types of information are represented in current genre labels? and (2) What facets and foci can systematically describe this embedded information?

## Method
The study employs facet analysis, a method derived from library classification theory (Ranganathan, Spiteri), involving two main phases: Analysis and Synthesis. In the Analysis phase, the authors conducted a domain analysis by collecting empirical genre data from scholarly literature, commercial websites (e.g., Amazon, GameSpot, Mobygames), and online directories. They gathered 804 instances of genre labels, eliminated duplicates and non-applicable terms, and used card sorting to organize these labels into homogenous, mutually exclusive categories. These categories were then defined as "facets" based on seven guiding principles (differentiation, relevance, ascertainability, permanence, homogeneity, mutual exclusivity, and fundamental categories). In the Synthesis phase, the authors refined the indexing terms ("foci") by testing them against sample games to ensure clarity, consistency, and user warrant. Domain experts from the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) were consulted to evaluate term familiarity and appropriateness.

## Findings
The authors identified 12 distinct facets, each representing a different characteristic of division in video games, containing a total of 358 foci (indexing terms). The facets are: Gameplay (10 foci, e.g., Action, RPG), Style (100 foci, sub-facet of Gameplay, e.g., Platformer, Stealth), Purpose (7 foci, e.g., Education, Entertainment), Target Audience (18 foci, based on rating systems), Presentation (10 foci, e.g., 2D, Side-scrolling), Artistic Style (9 foci, e.g., Anime/Manga, Realistic), Temporal Aspect (7 foci, e.g., Real-time, Turn-based), Point-of-View (4 foci), Theme (22 parent/127 child terms, e.g., Fantasy, War), Setting (16 spatial/8 temporal foci, e.g., Urban, Medieval), Mood/Affect (15 foci, e.g., Horror, Humorous), and Type of Ending (5 foci, e.g., Branching, Infinite). The study found that current genre labels are heavily overloaded with multiple dimensions of information. The proposed scheme allows for multi-dimensional description, enabling better collocation of similar games. For example, *Super Mario Bros.* is classified under Gameplay: Action/Platformer, Presentation: 2D/Side-scrolling, and Artistic Style: Retro.

# Summary

Lee et al. (2014) address the critical need for standardized, multi-dimensional metadata to improve access to video games in library and information science contexts. The authors argue that current genre descriptors are unstandardized, undefined, and often conflate multiple information dimensions, leading to poor searchability and browsability. Existing systems, whether based on Library of Congress Subject Headings (which offer only five generic game headings) or commercial websites (which rely on inconsistent user-generated tags), fail to capture the complexity of video games as interactive media. To resolve this, the study aims to develop a faceted classification scheme that systematically represents the various characteristics embedded in video game genres.

The methodology employed is facet analysis, a technique rooted in library classification theory that divides a subject field into fundamental categories (facets) and their specific instances (foci). The authors conducted a domain analysis by collecting 804 genre labels from diverse sources, including commercial sites (Amazon, GameSpot), fan communities (Mobygames, Giantbomb), and scholarly literature. After removing duplicates and non-applicable terms, they utilized card sorting to organize these labels into homogenous categories. These categories were refined into 12 facets based on principles of mutual exclusivity, relevance, and ascertainability. The resulting scheme was tested against a small sample of diverse games (e.g., *Super Mario Bros.*, *Plants vs. Zombies*, *Persona 3*) to validate the applicability and clarity of the indexing terms. Domain experts from the Seattle Interactive Media Museum provided feedback on term familiarity.

The study identifies 12 facets comprising 358 foci. The primary facet is **Gameplay**, which includes ten core genres such as Action, RPG, Strategy, and Simulation. A sub-facet, **Style**, provides more granular distinctions (e.g., Platformer, Stealth). Other significant facets include **Purpose** (e.g., Education, Entertainment, Exercise), **Target Audience** (derived from rating boards like ESRB), **Presentation** (technical display modes like 2D, Side-scrolling), and **Artistic Style** (aesthetic qualities like Anime/Manga, Realistic). The scheme also incorporates **Temporal Aspect** (e.g., Real-time, Turn-based), **Point-of-View**, **Theme** (with 127 sub-themes under 22 categories), **Setting** (spatial and temporal environments), **Mood/Affect** (emotional tone), and **Type of Ending** (e.g., Branching, Infinite).

The findings demonstrate that video game genres are inherently multi-dimensional. For instance, a single game can be described simultaneously by its gameplay mechanics, artistic aesthetic, narrative theme, and emotional tone. The proposed scheme allows for this complexity by enabling the combination of foci from different facets to create precise compound descriptors. This approach facilitates better collocation of similar games that might otherwise be separated in hierarchical systems.

The authors acknowledge limitations, including the inevitable incompleteness of the scheme due to the evolving nature of video games and the potential ambiguity of certain terms for non-expert users. They note that some facets, such as Setting or Mood, may not be relevant to all games (e.g., simple puzzle games). Future work involves expanding the corpus of tested games, refining definitions for all foci, and conducting usability studies with gamers to assess the practical utility of the scheme. Ultimately, this research contributes to the development of a comprehensive metadata schema for video games, aiming to enhance discovery and access for diverse stakeholders, including librarians, educators, and players.
