They find that successful collections require dedicated game laboratories for hardware maintenance and access, as physical media are fragile and formats vary widely (cartridges, tapes, discs). The CVGA case study demonstrates that an "open play" model, rather than a restricted "white glove" approach, facilitates discovery and research, despite increased wear on equipment. # Summary

Wood and Carter (2018) examine the integration of video games into university library collections, framing them as significant cultural objects requiring rigorous archival and curatorial practices. The article begins by contextualizing the historical trajectory of video games from early experimental programs like *Spacewar!* to contemporary media, highlighting the parallel evolution of computer technology and game design. The authors argue that libraries face unique challenges in managing these "transmedial" artifacts, which exist across various hardware platforms and formats, complicating traditional cataloging efforts.

The literature review component identifies key issues in collection development, including the lack of consistent terminology in Library of Congress standards and the rapid obsolescence of technology. The authors cite previous studies indicating that successful video game collections require more than just software; they necessitate the preservation of hardware (consoles, controllers) to ensure playability. Selection criteria discussed in the literature range from technical merit and aesthetic quality to historical significance and educational utility. The authors note a shift in some libraries from casual entertainment-focused collections to intentional archives supporting interdisciplinary research in fields such as art history, sociology, and computer science.

Central to the article is a case study of the University of Michigan’s Computer and Video Game Archive (CVGA), established in 2008. The CVGA houses approximately 8,000 titles across seventy systems, spanning from the early 1970s to the present. Unlike traditional archives that restrict access to preserve materials, the CVGA employs an "open play" model, allowing users to interact with the games on-site at twenty gaming stations. This approach prioritizes experiential learning and discovery over strict preservation, acknowledging that wear and tear are inevitable but manageable through repair techniques and replacement caches. In 2017, the archive recorded nearly 5,000 visitors and over 16,000 item usages, demonstrating high engagement from both academic and public users.

The authors detail how the CVGA supports humanities research by providing access to games as primary texts. Examples include analyses of race in *FIFA* games, historical trauma in Civil War-themed games, and music theory through video game soundtracks. The archive also facilitates creative pedagogy, such as using games to teach English language skills or studying the effects of multitasking via racing games. The authors emphasize that video games are increasingly recognized as art forms, citing exhibitions at institutions like the Smithsonian American Art Museum.

In their implications for library practice, Wood and Carter advocate for a more robust integration of video games into humanities infrastructure. They propose that librarians collaborate with visual resources centers to create image databases containing high-quality captures of game visuals, analogous to film stills or art reproductions. This would require establishing clear metadata standards and leveraging fair use guidelines similar to those used in the visual arts. The article concludes that university libraries play a crucial role in shaping the discipline of game studies by providing the necessary technological infrastructure and interdisciplinary resources to support cutting-edge research and pedagogy.
