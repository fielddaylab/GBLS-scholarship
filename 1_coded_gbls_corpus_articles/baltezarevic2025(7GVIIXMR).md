Specifically, it seeks to assist educators, legislators, and game creators in recognizing the value of DGBL to enhance student requirements and academic outcomes. The research tests four hypotheses concerning learning efficiency, motivation/participation, the role of rewards/feedback/competition, and AI-driven personalization.

## Findings
All four hypotheses were confirmed with statistically significant results (p < 0.01). 
1. H0: Strong positive correlation between digital gaming as part of learning content and learning effectiveness (Pearson’s r = 0.571; Spearman’s rho = 0.563).
2. H1: Strong positive correlation between DGBL providing a dynamic/engaging environment and increased student motivation (Pearson’s r = 0.624; Spearman’s rho = 0.600).
3. H2: Strong positive correlation between DGBL including rewards/feedback/competition and improved learning outcomes (Pearson’s r = 0.445; Spearman’s rho = 0.450).
4. H3: Strong positive correlation between AI-supported DGBL enabling personalization and dynamic adaptation to student performance (Pearson’s r = 0.582; Spearman’s rho = 0.577).
The scale demonstrated high internal consistency (Cronbach’s alpha = .913).

# Summary

This article by Baltezarević and Baltezarević (2025) investigates the perceived effects of Digital Game-Based Learning (DGBL) on students' academic performance. The primary objective is to gather empirical data on stakeholder attitudes toward DGBL to inform educators, policymakers, and game developers. The study operates under four hypotheses: that combining digital games with educational content increases learning efficiency; that dynamic DGBL environments boost motivation and participation; that incorporating rewards, feedback, and competition improves learning outcomes; and that AI-supported personalization allows for dynamic adaptation to individual student performance.

The methodology involves a quantitative survey distributed via email to 350 individuals at Megatrend University in Belgrade, Serbia. The final valid sample comprised 328 respondents, including students across various study levels, faculty members, and administrative staff. The demographic profile of the participants was predominantly male (56.7%) and aged between 18 and 35 years (58% combined). Educational attainment varied, with significant representation from those holding high school/college degrees (41.5%) and master’s degrees (20.4%). Data were analyzed using IBM SPSS Statistics, employing descriptive statistics, Pearson’s and Spearman’s correlation coefficients, Chi-square tests, and Cronbach’s alpha for reliability assessment. The instrument used a five-point Likert scale, demonstrating high internal consistency (α = .913).

The findings confirm all four hypotheses with statistical significance (p < 0.01). First, there is a strong positive correlation between the integration of digital games into learning content and perceived learning effectiveness (Pearson’s r = 0.571). Second, participants strongly associate dynamic and engaging DGBL environments with increased student motivation and participation (Pearson’s r = 0.624). Third, the inclusion of game mechanics such as rewards, feedback, and competition is positively correlated with improved learning outcomes (Pearson’s r = 0.445). Fourth, AI-enabled personalization in DGBL is strongly linked to the dynamic adaptation of learning materials to individual student performance (Pearson’s r = 0.582).

The discussion contextualizes these findings within broader literature, noting that DGBL offers immediate feedback, fosters collaboration, and enhances problem-solving skills compared to traditional instruction. However, the authors acknowledge critical limitations and challenges. These include the potential for digital game addiction, cybersecurity risks (such as phishing and data breaches), high resource costs for implementation, and the risk of distraction if games are not properly monitored. Furthermore, the study relies on self-reported attitudes rather than direct measures of academic performance, limiting causal inference regarding actual learning gains.

The implications suggest that DGBL holds significant potential for enhancing educational engagement and efficiency, particularly when supported by AI for personalization. The authors recommend future research explore immersive technologies like Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR), including haptic interfaces, to deepen student involvement. They also emphasize the need for robust cybersecurity measures and pedagogical alignment to mitigate risks. While the study provides strong evidence of positive stakeholder perceptions regarding DGBL, it calls for further empirical investigation into long-term impacts and broader demographic applications to fully realize the approach's potential in educational settings.
