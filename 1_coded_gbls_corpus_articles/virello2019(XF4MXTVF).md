Specifically, it provides a case study on transforming a standard Free Comic Book Day (FCBD) event into a full-scale "Library Comic Con" to provide patrons with experiences they might not otherwise afford or access.

## Findings
The author reports significant increases in attendance and engagement as the program evolved from passive activities to interactive, immersive experiences involving live-action characters, hands-on stations, and community partnerships. Key findings include:
1. **Engagement through Immersion:** Incorporating live cosplayers and interactive games (e.g., "Kryptonite Disposal," scavenger hunts) significantly increased participation compared to passive crafts.
2. **Community Partnerships:** Collaborating with local comic shops, media outlets, Boy Scout troops, and other convention organizers provided free publicity, donations, and resources, reducing financial burden.
3. **Safety and Policy Management:** Successful large-scale events required strict adherence to safety protocols, including traffic control via police assistance, clear dress codes regarding costumes and props, and monitoring of attendee numbers for fire code compliance.
4. **Staff Involvement:** Leveraging in-house staff talent and creativity was essential for stretching the budget and creating diverse activities.
Evidence: "Kids and adults like to get up close and personal with the action... When you’re allowed to touch everything, it becomes that much more real" (p. 33); "Getting out in my community... essentially getting my program free publicity" (p. 32)

# Summary

Molly Virello’s 2019 article, "Putting the 'Extra' in Extraordinary: Creating a Library Comic Con," presents a detailed narrative case study on the evolution of library programming from standard events to large-scale, immersive experiences. The primary objective is to argue for the role of libraries in closing access gaps by providing equitable opportunities for patrons to engage with pop culture phenomena, such as comic conventions, which are often financially inaccessible to many community members. Virello posits that librarians should leverage their personal passions to drive program innovation, asserting that staff enthusiasm is a critical component of successful patron engagement.

The methodology is reflective and descriptive, chronicling the author’s experience over four years (2013–2016) at the Southington Library in Connecticut. The study begins with a traditional Free Comic Book Day (FCBD) event in 2013, which was characterized as a "passive program" featuring crafts and coloring sheets, attracting approximately 30 attendees. Recognizing the limitations of this format, Virello initiated changes in 2014 by introducing live-action cosplayers and interactive stations, such as "Kryptonite Disposal" and trivia contests. This shift resulted in a doubling of attendance to 70 participants. The following year, after attending commercial comic conventions to understand their appeal, the program expanded further, incorporating media partnerships for publicity and growing to over 200 attendees, necessitating a move to a larger venue within the library.

The culmination of this iterative process was the inaugural Southington Library ComiCon in 2016, themed around Pokémon due to its cultural relevance at the time. This event attracted nearly 900 participants. Virello details several key strategies that contributed to this success. First, extensive community partnerships were utilized to mitigate budget constraints. Local comic shops donated materials, media outlets provided free publicity, and other convention organizers loaned exhibits and props. Additionally, a partnership with a local Boy Scout troop facilitated a recycling drive, contributing to the library’s expansion fund. Second, safety and logistics were rigorously managed. The event required police assistance for traffic control in the parking lot, strict enforcement of dress codes regarding costumes and replica weapons, and careful monitoring of attendee numbers to comply with fire codes. Third, staff involvement was central; librarians contributed their own talents to create crafts, lead storytimes, and design activities, ensuring a diverse and engaging schedule that kept patrons occupied throughout the day.

The findings indicate that immersive, hands-on experiences significantly enhance patron engagement compared to passive programming. The transition from static displays to interactive elements like live characters, video game tournaments, and scavenger hunts created a more dynamic environment that appealed to both children and adults. Virello emphasizes that the success of such programs relies on flexibility, community collaboration, and the willingness of librarians to embrace their own interests. The article concludes by encouraging other librarians to identify local interests—whether related to Renaissance fairs, baking shows, or historical dramas—and adapt them into library programming. By doing so, libraries can serve as accessible hubs for cultural participation, fostering community connection and providing valuable experiences without imposing financial barriers on patrons. This case study serves as a practical guide for implementing large-scale, games-based, and pop-culture-oriented services in public library settings.
