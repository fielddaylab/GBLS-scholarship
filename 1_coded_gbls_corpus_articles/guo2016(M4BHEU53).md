## Purpose
The article aims to describe the user-centered design process of an information literacy (IL) educational game titled *Library Escape*. It seeks to address gaps in digital game-based learning (DGBL) literature regarding higher-order thinking skills and the frequent exclusion of potential users from the design process. The study demonstrates how active user involvement through participatory design and evaluation can inform the creation of effective IL games.
Evidence: "This study aims to fill these research gaps by designing and evaluating an IL educational game, Library Escape. A user-centered approach was adopted, where potential users were actively involved as designers and testers." (p. 331)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employed a two-stage qualitative design methodology. The first stage was a participatory design (PD) workshop conducted as a focus group discussion to generate a low-fidelity prototype. The second stage involved individual user evaluation interviews to assess and refine the prototype into a high-fidelity version. The game structure was guided by Kuhlthau’s Information Search Process (ISP) Model.
Evidence: "The method of the present study is comprised of two stages: the first is a PD workshop in the form of focus group discussion, which yielded a low-fidelity game prototype; the second stage is a user evaluation in the form of individual interviews, where the game prototype was further refined." Confidence: high

## Population and Data
**Stage 1 (PD Workshop):** Seven university students (3 men, 4 women; ages 21–27) were recruited via mass email and campus advertisements. The group included 3 undergraduates, 1 master’s student, and 3 doctoral students from diverse majors (sociology, marketing, communication, art history, computer science, industrial design, graphic design). All were experienced gamers (playing ≥1 hour/week).
**Stage 2 (User Evaluation):** Ten university students (6 men, 4 women; ages 21–29) participated in individual interviews. This group included 4 previous PD participants and 6 new participants. Majors included journalism, banking, knowledge management, electronic engineering, computer engineering, animation, computer science, information studies, and graphic design. All were experienced gamers.
Evidence: "7 university students were recruited for the PD workshop... In total, 10 university students from a major local university were recruited; 4 had attended the previous PD workshop, and 6 were participating for the first time." (pp. 335–336)
Confidence: high

## Findings
The study produced two prototypes of *Library Escape*, a role-playing adventure game structured around six missions corresponding to the ISP Model stages. Key findings from the design process included:
1. **Design Insights:** Successful educational game design requires being accommodating yet pragmatic toward user input, balancing enjoyment with learning objectives, and maintaining flexibility within theoretical frameworks.
2. **Prototype Evolution:** The low-fidelity prototype featured a linear narrative where a student (Tom) escapes a library with help from a librarian (Ms. Senka). User feedback indicated the story was "unconvincing" and interactions were "mechanical."
3. **High-Fidelity Revisions:** Based on evaluation, the high-fidelity prototype introduced a ghost librarian (Mr. Senka) seeking a magic wand, adding suspense and dual motivations for the player. The backstory format changed from video to comic strips for clarity. Quests were revised to focus on practical skills (e.g., using Google Scholar, OPAC) rather than abstract models. New object types (explorer, bonus, gateway) were added to encourage exploration.
Evidence: "The findings indicated that involving potential users in the early design phase is a valuable and worthwhile practice... The feedback from the user evaluation was adopted to enhance and enrich the previous design, producing a high-fidelity prototype with improved game play and the learning experience." (p. 352)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors argue that involving potential users early in the design process is critical for creating engaging and pedagogically sound educational games. The study suggests that while theoretical frameworks like the ISP Model provide necessary structure, designers must allow player agency within missions to enhance immersion. The resulting game prototype is designed to be generalizable across different library contexts, unlike many institution-specific IL games. Future work should include expert inspections and empirical testing of the playable version.
Evidence: "The study provides a good example of continuous dialogue with potential users... Our study infuses a general theoretical model, the ISP Model, into an IL game design, thus making it reusable and generalizable by different libraries." (pp. 352–353)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This article details the user-centered design process of *Library Escape*, a digital game intended to teach information literacy (IL) skills to university students. The authors, Guo and Goh, identify a gap in existing literature where educational games often fail to engage users or teach higher-order thinking skills due to a lack of user involvement during development. To address this, the study employs a participatory design (PD) approach, actively involving potential users as co-designers and testers.

The methodology consisted of two phases. First, a PD workshop was conducted with seven university students from diverse academic backgrounds. This focus group session resulted in a low-fidelity prototype of *Library Escape*, a role-playing adventure game structured around the six stages of Kuhlthau’s Information Search Process (ISP) Model: task initiation, topic selection, prefocus exploration, focus formulation, information collection, and search closure. The initial design featured a protagonist, Tom, trapped in a library who must complete quests with the help of a librarian to escape.

Second, individual interviews were conducted with ten university students (four from the initial workshop and six new participants) to evaluate the low-fidelity prototype. Participants found the initial narrative "boring" and the interactions "mechanical," noting that the balance between learning and enjoyment was skewed too heavily toward instruction. They suggested improvements such as varying difficulty levels, clarifying instructions, and enhancing the story’s engagement.

Based on this feedback, the authors developed a high-fidelity prototype. Significant revisions included changing the backstory presentation from video to comic strips for faster comprehension and altering the librarian character from a woman to a ghost named Mr. Senka who needs help finding his magic wand. This change added suspense and gave players a dual motivation: learning IL skills and helping the ghost. The game’s quests were also refined to focus on practical, hands-on skills (e.g., using Google Scholar, understanding plagiarism) rather than abstract theoretical models, which participants found less relevant. Additionally, new object types were introduced to encourage exploration and reward curiosity.

The discussion highlights three key design insights: designers must be pragmatic in accommodating user feedback while maintaining pedagogical integrity; a careful balance between enjoyment and learning is essential, often achieved through compelling narratives; and while theoretical frameworks provide structure, players should have agency within individual missions to enhance immersion. The authors conclude that the user-centered approach yielded a more robust and generalizable IL game prototype. Limitations noted include the reliance on self-reported data from focus groups and interviews without observational data or expert inspection, suggesting future studies should employ mixed methods and test the final playable version empirically.
