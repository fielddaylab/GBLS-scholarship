# Structured Extraction

## Purpose
The article aims to document the development, implementation, and assessment of a card game designed as a library orientation activity for new transfer students at James Madison University (JMU). The primary goals were to present library information in an interactive, engaging manner; facilitate social interaction among transfer students to build a sense of community; and address logistical constraints associated with short, concurrent orientation sessions.
Evidence: "Librarians and staff at a public university drew upon previous experience with instructional games to develop, implement, and assess a card game as a library orientation activity for new transfer students." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employed a developmental and evaluative approach. The authors adapted an existing *Apples to Apples*-style card game previously used for student employee training. Modifications included revising content to reflect library user needs, adding correct answers to the back of question cards to eliminate the need for constant staff moderation, and updating graphics. The game was implemented during transfer student orientation sessions in 2015 and formally assessed via an IRB-approved survey distributed to attendees in 2016.
Evidence: "A formal assessment of the card game was conducted during the 2016 transfer student orientation... At the end of each session, an IRB-approved survey was distributed to attendees asking for their opinions about the activity." (Assessment section)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The population consisted of new transfer students attending optional library orientation sessions at JMU. In 2015, approximately 100 students participated in the initial implementation. In 2016, a total of 99 students attended the library sessions. Of these, 85 completed surveys were returned and analyzed. The data included quantitative responses to yes/no/not sure questions regarding enjoyment, learning, comfort, and recommendation, as well as qualitative open-ended comments.
Evidence: "The library sessions offered during transfer student orientation in 2016 were attended by a total of 99 students... Students returned 85 completed surveys." (Assessment section)
Confidence: high

## Findings
Survey results indicated overwhelmingly positive responses. Quantitatively, 93% of respondents found the game enjoyable, 92% reported it helped them learn about the library, 86% felt more comfortable in the library, and 89% recommended the activity for future orientations. Qualitatively, students described the game as "awesome" and "better than reading a pamphlet." Some respondents suggested including more humorous cards or adding a scavenger hunt component. Facilitators observed increased social interaction, with students exchanging contact information and engaging in conversation.
Evidence: "A large majority of respondents agreed that the card game was enjoyable (93%) and helped them both to learn about (92%) and feel more comfortable in the library (86%)." (Assessment section)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that the card game format is an effective, low-cost, and flexible tool for library orientation, particularly for transfer students who may face social integration challenges. The subjective nature of the game reduces anxiety associated with factual recall, making it accessible to students with varying prior knowledge. The authors recommend continuing to use and update the game, potentially creating subject-specific "expansion packs" or versions focused solely on humor to reduce frustration when correct answers are not played. Further assessment is needed to measure long-term effectiveness on information literacy and socialization.
Evidence: "Survey responses and facilitator observations... indicate that this project succeeded in its goal of creating a fun, social, flexible, and inexpensive introduction to JMU Libraries." (Conclusion)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This article details the creation and evaluation of a library orientation card game designed specifically for new transfer students at James Madison University (JMU). The authors, Kelly Giles, Kristen Shuyler, Andrew Evans, and Jon Reed, sought to address the unique challenges faced by transfer students, including social integration difficulties and gaps in information literacy instruction. Traditional lecture-based orientations were deemed insufficient due to logistical constraints, such as short session durations (approximately 30 minutes) and limited staff availability. Furthermore, literature suggests that transfer students benefit significantly from activities that foster a sense of community, which correlates with academic success.

The methodology involved adapting an existing *Apples to Apples*-style card game previously developed for student employee training. The original game required moderators to explain correct answers, which was impractical for the large, unmoderated groups expected at orientation. To resolve this, the development team revised the cards to focus on library policies, services, and resources relevant to patrons rather than staff. Crucially, they added the correct answer to the back of each question card, allowing players to self-correct after judging rounds without constant librarian intervention. The final deck consisted of 27 question cards and 144 answer cards, featuring a mix of factual library information and humorous pop culture references.

The game was first implemented in 2015 with approximately 100 transfer students. Facilitators observed that the activity successfully prompted conversation and laughter, serving as an effective icebreaker. Students were seen exchanging contact information, indicating successful social bonding. In 2016, a formal assessment was conducted using an IRB-approved survey distributed to 99 attendees. Eighty-five completed surveys were analyzed. The quantitative results were highly positive: 93% of respondents found the game enjoyable, 92% reported it helped them learn about the library, 86% felt more comfortable in the library environment, and 89% recommended the activity for future orientations. Qualitative feedback described the game as "awesome" and superior to traditional pamphlets, though some students requested more humor or additional interactive elements like scavenger hunts.

The authors argue that the *Apples to Apples* format is particularly suitable for this context because it does not require players to know factual answers to win; rather, winning depends on subjective judgment of humorous or creative responses. This design reduces "library anxiety" and allows students with varying levels of prior knowledge to participate equally. The study highlights that while the game may not deeply teach information literacy skills due to time constraints, it effectively introduces library resources in a low-stakes environment and facilitates peer connections.

The implications for games-based library services are significant. The project demonstrates that tabletop card games can be cost-effective, scalable, and adaptable tools for outreach. The authors note that the game has since been used with other populations, including student athletes and new faculty, suggesting broad applicability. They propose future developments such as subject-specific expansion packs or a version containing only humorous answers to further reduce frustration when factual cards are not selected. However, they acknowledge the need for further assessment to measure the long-term impact of the game on information literacy outcomes and social integration. The study serves as a practical case study for librarians seeking to engage transfer students through playful, interactive design.
