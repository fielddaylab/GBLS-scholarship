## Findings
Key findings include: (1) User roles are fluid and contextual; individuals often embody multiple roles (e.g., player and parent), leading to varying information needs. (2) Proxy users (parents, librarians, educators) have distinct needs from direct players, focusing on content appropriateness, educational value, and technical compatibility rather than gameplay mechanics. (3) Genre metadata is highly valued but often confusing or conflated with theme/subject; participants desire more nuanced subject metadata. (4) Visual metadata (screenshots, gameplay videos) is critical for decision-making, often outweighing textual descriptions. (5) Relationship-based metadata (series, influences, mods) is important for discovery, particularly for scholars and collectors. (6) Users rely on a combination of reviews, ratings, rankings, and recommendations ("the four Rs") to triangulate information. (7) Appeal factors such as fellowship, narrative, challenge, and visual stimulation drive game selection more than broad genre labels.
Evidence: "Interviews revealed the restricted nature of initial user type definitions... distinctions between user groups are not definite... Genre was by far the most discussed metadata element... Visual information including visual style, representative art, screenshots, gameplay videos and trailers was desirable to users of all types."
Confidence: high

## Implications
Design implications for video game information systems include: designing for target user populations with fluid roles; enabling recommendations based on specific appeal factors rather than just genre; offering multiple automatic organization options for digital collections; and providing relationship-based, user-generated, subject, and visual metadata. Systems should accommodate both personal and proxy search modes.
Evidence: "From this user data, we derive and discuss key design implications for video game information systems: designing for target user populations, enabling recommendations based on appeals, offering multiple automatic organization options and providing relationship-based, user-generated, subject and visual metadata."
Confidence: high

# Summary

This qualitative study by Lee, Clarke, and Rossi (2016) addresses a gap in library and information science literature regarding how users discover, access, and organize video games as information objects. While previous research has focused on player behavior within games or general information seeking, little empirical data existed on the metadata needs of diverse stakeholders outside of gameplay contexts. The authors conducted 56 semi-structured interviews with a stratified sample of participants over 18 years old, including gamers, parents, collectors, industry professionals, librarians, educators, and scholars. Recruitment utilized snowball sampling through various digital and physical venues. Data were analyzed using an iterative coding process akin to grounded theory, resulting in a codebook covering gaming experience, collection behavior, information needs, metadata types, appeal factors, and user characteristics.

The study’s primary finding is that user roles are not static; individuals often embody multiple personas (e.g., a gamer who is also a parent or scholar), leading to fluid information needs depending on the context. Consequently, the authors argue against rigid user profiling in information systems. Instead, systems should accommodate different "user modes," such as seeking information for oneself versus acting as a proxy for others. Proxy users, including parents and librarians, exhibited distinct needs from direct players. They prioritized extrinsic information such as content appropriateness (violence, profanity), educational value, technical compatibility (hardware requirements, pause functionality), and cost. Direct players, conversely, sought detailed content information regarding mechanics, narrative, and visual style.

Regarding metadata, genre was identified as the most frequently discussed element but also a source of confusion, often conflated with theme or subject. Participants expressed difficulty with broad genre labels and desired more nuanced subject metadata, such as themes (e.g., cyberpunk, female protagonists) and tropes. Visual metadata, including screenshots and gameplay videos, was deemed critical for decision-making, often providing more accurate insights than textual descriptions. Furthermore, relationship-based metadata—such as series connections, influences, and modifications—was highly valued by collectors, scholars, and industry professionals for tracing the history and impact of games.

The authors also explored the "four Rs": reviews, ratings, rankings, and recommendations. Participants triangulated these sources to make purchase decisions, with proxy users preferring professional reviews (e.g., Common Sense Media) and direct players valuing user-generated content. However, concerns regarding bias and reliability in both professional and user reviews were noted. Additionally, the study identified specific "appeal factors" that drive game selection, such as fellowship, narrative, challenge, visual stimulation, and fantasy. The authors suggest that recommendation systems should leverage these appeal factors rather than relying solely on genre matching.

Finally, the study highlights challenges in organizing digital game collections, which often become cluttered due to impulse buys and sales. Participants expressed a need for flexible organization tools that allow sorting by status (e.g., played, to-play) or other custom criteria. The authors conclude that effective video game information systems must be robust enough to handle complex metadata relationships, support diverse user roles and modes, and integrate visual and appeal-based discovery features to meet the varied needs of all stakeholders.
