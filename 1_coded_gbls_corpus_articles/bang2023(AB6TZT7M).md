## Purpose

A secondary goal is to determine the extent to which teachers value the tool for supporting instruction and student engagement.

## Method
The study employed a blocked cluster randomized controlled trial design. Forty-one classrooms from 11 high-needs elementary schools in Southern California were randomly assigned to either a treatment condition (using My Math Academy for ~60 minutes/week over 12–13 weeks) or a control condition (business-as-usual instruction). Data collection included pre- and post-assessments of student math knowledge, usage data from the app, teacher surveys, semi-structured interviews with a subsample of teachers, and classroom observations. Quantitative analysis used Hierarchical Linear Modeling (HLM) to estimate treatment effects, adjusting for baseline measures and demographics. Qualitative data were analyzed using NVivo to identify themes regarding implementation fidelity and user experience.

Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 988 students (507 treatment, 481 control) from kindergarten, first, and second grade. After excluding 34 opt-outs and focusing on K-1 grades, the analytic sample included 886 students who completed both pre- and post-assessments. The schools served predominantly Latinx students (77.9–95.5%) with high proportions of English Learners (35–75%) and students eligible for free/reduced-price lunch (70–90%). Teachers received professional development on the app. Data sources included researcher-developed math assessments aligned to Common Core standards, app usage logs (time spent, skills mastered), teacher surveys (Likert scales), interview transcripts, and observation protocols.

## Findings
Quantitative results indicated that treatment students made statistically significant gains in math knowledge compared to controls, with a small effect size (d = 0.11, p = 0.026). The impact was stronger for kindergarteners (effect size = 0.16) than first graders (not significant). Treatment students showed significantly greater mastery of difficult skills, such as subtracting without regrouping and adding three-digit numbers. There was a strong correlation (r = 0.73) between the number of skills mastered in the app and post-test performance. Qualitative findings revealed that teachers found the app easy to use and valuable for personalizing instruction. Teachers reported that the app helped adapt to students' Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD), provided useful embedded assessments, and increased student engagement and motivation. Observations confirmed high levels of student engagement and effective implementation with minimal technical barriers.
Evidence: "Treatment students... outperformed their control group peers... effect size = 0.11... greatest impacts among students with lower levels of math knowledge... Teachers surveyed found My Math Academy easy to use... recognized it as a valuable learning resource." (Abstract; Results)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that adaptive, game-based learning tools like My Math Academy can effectively support personalized learning and improve early math skills, particularly for students from low-socioeconomic backgrounds. The study suggests such tools can help close achievement gaps by providing differentiated instruction that addresses individual student needs and challenges often overlooked in whole-class settings. The findings support the integration of developmentally appropriate digital games into early childhood education to enhance engagement and mastery of foundational math concepts.

# Summary

This study evaluates the efficacy of "My Math Academy," an adaptive, game-based digital learning application designed for early elementary students. The primary objective was to determine if the app, which offers personalized content and embedded assessments, could improve math learning outcomes and engagement for kindergarten and first-grade students when used as a supplement to standard curricula. A secondary objective involved assessing teacher perceptions of the tool’s utility in supporting instruction.

The research employed a blocked cluster randomized controlled trial design involving 41 classrooms across 11 high-needs elementary schools in Southern California. The participant pool consisted of 988 students, with the final analytic sample comprising 886 kindergarten and first-grade students who completed both pre- and post-intervention assessments. The demographic profile of the sample was predominantly Latinx (77.9–95.5%) with high rates of English Learners (35–75%) and eligibility for free or reduced-price lunch (70–90%), reflecting a low-socioeconomic status context. Treatment classrooms (n=21) implemented My Math Academy for approximately 60 minutes per week over 12–13 weeks, while control classrooms (n=20) continued with business-as-usual instruction. Data collection methods included researcher-developed math assessments aligned to Common Core State Standards, app usage logs, teacher surveys, semi-structured interviews, and classroom observations. Quantitative analysis utilized Hierarchical Linear Modeling (HLM) to account for nested data structures and adjust for baseline differences.

The results demonstrated that students in the treatment group achieved statistically significant gains in math knowledge compared to the control group, with an overall effect size of 0.11 (p = 0.026). Subgroup analyses revealed that the intervention had a more pronounced impact on kindergarteners (effect size = 0.16) than on first graders, where the difference was not statistically significant. The study found that My Math Academy particularly improved performance on difficult skills, such as subtracting without regrouping and adding three-digit numbers, areas often challenging for young learners. A strong positive correlation (r = 0.73) was observed between the number of skills mastered within the app and post-test scores, suggesting that engagement with the adaptive content directly translated to measurable learning gains.

Qualitative data from teacher surveys, interviews, and observations provided context for these quantitative findings. Teachers reported that My Math Academy was easy to use and valuable for personalizing instruction. They highlighted three key benefits: the utility of embedded assessments for monitoring progress, the app’s ability to adapt to each student’s Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD), and its capacity to maintain student engagement through challenging content. Observations confirmed high levels of student engagement, with students actively participating in gameplay and demonstrating persistence with difficult concepts. Teachers noted that the app allowed them to differentiate instruction effectively, supporting both advanced learners and those needing remediation, thereby alleviating some challenges associated with diverse classroom abilities.

The authors acknowledge several limitations. First, treatment teachers did not strictly adhere to instructions to exclude other supplemental digital interventions, which may have diluted the observed effects. Second, the assessment used was researcher-developed rather than a standardized test, potentially limiting generalizability. Third, the study could not fully account for English language proficiency due to data availability issues, raising concerns that post-test scores might reflect language skills alongside math knowledge. Finally, the intervention duration was limited to 12 weeks due to a school district strike, which may have constrained the full potential of the program’s impact. Despite these constraints, the study concludes that adaptive game-based learning tools can serve as effective supplements to early math instruction, particularly for underserved populations, by fostering personalized learning pathways and enhancing student motivation.
