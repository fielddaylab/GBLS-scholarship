# Structured Extraction

## Purpose
The study aimed to investigate the effects of three distinct types of game-based learning supports (modeling conceptual knowledge only, procedural knowledge only, and a combination of both) on middle school students' mathematical achievement and perceived game flow. Specifically, it sought to determine how these supports influence knowledge acquisition related to mathematical problem solving and whether they interrupt or maintain the state of game flow.
Evidence: "Therefore, the current study aims to investigate the design and impact of variant game-based learning supports on students’ math achievement and game flow." (Introduction)
Confidence: high

## Method
The researchers employed a pretest–posttest experimental design with repeated measures. The independent variable was the type of learning support provided before gameplay: conceptual knowledge only (LSCK), procedural knowledge only (LSPK), or combined conceptual and procedural knowledge (LSCPK). Participants engaged in a 4-day workshop involving a tutorial, training levels, and two intervention days where they completed specific learning support activities followed by game-based math tasks. Data were collected via pretests, two posttests measuring math performance, and a post-game questionnaire assessing perceived game flow. Statistical analyses included mixed-design ANOVA for math achievement and one-way ANOVA for game flow.
Evidence: "The study was conducted as a pretest–posttest experimental design with repeated measures... The independent variable was the type of learning support (conceptual, procedural, and the combination of the two)." (Methodology)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 41 sixth-grade students (19 male, 22 female, ages 11–12) from four classes at a middle school in the western United States. Initially, 45 participants were recruited, but 4 completed the full workshop. Participants were randomly assigned to one of three conditions: LSCK (n=13), LSPK (n=14), and LSCPK (n=14). The intervention utilized "ERebuild," a 3D architecture game focused on ratios, proportional reasoning, and geometry. Math performance was measured using isomorphic test items aligned with Common Core State Standards, while game flow was assessed using a customized seven-item Likert scale derived from the GameFlow questionnaire.

Confidence: high

## Findings
The results indicated a significant interaction between measurement time and learning support type on math achievement ($F(4, 76) = 4.04, p < .05$). All groups showed significant improvement from pretest to posttests. However, post hoc comparisons revealed that the LSPK condition (procedural knowledge only) was significantly more effective in promoting overall math test performance than the LSCPK condition, which in turn was significantly more effective than the LSCK condition. Regarding game flow, there were no significant differences among the three groups ($F(2, 38) = 0.56, p = 0.58$). All groups reported positive dispositions toward game flow, with mean scores ranging between 3.00 and 4.43 on a 5-point scale.
Evidence: "The results indicated that the learning support with procedural knowledge only... was significantly more effective in promoting students’ knowledge acquisition related to mathematical problem solving." (Abstract); "No significant effect of the types of supports was found on participants’ perceived game flow." (Abstract)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors propose design heuristics for game-based learning supports. They suggest that content-specific modeling should be provided before game tasks to avoid interrupting game flow or causing cognitive overload during play. The findings challenge the assumption that "more is better," as combined support was less effective than procedural-only support in this context. The study recommends increasing interactivity with learning materials to prevent passive processing and suggests future research focus on intrinsically embedded supports using evidence-centered design, larger sample sizes, control groups, and in-situ flow measurement techniques.
Evidence: "First, there is strong evidence that students’ math performance can substantially differ by integrating distinct types of learning supports in GBL... Second, the current study suggests that content-specific learning support in the form of modeling should be given before the game-based learning task..." (Implications)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This study by Pan and Ke (2023) examines the efficacy of different types of learning supports within a game-based learning (GBL) environment, specifically focusing on their impact on sixth-grade students' mathematical performance and perceived game flow. The research addresses a central challenge in educational gaming: facilitating the transformation of intuitive learning into generative learning without disrupting the immersive experience of gameplay. The authors utilized a value-added approach, embedding modeling supports into an existing 3D architecture game called "ERebuild," which focuses on ratios, proportional reasoning, and geometry.

The methodology employed a pretest–posttest experimental design with repeated measures. Forty-one sixth-grade students from a middle school in the western United States were randomly assigned to one of three conditions: learning support with conceptual knowledge only (LSCK), procedural knowledge only (LSPK), or a combination of both (LSCPK). The supports were delivered as interactive completion tasks prior to engaging with specific game levels. Conceptual support involved creating relationship charts and answering multiple-choice questions to map semantic structures, while procedural support provided step-by-step computational instructions via fill-in-the-blank exercises. Math achievement was assessed using isomorphic test items aligned with Common Core State Standards, and game flow was measured using a customized seven-item Likert scale administered after gameplay.

The empirical results revealed significant differences in learning outcomes based on the type of support provided. A mixed-design ANOVA indicated a significant interaction between time and support type. While all groups demonstrated improved math scores from pretest to posttest, the LSPK group (procedural knowledge only) achieved significantly higher overall performance than both the LSCPK and LSCK groups. The LSCPK group outperformed the LSCK group, making conceptual-only support the least effective condition. The authors attribute this hierarchy to cognitive load theory; procedural knowledge may be acquired more rapidly within the limited intervention time (20 minutes per level), whereas conceptual mapping may impose higher cognitive demands or require more extensive training for transfer to conventional assessments. Additionally, the interactive nature of the supports in this study likely promoted active processing compared to passive review methods used in prior research.

Regarding game flow, the study found no statistically significant differences among the three conditions ($p = 0.58$). All participants reported positive flow experiences, suggesting that providing pre-task learning supports did not disrupt immersion or enjoyment. This finding aligns with previous literature indicating that scaffolding provided before gameplay can facilitate learning without interrupting the flow state during active play. Descriptively, the LSPK group reported the highest overall flow scores, though this difference was not statistically significant.

The authors acknowledge several limitations, including the small sample size, the absence of a control group (no-support condition), and the extrinsic nature of the learning supports. They recommend future studies employ larger samples, include control groups, and utilize evidence-centered design to intrinsically embed supports within game mechanics. Furthermore, they suggest using in-situ measurement tools like Flowometer to capture dynamic changes in flow rather than relying solely on post-game reflections. Overall, the study provides empirical evidence that procedural modeling supports are particularly effective for immediate math performance gains in GBL contexts and that such supports can be integrated without compromising the player's flow experience.
