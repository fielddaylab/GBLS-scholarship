### Summary
# **Pan, Y., & Ke, F. (2023). Effects of game-based learning supports on students' math performance and perceived game flow. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 71*(2), 459-479. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-022-10183-z**

Pan and Ke examine how different forms of learning support affect middle-school students' mathematics performance and perceived game flow inside a digital learning game. The article is located in educational technology and K-12 mathematics education rather than library services. Its central design problem is that educational games can generate engagement and intuitive activity without necessarily producing explicit, transferable knowledge. The authors therefore study learning supports as value-added features that might help students transform game play into generative learning.

The game used in the study, *ERebuild*, is a 3D multi-level architecture game designed to support middle-school math problem solving. Players reconstruct a neighborhood after a natural disaster by solving math tasks embedded in simulated building scenarios. The selected School episode focused on ratio reasoning and area. Students had to calculate available container area, determine the area required by different resident groups, trade for materials, and then construct the designated building by placing, moving, and rotating objects. The study compared three kinds of modeling support: conceptual knowledge support through relationship charts and multiple-choice model-selection prompts; procedural knowledge support through step-by-step computational fill-in-the-blank prompts; and a combined conceptual-plus-procedural condition.

The study used a pretest-posttest experimental design with repeated measures during a four-day game-based learning workshop. Forty-one sixth-grade students from four classes at a western U.S. middle school completed the full workshop. Students were randomly assigned to conceptual knowledge only, procedural knowledge only, or combined conceptual/procedural support. After a pretest and a tutorial/training day, students completed two intervention sessions, each with 20 minutes of learning-support activity, 25 minutes of game-based math task work, and a 15-minute posttest. Math achievement was measured with three isomorphic sets of word problems aligned to Common Core ratio reasoning and geometry standards. Perceived game flow was measured after play with a customized seven-item GameFlow instrument.

Results showed that students in all three conditions improved from pretest to posttests, but support type mattered. The procedural knowledge condition produced the strongest math performance, followed by the combined condition, while conceptual knowledge support alone was least effective. The authors interpret this partly as a timing and cognitive-load issue: short pre-game supports may help students construct useful action procedures more quickly than abstract relationship charts, and combined supports may have imposed too much processing demand during limited exposure. The type of support did not produce statistically significant differences in perceived game flow, though all groups reported generally positive flow experiences.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful as a design caution but not as direct GBLS evidence. It suggests that library games, especially instruction games, should not assume that more explanation is always better. Supports may need to be interactive, timed before difficult tasks, embedded in game mechanics, and matched to the knowledge users need to act. However, the study does not involve libraries, librarians, information literacy, public programming, circulation, collections, or community learning spaces. Its setting is a middle-school math workshop, its outcome is math problem solving, and its implementers are educational researchers rather than library workers. It should therefore remain outside the main review except as part of the broader game-based learning background that informs GBLS design choices.
