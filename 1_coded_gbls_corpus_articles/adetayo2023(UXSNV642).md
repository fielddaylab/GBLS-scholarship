# Summary

This article by Adetayo et al. (2023) addresses the pedagogical challenges associated with teaching cataloguing and classification in Nigerian Library and Information Science (LIS) schools. The authors argue that these core librarianship skills are often perceived by students as technical, monotonous, and overly theoretical, leading to disengagement and inadequate practical preparation for professional roles. To counter this, the study explores gamification—a technology-enhanced approach that integrates game mechanics into non-gaming educational contexts—as a strategy to improve student motivation, engagement, and learning outcomes.

The methodology consists of a comprehensive literature review rather than primary empirical data collection. The authors synthesize existing research on gamification in education, the current state of LIS curricula in Nigeria, and technological platforms suitable for implementation. The review highlights that while gamification has been applied in various educational sectors (e.g., economics, general e-learning), its application to cataloguing and classification remains underexplored, particularly in the Nigerian context where awareness among librarians is low.

The study identifies several critical issues in current LIS education in Nigeria. Many students enter the field without strong intrinsic interest, often viewing librarianship as "books and boring." The curriculum heavily emphasizes theory over practice, focusing on standards like AACR2 and LCSH while neglecting hands-on experience with modern tools. This disconnect results in graduates who lack the technical proficiency required by employers. Furthermore, students frequently exhibit negative attitudes toward cataloguing courses, associating them with difficulty and redundancy.

To address these challenges, the authors propose a conceptual model for gamifying cataloguing and classification education, supported by cloud computing infrastructure. Cloud computing is recommended to reduce the financial burden on institutions by eliminating the need for extensive hardware upgrades, allowing for scalable, accessible learning environments. The proposed framework rests on five pillars: motivation, short-term tasks, reward systems, task design, and teamwork.

Regarding motivation, the authors draw on Self-Determination Theory, distinguishing between intrinsic and extrinsic motivators. They argue that gamification should foster intrinsic motivation by creating a "flow" state where students are absorbed in challenging yet achievable tasks. Extrinsic rewards (e.g., points) should be used carefully to avoid undermining intrinsic interest, with an emphasis on unexpected rewards and positive verbal feedback.

The model breaks down complex cataloguing processes into short-term, manageable tasks. For instance, instead of overwhelming students with full bibliographic records, the gamified system would guide them through sequential steps: identifying tools, describing physical features, assigning subject headings, and finally creating class marks. Each step provides immediate feedback, allowing students to correct errors in a low-stakes environment. Task design is structured to increase in difficulty progressively, ensuring that students build competence gradually. Teamwork is integrated to encourage peer collaboration and the sharing of strategies, enhancing social learning.

The authors illustrate how this model could be implemented using cloud-based platforms (e.g., Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure) to deliver interactive modules. For example, a classification game might require students to drag and drop items into correct Library of Congress categories to earn points. This approach transforms repetitive drills into engaging activities, potentially improving retention and practical skill acquisition.

However, the study acknowledges significant limitations and barriers to implementation. The primary constraint is the lack of empirical evidence specifically linking gamification to improved outcomes in Nigerian LIS cataloguing courses; the proposed model is theoretical and requires validation through future empirical studies. Additionally, financial constraints pose a major challenge, as developing high-quality gamified content involves costs for design, testing, and specialist consultation. Technological adoption is another hurdle, particularly among older faculty members who may lack proficiency with digital tools. The authors recommend targeted training workshops for academics and advocate for stakeholder collaboration to secure funding and policy support.

In conclusion, Adetayo et al. present a compelling case for integrating gamification into LIS education in Nigeria. By leveraging cloud computing and game-based learning principles, library schools can potentially transform the perception of cataloguing from a tedious obligation to an engaging, skill-building experience. The article serves as a foundational blueprint for future research and practical implementation, emphasizing the need for institutional support, financial investment, and faculty development to realize these benefits.
