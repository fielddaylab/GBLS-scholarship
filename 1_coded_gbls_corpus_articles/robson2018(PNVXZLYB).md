## Population and Data
Eight copies of two tabletop games: *Ticket to Ride* (TR) and *Betrayal at House on the Hill* (BHH). Each title had four copies treated with different preservation/processing methods. The items were circulated in the University of North Texas Media Library from July 15, 2015, to May 1, 2016. Data included circulation counts, damage assessments (graded 0–3), piece loss tallies, and staff time/cost calculations.

## Findings
1. **Circulation Results:** Damage across all copies was minimal to minor; all games remained playable. Loss rates were negligible (<1%) regardless of processing level. Copy 4 (full preservation/processing) had zero loss/damage but required significantly more staff time (e.g., ~26 hours for BHH vs. 7 minutes for Copy 1).
2. **Cost-Benefit:** The salary cost to fully process a game equaled the cost of replacing it entirely (or up to twelve times the cost for complex games like BHH), making full processing economically inefficient given the low loss rates of minimal processing.
3. **Stress Testing:** 
   - Cards: Sleeving mitigated liquid damage but could trap contaminants if not sealed; sealed sleeves prevented all damage. Unprotected cards warped but remained usable.
   - Chipboard Tokens/Tiles: Highly susceptible to water/soda damage (layer separation). Contact paper fully protected tiles, but application time was prohibitive. PVA and spray offered limited protection.
   - Box Corners: Cloth tape reinforcement effectively reduced corner damage compared to no treatment or clear tape.
   - Instructions: Binding methods (Velo, pamphlet binders) provided superior protection against physical impact compared to sewing or sleeves.
Evidence: "The results of this research show that there is no real benefit to preserving and processing a circulating tabletop game collection to the fullest extent... The loss rate is negligible between no processing and full processing."
Confidence: high

## Implications
Libraries should adopt minimal processing (single barcode, bagging pieces) as it is cost-effective given low loss rates. Preservation efforts should be targeted: sleeve cards for high-use games to prevent wear/contamination, reinforce box corners with cloth tape, and use binding for instruction booklets. Full preservation of all components is not justified by the marginal reduction in damage/loss.

# Summary

This article presents an empirical study conducted by librarians at the University of North Texas (UNT) Media Library to establish best practices for circulating tabletop game collections. Recognizing a lack of professional literature on this topic, the authors designed an experiment to evaluate the efficacy of various preservation and processing methods against the costs in staff time and resources. The study focused on two popular titles, *Ticket to Ride* (TR) and *Betrayal at House on the Hill* (BHH), using four copies of each game. Each copy underwent a different level of intervention, ranging from no treatment (Copy 1) to minimal processing (Copy 2), moderate preservation/processing (Copy 3), and extensive preservation/processing (Copy 4).

The primary phase involved an eight-month circulation period (July 2015–May 2016). The authors tracked circulation frequency, damage severity (graded on a 0–3 scale), piece loss, and the time required for processing. Results indicated that damage across all copies was minimal to minor, with no game becoming unusable. Crucially, the loss rate was negligible across all conditions, remaining below 1% regardless of the intensity of preservation or processing. For instance, TR Copy 1 (minimal processing) lost one piece after 25 circulations, while TR Copy 4 (full processing) lost zero pieces after 15 circulations. However, the labor cost disparity was stark: fully processing BHH Copy 4 took approximately 26 hours, costing roughly twelve times the price of a new game, whereas minimal processing took only seven minutes. The authors concluded that the marginal gain in preservation from full processing did not justify the significant expenditure of staff time.

To further assess durability, the authors conducted controlled stress tests exposing game components to water, soda, cheese dip, and salsa for varying durations, as well as physical impact tests on boxes and instruction booklets. These tests revealed material-specific vulnerabilities. Cardstock warped when exposed to liquids but remained usable; sleeving cards mitigated liquid damage, though open sleeves could trap contaminants if not properly cleaned. Chipboard tokens and tiles were highly susceptible to delamination from moisture, with contact paper offering complete protection but requiring prohibitive application time. Box corners reinforced with cloth tape showed superior resilience compared to untreated boxes or those with clear tape. Instruction booklets bound in pamphlet binders or Velo binding sustained no damage from impact, outperforming sewn or sleeved alternatives.

Based on these findings, the authors recommend a targeted approach for library collections. They advocate for minimal processing—specifically, a single barcode on the box and bagging game pieces to prevent loss—as sufficient for most items. Preservation efforts should be prioritized for high-use games: card sleeves are recommended to extend lifespan and prevent wear-based advantages in gameplay; cloth tape should be used to reinforce box corners; and durable binding methods should be employed for instruction manuals. The study concludes that tabletop games can be circulated effectively without extensive preservation protocols, as the cost of full protection outweighs the benefits given the inherent durability of modern game components and low loss rates.
