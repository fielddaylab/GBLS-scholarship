# Summary

Jared Alexander Seay’s 2018 column, "Considering Games and Gamification in Libraries & Associated Entities — How the Longing for Tabletops has Revitalized Games," published in *Against the Grain*, explores the intersection of analog gaming and library services. The article argues that the resurgence of board games and tabletop activities in libraries is driven by a societal counter-movement against digital isolation, termed "High Tech High Touch." Seay posits that while technology dominates communication, there is a growing demand for tangible, face-to-face human interaction, which libraries can facilitate through analog gaming.

The author begins with an anecdotal account from his professional experience as a library instructor. Approximately five years prior to publication, he attempted to introduce a board game to a classroom of 27 university students. He observed that none of the students had ever played a board game, leading him to initially conclude that the analog game era had ended for this demographic. However, Seay revises this perspective by noting the concurrent rise of board game cafes and the broader tabletop gaming renaissance. He cites market data indicating that the hobby game market exceeded $1.4 billion in 2016 with a growth rate of 21%, suggesting that analog games are not obsolete but are experiencing renewed popularity.

Seay attributes this resurgence to the concepts outlined by John Naisbitt in *Megatrends* (1982) and *High Tech High Touch* (1999). Naisbitt theorized that as society becomes more technologically saturated, individuals increasingly crave personal, human contact to counterbalance digital stimuli. Seay supports this with personal observations of social behavior, noting instances where individuals physically present together communicate exclusively via mobile devices, highlighting a disconnect between physical proximity and genuine interaction. He further references an informal poll of approximately a dozen students at his library’s reference desk, which indicated a preference for physical books over digital screens due to tactile qualities like smell and feel. This preference for tangible objects is presented as parallel to the appeal of tabletop games.

The article identifies specific benefits of analog gaming in library contexts. Seay highlights that board game cafes thrive on providing a "sense of community" and an opportunity for patrons to disengage from smartphones. He argues that libraries, as traditional centers for community activity, are well-suited to host these experiences. The author notes that libraries have already begun adopting escape rooms, describing them as "high touch, interactive game environments." He suggests that this trend signals potential for other immersive group learning experiences, such as Live Action Role-Playing (LARP) and megagames. Seay claims that humans achieve optimal synergy in intermingled physical groups, implying that analog games can enhance collaborative learning and social cohesion more effectively than solitary digital interactions.

Seay concludes that the analog resurgence does not replace digital technology but serves as a complementary reprieve from it. He asserts that libraries should leverage these "old school" high-touch activities to revitalize their spaces and services. The article implies that by embracing tabletop games and immersive analog experiences, libraries can address the modern need for authentic human connection while maintaining their role as hubs for education and community engagement. The author’s tone is reflective and advocacy-oriented, urging librarians to recognize the value of tactile, synchronous communication in an increasingly digital world.
