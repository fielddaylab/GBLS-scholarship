# Summary

This qualitative study by Lorri M. Mon (2012) investigates the dynamics of professional identity formation for librarians and educators operating within the virtual world of Second Life. The research addresses a gap in Library and Information Science (LIS) literature regarding "avatar-mediated communication," specifically how professionals establish trust and credibility in graphical, immersive environments that are often perceived as game-like rather than serious professional spaces.

The methodology employed an ethnographic approach involving participant observation, workplace visits, and semi-structured interviews. The author immersed herself in the Second Life librarian and educator communities for over 18 months prior to data collection to acquire functional skills and cultural knowledge of the environment. Data collection involved 12 in-depth interviews with seven librarians and five educators conducted between May and December 2008, as well as observations of 50 virtual library workplaces between May 2008 and January 2009. The analysis was framed using Erving Goffman’s theories on the presentation of self, impression management, and audience segregation.

The findings reveal that professionals face a dual challenge in managing their avatar identities to satisfy two distinct user groups: "newbies" (new users) and "residents" (seasoned users). To establish credibility with newbies, librarians and educators typically adopted conservative, humanoid avatar appearances and utilized traditional workplace imagery such as books, bookshelves, computers, and reference desks. These visual cues helped frame the interaction as professional and scholarly, countering the perception of Second Life as merely a game. However, residents often judged professionalism based on the sophistication and quality of the avatar’s appearance. Consequently, many professionals felt compelled to invest time and virtual currency into upgrading their avatars with high-quality skins, clothing, and animations to avoid being stigmatized as "newbies" themselves and to connect with the sartorial expectations of the resident community.

The study also identified the use of alternative ("alt") identities by 42% of participants. These secondary accounts were used for privacy, to avoid interruptions from instant messages directed at their professional avatars, or for specific role-playing tasks in educational scenarios. Despite these alternatives, the primary avatar remained the core representation of the professional self.

The implications of this research highlight the performative nature of professional identity in virtual worlds. Librarians and educators must actively manage visual elements to bridge the gap between traditional professional expectations and the unique cultural norms of virtual environments. The study notes that while generalizability is limited by the small sample size and the rapidly changing nature of virtual platforms, the findings offer critical insights for developing best practices in avatar-mediated library services and distance education. It underscores the importance of visual literacy and impression management as essential skills for professionals working in graphical online settings.
