## Purpose
The report aims to present results from the 2022 Public Library Association (PLA) Annual Survey, which gathered data on how U.S. public libraries serve and sustain their communities through programs, services, partnerships, and facilities. It seeks to establish a baseline for future comparison (next survey in 2025) regarding library engagement with community needs such as literacy, workforce development, food insecurity, and sustainability.
Evidence: "The results of the Public Library Association (PLA) 2022 Public Library Services for Strong Communities Survey address the myriad ways libraries nationwide serve and sustain their communities... This first year of results provide a baseline so that when we ask these questions again in 2025, we will see how the field has evolved."
Confidence: high

## Method
The study utilized a cross-sectional survey design. The PLA fielded the "Public Library Services for Strong Communities Survey" in fall 2022. It was the third of a rotating set of three topical surveys. All questions were optional. Results are weighted to account for differences between responding libraries and the universe of all U.S. public libraries. Data analysis was performed by external consultants (Karen Drill and James Price).
Evidence: "PLA fielded the Public Library Services for Strong Communities Survey in fall 2022... The results presented below are weighted to account for differences between the responding libraries and the universe of all U.S. public libraries."
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The population consisted of all U.S. public libraries at the administrative entity level, as defined by the Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS). A total of 1,167 libraries completed the survey, representing a response rate of 12.6%. The data is categorized by locale type: city (6% of entities, serving 35% of population), suburban (25% of entities, serving 42% of population), and town/rural (70% of entities, serving 23% of population).

## Findings
**Community Needs:** Libraries primarily address literacy/educational achievement (84.7%), digital equity (66.6%), and civic engagement (62.4%). Future priorities include climate change/sustainability (27.5%) and accessibility (26.6%).
**Services:** 98% of libraries have at least one partnership. Job and career services are offered by 77.5% of libraries. Informal services are more common than formal ones, largely due to staffing constraints (73.3% cited lack of staff/partners).
**Programs:** Summer reading programs are ubiquitous (99.1% overall). For teens, the top programs are summer reading (86.1%), games and gaming (65.6%), and STEAM (59.1%). City libraries offer a wider variety of program types on average (23.7 types) compared to town/rural libraries (14 types), though town/rural libraries offer more programs per capita.
**Facilities:** 40.3% of library buildings have not undergone major renovation since 2000 or earlier. 50% of libraries have special emergency designations. Mobile programming facilities exist in 17.5% of libraries.
**Evaluation:** 91.3% of libraries measure outputs (e.g., circulation), but only 29% engage in outcome measurement.
Evidence: "The majority of libraries (68%) offer elections services... Almost all libraries (99%) have summer reading programs... 78% of libraries offer job and career services currently... 40% of library buildings have not undergone major renovations in over two decades... Only about a third of libraries (29%) engage in outcome measurement."
Confidence: high

## Implications
The report implies that while libraries are strong community hubs, they face significant capacity challenges regarding funding and staffing. It suggests that partnerships are critical for expanding services without increasing internal burden. The data highlights a need for infrastructure investment, particularly in town/rural areas where buildings are older. The low rate of outcome measurement suggests a gap in demonstrating impact beyond simple usage statistics.
Evidence: "Capacity—particularly funding and staffing—remains a persistent issue... Building partnerships can benefit the library... enabling the sharing of expertise... Capturing outcomes can help libraries tell the story of not only how much they do, but also how patrons benefit."
Confidence: high

# Summary

The Public Library Association’s 2023 report, *Public Library Services for Strong Communities Report*, presents findings from the 2022 PLA Annual Survey. The primary objective was to establish a baseline of data regarding how U.S. public libraries address community needs through services, programs, partnerships, and facilities. The survey was administered in fall 2022 to all U.S. public libraries at the administrative entity level. Of the invited population, 1,167 libraries responded, yielding a 12.6% response rate. The data was weighted to reflect the national universe of public libraries and analyzed by locale: city, suburban, and town/rural.

The findings indicate that libraries continue to prioritize core services related to literacy, education, and civic engagement. Specifically, 84.7% of libraries are currently addressing literacy and educational achievement, while 66.6% focus on digital equity. Summer reading programs remain a cornerstone service, with 99.1% of libraries offering them for children, teens, or adults. In the realm of teen programming, games and gaming programs (65.6%) and STEAM programs (59.1%) are prominent alongside summer reading. For adult audiences, book clubs (84%) and art/music programs (68.5%) are most common. Notably, workforce development is an emerging focus; 77.5% of libraries offer job and career services, and 23.8% have specific workforce development programs.

Partnerships are nearly universal, with 97.5% of libraries reporting at least one partnership. However, these collaborations are predominantly informal (95%) rather than formal (59%). The most common partners are K-12 schools (89.7%) and local governments (85.2%). The report emphasizes that partnerships allow libraries to expand their reach despite resource constraints.

Significant challenges were identified regarding infrastructure and capacity. Approximately 40% of library buildings have not undergone major renovations since 2000 or earlier, with town/rural facilities being the oldest on average. Staffing shortages (cited by 73.4% of respondents) and funding limitations are the primary barriers to expanding services. Consequently, many libraries provide support services informally rather than formally, often due to a lack of trained staff or partners.

Regarding evaluation practices, while 91.3% of libraries track output measures such as circulation and attendance, only 29% engage in outcome measurement to assess the impact on patron learning and behavior. The report concludes that while libraries are adapting to post-pandemic realities and expanding into areas like food insecurity support (addressed by 31.6% of libraries) and sustainability initiatives, sustained investment in staff, funding, and physical infrastructure is critical for maintaining their role as resilient community hubs.
