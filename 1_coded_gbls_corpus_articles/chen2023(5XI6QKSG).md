## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 61 seventh-grade students (mean age 13) from a secondary school in Taiwan. Students shared similar socio-economic status. Prior gaming experience was high for non-educational games (90%) but low for educational games (22%). ## Findings
Adaptive scaffolding had a significant positive effect on learning performance compared to fixed scaffolding, with a large effect size ($F(1,59)=17.847, p=.00, \eta^2=0.263$). Regarding engagement, the adaptive group showed significantly higher levels of self-regulation ($p=.017$), deep strategy ($p=.008$), and emotional engagement ($p=.013$) compared to the fixed group. However, there was no significant difference in behavioral engagement between the two groups ($p=.873$). Contrary to hypotheses, none of the four engagement dimensions moderated the relationship between scaffolding type and learning performance ($p > .05$).

# Summary

Chen, Law, and Huang (2023) present a quasi-experimental study investigating the efficacy of adaptive scaffolding versus fixed scaffolding within a digital game-based learning (DGBL) environment. The primary objective was to determine how adaptive instructional support influences students' conceptual understanding of physics (specifically Newton’s laws) and their multidimensional engagement, defined as cognitive (self-regulation and deep strategy), emotional, and behavioral. This study addresses a gap in the literature where previous meta-analyses have shown inconsistent results regarding DGBL effectiveness, particularly in science education, and where few studies have examined adaptive scaffolding through a comprehensive engagement framework.

The participants were 61 seventh-grade students from a secondary school in Taiwan, randomly assigned by class to either a fixed scaffolding group ($N=30$) or an adaptive scaffolding group ($N=31$). The intervention utilized "Summon of Magicrystal," a computer-based interactive game designed to teach physics concepts through gameplay mechanics involving time, distance, and velocity. In the fixed condition, students received pre-planned scaffolds in a static sequence regardless of their individual progress. In the adaptive condition, an automated system analyzed student log data, game progress, and answer results to trigger scaffolds only when specific behavioral thresholds indicated a need for support, thereby tailoring assistance to individual cognitive levels.

Data were collected via pre- and post-tests measuring conceptual understanding and validated questionnaires assessing four engagement subscales: self-regulation, deep strategy, behavioral engagement, and emotional engagement. The study employed a 2x2 factorial ANCOVA with pre-test scores as covariates to analyze performance and moderation effects, and a one-way MANOVA to compare engagement levels between groups.

The results indicated that adaptive scaffolding significantly improved learning performance compared to fixed scaffolding, demonstrating a large effect size ($\eta^2=0.263$). Students in the adaptive group achieved higher post-test scores than their counterparts in the fixed group. Regarding engagement, the adaptive scaffolding group exhibited significantly higher levels of self-regulation, deep strategy use, and emotional engagement compared to the fixed group. However, no significant difference was found in behavioral engagement between the two conditions. The authors hypothesize that while adaptive scaffolds reduced unnecessary cognitive load and allowed for deeper cognitive processing, they did not necessarily increase observable participation or effort metrics captured by behavioral engagement scales.

Furthermore, the study tested whether engagement moderated the relationship between scaffolding type and learning performance. Contrary to expectations, none of the four engagement dimensions showed a significant interaction effect with scaffolding type on learning outcomes. This suggests that while adaptive scaffolding enhances both performance and certain engagement types, the mechanism by which it improves learning is not statistically mediated or moderated by these specific engagement measures in this context.

The authors conclude that adaptive scaffolding offers substantial benefits for DGBL by supporting diverse learner needs without disrupting game flow or imposing excessive cognitive load. They recommend future research explore different methods for generating adaptive support, investigate the impact on intrinsic motivation more thoroughly, and develop more inclusive measures for emotional engagement. The study provides robust empirical evidence supporting the integration of adaptive technologies in educational games to enhance both academic achievement and specific facets of learner engagement.
