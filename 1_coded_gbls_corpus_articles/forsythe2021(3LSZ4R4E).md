# Summary

Carlie Forsythe’s 2021 article, "Game On! Reflections of a Board Game Librarian," provides a reflective examination of non-traditional library collections, with a specific focus on board games within an academic community college setting. The author situates this work within broader trends in librarianship, noting the shift from independent to collaborative learning and the growing acceptance of non-traditional items such as tools, seed libraries, and gaming consoles. These collections are argued to serve multiple institutional goals: attracting hard-to-reach populations, promoting social inclusivity, and enhancing patron well-being (Michnik & Eriksson, 2014; Söderholm, 2015).

The core of the article details the author’s practical experience managing a board game collection of over 25 titles for nearly two years. The methodology relies on first-hand observation, literature review, and feedback from an anonymous five-question survey administered during programming events. The target demographic at this institution includes a significant population of international students, who were found to engage more readily with programming than domestic peers. Consequently, the collection development strategy prioritized language-independent games (e.g., Tsuro) and titles with low complexity ("lighter weight") to facilitate accessibility. Selection criteria also included user ratings from BoardGameGeek and playing times under 45 minutes to allow for multiple games per session.

Operational considerations are thoroughly addressed. The collection is non-circulating but available for in-library use, tracked via manual sign-out forms requiring student IDs. Preservation techniques include reinforcing boxes with clear tape, maintaining inventory lists on box lids, and bagging components. RFID tags prevent theft. Programming evolved based on patron feedback; initial monthly two-hour sessions were deemed insufficient, leading to a shift to bi-monthly three-hour events. Marketing relied on physical posters, social media, and partnerships with campus organizations like the international center.

The findings highlight several benefits: board games helped dispel library anxiety, encouraged regular attendance, and provided students with access to costly recreational items they might not otherwise afford. Socially, these events fostered opportunities for language practice, global citizenship, and critical thinking. However, challenges included limited financial resources, difficulties in securing volunteers, and fluctuating student attendance.

A significant portion of the article addresses the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, which suspended in-person activities starting in March 2020. The author discusses the broader shift toward e-resources and virtual programming, noting that while this expanded access for some, it created barriers for those lacking high-speed internet. To adapt, libraries like Odum Library at Valdosta State University utilized platforms such as Discord to host virtual game nights using simulators like Board Game Arena and Tabletopia. The author compares these platforms, noting differences in cost, learning curves, and game availability. Although virtual attendance was lower than in-person events, participants valued the programming as a means to combat social isolation.

The article concludes that non-traditional collections are effective tools for achieving library objectives related to equity and engagement. It suggests that the adaptations made during the pandemic, particularly virtual gaming services, may continue to be valuable strategies for expanding patron access in the future. The work serves as both a case study and a practical guide for librarians considering similar initiatives, emphasizing the importance of aligning collections with institutional values and remaining responsive to patron feedback.
