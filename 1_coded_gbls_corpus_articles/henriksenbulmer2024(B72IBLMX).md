The authors sought to address the lack of existing games that effectively educate this demographic on privacy risks through an interactive, non-threatening medium.

## Population and Data
The study involved two distinct participant groups. First, a Prolific survey included 90 adults (parents, guardians, educators) and 91 young people (aged 18–25) in the UK to inform design preferences and risk perceptions. Second, the game evaluation phase involved 27 undergraduate students aged 18–25 from Bournemouth University, divided into focus groups of 4–5 players. Data consisted of survey responses regarding privacy concerns and game preferences, as well Likert-scale questionnaire data measuring player confidence before and after gameplay.

## Findings
The Prolific survey revealed divergent risk perceptions: educators were primarily concerned with cyberbullying and predators, while young people were more concerned with privacy settings, phishing, scams, and hacking. The game prototype evaluation showed that all 27 participants reported increased confidence across all five privacy categories after playing. Statistical analysis confirmed significant improvements in knowledge retention, particularly in "managing your information" and "relationships and reputation" (p < 0.01), and "cyber-bullying and trolling" and "self-identity and well-being" (p < 0.05). Participants found the game easy to use, engaging, and effective for passive learning through discussion.
Evidence: "The Wilcoxon... Test found an improvement in knowledge across all the categories. The most signiﬁcant gains were seen in the 'managing your information' and 'your relationships and reputation' categories (p < 0:01)... Overall, players were in agreement that the game was easy to play, and was not too complex." (Section 8)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that game-based learning is an effective tool for disseminating privacy education to young people in a safe, non-threatening manner. The study suggests that interactive games can facilitate passive learning and encourage critical discussion among peers. Future work includes developing a digital version of the game, expanding question sets for diverse demographics (including older adults), and conducting further trials with younger participants and educators to broaden the evidence base.

# Summary

Henriksen-Bulmer et al. (2024) present a case study detailing the development and evaluation of a bespoke board game designed to educate young people on digital privacy and online safety. The authors argue that while young people are heavy users of social media, they often lack awareness of the long-term consequences of their digital footprints, such as data breaches, cyberbullying, and identity theft. Existing educational tools are limited, prompting the creation of a game-based learning (GBL) intervention aimed at passively teaching privacy concepts through interactive play.

The study utilized a mixed-methods approach within a case study framework. Initially, the researchers conducted a literature review to identify key privacy risks, categorizing them into five areas: managing information, self-identity and well-being, cyberbullying and trolling, relationships and reputation, and privacy and security. To inform the game’s design, an online survey was administered via Prolific to 90 adults (educators/parents) and 91 young people (aged 18–25). The survey results highlighted a disconnect in risk perception: educators were most concerned about cyberbullying and predators, whereas young people prioritized concerns regarding privacy settings, phishing, scams, and hacking. These insights guided the creation of question cards for each category.

The game prototype combined mechanics from *Snakes and Ladders* and *Trivial Pursuit*, featuring a circular board, category tokens, and shield tokens to modify gameplay. The evaluation phase involved 27 undergraduate students (aged 18–25) from Bournemouth University, who participated in focus groups of 4–5 players. Participants completed pre- and post-game questionnaires assessing their confidence and knowledge across the five privacy categories.

The findings indicated that the game successfully achieved its intended learning outcomes. All participants reported increased confidence after playing. Statistical analysis using the Wilcoxon test revealed significant improvements in self-rated knowledge, particularly in "managing your information" and "relationships and reputation" (p < 0.01), as well as "cyber-bullying and trolling" and "self-identity and well-being" (p < 0.05). Qualitative feedback suggested that the game was engaging, easy to use, and facilitated valuable peer discussions about online safety, thereby reinforcing passive learning.

The authors acknowledge significant limitations, primarily driven by the COVID-19 pandemic. The inability to access younger participants or diverse educational settings meant the evaluation was restricted to university students, limiting the generalizability of the findings to the broader 16–25 demographic. Additionally, the original plan to develop a digital version of the game was delayed. Despite these constraints, the study demonstrates that GBL can be an effective pedagogical tool for raising awareness of digital privacy risks in a non-threatening environment. The authors recommend future iterations include digital formats and expanded testing with younger cohorts and educators to validate the tool’s efficacy across different age groups and contexts.
