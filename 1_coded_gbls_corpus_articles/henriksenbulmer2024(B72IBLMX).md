### Summary
# **Henriksen-Bulmer, J., Rosenorn-Lanng, E., Corbin-Clarke, S., Ware, S., Melacca, D., & Fenge, L.-A. (2024). Using game-based learning to teach young people about privacy and online safety. *Interactive Learning Environments, 32*(10), 6430-6450. https://doi.org/10.1080/10494820.2023.2265424**

Henriksen-Bulmer, Rosenorn-Lanng, Corbin-Clarke, Ware, Melacca, and Fenge present a case-study account of designing and evaluating a bespoke board game about digital privacy and online safety for young people aged 16-25. The article is not a library study, but it is relevant to the GBLS review as adjacent evidence for game-based digital-literacy instruction. The authors begin from the premise that young people use the internet and social media to communicate, experiment with identity, and build relationships, but that they also face online risks such as oversharing, cyberbullying, trolling, scams, privacy breaches, grooming, harmful content, reputational damage, and commercial data collection. They argue that software controls and parental monitoring are insufficient on their own; young people need digital literacy, privacy awareness, and resilience.

The project sought to create a privacy game that would passively teach players how to stay safe online. The design fused expertise from computing, gaming, health, and social care. The resulting physical prototype was a cross between *Trivial Pursuit* and *Snakes and Ladders*: players moved around a board, answered questions from different privacy categories, collected category tokens, used shield tokens, and used portals to move more easily between areas. The game was deliberately physical rather than digital because the team's survey work found strong youth preference for a physical board game, even though educators and adults were nearly evenly split between physical and digital formats.

The article's design process is useful because it makes the learning architecture visible. The authors reviewed privacy and online-safety frameworks, including Solove's taxonomy of privacy harms, Finn et al.'s privacy categories, Kaspersky's list of online dangers, the UK Council for Internet Safety framework, Ofcom's harm groupings, and Willoughby's risk categories. They then consolidated these into five game categories: managing your information; self-identity and well-being; cyber-bullying and trolling; your relationships and reputation; and privacy and security. The game questions were informed by the literature and by a Prolific survey of 90 adults, parents, guardians, and educators, plus 91 young people aged 18-25. The survey showed both overlap and mismatch between youth and adult concerns: young people were especially concerned with privacy and security, phishing and scams, and location tracking, while adults were more concerned about cyberbullying, sexual predators, privacy, and protection of personal information.

The prototype was evaluated through four focus groups with 27 undergraduate students aged 18-25. Participants completed pre-game and post-game questionnaires about confidence across the five categories, along with usability and play-experience questions. The authors report that all players felt more confident across all five categories after playing. Wilcoxon tests showed improvement across all categories, with the most significant gains in managing your information and your relationships and reputation. Cyber-bullying and trolling and self-identity and well-being also showed significant positive shifts. Participants generally found the game easy to play and not too complex, and the authors observed that most groups discussed questions and possible answers, even when the current question was not their own.

For GBLS, the strongest lesson is that a low-tech board game can make online-safety instruction conversational, social, and non-threatening. The game did not simply quiz individual players; it created a shared space where questions, wrong answers, explanations, and peer talk could reinforce learning for everyone at the table. This matters for libraries because privacy, misinformation, cyberbullying, scams, passwords, account safety, and digital footprints are common public-library and school-library digital-literacy concerns. The article suggests that libraries do not need sophisticated digital games to teach these topics. A well-designed physical game can support discussion, immediate feedback, safe failure, and confidence-building while avoiding some barriers attached to computers, accounts, or specialized software.

The application to GBLS should be cautious. The study did not take place in a library, and the evaluation involved a small, convenient sample of university students rather than the full intended 16-25 age range. The outcome measures were immediate self-reports of confidence rather than long-term behavioral change or objective privacy skills. The authors also note that COVID-19 limited access to younger participants and delayed development of a digital version. Still, the study offers a useful transferable model for information-literacy and digital-safety programming: define risk categories from current frameworks, generate questions from both research and local participant concerns, test the game with users, keep the rules simple, and build for discussion rather than content delivery alone.
