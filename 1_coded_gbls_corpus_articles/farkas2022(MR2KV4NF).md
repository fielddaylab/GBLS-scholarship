## Purpose
The article aims to develop and evaluate a multi-level, game-based learning (ML-GBL) methodology for teaching business process (BP) concepts and the role of information systems (IS) in an introductory undergraduate Information Systems course. The specific objectives are to enable students to define BP concepts, explain BPs to stakeholders, and describe the relationship between IS and BPs, particularly through Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems.
Evidence: "This research seeks an effective teaching methodology through which students learn the role of an IS in BP... result in students being able to 1) define BP concepts, 2) explain a BP to business stakeholders, and 3) describe the role of IS in BP."
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employs an experiential learning framework consisting of four sequential classroom exercises (games) that increase in complexity. The first two games involve physical activities (M&M inspection and card sorting) to teach basic BPMN notation and roles. The third game involves manufacturing a gift product to introduce dependent processes. The final game utilizes an SAP® ERP simulation to model complex business processes involving data flow and parallelism. Effectiveness was assessed via student surveys using a seven-point Likert scale, Pearson correlation coefficients, and t-tests comparing two different classes.
Evidence: "The developed methodology consists of four classroom exercises that begin with a simple process and culminate with an ERP simulation... A survey of the student’s perception and the researchers’ ad hoc observations demonstrates the effectiveness of the developed methodology."
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The participants were undergraduate students enrolled in two sections of an introductory Information Systems course at the University of Tampa. The total population was 86 students, with a response rate of 72.1% (63 respondents). Class 1 had 43 students (81.1% response rate), and Class 2 had 33 students (57.6% response rate). Data included survey responses regarding learning opinions and study habits, as well as instructor observations.
Evidence: "A survey was provided to students in two classes totaling 86 students, of which 63 responded... Class 1 n=43... Class 2 n=33."
Confidence: high

## Findings
Survey results indicated that students perceived the activities as beneficial and engaging. Strong correlations (r > 0.7) were found between positive opinions of the initial games (M&M inspection, card sorting) and the utility of the BPMN diagramming tool. Students reported that hands-on activities helped them grasp complex concepts better than traditional methods. However, statistical analysis revealed significant differences in responses between the two classes (p < 0.05), with Class 2 showing lower correlation strength and different mean responses, potentially due to remote delivery during the pandemic and self-selection bias.
Evidence: "The strongest correlation (r > 0.8) is between questions E8_2 and E8_3... The analysis shows a moderate to strong correlation between many of the questions... A t-Test was performed... the responses were not equal (P(T<=t) < α: 0.025336 < 0.05)."
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that the ML-GBL approach is effective for teaching BP concepts and increasing student engagement. They recommend future research to compare this method with other pedagogical strategies and to identify student characteristics best suited for this approach. They also note the challenge of delivering experiential learning in remote environments and suggest adaptations for non-colocated students.
Evidence: "Future work should consider evaluating the effectiveness of these MLGBL activities compared to other pedagogic methods... Future work should seek adaptations to accommodate these challenges [remote delivery]."

# Summary

This article presents a pedagogical intervention designed to teach business process (BP) modeling and the integration of information systems (IS) within an introductory undergraduate Information Systems course. The authors argue that traditional teaching methods often fail to convey the multidisciplinary nature of BPs, leading to limited student understanding. To address this, they developed a multi-level game-based learning (ML-GBL) framework consisting of four sequential exercises that progressively increase in complexity.

The methodology begins with two low-stakes physical games. The first exercise involves inspecting bags of M&Ms to identify defective candies, teaching students foundational BPMN 2.0 concepts such as start/end events, activities, and gateways. The second exercise uses card sorting to introduce the concept of roles and pools, demonstrating how responsibilities are assigned within a process. These initial games require minimal technical setup and focus on critical thinking and basic notation.

The third exercise escalates complexity by having student teams design and manufacture gift products using craft materials. This activity introduces dependent discrete processes, requiring students to manage concurrent tasks and understand the relationship between predecessor and successor activities. Students create high-level BPMN diagrams using Cawemo®, a collaborative online tool, reinforcing the link between physical actions and process modeling.

The final exercise utilizes an SAP® Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) simulation game. Student teams operate a simulated water bottle distribution company, making decisions on pricing, inventory, and advertising. Unlike previous exercises, students are not provided with explicit BP descriptions; instead, they must infer the underlying business processes from their interactions with the ERP system. This stage introduces advanced concepts such as data flow, parallelism, and time-initiated events. Students are tasked with creating comprehensive BPMN diagrams that include the IS as a distinct role within the process.

To evaluate effectiveness, the authors surveyed 86 students across two course sections. Of these, 63 students completed an eleven-item Likert scale survey assessing their perceptions of learning and studying. Results indicated strong positive correlations between engagement with the initial games and perceived learning benefits. Students reported that hands-on activities made abstract concepts more tangible and improved their understanding of BP diagrams. However, statistical analysis revealed significant differences between the two classes. Class 2, which experienced remote delivery for the final simulation due to the pandemic, showed lower response consistency and different mean scores compared to Class 1. The authors attribute these discrepancies to self-selection bias in survey participation, variations in instructional delivery, environmental stressors associated with remote learning, and inherent differences in student demographics.

The study concludes that the ML-GBL approach is effective in enhancing student engagement and comprehension of BP concepts. It highlights the value of scaffolding complex topics through simple, relatable activities before introducing technical simulations. The authors acknowledge limitations related to sample bias and the challenges of replicating experiential learning in virtual environments, suggesting future research focus on comparative pedagogical studies and adaptations for remote instruction.
