### Summary
# **Book list: How Dungeons & Dragons in your library aids social emotional learning. (2023, May 7). *MacKids School & Library*. https://www.mackidsschoolandlibrary.com/book-list-how-dungeons-dragons-in-your-library-aids-social-emotional-learning/**

This MacKids School & Library post is a publisher-facing book list for librarians, teachers, and school-library staff interested in Dungeons & Dragons-adjacent reading recommendations. It opens by claiming that D&D can help kids and teens develop math, reading comprehension, mental health, storytelling, character creation, role-playing, identity exploration, and social behavior. It also notes the renewed popularity of D&D through *Stranger Things*, actual-play podcasts, TikTok, Critical Role, and after-school or library clubs.

The article frames D&D as especially appealing for neurodiverse and queer youth because it can offer a safer social space for friendship, identity exploration, pronoun experimentation, and participation in a world where diversity and friendship matter. It briefly points readers to International Literacy Association resources on D&D instructions, webinars, skill-building, and classroom resources. However, it does not cite specific studies, describe a library program, or provide original evidence for its social-emotional learning claims.

Most of the post is a readers' advisory list of fantasy, adventure, graphic novel, and quest-oriented books that may appeal to children and teens who play or like D&D. The list includes *The Adventure Zone: Here There Be Gerblins*, *Fart Quest*, *Dungeon Critters*, *Complete the Quest: The Poisonous Library*, *The Sunbearer Trials*, *Six of Crows*, *Theo Tan and the Fox Spirit*, *The Well*, *Prunella and the Cursed Skull Ring*, *The Golden Age*, *Little Thieves*, *The Marvellers*, *Dauntless*, *A Blade So Black*, *Cat's Cradle: The Golden Twine*, *Girl Giant and the Monkey King*, *They Threw Us Away*, *Con Quest!*, and *Julia's House for Lost Creatures*. The annotations emphasize quests, teamwork, fantasy worlds, magical companions, heists, identity discovery, fandom, and books that resemble or complement role-playing campaigns.

For the larger GBLS project, the post is useful as practice ephemera rather than review evidence. It shows how a children's publisher imagines D&D library clubs as part of a broader advisory ecology: game play can lead to fantasy reading, graphic novels, podcast fandom, displays, and school-library conversations about belonging and identity. A librarian could use the list to build a D&D display, recommend read-alikes to players, or connect a TTRPG club to collection development. The post also reinforces that D&D programs often sit at the intersection of play, reading, SEL, neurodiversity, queer youth belonging, and fandom.

Its limits are significant. The piece is promotional, product-centered, and not research-based. It does not report patron outcomes, staff practice, facilitation methods, access barriers, safety tools, collection data, or program assessment. It should therefore not be used as evidence that D&D in libraries improves social-emotional learning, but it can remain in the background as an example of advisory and publisher discourse around D&D-oriented library service.
