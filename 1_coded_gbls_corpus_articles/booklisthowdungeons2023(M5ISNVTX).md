# Summary

This 2023 article from *Booklist*, titled "How Dungeons & Dragons in Your Library Aids Social Emotional Learning," serves as a curatorial resource for librarians and educators. The primary objective is to advocate for the integration of Dungeons & Dragons (D&D) into library programming by highlighting its educational and psychological benefits, specifically in the realm of social-emotional learning (SEL). The author argues that D&D is not merely a recreational activity but a tool that enhances math skills, reading comprehension, mental health, and social behaviors among children and teens.

The article posits that D&D has experienced a resurgence in popular culture, driven by media such as *Stranger Things*, the *Adventure Zone* podcast, and streaming platforms like Critical Role. This cultural shift has introduced role-playing games to a new generation, offering them a platform to explore identity, including experimenting with pronouns, and to alleviate social anxiety. The text explicitly notes that D&D is particularly beneficial for neurodiverse and queer gamers, providing a "safe place where diversity and friendship is more powerful in a world of social challenges."

Methodologically, the article does not present empirical data or original research. Instead, it functions as a thematic booklist, recommending titles that resonate with the mechanics and themes of D&D. The author selects books that feature quests, character development, teamwork, and fantasy elements, aiming to bridge the gap between gaming interests and traditional reading materials. The list includes a diverse range of formats, such as graphic novels, middle-grade fiction, and young adult literature, targeting age groups from 4 to 18 years old.

Key recommendations include *The Adventure Zone: Here There Be Gerblins*, described as a primer for storytelling; *Fart Quest* and *Dungeon Critters*, which blend humor with adventure; and *Six of Crows*, noted for its heist narrative similar to D&D campaigns. Other titles like *Con Quest!* and *Julia’s House for Lost Creatures* are highlighted for their focus on community building and inclusive fantasy settings. The article emphasizes that these books can serve as "crossover titles" for teens and help librarians create engaging displays that appeal to students who may not typically engage with library resources.

The implications for library services are clear: the author encourages libraries to host D&D events and after-school clubs to foster social-emotional growth. By providing access to both the game and related literature, libraries can support students in developing critical skills such as collaboration, problem-solving, and self-expression. The article concludes by directing readers to additional resources from the International Literacy Association (ILA) for further guidance on implementing D&D in educational settings. Overall, the piece underscores the value of recognizing gaming as a legitimate avenue for literacy and social development in library contexts.
