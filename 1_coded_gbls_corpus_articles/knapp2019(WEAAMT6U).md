### Summary
# **Knapp, N. F. (2019). Using technology to foster "real reading" in the school library and beyond. *Knowledge Quest, 48*(1), 54-59.**

Knapp argues that school librarians' literacy and technology roles should not be treated as competing responsibilities. The article is not primarily about game-based library services, but it is useful for GBLS because it shows how digital tools, including game-based reading programs and game-related online texts, can support school-library literacy work. Knapp begins from the concern that technology use may distract from reading practice or from school librarians' mission of helping students become lifelong readers. She responds that technology can support traditional literacy development when used deliberately.

The article identifies five ways technology can foster "real reading." First, technology can make basic literacy practice more effective and more engaging. Knapp explicitly names game-based programs for early readers, including commercial and free reading games, as useful add-ons for vocabulary, decoding, word recognition, and repetitive practice. She cautions that such programs should not become the primary means of teaching reading, but argues that they can motivate novice readers to practice skills that flash cards and worksheets often make disengaging.

Second, technology increases the number and variety of texts available to readers. Knapp discusses e-books, free online book repositories, student-created books, and online informational texts. For GBLS, the most relevant point is her argument that school librarians can capitalize on students' fascination with a videogame or movie by guiding them toward related websites and fan fiction written in favorite imaginary worlds. She names fan fiction sites, teen writing platforms, sports sites, craft sites, and other online spaces as legitimate routes into reading when librarians help students judge quality, appropriateness, and credibility.

Third, technology can scaffold texts for struggling readers and writers through text-to-speech, translation, read-aloud, speech-to-text, e-book supports, and accessibility tools. Fourth, it can personalize and differentiate instruction by offering texts at different reading levels, multilingual supports, simplified reference articles, tutorials, and homework-help resources. Fifth, it can make reading social through student-written reviews, QR codes linking to reviews, private reading groups, likes/dislikes, book talks, book trailers, YouTube, Amazon, Goodreads, and other literacy-focused platforms.

For the GBLS project, Knapp is best used as a supporting literacy source rather than as evidence about game programs. It helps explain why games, game-related stories, fan communities, and digital reading practices can belong in a school-library literacy ecology. A student who reads fan fiction connected to a videogame, plays a reading-skills game, records a book trailer, or reviews a book on a social platform is participating in a broader network of technology-supported reading practices that school librarians can guide.

The limitations are clear. The article is a practitioner-facing overview, not an empirical study, and games are only one part of its broader argument about technology and reading. It should not be used to claim that game-based reading tools improve literacy outcomes on their own. Its value is in the service logic: school librarians can connect technology, games, fandom, accessibility, differentiated texts, and social sharing to support reading motivation and access.
