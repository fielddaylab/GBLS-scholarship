## Findings
The article identifies five key mechanisms through which technology enhances literacy:
1. **Engagement in Basic Skills:** Game-based software (e.g., PBS Kids, EducationWorld) makes repetitive practice of decoding and vocabulary more engaging than traditional worksheets, serving as effective add-on components for novice readers.
2. **Access to Texts:** E-books and online resources (e.g., Project Gutenberg, International Children’s Digital Library) increase the variety and availability of texts, offering privacy for struggling readers and access to diverse cultural materials.
3. **Scaffolding:** Tools like text-to-speech (Google Read&Write), speech-to-text, and translation features support struggling readers, English language learners, and special needs students by making complex texts more accessible.
4. **Differentiation:** Platforms like Newsela and Simple English Wikipedia allow educators to provide the same content at varying reading levels, enabling personalized instruction for diverse learners.
5. **Social Reading:** Social media platforms (e.g., Goodreads, Biblionasium) and digital sharing tools foster a "culture of reading" by allowing students to share reviews, book talks, and recommendations with peers.

# Summary

Nancy Flanagan Knapp’s 2019 article, "Using Technology to Foster 'Real Reading' in the School Library and Beyond," addresses a prevalent concern among school librarians: the perceived tension between integrating digital technology and fostering traditional literacy skills. Drawing on two decades of research confirming the positive impact of adequately staffed school libraries on student achievement, Knapp argues that technology and literacy are complementary forces. The article serves as a professional guide, outlining five specific ways librarians can leverage digital tools to enhance reading proficiency, engagement, and accessibility for students of all levels.

First, Knapp posits that technology can make the practice of basic literacy skills more effective and engaging. While traditional methods like flashcards are often disengaging, game-based software programs (such as those from PBS Kids or EducationWorld) provide repetitive practice in vocabulary and decoding within motivating contexts. The author notes that while these games should not replace primary instruction, they serve as valuable supplementary tools for novice readers.

Second, the article highlights technology’s role in expanding access to texts. E-books and online libraries (e.g., Project Gutenberg, Unite for Literacy) offer a vast array of free resources, including classics and multicultural materials. Knapp emphasizes that digital reading provides privacy for struggling readers, eliminating the embarrassment associated with slow progress or "unpopular" reading choices. Librarians are encouraged to guide students through the technical process of accessing these resources, thereby normalizing e-reading as part of their daily digital lives.

Third, technology offers critical scaffolding for struggling readers and writers. Built-in features such as text-to-speech, speech-to-text, and translation tools (e.g., Google Read&Write) can define words, pronounce phrases, and translate texts into multiple languages. These tools are particularly beneficial for English language learners and students with special needs, allowing them to access complex digital texts that might otherwise be inaccessible. Knapp suggests librarians provide professional development to help teachers utilize these features effectively.

Fourth, the article discusses how technology enables personalized and differentiated instruction. Platforms like Newsela and Simple English Wikipedia allow educators to present the same content at varying reading levels (e.g., different Lexile levels). This ensures that all students in a classroom can engage with shared instructional materials at an appropriate difficulty level, supporting diverse learning needs without segregating students by ability.

Finally, Knapp argues that technology can foster the social aspects of reading. Digital platforms such as Goodreads and Biblionasium allow students to share reviews, track their reading progress, and connect with peers over shared literary interests. By creating a "culture of reading" through digital means, librarians can motivate new readers and encourage avid readers to share their enthusiasm.

In conclusion, Knapp asserts that school librarians do not need to choose between technology and traditional literacy. Instead, by incentivizing practice, expanding text availability, scaffolding access, differentiating instruction, and promoting social reading, librarians can use technology to make literacy more accessible, exciting, and inclusive for all students. The article serves as a call to action for librarians to take a leading role in integrating these tools to support holistic literacy development.
