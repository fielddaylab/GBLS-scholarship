## Findings
All participants reported that TRPGs positively impacted at least one literacy skill. Speaking was the most universally improved skill, followed by reading, listening, and writing. Long-term gamers (9–35 years of experience) reported positive impacts across all four literacy domains, whereas short-term gamers (2–3 years) showed mixed results, particularly in writing. Regarding reading interests, TRPGs generally increased the quantity of leisure reading but did not significantly influence genre preferences; participants primarily read science fiction and fantasy. Educators played little to no role in introducing or supporting gaming culture, with participants noting this was not an obstacle. Participants also reported improvements in non-literacy skills such as critical thinking, math, social confidence, and empathy.
Evidence: "All participants named speaking as a literacy skill positively impacted by TRPG playing... All of the long-term TRPG gamers saw a positive influence in all four literacy areas... In most cases, playing TRPGs also led to a greater quantity of leisure reading. However, TRPGs did not appear to influence which genres participants read... none saw a need for educators to take an active role in encouraging TRPG gaming among students." # Summary

This graduate research paper by Stefanie L. B. Kaylor investigates the intersection of tabletop role-playing games (TRPGs), such as *Dungeons & Dragons* and *Pathfinder*, with adolescent literacy development and leisure reading habits. The study addresses a gap in existing literature, which has largely focused on TRPGs within formal classroom settings, by examining the impact of leisure-time gaming on players' self-reported literacy skills and reading interests.

The methodology employed a qualitative design utilizing semi-structured interviews with seven participants from central Iowa. The sample was purposively selected to include individuals who played TRPGs during their teenage years, comprising two current high school students and five adults. A significant limitation noted by the author is that all participants self-identified as male, and the researcher’s own background as a TRPG gamer may introduce bias. Participants were categorized into "short-term" gamers (2–3 years of experience) and "long-term" gamers (9–35 years of experience) to assess the impact of duration on skill development.

The findings indicate a generally positive correlation between TRPG participation and literacy skill enhancement. All seven participants reported improvements in at least one area of literacy: reading, writing, speaking, or listening. Speaking was the most consistently improved skill, attributed to the constant verbal communication required during gameplay, including character role-play and group negotiation. Long-term gamers reported positive impacts across all four literacy domains, whereas short-term gamers showed more variable results, particularly regarding writing skills. The study highlights specific mechanisms for improvement: reading rulebooks motivated players with dyslexia or low interest to engage with complex texts; the need to comprehend technical and narrative elements improved fluency and comprehension strategies; and the collaborative nature of the game enhanced listening and social communication skills.

Regarding leisure reading, TRPGs appeared to increase the quantity of reading among participants but did not significantly alter genre preferences. Most participants who read for leisure favored science fiction and fantasy, genres often aligned with TRPG settings. However, the connection was not universal; some participants reported no change in reading habits, while one noted a decrease in reading due to increased interest in writing.

A critical finding concerns the role of educators. Participants reported that schools and teachers played minimal to no role in introducing them to TRPGs or supporting their gaming activities. Gaming was primarily facilitated by peers and family members outside of school hours. Despite this lack of institutional support, participants did not view it as a barrier to engagement. The author argues that educators, particularly librarians, should actively welcome TRPGs in library spaces. Providing access to expensive rulebooks can lower financial barriers for students, and recognizing the educational value of gaming can help engage reluctant readers. The study concludes that while TRPGs are primarily recreational, they offer substantial benefits for literacy, critical thinking, and social development, warranting further large-scale empirical research.
