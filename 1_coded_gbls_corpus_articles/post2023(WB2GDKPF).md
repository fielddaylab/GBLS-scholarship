## Findings
The study found that descriptive cataloging for digital indie games closely resembles practices for other electronic resources, with adaptations needed to distinguish text-based interactivity from visual media. Subject cataloging requires a hybrid approach, combining Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) for narrative content with OLAC genre terms for gameplay mechanics; however, existing controlled vocabularies often lack terminology aligned with community usage (e.g., "interactive fiction"). Name authority control revealed a significant gap in the LCNAF for indie game creators. The authors created 33 new name authority records to represent individual contributors (authors, editors, programmers), arguing that this granular attribution is essential for accurately reflecting the production model of indie games, which differs from the corporate-centric attribution of mainstream titles.

## Implications

The study suggests that current OLAC best practices are a strong foundation but may require supplementation to handle the dynamic nature of digital updates and the specific production realities of independent developers.

# Summary

This article presents an action research study examining the cataloging challenges associated with digitally distributed independent (indie) video games, specifically focusing on text-based narrative titles. As digital distribution supplants physical media and indie game production expands, library cataloging practices must adapt to represent these diverse cultural artifacts accurately. The authors argue that while existing guidelines from the Online Audiovisual Catalogers (OLAC) provide a necessary foundation, they often prioritize mainstream commercial games produced by large corporate entities. This study seeks to bridge that gap by analyzing how descriptive cataloging, subject access, and name authority control can be tailored for indie narrative games, which share characteristics with both traditional literature and interactive software.

The methodology involved the creation of 18 original MARC bibliographic records for games published by Choice of Games, a small publisher specializing in text-based interactive fiction. Two co-authors performed the cataloging using standard library systems (Connexion Client and WorldShare Management Services), while the full research team engaged in semi-structured interviews to reflect on the process. The resulting records were compared against existing practices at the University of Michigan Library, which holds a significant collection of digital narrative games.

In terms of descriptive cataloging, the authors found that digital indie games resemble other electronic resources more than physical media. They utilized MARC fields to designate these titles as computer programs with text-based content, distinguishing them from mainstream games typically coded as moving images. The study highlights the complexity of version control in digital distribution; while minor patches do not require new records, significant expansions or platform-specific releases may necessitate distinct bibliographic entries. The authors note that indie publishers like Choice of Games often provide clear bibliographic metadata similar to book copyright pages, facilitating accurate description compared to the opaque credit structures of many mainstream titles.

Subject cataloging presented a hybrid challenge. Because narrative games are driven by story and choice, they require subject headings that reflect their literary content (e.g., "Cyberpunk culture," "Detectives") alongside genre terms that capture their interactive mechanics (e.g., "Adventure games," "Role-playing games"). The authors observed that existing controlled vocabularies, such as LCSH and the OLAC Video Game Genre Vocabulary, often use terminology out of sync with the gaming community. For instance, terms like "Plot-your-own stories" fail to convey the interactive depth of digital narrative games. Consequently, the researchers supplemented controlled headings with uncontrolled terms used by players, such as "interactive fiction" and "text-based games," to enhance discoverability.

A significant finding pertains to name authority control. Mainstream video games are often attributed to corporate entities due to large, specialized development teams. In contrast, indie narrative games frequently involve smaller teams or individual authors with distinct creative roles. The study revealed a lack of existing Library of Congress Name Authority File (LCNAF) records for many indie creators. To address this, the authors created 33 new name authority records for individuals involved in writing, editing, and programming. This granular attribution is argued to be essential for accurately representing the production model of indie games and integrating these creators into the broader bibliographic universe.

The implications for library practice are substantial. The authors recommend that catalogers adopt a flexible approach, mixing controlled vocabularies with community-derived terminology to support user discovery. They emphasize the importance of creating name authority records for indie game developers to ensure consistent access points across institutions. Furthermore, the study suggests that as digital distribution evolves, cataloging standards may need further refinement to handle dynamic updates and diverse release models characteristic of independent game development. Ultimately, the research advocates for recognizing indie games not merely as software, but as significant cultural works deserving of rigorous and nuanced bibliographic representation.
