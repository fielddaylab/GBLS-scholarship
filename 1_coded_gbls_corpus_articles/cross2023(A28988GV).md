## Purpose
To explore and detail the demographics, motivations, and preferences of board gamers, and to compile an open-access dataset for use by researchers, educators, clinicians, and game designers. The study aims to fill a gap in existing literature regarding the specific characteristics of the modern board gaming population.

## Method
A quantitative survey design was utilized. Data collection occurred over two months via Qualtrics. The survey instrument was developed through consultations with players, designers, distributors, academics, and clinicians, as well as findings from a Delphi panel. Participants responded to questions regarding demographics, gaming experience, preferences for game elements (length, player count, style, mechanics, themes), motivations, and social importance of gaming. Statistical analysis included Friedman tests and Durbin-Conover pairwise comparisons for non-normally distributed data.

Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 1,603 individuals who completed the questionnaire. Participants were recruited from board gaming social media groups (Reddit, Facebook), forums, and a large game distributor’s mailing list (Asmodee). The sample was predominantly male (74.2%), white (60.6%), and highly educated (over 50% with at least an undergraduate degree). The mean age was 32.38 years (SD = 9.21). Geographic distribution included significant representation from the UK/USA (38.5%), China (25.5%), and France (18.3%). Approximately 27% of participants reported at least one mental health or neurodevelopmental condition, with depression (13.1%) and anxiety (12.2%) being the most common.

Confidence: high

## Findings
Demographically, the population aligns with assumptions of being primarily middle-aged, educated, white males, though with notable diversity in nationality. In terms of preferences, most gamers identified as mid-core players preferring in-person play (58%) over online formats. Preferred game characteristics included 3-4 players, durations of 30-60 minutes, and European or hybrid styles over American styles. Participants rated gameplay and mechanics as the most important criteria for game choice, while components were least important. Social interaction and strategizing were the primary motivations for play, whereas competition was the least motivating factor. Mechanically, action point allowance, deck building, and worker placement were preferred; player elimination was the least desired mechanic. Thematically, fantasy and sci-fi were rated highest, while war themes were rated lowest. Subgroup analyses revealed distinct preferences, such as women preferring gateway and abstract games with animal themes, and individuals with depression showing higher motivation for escapism and a dislike for war-themed games or player elimination mechanics.

Confidence: high

## Implications
The dataset provides a resource for tailoring board game selections to specific populations, including those with mental health conditions or neurodivergence. It supports clinicians and educators in selecting appropriate games for therapeutic or educational interventions by linking demographics and conditions to specific mechanical and thematic preferences. For game designers, the data offers insights into market preferences, potentially aiding in the development of serious games or titles targeted at underrepresented groups, such as women or non-binary individuals. The authors suggest that understanding these nuances can improve player engagement and well-being outcomes.

# Summary

This article presents a large-scale empirical study on the demographics, preferences, and motivations of modern board gamers. The primary objective was to address a lack of detailed data regarding this population by creating an open-access dataset comprising over 1,600 participants. The authors argue that understanding these factors is crucial for game designers, educators, and clinicians who utilize board games for entertainment, education, or therapeutic intervention.

The methodology involved a quantitative survey distributed via Qualtrics over a two-month period. Recruitment targeted active community members through social media groups, forums, and distributor mailing lists. The instrument was refined through consultations with industry professionals and academics. The final sample of 1,603 participants was predominantly male (74.2%), white (60.6%), and highly educated, with a mean age of 32.38 years. Notably, the sample included significant representation from China (25.5%) and France (18.3%), alongside the UK and USA. A substantial portion of the sample (27%) reported having a mental health or neurodevelopmental condition, most commonly depression and anxiety.

Key findings indicate that the majority of participants identified as mid-core players who prefer in-person gameplay over digital formats. Preferences skewed toward games with 3-4 players, durations of 30-60 minutes, and European or hybrid styles rather than American styles. Gameplay and mechanics were cited as the most critical factors in game selection, whereas physical components were least important. Social interaction emerged as the primary motivation for playing, surpassing competition. Mechanically, participants favored action point allowance, deck building, and worker placement, while strongly disliking player elimination. Thematically, fantasy and sci-fi were preferred, with war themes receiving the lowest ratings.

The study further analyzed subgroups to inform tailored interventions. For instance, women showed a higher preference for gateway and abstract games with animal themes compared to men. Individuals with depression reported higher motivation for escapism and a distinct aversion to war themes and player elimination mechanics, preferring instead horror themes and storytelling mechanics. These granular insights allow clinicians to match game features to the specific needs of patients with conditions such as autism or ADHD.

The authors acknowledge limitations, including potential sampling bias due to online recruitment and English-language proficiency requirements. They also note the gender imbalance in the sample, which mirrors broader industry trends but may limit generalizability for female and non-binary populations. Despite these constraints, the dataset offers a valuable tool for cross-referencing demographic data with game design elements, supporting evidence-based choices in serious game development and therapeutic practice. The open-access nature of the data encourages further research into the intersection of board gaming, mental health, and educational outcomes.
