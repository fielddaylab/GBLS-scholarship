### Summary
# **Cross, L., Piovesan, A., Sousa, M., Wright, P., & Atherton, G. (2023). Your move: An open access dataset of over 1500 board gamer's demographics, preferences and motivations. *Simulation & Gaming.* https://doi.org/10.1177/10468781231189493**

Cross, Piovesan, Sousa, Wright, and Atherton report and release an open dataset on board gamer demographics, preferences, and motivations. The article is not a library study, but it is useful adjacent evidence for GBLS because many library tabletop services must make practical choices about which games to collect, circulate, teach, or use in programs for different audiences. The authors argue that modern board games have grown commercially and culturally, and that games are increasingly used for serious purposes in education, therapy, mental health, social interaction, reading, numeracy, and other interventions. Their core claim is that people selecting or designing board games need better information about who board gamers are and which mechanics, themes, formats, and motivations different groups prefer.

The study used an English-language Qualtrics survey of 1,603 board gamers recruited through board gaming social media groups, forums, subreddits, Facebook groups, and a large game distributor. The sample was deliberately aimed at active hobby participants rather than the general public. Respondents reported demographics, diagnoses of mental health or neurodevelopmental conditions, gaming experience, hours played per month, preferred platform, preferred player count, game length, components, style, classification, type, mechanics, themes, choice criteria, motivations, social importance, and sense of community. The survey categories were developed through consultation with players, designers, distributors, academics, clinicians, and BoardGameGeek terminology.

The broad demographic picture confirmed some existing assumptions while complicating them. The sample was mostly men, mostly white, highly educated, and concentrated in the 18-45 age range, with a mean age just over 32. More than half had an undergraduate degree and about one-fifth had a postgraduate degree. At the same time, the dataset included a large Chinese subgroup, a substantial number of women, and more than one-quarter of respondents who reported at least one mental health or neurodevelopmental condition. The most common reported conditions were depression, anxiety, autism, dyslexia, and ADHD.

The preference findings are especially useful for service design. Most respondents identified as casual or mid-core/core players, played 5-29 hours per month, and preferred in-person play over online play. They most preferred games for 3-4 players and 30-60 minute sessions, with cards and boards rated above dice or app/web hybrids. Competitive all-vs-all and fully cooperative styles were both rated highly, while one-vs-all was lowest. European and hybrid games were preferred over American-style games. In mechanics, action point allowance, deck/bag/pool building, worker placement, and engine building were strong, while player elimination and memory were weak. Fantasy, science fiction, and adventure themes rated highest, while war, horror, and transport themes rated lower overall. Gameplay and mechanics mattered most when choosing games; components mattered least. Social interaction and strategising were the strongest motivations, while competition was lowest.

For GBLS, the article helps libraries think beyond simply adding "popular board games." Its findings suggest that group size, session length, social motivation, player elimination, theme, complexity, and accessibility matter when designing programs or circulating collections. Libraries planning a one-shot game night, a recurring board game group, a school or youth program, a wellness-oriented program, or an intergenerational event can use this article as a reminder to match game choices to audience, goals, and local constraints. The dataset also supports inclusion work: different age groups, genders, and neurodivergent or mental-health subgroups may prefer different mechanics, themes, lengths, and motivations, so a collection or program built around one imagined "board gamer" can quietly exclude many potential participants.

The article should be used carefully. Because participants were recruited from hobby board game spaces and received a Steam board game key, the sample is not representative of library patrons, casual families, non-English-speaking communities, or people who might try board games only because the library lowers access barriers. The dataset can inform selection and design, but it should not substitute for local needs assessment, patron feedback, or inclusive facilitation. In the GBLS review, Cross et al. are best treated as adjacent audience and design evidence for tabletop services rather than direct evidence about library program outcomes.
