# Summary

Breanne Kirsch’s 2019 editorial, "Virtual Reality: The Next Big Thing for Libraries to Consider," reflects on the growing prominence of virtual reality (VR) in higher education and its implications for academic libraries. Drawing from her attendance at the EDUCAUSE Annual Conference and her role as University Librarian at Briar Cliff University, Kirsch provides a practical overview of VR trends, content creation methods, and operational considerations for library implementation. The article serves as an introductory guide for librarians seeking to understand and integrate VR technologies into their service models.

Kirsch observes that VR has become a significant focus in higher education technology, evidenced by the numerous sessions dedicated to it at EDUCAUSE. She notes that while VR is still emerging in many institutions, certain disciplines are already leveraging it extensively. Health sciences libraries are identified as leaders in this space, utilizing VR for medical procedural training where real-world practice opportunities may be limited. Similarly, STEM fields are adopting VR to simulate laboratory environments, allowing students to engage with expensive or complex equipment virtually without the associated financial burden of purchasing physical assets.

The article distinguishes between high-fidelity VR content creation and more accessible alternatives. Kirsch acknowledges that developing immersive experiences using professional tools like Unity is resource-intensive and technically demanding. However, she highlights lower-barrier options for librarians, such as using 360-degree cameras or even smartphones equipped with apps like Google Street View. She shares her own experience of creating a 360-degree tour of her library using an iPhone. While she admits the resulting quality was insufficient for viewing on dedicated VR headsets like the Oculus Go, she characterizes it as a viable, low-cost entry point for libraries interested in virtual tours. These tours could potentially be used to teach information literacy or search strategies within a virtual environment, although Kirsch notes that current technology may make this challenging.

A significant portion of the text is dedicated to the practical and ethical considerations of offering VR services, specifically through the circulation of standalone headsets like the Oculus Go. Kirsch outlines several risks that libraries must manage. Health and safety are primary concerns; she lists motion sickness, dizziness, and potential epileptic seizures as adverse reactions that patrons may experience. She emphasizes the importance of informing users of these risks prior to use and instructing them to stop immediately if they feel unwell. Hygiene is another operational issue, addressed by providing cleaning wipes for shared devices.

Privacy and financial security are also critical. Kirsch explains that VR headsets often require association with a tablet or smartphone for app management, necessitating passcodes to prevent unauthorized purchases using linked credit cards. She also raises privacy concerns regarding social VR applications, leading her decision not to install such apps on circulated devices. Furthermore, she warns about the psychological impact of certain content, noting that realistic horror experiences can cause distress, requiring staff to ensure users are prepared for the intensity of the simulations. Staffing is identified as a final key consideration; trained personnel are necessary to assist students with device operation and troubleshooting. Kirsch notes that her library trains student workers to facilitate this support.

Despite these challenges, Kirsch argues that the benefits of VR integration outweigh the risks. She positions libraries as natural hubs for supporting emerging technologies, drawing parallels to their established roles in providing access to tablets, laptops, 3D printers, and makerspaces. Her recommendations are scalable: libraries with limited resources can begin by circulating a single headset or creating simple virtual tours, while those with more funding might establish dedicated VR labs or support faculty in creating complex VR content. The article concludes with an optimistic outlook on the future of VR in education, urging librarians to prepare for continued growth in this sector, particularly with the advent of wireless headsets. By addressing both the opportunities and the logistical hurdles, Kirsch provides a balanced perspective for librarians evaluating the adoption of virtual reality services.
