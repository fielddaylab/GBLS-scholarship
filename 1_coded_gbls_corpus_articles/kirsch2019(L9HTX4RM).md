### Summary
# **Kirsch, B. (2019). Virtual reality: The next big thing for libraries to consider. *Information Technology and Libraries, 38*(4), 4-5. https://doi.org/10.6017/ital.v38i4.11847**

Kirsch's short *Information Technology and Libraries* editorial is a practitioner reflection on virtual reality after the 2019 EDUCAUSE Annual Conference. It is useful for the GBLS review because it captures a moment when higher education libraries were beginning to treat VR as a service area akin to makerspaces, device lending, laptops, tablets, 3D printers, and other emerging technologies. The article is not an assessment study, but it names practical service choices and risks that recur in later VR library literature.

The piece begins by observing that EDUCAUSE had more than 20 programs and sessions about virtual reality, alongside broader technology trends such as esports in high schools and higher education. Kirsch notes that some institutions were creating VR content while others were using pre-created content. Content creation was especially visible in medical and health sciences contexts, where VR can let students practice procedures they may encounter infrequently in workplaces, and in STEM contexts, where VR laboratories may simulate expensive equipment or lab experiences.

Kirsch gives particular attention to 360-degree content as a lower-barrier entry point. Creating full VR content in tools such as Unity can be difficult and time-consuming, but educators can use 360-degree cameras, Matterport scans, or even smartphone tools such as Google Street View to build virtual settings. Kirsch reports creating a free, basic 360-degree library tour with an iPhone and Google Street View, noting that it was not high quality enough for headset VR but was a useful budget-conscious starting point. This distinction matters for GBLS because it separates scalable virtual orientation and tour work from more intensive immersive content development.

The article then turns to library service models. Libraries might create 360-degree tours, develop higher-quality VR experiences, teach information literacy or search strategies in VR environments, offer VR labs, circulate standalone headsets such as Oculus Go or Oculus Quest, or support local VR content creation with cameras and software. Kirsch explicitly compares the opportunity to makerspaces: libraries are well situated to support VR in education if they pair access with trained support.

The most concrete GBLS contribution is the risk and operations checklist from Kirsch's own library, which circulated an Oculus Go. She names health risks such as motion sickness, dizziness, and epileptic seizures; the need to tell students to stop use if they have adverse reactions; cleaning wipes and sanitation for shared equipment; app updates and paid-app controls through associated tablets or phones; passcodes to prevent unauthorized purchases; privacy concerns with social apps; the emotional realism of scary apps; and the need for trained staff or student workers who can teach others to use the headset. These concerns align closely with later implementation studies and help make VR a managed library service rather than a novelty purchase.

For the larger GBLS project, Kirsch should be used as practical framing. It supports a conservative implementation argument: libraries can start small with a 360-degree tour or a circulating headset, but they still need policy, training, content selection, privacy decisions, sanitation, safety warnings, and staff capacity. Its limitations are clear: it is brief, anecdotal, and does not report patron outcomes or usage data. Still, it is a useful bridge between enthusiasm for VR and the mundane infrastructure that determines whether patrons can actually use it responsibly.
