Specifically, it seeks to determine how these experts describe and organize visual styles, whether the existing taxonomy reflects their terminology, and what new terms should be added.

# Summary

This article reports on a qualitative study investigating how creative professionals describe and categorize the visual styles of video games, with the goal of improving metadata standards for game retrieval. The authors argue that existing classification systems, such as Dewey Decimal or standard cataloging rules, are ill-suited for visual media, and current digital platforms lack robust search options beyond genre or platform. While prior research established a Video Game Metadata Schema (VGMS) and a visual style taxonomy tested on general players, this study focuses on expert users—those involved in the creation process—to assess the taxonomy's relevance to professionals.

The methodology employed a think-aloud card sorting technique with eight participants from the DigiPen Institute of Technology. The sample included six artists and two programmers, all actively engaged in game development projects. Participants were presented with 48 cards featuring screenshots of video games representing a broad array of visual styles. They were instructed to sort the images into groups based on perceived visual style similarities and to label each group using their own terminology. The sessions were video-recorded, and the resulting verbalizations and written labels were analyzed using NVIVO software to extract descriptive terms.

The analysis yielded 121 distinct terms, which clustered into four prominent categories: Abstract, Realistic, Stylized, and Text. "Stylized" functioned as an umbrella term encompassing subcategories such as Anime, Cartoon, Handicraft, and Lego. Notably, participants frequently generated technique-related descriptors (e.g., Cel-shaded, Pixel art, Low poly), which had been removed from the current VGMS taxonomy to simplify it for general users. The authors note that this omission may hinder retrieval for expert users who rely on technical distinctions. Additionally, 56 terms were unique to single participants, indicating a "long tail" of specialized vocabulary.

A significant finding was the conflation of visual style with other metadata elements. Participants often used terms related to mood (e.g., "Depressing," "Cute"), gameplay mechanics (e.g., "Real-time strategy"), dimension (e.g., "2D"), and point of view. In cases where participants lacked specific vocabulary, they resorted to using game titles as proxies for style (e.g., labeling a screenshot "Tetris" or "Mirror’s Edge"). The physical arrangement of cards by some participants suggested that visual styles are perceived as existing on a continuum—particularly between abstract and realistic—rather than in rigid, discrete categories.

The authors conclude that the current taxonomy should be revised to better accommodate expert users. They recommend reinstating the "Technique" facet as an optional element within the VGMS. Furthermore, they propose adding an "Intention" facet to capture temporal aspects and creator intent, such as whether a pixelated style is a technological limitation of the past or a deliberate retro homage in contemporary design. The study underscores the semantic complexity of visual style description and advocates for flexible metadata schemas that can adapt to the specific needs of different user groups, from general players to professional curators and developers.
