## Summary

Sitzmann examines whether computer-based simulation games improve adult training outcomes and which features of games and instructional settings account for differences in effectiveness. She defines simulation games broadly as computer-delivered instruction that immerses trainees in decision making within an artificial environment so they can learn from the consequences of their choices. Because the boundaries between games and simulations are often blurred, the category includes interventions with varying levels of entertainment, realism, competition, and fidelity. The review is grounded in interactive cognitive complexity theory, which proposes that simulation games can affect learning by engaging cognitive and affective systems together.

The meta-analysis includes 65 independent samples and 6,476 trainees from research published or presented between 1976 and 2009, including 16 unpublished studies. It evaluates self-efficacy, declarative knowledge, procedural knowledge, and retention. The moderator analysis considers entertainment value, whether instructional content is conveyed actively or passively, whether trainees have unlimited access, whether the game is supplemental or stand-alone instruction, and whether comparison conditions use active or passive methods. Methodological moderators include random assignment, pretest-posttest design, publication status, and publication year.

Average outcomes favored simulation games: posttraining self-efficacy was reported as 20% higher, declarative knowledge 11% higher, procedural knowledge 14% higher, and retention 9% higher than comparison conditions. Those averages conceal important design effects. Active simulation-game instruction outperformed comparison conditions, while games that conveyed most instructional content passively did not. Unlimited access was associated with stronger effects than limited access. Most decisively, games used as supplements to other instruction yielded positive results, whereas stand-alone games did not outperform alternatives. Comparison groups using active instruction sometimes learned more than game groups; when game and comparison methods were matched for activity level, their effectiveness was nearly equivalent.

The review also finds strong publication bias. Published studies produced a large positive pooled effect, while unpublished studies did not show an advantage for simulation games. When moderators were considered jointly, supplemental rather than stand-alone use was the only instructional moderator with a significant main effect. Other moderators were interrelated, making it difficult to isolate their independent causal contribution. The evidence base was also thin for motivation, effort, reactions, transfer to work, and other outcomes often invoked to justify games.

Sitzmann's practical argument is therefore not that games cause learning by themselves. Simulation games work as instructional tools when their design actively engages learners, when participants have sufficient opportunities to practice, and when play is embedded in a larger program that includes prior instruction and debriefing. She emphasizes that experience alone does not guarantee learning and that reflection is needed to connect simulation events to work-related knowledge and transfer. She also notes substantial development costs and unresolved questions about return on investment.

For the larger GBLS project, these findings are useful as a warning against medium-level claims. Libraries considering simulation games for staff training, instruction, or public learning should define the learning purpose, provide facilitation and debriefing, and compare the intervention with credible active alternatives. Unlimited access and repeat practice may matter, but they create infrastructure and staffing implications that must be considered. Because the evidence concerns workplace and university training rather than library services, the article should frame the introduction's account of the broader evidence landscape and the rationale for a library-specific synthesis, not function as direct evidence about patrons or GBLS outcomes.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

**Evidentiary challenge.** The published literature strongly favors simulation games, but unpublished studies do not. The article also shows that active comparison methods can equal or outperform games. This qualifies any value-level preference for games by demonstrating that instructional design and publication processes, not the medium alone, shape the apparent outcome. Proposed response: maintain `avoid_overclaiming_evidence` and evaluate GBLS interventions against credible alternatives.

**Value tension.** The source's organizational-training frame emphasizes performance, transfer, and return on investment rather than libraries' public, cultural, and community purposes. This difference should not be treated as a defect in the source, but it limits direct application to GBLS. No change to `explicit_values.md` is proposed.

## Evidence Cautions and Limitations

The review combines heterogeneous interventions and comparison groups, and the design moderators frequently co-occur. Publication status sharply changes the result, while evidence for motivation and transfer is sparse. Percentage descriptions summarize standardized effects and should not be read as universal gains. The findings cannot establish that simulation games will produce similar outcomes in public, school, or academic library services.
