## Purpose
The article aims to statistically summarize the literature on the instructional effectiveness of computer-based simulation games for teaching work-related knowledge and skills to adults. It seeks to determine if game play improves adult work competencies and to identify which simulation game design features and instructional contexts maximize learning outcomes, addressing debates regarding their efficacy compared to other methods.
Evidence: "The goal of this study is to statistically summarize the literature on the instructional effectiveness of computer-based simulation games for teaching work-related knowledge and skills... Furthermore, it will clarify the characteristics of simulation games that are essential for maximizing learning outcomes." (p. 490)

## Method
The study employs a meta-analytic approach using Hedges and Olkin’s (1985) method. It analyzes data from 65 independent samples across 55 research reports. The author conducted literature searches in PsycInfo, ERIC, and Digital Dissertations, supplemented by manual reference list checks and contacts with experts. Effect sizes ($d$) were calculated for post-training outcomes comparing simulation game groups to comparison groups. Moderator analyses were performed using homogeneity tests ($Q_B$, $Q_W$) and weighted least squares (WLS) regression to examine theoretical and methodological moderators.

The data comprises 6,476 trainees from 65 independent samples. The participants were predominantly undergraduate students (77%), with smaller proportions of graduate students (12%), employees (5%), and military personnel (6%). The average age was 23 years, and 52% were male. Studies included only nondisabled adults aged 18 or older where training facilitated job-relevant knowledge or skills. Comparison groups varied from no-training controls to alternative instructional methods.

## Findings
Simulation games yielded significantly higher post-training self-efficacy ($d = .52$), declarative knowledge ($d = .28$), procedural knowledge ($d = .37$), and retention ($d = .22$) compared to comparison groups. Moderator analyses revealed that simulation games were more effective when they actively engaged trainees rather than passively conveying material, when trainees had unlimited access to the game, and when the game supplemented other instructional methods rather than standing alone. Conversely, simulation games were less effective than comparison methods when the comparison group received active instruction (e.g., computerized tutorials). Entertainment value did not significantly moderate learning outcomes. Strong evidence of publication bias was found, with published studies showing larger effect sizes ($d = .52$) than unpublished ones ($d = -.10$).
Evidence: "posttraining self-efﬁcacy was 20% higher, declarative knowledge was 11% higher, procedural knowledge was 14% higher, and retention was 9% higher... However, the results provide strong evidence of publication bias... Trainees learned more... when simulation games conveyed course material actively rather than passively, trainees could access the simulation game as many times as desired, and the simulation game was a supplement to other instructional methods rather than stand-alone instruction." (Abstract; pp. 508–511)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The findings suggest that simulation games are effective for enhancing adult learning outcomes, particularly when integrated into broader instructional programs and designed to promote active engagement. The significant publication bias indicates that the true effectiveness of simulation games may be lower than previously reported in published literature. Practitioners should prioritize active learning features and unlimited access over entertainment value to maximize instructional utility.
Evidence: "Accurate effect size estimates are imperative for both researchers and practitioners... This meta-analysis addressed these debates by examining both how much adult trainees learn from computer-based simulation games... and the instructional and contextual factors that contribute to higher levels of learning." (pp. 492, 512)

# Summary

This article presents a comprehensive meta-analytic examination of the instructional effectiveness of computer-based simulation games for adult training. The primary objective was to resolve conflicting evidence regarding whether simulation games enhance work-related knowledge and skills compared to other instructional methods, and to identify specific design features and contextual factors that moderate their effectiveness. The study focuses exclusively on adult populations to provide precise estimates for workplace and higher education contexts, distinguishing itself from previous reviews that often conflated child and adult samples.

The methodology involved a systematic literature search across PsycInfo, ERIC, and Digital Dissertations, supplemented by manual searches of reference lists and expert contacts. The final analysis included 65 independent samples from 55 research reports, encompassing 6,476 trainees. The sample was predominantly composed of undergraduate students (77%), with a smaller representation of graduate students, employees, and military personnel. Inclusion criteria required studies to report post-training outcomes allowing for the calculation of effect sizes ($d$), involve nondisabled adults aged 18 or older, and focus on job-relevant skills. Comparison groups varied across studies, ranging from no-training controls to alternative instructional methods such as lectures, readings, or active tutorials.

The results indicate that simulation games are generally more effective than comparison methods across key training outcomes. Specifically, trainees using simulation games demonstrated significantly higher post-training self-efficacy ($d = .52$), declarative knowledge ($d = .28$), procedural knowledge ($d = .37$), and retention ($d = .22$). These findings support theoretical frameworks such as interactive cognitive complexity theory, which posits that simulation games engage both affective and cognitive processes simultaneously.

Moderator analyses revealed critical conditions under which simulation games are most effective. Learning outcomes were significantly enhanced when the simulation game actively engaged trainees in learning rather than passively conveying information ($d = .49$ vs. $-.11$). Additionally, unlimited access to the simulation game resulted in higher learning gains compared to limited access ($d = .68$ vs. $.31$). The instructional context also played a pivotal role; simulation games were more effective when used as a supplement to other instructional methods rather than as standalone instruction ($d = .51$ vs. $-.12$). Conversely, simulation games were less effective than comparison groups when the comparison group received active instruction, particularly computerized tutorials ($d = -.70$). Notably, entertainment value did not significantly moderate learning outcomes, suggesting that intrinsic motivation features alone do not drive instructional effectiveness.

A significant limitation identified in the study is strong evidence of publication bias. Published studies reported substantially larger effect sizes ($d = .52$) compared to unpublished studies ($d = -.10$), indicating that the literature may overestimate the true effectiveness of simulation games. Methodological moderators such as random assignment and study design rigor did not significantly influence outcomes, but the year of publication showed no trend, suggesting stability in effectiveness over time. The author concludes that while simulation games are valuable tools for adult learning, their efficacy is contingent upon active engagement, integration into broader instructional programs, and unrestricted access, rather than entertainment value alone.
