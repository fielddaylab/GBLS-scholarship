## Findings

# Summary

This article by Simpson and Midgley (2023) details the design, execution, and qualitative evaluation of "clinical board games cafes" implemented within an undergraduate nursing program at the University of Nottingham. The initiative was developed in response to curriculum changes that increased simulated clinical learning for first-year students, aiming to provide a low-cost, engaging alternative to traditional lectures. Drawing on literature regarding game-based learning, gamification, and serious games, the authors posit that board games can simplify complex issues, foster teamwork, and create safe environments for experiential learning.

The methodology involved hosting three 2-hour cafe-style sessions during the second half of semester one. Twenty-four first-year nursing students from adult, child, mental health, and learning disability fields participated in each session. The environment was designed to be informal, featuring hot drinks, biscuits, background music, and table arrangements limiting groups to eight students to facilitate interaction. Academic staff were present to serve refreshments and facilitate discussion but did not direct the gameplay, allowing for student-led learning. The games used were commercially produced clinical board games focused on hydration, drug rounds, and infection control, alongside modified versions of classic games like *Operation* that incorporated anatomy and physiology questions. Textbooks and reference materials were available for students to consult during play.

Data were collected via simple qualitative questionnaires administered at the end of each session, with written consent obtained for sharing responses. The authors conducted a thematic analysis of this feedback. The findings revealed three dominant themes. First, students valued the "different and safe way to learn." Many reported that the relaxed atmosphere reduced the pressure associated with traditional seminar groups, encouraging them to ask questions they might otherwise consider "silly." This environment allowed for repetition and hands-on practice, which helped embed clinical concepts. Second, the cafes provided significant social opportunities. Nursing students, particularly mature students or those with caring responsibilities who often miss evening university events, appreciated the chance to socialize during scheduled teaching time. The sessions facilitated peer-to-peer knowledge exchange and helped build support networks across different nursing fields. Third, participation enhanced confidence and teamwork. Students reported feeling better prepared for clinical placements and medication calculations. Staff observations noted positive interactions, with more experienced students supporting quieter peers, thereby fostering a sense of belonging and breaking down hierarchical barriers between faculty and students.

The authors discuss these findings in the context of Kolb’s experiential learning theory and Bandura’s social cognitive theory, suggesting that the cafes allowed students to reflect on experiences and learn through observation and interaction. They acknowledge potential limitations noted in broader literature, such as the risk that competition may threaten some learners or that noisy environments may challenge neurodiverse students. However, the positive feedback warranted a recommendation for integrating this model into teaching programs.

Regarding implications, the authors suggest that the cafe model is adaptable for various educational stages and settings, including postgraduate training and clinical practice areas for staff development. They identify gaps in current game availability, specifically regarding mental health skills, and propose future developments such as interdisciplinary sessions and allowing students to borrow games from the library for self-directed learning. Crucially, the authors emphasize that while short-term feedback is favorable, there is a paucity of data on long-term outcomes. They call for more robust evaluation methods to determine if game-based learning leads to sustained behavioral changes and improved patient care outcomes. The article concludes that board games offer a valuable, inclusive complement to traditional healthcare education, enhancing both clinical knowledge retention and interpersonal skills.
