# Summary

This article by Horban and Skachenko (2019) provides a comprehensive review of gamification strategies employed by academic libraries in the United States, Canada, and Portugal to enhance information literacy and combat plagiarism. The authors posit that while gamification is well-established in Western academia, it remains under-researched in Ukraine. Consequently, the study aims to synthesize international experiences to inform Ukrainian library practices.

The methodology relies on content analysis and synthetic processing of existing literature rather than primary empirical data collection. The core of the article is a detailed examination of twelve specific digital games developed by various university libraries. These games are categorized by their pedagogical objectives: teaching citation styles, identifying plagiarism types, and improving search strategies.

Key examples analyzed include "Gaming Against Plagiarism" (GAP) from the University of Florida, which utilizes three mini-games to help graduate students recognize research misconduct such as fabrication and falsification. Another notable example is "Gamàgio," a platformer game developed in Portugal that tests students' understanding of plagiarism through progressive difficulty levels. The authors also describe simpler interactive tools like the "Tic-Tac-Toe Citation Game" at James Madison University, which helps students identify correct citation formats for different source types, and the "Magnetic Keyword" game, designed to improve database search skills.

The findings indicate that gamification leverages key motivational elements identified in Self-Determination Theory: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. The authors argue that games provide immediate feedback, a critical component for learning complex skills like citation formatting and ethical research practices. They note that many of these resources are freely available online, allowing libraries without development budgets to link to partner institutions' games. For instance, Rutgers University’s "The Cite is Right!" game is recommended by multiple academic libraries.

The article highlights that the success of these initiatives depends on engaging design, clear learning objectives, and appropriate length. The authors observe that students, particularly "digital natives" (ages 18–25), respond positively to competitive and interactive elements. Evidence from cited studies, such as McCabe and Wise’s assessment of games at James Madison University, suggests that student knowledge levels improve after engaging with these tools.

In conclusion, Horban and Skachenko assert that gamification is a potent tool for transforming library instruction. They recommend that Ukrainian libraries adopt these proven international models to foster academic integrity and increase user engagement. The authors emphasize that while the pedagogical value of games is clear, further research is needed to understand how best to integrate them into broader library service promotion strategies. This review serves as a foundational resource for librarians seeking to implement game-based learning modules in their own institutions.
