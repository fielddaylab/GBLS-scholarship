# Summary

This qualitative study by Manzano-León et al. (2024) investigates the efficacy of Digital Game-Based Learning (DGBL) in raising awareness about transgender and gender diverse (TGD) individuals among undergraduate Social Education students in Spain. The research addresses the broader context of transphobia and stigma, which are rooted in rigid binary conceptions of gender and contribute to significant mental health challenges for TGD populations. The authors posit that traditional educational methods, such as lectures and debates, may be insufficient for changing deep-seated prejudices, whereas immersive methodologies like video games can foster empathy and understanding through narrative engagement.

The primary objective was to evaluate whether the narrative video game "A Normal Lost Phone" could enhance students' sensitivity to TGD issues and develop relevant professional competencies. The study employed a qualitative design involving five focus groups and participant observation. The sample consisted of 35 undergraduate students (29 female, 5 male, 1 non-binary) with a mean age of 20.52 years, selected via convenience sampling from a mandatory Social Education course. Participation in the specific game-based activity was voluntary. The intervention comprised two 2-hour sessions where students played the game cooperatively in teams, followed by a critical essay and optional focus groups to discuss their experiences.

The video game "A Normal Lost Phone" was selected for its trans-friendly narrative, which follows a transgender protagonist navigating identity concealment, family intolerance, and social support systems. The researchers conducted a thematic analysis of the focus group data using ATLAS.ti software, establishing a threshold of at least three instances for including sub-themes to ensure relevance.

The findings were categorized into three main themes: narrative and gameplay elements, training through the video game, and raising awareness towards TGD people. Quantitative summaries of qualitative responses indicated high levels of student engagement; 97.3% of participants reported high interest and motivation, describing the experience as fun, creative, and distinct from traditional learning methods. Regarding professional development, 91.9% of students perceived the activity as beneficial for their Social Education training, with 73.0% noting specific improvements in understanding how to treat TGD individuals professionally. Furthermore, 94.6% of participants reported a heightened awareness of the social realities faced by TGD people, citing the game's ability to simulate lived experiences and foster empathy. Additionally, 59.5% highlighted the game's potential in preventing violence and harassment against gender minorities.

The authors discuss these results as evidence that video games can serve as effective pedagogical tools for promoting sensitivity and reducing stigma. The immersive nature of the game allowed students to internalize perspectives different from their own, which is crucial for future social educators who will work with vulnerable populations. The study suggests that such methodologies can enhance student engagement and learning outcomes more effectively than traditional instruction.

However, the authors acknowledge several limitations. The sample size was relatively small and demographically homogeneous (predominantly young women), limiting the generalizability of the findings to other age groups, genders, or professional sectors. The voluntary nature of the participation may have introduced selection bias, as students with pre-existing positive attitudes toward gaming or TGD issues might have been more likely to participate. Additionally, the study did not measure participants' prior knowledge or experiences with TGD topics, which could have influenced their responses. Finally, the short duration of the intervention (four hours) prevented the assessment of long-term retention or behavioral changes. The authors recommend future longitudinal studies and quasi-experimental designs to further validate the effectiveness of DGBL in educational settings.
