## Findings
The analysis reveals significant deficiencies in all surveyed vocabularies:
1. **LCSH**: The heading "Fantasy games" is inaccurate as it restricts TTRPGs to the fantasy genre, ignoring other settings. Its structure is inconsistent, grouping TTRPGs with wargames, video games, and choose-your-own-adventure books under 137 narrower terms. It fails to disambiguate between the game itself (form) and scholarship about the game (subject).
2. **AAT**: The heading "Role-playing game" lacks scope notes and is hierarchically placed under "Game equipment," treating TTRPGs as physical artifacts rather than rule-based forms, which misrepresents their primary nature as text/rulebooks.
3. **Wikidata**: While it offers the most comprehensive definition distinguishing TTRPGs from computer roleplaying games (CRPGs) and live-action role-playing games (LARPs), it fails to disambiguate between subject and genre/form, making it unclear if the heading applies to the game or literature about the game.
4. **LCGFT**: The 2020 addition of "Role-playing Games" provides a scope note but fails to distinguish TTRPGs from CRPGs or LARPs. The definition is broad enough that video games with roleplaying elements could incorrectly be assigned this heading.
5. **GTTG**: This vocabulary offers the most accurate distinction by explicitly limiting application to tabletop games. However, its definition requires a "Game Master," which excludes solo-play TTRPGs. Its narrow scope makes it impractical for widespread adoption in general academic libraries.
Evidence: "Table IV, a comparison of TTRPG headings by vocabulary of whether or not they meet criteria for accurate description" (Page 27); "Controlled vocabularies currently offer no ideal solutions for describing TTRPGs." (Page 30)

## Implications
The author argues that the lack of standardized, accurate metadata hinders the discoverability of TTRPGs in academic libraries, particularly as scholarly interest in game studies grows. Short-term solutions involve local policy adjustments, such as consistent use of LCSH form subdivisions (e.g., "Rules"), though these are technically imperfect. Long-term solutions require amendments to major controlled vocabularies to create distinct headings for TTRPGs that differentiate them from CRPGs, LARPs, and secondary literature. The author also suggests further research into automated methods for identifying and tagging TTRPG records in large repositories like WorldCat.

# Summary

Taylor Skelton Smith’s 2024 article, "A Dicey Situation," addresses the critical issue of metadata inadequacy regarding tabletop roleplaying games (TTRPGs) in library catalogs. As TTRPGs gain academic legitimacy and presence in library collections, the author argues that existing controlled vocabularies fail to provide accurate access points, thereby impeding user discovery. The study systematically evaluates five controlled vocabularies—Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH), Getty’s Art and Architecture Thesaurus (AAT), Wikidata, Genre Terms for Tabletop Games (GTTG), and Library of Congress Genre/Form Terms (LCGFT)—based on three criteria: definition accuracy, structural hierarchy, and disambiguation between subject and genre/form.

The analysis reveals that LCSH’s primary heading, "Fantasy games," is fundamentally flawed. It incorrectly associates all TTRPGs with the fantasy genre, ignoring diverse settings such as science fiction or horror. Furthermore, its hierarchical structure is inconsistent, grouping TTRPGs with unrelated formats like wargames and video games under 137 narrower terms. Crucially, LCSH fails to distinguish between the game itself (a form) and scholarship about the game (a subject), leading to confusion in catalog records. Similarly, AAT categorizes "Role-playing game" under "Game equipment," treating TTRPGs as physical artifacts rather than rule-based textual forms, which misrepresents their primary function.

Wikidata offers a more precise definition by distinguishing TTRPGs from computer roleplaying games (CRPGs) and live-action role-playing games (LARPs). However, it lacks a mechanism to disambiguate between subject and genre/form, leaving users uncertain whether the heading applies to the game or literature about it. LCGFT, introduced in 2020, provides a scope note for "Role-playing Games" but remains too broad, failing to exclude CRPGs or LARPs. GTTG emerges as the most accurate vocabulary due to its explicit focus on tabletop games, but its definition erroneously requires a "Game Master," excluding solo-play TTRPGs. Additionally, its narrow scope limits its practicality for widespread adoption in general academic libraries.

Smith concludes that no current controlled vocabulary offers an ideal solution for describing TTRPGs. The author recommends short-term local policies, such as consistent use of LCSH form subdivisions, while advocating for long-term amendments to major vocabularies to create distinct, accurate headings. The article highlights the need for standardized metadata to support the growing scholarly interest in game studies and to ensure TTRPGs are discoverable as primary source materials.
