# Summary

This case report by Capdarest-Arest, Opuda, and Stark (2019) details the design, implementation, and evaluation of an interactive workshop aimed at teaching health sciences librarians how to incorporate low-tech gamification into library instruction. The authors argue that while high-tech games are prevalent in literature, they often require resources (time, money, coding skills) that many librarians lack. In contrast, low-tech games offer accessibility and foster face-to-face collaboration. The study’s primary objective was to determine if a short, hands-on session using design-thinking principles could increase librarians' confidence and intent to use gamification in their instructional settings.

The methodology centered on a 90-minute special content session at the 2018 Medical Library Association (MLA) annual meeting, titled “Design, Play, Learn.” The pedagogical framework integrated multimodal instruction, including pre-session readings (flipped classroom), a brief didactic lecture, and active learning through game prototyping. The session utilized the design-thinking process—empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test—to guide participants in creating tangible, low-tech games using accessible office supplies. A sample card game focused on database searching was provided as a reference model. Data were collected via anonymous five-item Likert scale surveys administered before and after the session to measure changes in confidence, experience, and future usage intent.

The population consisted of health sciences librarians attending MLA ’18. The session format was informal, with no mandatory registration, allowing participants to join or leave at will. This flexibility introduced a limitation in tracking exact attendance numbers; however, 43 participants completed the pre-session survey, and 35 completed the post-session survey. The authors acknowledge that the "ebb and flow" of attendees made it difficult to calculate an accurate return rate for the surveys.

The findings demonstrated substantial positive shifts in participant attitudes. Confidence in using gamification increased from 28% (12 out of 43 respondents) pre-session to 83% (29 out of 35 respondents) post-session. Similarly, the intent to incorporate session learnings into future instruction rose from 77% to 94%, and the likelihood of using the specific game created during the workshop increased from 46% to 86%. Qualitative feedback reinforced these quantitative results, with participants describing the session as highly helpful and noting that it facilitated professional networking and immediate application of skills in their own institutions.

The authors conclude that brief, hands-on workshops are effective for building librarian confidence in game design. They identify several implications for future practice, including the need for extended time for debriefing and sharing, the value of allowing participants to play sample games before designing their own, and the potential benefit of splitting sessions into design-focused and assessment-focused components. The study underscores the viability of low-tech gamification as a resource-efficient strategy for enhancing library instruction engagement.
