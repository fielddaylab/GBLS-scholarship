## Findings
The article finds that video game cataloging has been characterized by inconsistent terminology and lagging standards. Key findings include:
1. Descriptive rules evolved from treating games as "machine-readable data files" (AACR2 Chapter 9) to "electronic resources," with frequent changes in General Material Designations (GMDs) such as [computer file], [interactive multimedia], and [electronic resource].
2. Physical description practices varied widely, leading to difficulties in automated identification of disc types due to the lack of controlled vocabulary for carriers like CD-ROMs and DVD-ROMs.
3. Subject and genre headings are problematic because LC established game genres as topical headings rather than genre headings, creating a policy gap since LC no longer catalogs games within its library workflow.
4. Uniform title practices are contradictory; rules for computer programs do not explicitly cover games, leading to confusion regarding qualifiers (e.g., "Computer game" vs. "Video game") and conflicts with existing subject headings.
Evidence: "Consistent terminology has been elusive... # Summary

Greta de Groat’s 2015 article, "A History of Video Game Cataloging in U.S. Libraries," provides a comprehensive historical analysis of the challenges librarians have faced in cataloging video games from the late 1970s to the present. The primary objective is to trace the evolution of descriptive cataloging rules, MARC encoding practices, and subject/genre heading policies, demonstrating how these standards have consistently lagged behind technological advancements in the gaming industry. The article argues that because video games are a relatively new material type, they have never had specific, dedicated cataloging rules, resulting in an "awkward fit" within traditional library workflows and significant inconsistency in bibliographic records.

The methodology is a qualitative historical review of standard-setting documents, including Anglo-American Cataloguing Rules (AACR2), Resource Description and Access (RDA), MARC 21 formats, and Library of Congress (LC) policy statements. De Groat examines how video games were initially subsumed under rules for "machine-readable data files" in the 1978 AACR2. She details the iterative changes in Chapter 9 of AACR2, which evolved from describing physical media to focusing on electronic resources. A key finding is the instability of terminology; General Material Designations (GMDs) shifted from [machine-readable data file] to [computer file], then briefly to [interactive multimedia], and finally to [electronic resource]. This constant flux created confusion for catalogers and users alike.

Regarding physical description, the article highlights the difficulty in standardizing the representation of game carriers. While early rules attempted to specify physical units like "computer chip cartridge" or "computer laser optical disk," the introduction of "conventional terminology" options in later revisions led to a wide diversity in how CD-ROMs and DVD-ROMs were described. This lack of controlled vocabulary hinders automated searching and collocation of materials. The transition to RDA introduced content, media, and carrier types, but De Groat notes that these categories still fail to adequately capture the interactivity and complex media integration inherent in video games, treating them similarly to passive moving images.

The analysis extends to MARC 21 encoding, noting that specific fields for computer files (such as field 008/26 for type of computer file) were often underutilized or misapplied. For instance, many catalogers continued to use "interactive multimedia" codes even after "computer games" became an available option. Furthermore, technical details like system requirements were often relegated to unindexed note fields (e.g., field 538) rather than indexed fields, limiting discoverability.

A significant portion of the article addresses subject and genre cataloging. De Groat identifies a critical policy gap: while LC controls subject headings, it no longer catalogs video games within its library division, leaving policies outdated and contradictory. Game genres are largely established as topical headings (for books *about* games) rather than genre/form headings for the games themselves. This forces catalogers to use workarounds, such as applying topical headings as local genre terms, which creates inconsistency across libraries. The distinction between "computer games" and "video games" in LCSH is also criticized as unclear and unhelpful, with no clear definition provided by LC.

Finally, the article discusses uniform title practices, noting that rules for computer programs do not explicitly cover video games. This leads to ambiguity in handling title conflicts and determining appropriate qualifiers. De Groat concludes that while descriptive cataloging has seen some improvement with RDA and updated MARC fields, subject and genre policies remain "intractable." She calls for a complete overhaul of LC’s approach to game genres and subject headings to provide the consistency and logic necessary for effective access to these complex materials. The article serves as a critical resource for understanding the structural barriers in library metadata that affect the discoverability and organization of video games.
