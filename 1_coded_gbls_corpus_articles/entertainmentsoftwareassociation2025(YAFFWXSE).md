## Purpose
The report aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the U.S. video game industry, detailing player demographics, behaviors, perceptions, spending trends, and the social impact of gaming. It seeks to demonstrate that video games are a universally popular form of entertainment across all demographics and highlight their positive contributions to mental health, family bonding, and economic activity.

## Method
The data is derived from a quantitative online survey conducted by YouGov in the United States between February 11 and 25, 2025. The survey utilized a proprietary online panel with 5,000 total respondents. Data was weighted to be representative of the overall U.S. population regarding age, gender, ethnicity, education, census region, and player status. Spending data was sourced from Circana and Sensor Tower.

Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 4,000 gamers (defined as individuals spending at least one hour per week playing video games on any platform) and 1,000 non-gamers. The gamer subset included 757 children/teens (ages 8–17, surveyed under parental supervision) and 3,243 adults (ages 18+). The report extrapolates these findings to estimate that 205.1 million Americans (ages 5–90) play video games regularly.
Evidence: "n=4,000 Gamers (including n=757 kids/teens, n=3,243 adults)... n=1,000 Non-Gamers (adults)" (Methodology)

## Findings
**Demographics and Prevalence:** 64% of the U.S. population plays video games at least one hour per week. Gaming is prevalent across all generations, with 83% of Gen Alpha (ages 5–12), 78% of Gen Z, 68% of Millennials, 52% of Gen X, 49% of Boomers, and 36% of the Silent Generation playing weekly. The average age of a player is 36.
**Perceptions and Benefits:** Adults agree that video games provide mental stimulation (81%), stress relief (79%), and help improve cognitive skills (76%). 67% of parents believe gaming offers more potential benefits for children than social media.
**Social and Family Dynamics:** 82% of parents who play games do so with their children. 72% of players have played with others, and 55% do so weekly. Younger generations (Gen Z and Millennials) report meeting new friends or partners through gaming.
**Accessibility:** 21% of adult players report having a disability. 49% of players with disabilities rate accessibility as extremely/very important, though more than half believe video games are very accessible. Key desired features include adjustable text size (50%) and difficulty levels (39%).
**Economic Impact:** Total consumer spending on video games in 2024 was $59.3 billion, up 32% since 2019. This outpaces combined spending on movies, TV, and music.
Evidence: "205.1 million Americans now play video games regularly." (Foreword); "Total Consumer Spending on Video Games in 2024... $59.3 billion" (Spending Trends)
Confidence: high

# Summary

The *Essential Facts About the U.S. Video Game Industry* (2025), published by the Entertainment Software Association (ESA), presents a comprehensive statistical overview of video game consumption, demographics, and economic impact in the United States. The primary objective of the report is to demonstrate the ubiquity of gaming across all demographic segments and to highlight its positive social, cognitive, and economic contributions. The data is derived from a quantitative online survey conducted by YouGov between February 11 and 25, 2025, involving 5,000 respondents (4,000 gamers and 1,000 non-gamers). The sample was weighted to reflect the broader U.S. population in terms of age, gender, ethnicity, and education.

The findings indicate that video games are a dominant form of entertainment, with an estimated 205.1 million Americans (ages 5–90) playing regularly. Crucially for library services, gaming is not limited to youth; while 83% of Gen Alpha (ages 5–12) play weekly, significant portions of older generations also engage: 52% of Gen X, 49% of Boomers, and 36% of the Silent Generation. The average age of a player is 36 years old. This broad demographic reach suggests that games-based services can appeal to a wide audience, including adult patrons who may not traditionally identify as "gamers."

Regarding perceptions and attitudes, the report highlights strong positive associations with gaming. Among adults, 81% agree that video games provide mental stimulation, 79% cite stress relief, and 76% believe they help improve cognitive skills. Furthermore, 67% of parents perceive gaming as offering more potential benefits for their children than social media. This perception is supported by behavioral data showing that 82% of parents who play games do so with their children, often to socialize or spend quality time together. For library programs, this underscores the potential for intergenerational gaming events and family-oriented game nights as effective community engagement tools.

Social connectivity is another key finding. 72% of players have played with others, and 55% do so weekly. Younger generations, particularly Gen Z (70%) and Millennials (61%), report meeting people through video games they would not have otherwise met. This supports the use of gaming as a mechanism for building community and fostering social interaction in library settings. Additionally, 89% of players have played online, indicating a high level of digital literacy and comfort with networked environments.

The report also addresses accessibility, noting that 21% of adult players report having a disability. While more than half of these players believe video games are very accessible, 49% rate overall accessibility as extremely or very important. Desired features include adjustable text size (50%) and difficulty levels (39%). This data implies that libraries should prioritize acquiring games with robust accessibility options to ensure inclusive service provision.

Economically, the industry remains robust, with total consumer spending reaching $59.3 billion in 2024, a 32% increase since 2019. This financial scale reinforces the cultural significance of gaming and justifies institutional investment in games-based resources. The report concludes that video games are transforming beyond mere entertainment into tools for education, social connection, and innovation, providing a strong evidence base for libraries to integrate gaming into their core services.
