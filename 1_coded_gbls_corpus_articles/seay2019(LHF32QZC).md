# Summary

Jared Alexander Seay’s 2019 column, "Considering Games in Libraries and Such — A Liminal Response to Watching Paint Dry," published in *Against the Grain*, offers a reflective analysis of student disengagement in library instruction and advocates for games-based learning as a pedagogical solution. The article begins with a narrative anecdote describing an 8 AM library instruction session characterized by profound student apathy. Seay describes the students as "severely uninspired and unengaged," noting that despite their need for critical research skills, they exhibited no perceptible feedback or interest in the traditional "chalk and talk" presentation. He identifies two primary contextual factors contributing to this disengagement: the early morning timing of the session and the prevalence of "IKTA (I Know This Already) syndrome," where students perceive little value in library instruction due to the ease of accessing information via search engines like Google.

Rather than accepting this disengagement as simple boredom, Seay pivots to an analysis of the underlying psychological mechanisms at play, drawing on the work of Dr. Mark Carnes, a history professor at Barnard College and creator of the "Reacting to the Past" role-immersion games. Seay highlights Carnes’ discovery that student silence and apparent disinterest often mask deep-seated anxiety. Through individual interviews with students from a failed seminar, Carnes learned that students feared revealing their intellectual insufficiency in front of both the instructor and their peers. This fear of appearing foolish or inadequate led to a "sophisticated disinterest," where students withdrew emotionally to protect their fragile sense of self. Seay argues that this dynamic is not unique to history seminars but is likely prevalent in library instruction, where students may feel intimidated by the complexity of academic databases and the perceived authority of the librarian.

The core argument of the article centers on the concept of "liminality." Seay defines liminal spaces as transitional thresholds where normal social rules, status hierarchies, and expectations are suspended or subverted. He posits that traditional classroom settings reinforce these hierarchies, exacerbating student anxiety. In contrast, games-based environments—particularly role-immersion games and escape rooms—create liminal spaces that invert these structures. By assuming alternative identities within a game context, students are freed from the pressure of their real-world academic personas. This shift allows them to engage with challenging material without the fear of personal failure or peer judgment. Seay cites Carnes’ assertion that when the mentor’s role is minimized and students are placed in an impassioned, dramatic context, they are more likely to engage deeply with abstract texts and concepts.

Seay extends this theoretical framework to library services, suggesting that the same principles apply to information literacy instruction. He argues that games can transform the "nightmare" of the unresponsive classroom into an engaging learning experience by providing a safe environment for trial and error. The article concludes with Seay’s intention to implement an escape room themed around library research ("Get out of Library Hell") to test this hypothesis in his own practice. While the article does not present empirical data from Seay’s own implementation, it strongly implies that games-based library services can mitigate the affective barriers to learning by leveraging the psychological safety provided by liminal game spaces. The piece serves as a conceptual bridge between educational psychology and practical library pedagogy, urging librarians to consider the emotional state of students alongside their informational needs.
