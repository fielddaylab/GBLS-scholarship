## Findings
The author reports that the gaming programs successfully created positive social links and fostered behaviors aligned with developmental assets such as family support, positive peer influence, and cultural competence. Specific findings include:
1. **Social Integration:** Multiplayer games facilitated interaction across age and gender lines. For instance, boys eventually participated in dance games initially perceived as "girly," and girls competed successfully in tournaments dominated by boys.
2. **Developmental Alignment:** Staff observed behaviors corresponding to assets like "Service to Others" (teen volunteers assisting) and "Safety" (staff managing physical space during active play).
3. **Program Evolution:** The success of video gaming led to the spin-off "Go Your Own Way" program, which utilized a simplified version of Go ("First Capture Go") to engage new players quickly.
4. **Challenges:** Younger children (around age 5) caused disruptions due to tantrums and lack of self-control, leading to an enforced age limit of 10+ for Play It Loud. Attempts to introduce educational games like Minecraft faced resistance from teens who preferred entertainment-focused play.

# Summary

Thomas Maluck’s 2013 article, "Tales from Play It Loud," presents a reflective case study on the implementation of multiplayer gaming programs at the Northeast Regional Branch of the Richland Library in Columbia, South Carolina. The primary objective is to document the operational experiences and social impacts of two distinct programs: "Play It Loud," focused on Nintendo Wii video games, and "Go Your Own Way," centered on the board game Go. Maluck argues that these programs successfully fostered positive social interactions among patrons and aligned with the Search Institute’s "40 Developmental Assets" for youth.

The methodology is qualitative and observational, relying on the author’s role as the supervising young adult librarian. The design of "Play It Loud" was informed by Scott Nicholson’s S.N.A.K.S. model (Strategy, Narrative, Action, Knowledge, Social), aiming to provide a variety of game archetypes to engage diverse players. The program utilized multiplayer Wii titles such as *Mario Kart*, *Super Smash Bros. Brawl*, and *Just Dance*, selected for their accessibility and appropriateness for teens and younger children. Attendance averaged 21 participants per monthly session, primarily aged 9–16, though it functioned as a family event. A secondary program, "Go Your Own Way," emerged from patron interest in board games during video game sessions. It utilized a simplified rule set ("First Capture Go") to lower the barrier to entry, attracting smaller groups of up to ten participants.

Key findings indicate that the programs facilitated significant social integration and behavioral development. Maluck reports that gaming bridged gender gaps; for example, teenage boys who initially mocked dance games eventually participated after observing peers enjoying them, while girls successfully competed in tournaments dominated by boys. The author maps observed behaviors to specific developmental assets, noting instances of "Positive Family Communication" where parents and children learned games together, and "Service to Others" where teen volunteers assisted with setup and tutoring. Cultural competence was also observed, with diverse groups interacting positively during play. However, the study notes limitations in managing younger children (around age 5), whose tantrums and lack of impulse control disrupted sessions for older participants. Consequently, an age limit of 10+ was enforced for "Play It Loud." Additionally, attempts to introduce educational games like *Minecraft* faced resistance from teens who preferred pure entertainment, highlighting a tension between recreational and educational goals in library programming.

The implications drawn by Maluck suggest that gaming programs can serve as effective engagement tools for libraries, building trust with youth and encouraging participation in other services. The collaboration between "Go Your Own Way" and a "Book Bingo" program demonstrated how cross-promotion could boost attendance for both initiatives. The article concludes that while challenges exist in balancing different age groups and educational objectives, the core goal of providing shared, positive gaming experiences was achieved. Maluck advocates for continued expansion of such programs, emphasizing the need for clear boundaries, staff supervision, and a diverse selection of games to accommodate various player preferences and intelligences.
