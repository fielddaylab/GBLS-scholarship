# Summary

This perspective piece by Huber et al. (2024) addresses the dual nature of Large Language Models (LLMs) in education: their capacity to provide scalable, personalized support versus the risk of fostering over-reliance and deskilling among learners. The authors argue that because LLMs utilize stochastic processes, their outputs can be erroneous or biased, necessitating critical evaluation by users with domain expertise. However, the low opportunity cost of using LLMs may tempt students to outsource cognitive tasks, thereby hindering the development of essential expertise and judgment competence.

The article proposes a two-step pedagogical approach to mitigate these risks. First, it advocates for a "playful" interaction with LLMs, specifically through prompt engineering. Drawing on Papert’s constructionist theory (1980), the authors suggest that treating LLM interaction as an exploratory, playful activity can help users develop the expertise required to critically assess AI-generated content. Prompt engineering is framed not just as a technical skill but as a form of practice that reinforces domain knowledge, similar to how programming Logo helped children understand geometry.

Second, and more centrally, the authors argue for integrating game-based learning (GBL) into this new AI-augmented pedagogy. Citing Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci), they note that well-designed games satisfy psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness, fostering intrinsic motivation. This intrinsic engagement is crucial because it encourages deep practice and reflection rather than superficial task completion or outsourcing to AI. The authors highlight that games provide safe spaces for failure and iterative learning, which are essential for expertise development.

A key contribution of the article is the proposal to use LLMs to *create* game-based learning environments. The authors identify two scenarios: (1) "Learning by generating games," where students use LLMs to design their own educational games, thereby reformulating their understanding of subject matter through constructionist activities; and (2) educators using LLMs to gamify existing materials or generate full-fledged learning games. This approach lowers the technical barriers to game development, which has historically limited the adoption of GBL in schools due to high production costs and complexity.

The article acknowledges significant challenges to widespread GBL adoption, including socio-cultural stigma against video games, limited accessibility of technology, and the difficulty of designing engaging educational games. However, they posit that LLMs can help overcome these barriers by assisting in the generation of code, artwork, and game mechanics, thus enabling a "tinkering" approach where educators and students iteratively refine learning materials.

In conclusion, Huber et al. argue that banning LLMs is neither practical nor beneficial. Instead, education should embrace a "new pedagogy of learning with AI" that leverages the motivational power of games and the generative capabilities of LLMs to foster human expertise, critical thinking, and deep engagement. The ultimate goal is not to replace human effort but to enhance it through meaningful, playful collaboration between humans and machines.
