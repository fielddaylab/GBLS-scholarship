## Purpose
The study aims to explore and document current library practices regarding the collection development, cataloguing, processing, and circulation of tabletop game collections. It seeks to address the lack of baseline data and professional guidance for librarians managing these materials by identifying norms, barriers, and existing procedures.

## Method
The researchers employed an online survey developed using Qualtrics software. The instrument was crafted with input from academic, public, and school librarians and tested by the American Library Association’s Games and Gaming Round Table. Data collection occurred in June and July 2015. Participants were recruited via a snowball sampling technique utilizing electronic outlets and discussion lists used by librarians across school, public, and academic sectors.

## Findings
The study found that practices for tabletop games are fragmented and inconsistent across libraries. Regarding cataloguing, only 31% of responding libraries create bibliographic records for tabletop games, while 51% do not; board games are the most frequently catalogued type. Standard subject headings (Library of Congress) were deemed insufficient by 78% of respondents, leading many to create local headings or use resources like Board Game Geek. Processing practices vary significantly; only 46% create item records with barcodes, and preservation efforts are minimal (e.g., only 6% sleeve cards). Circulation is common but often restricted to in-library use (65% allow in-house use only); among those who circulate externally, loan periods average 14+ days. Contrary to fears of loss, respondents reported low rates of missing pieces, and most libraries do not charge replacement fees. Programming is a key component, with 71% of libraries offering gaming events, often supported by community volunteers and local retailers.
Evidence: "The results show that tabletop games have a presence in libraries, but practices vary in regard to collection development, cataloguing, processing, and circulation." (Abstract); "Of those 94 [who answered], 31% do [create records], 51% do not..." (Cataloguing section)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors conclude that while libraries are fragmented in their procedures, the perceived barriers to tabletop collections—specifically regarding loss and complexity—are often unfounded. They argue that best practices and standardized guidance are needed to help librarians feel comfortable piloting these collections. However, they also assert that libraries do not need to wait for codified standards to begin collecting, as current imperfect practices still provide significant access. The study implies a need for professional organizations to develop resources to fully integrate games into library services.
Evidence: "Best practices and guidance are still needed to fully integrate games into libraries... Libraries do not need to wait for codified standards in order to launch tabletop game collections..." (Conclusion)

# Summary

This article presents the findings of a survey conducted by Slobuski, Robson, and Bentley to investigate library practices surrounding tabletop game collections. The study addresses a gap in professional literature, noting that while video game collections are well-documented, tabletop games (including board games, card games, and role-playing games) lack standardized guidance for acquisition, cataloguing, processing, and circulation.

The methodology involved an online survey distributed via snowball sampling through librarian discussion lists and electronic outlets in June and July 2015. The final sample comprised 119 libraries, predominantly public (66%) and academic (28%). Of these respondents, 81% maintained a tabletop game collection. The study aimed to determine how these collections are managed and what barriers exist to their integration.

Key findings reveal significant fragmentation in library practices. In terms of cataloguing, only 31% of libraries create bibliographic records for tabletop games, with 51% not cataloguing them at all. Among those that do, board games are the most frequently recorded item type. The authors note a dissatisfaction with standard Library of Congress subject headings, with many librarians resorting to local headings or external resources like Board Game Geek to ensure discoverability. Processing practices are similarly inconsistent; less than half of the libraries create item records with barcodes, and preservation measures such as sleeving cards are rare (6%).

Regarding circulation, 65% of libraries restrict tabletop games to in-house use only, while 34% lend them externally. For those that circulate, loan periods typically exceed 14 days, and most allow holds and renewals. A primary concern for librarians is the loss of game pieces; however, the data suggests this fear is often misplaced. Respondents reported low instances of missing components, and most libraries do not charge patrons for replacements. Instead, many rely on informal inventory checks or community support.

Programming emerged as a central function of these collections, with 71% of libraries offering gaming events such as teen nights, family game days, and tournaments. These programs often leverage external partnerships with local game stores and volunteers. The authors conclude that while best practices are needed to standardize workflows and alleviate librarian anxiety, the current evidence shows that tabletop games can be successfully integrated into library services without waiting for formal codification. The study advocates for a shift in attitude toward these materials, emphasizing that they meet cultural and recreational user needs effectively despite the lack of traditional library standards.
