### Summary
# **Slobuski, T., Robson, D., & Bentley, P. J. (2017). Arranging the pieces: A survey of library practices related to a tabletop game collection. *Evidence Based Library and Information Practice, 12*(1), 2-17. https://doi.org/10.18438/B8JG7H**

Slobuski, Robson, and Bentley provide a baseline survey of how libraries acquire, describe, process, circulate, and program around tabletop game collections. The article defines tabletop games broadly as games played in real-world social settings, including board games, dice games, card games, collectible card games, and role-playing games. Its core contribution to GBLS is operational: it shows that tabletop games are increasingly present in libraries, but that library practices remain fragmented because standards, professional guidance, and shared workflows lag behind patron interest and market growth.

The study begins by situating tabletop collections in a long library history of play. Libraries have supported chess clubs, toy libraries, circulating games, gaming programs, International Games Day events, and other play services for well over a century. Yet the authors argue that research and guidance for tabletop game collections lag behind work on video game collections. For librarians, the question is often no longer whether tabletop games belong in libraries, but how to select, catalog, process, lend, preserve, and make them discoverable. The article frames games as similar to other realia and special collections: if they are not cataloged, described, visible, and integrated into workflows, they become hidden and access remains staff-dependent.

Methodologically, the authors developed an online survey with input from academic, public, and school librarians and tested it with members of ALA's Games and Gaming Round Table. The survey was distributed in June and July 2015 through snowball sampling via library discussion lists and electronic outlets. One hundred nineteen libraries responded. Public libraries represented 66 percent of respondents, academic libraries 28 percent, and the rest special, curriculum-resource, school, or other libraries. Eighty-one percent of respondents reported having a tabletop game collection.

The collection-development findings show that libraries build these collections through mixed, often local channels. Among libraries that acquire games, 84 percent purchased games and 66 percent accepted donations. Most purchasing went through general online vendors such as Amazon or Barnes & Noble, but many libraries also used physical game stores, physical chain stores, online game vendors, library vendors, thrift stores, garage sales, and eBay. Budgets were generally small: 46 percent of responding libraries had up to $249 for tabletop games, 30 percent had no purchasing budget, 12 percent had $250-$500, and 12 percent had more than $500. Donation practices varied from accepting only complete, good-condition games that met collection needs to accepting nearly anything offered.

The cataloging results are especially important for GBLS access and discovery. Of 94 libraries answering the cataloging question, 31 percent created bibliographic catalog records for tabletop games, 51 percent did not, and 18 percent did so sometimes. Board games were the most frequently cataloged format, followed by card sets and role-playing guides. Only 22 libraries reported using OCLC to catalog games, and none said that more than 75 percent of their cataloged games already had OCLC records. Subject and genre access also remained inadequate: only 22 percent of 36 respondents found Library of Congress subject headings sufficient, while many used local headings drawn from BoardGameGeek, Father Geek, publisher descriptions, reviews, curricular areas, grade levels, awards, and game mechanisms. The authors conclude that perceived complexity and the absence of standards keep tabletop collections more hidden than other library materials.

Processing and circulation practices were similarly varied. Of 79 respondents, 46 percent created item records with barcodes for tabletop games, 46 percent did not, and 9 percent did so sometimes. Most libraries left games in original containers; 54 percent separated pieces into bags, 22 percent reinforced boxes, and only 6 percent sleeved cards. Circulation also depended on local definitions and workflows. Forty-four percent of 78 responding libraries reported circulating tabletop games, but when in-library and in-school use were counted alongside off-site borrowing, 65 percent allowed in-house use, 1 percent in-school use only, and 34 percent off-premises lending. Loan periods varied, but among circulating libraries 70 percent allowed holds and 76 percent allowed renewals.

One of the article's most useful findings is that feared loss and damage were less severe than many librarians expected. Forty-eight percent of 80 respondents counted pieces at each return, but 28 percent never counted pieces; only 34 percent purchased replacements when pieces were lost, and most did not charge replacement fees. Respondent comments repeatedly suggested that lost pieces and damage were rare, manageable, or not always fatal to play. Some libraries bought duplicate thrift-store copies for replacement parts, contacted publishers, made case-by-case judgments, or used creative methods such as weighing components to check completeness. The authors argue that fear of loss should not prevent circulation.

Programming also appears as a major part of tabletop collection use. Of 78 libraries answering the programming question, 71 percent offered programming around tabletop games. Programs included teen gaming events, family board game events, adult gaming events, board game design events, game jams, tournaments, lectures, club meetings, and other game days. Libraries often drew on staff and faculty, teen or student clubs, local board game meetups, game retailers, professional agencies, volunteers, local gamers, and community game stores. This matters for GBLS because tabletop collections are not isolated objects; they often become program infrastructure, partnership opportunities, and community connection points.

For the larger GBLS project, Slobuski et al. help translate tabletop enthusiasm into library operations. The article gives concrete evidence that tabletop games can be collected, cataloged, circulated, renewed, held, inventoried, displayed, programmed, and replaced, even without perfect standards. It also shows where infrastructure gaps remain: local metadata, subject and genre vocabularies, processing standards, component inventories, circulation policies, replacement practices, and professional comfort. The authors' conclusion is usefully pragmatic: libraries should not wait for codified standards before piloting discoverable, preserved, borrowable tabletop collections, but researchers and professional organizations should develop best practices that reduce local reinvention.

The limitations are typical of a snowball-sampled professional survey. The sample overrepresents libraries already connected to gaming conversations and cannot estimate the overall percentage of libraries with tabletop collections. Some questions also reveal inconsistent definitions of circulation. Still, the article is a key GBLS source because it provides one of the clearest field snapshots of tabletop collection practice before later operational studies such as Robson, Phillips, and Guerrero (2018).
