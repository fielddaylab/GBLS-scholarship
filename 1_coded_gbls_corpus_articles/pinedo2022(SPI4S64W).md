### Summary
# **Pinedo, R., Garcia-Martin, N., Rascon, D., Caballero-San Jose, C., & Canas, M. (2022). Reasoning and learning with board game-based learning: A case study. *Current Psychology, 41*(3), 1603-1617. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12144-021-01744-1**

Pinedo, Garcia-Martin, Rascon, Caballero-San Jose, and Canas present a qualitative case study of board game-based learning in a public school in Castilla y Leon, Spain. Their purpose is to understand what game-based learning means for a school's educational community and how it affects the teaching-learning process. The article defines GBL as the use of games to support learning in didactic environments or as educational tools, then connects play to twenty-first-century competencies, motivation, executive functions, reasoning, metacognition, cooperation, participation, and educational inclusion.

The study's setting is a public primary school that had introduced a GBL project beginning in the 2016-17 academic year. Teachers received training from board-game experts, introduced the methodology over two school years, and reserved weekly time and space for 30-to-60-minute board-game sessions. The school held 45 board games and changed the games each trimester. The design also included family participation: a family volunteer oversaw and encouraged each group of students while the form tutor moved among groups, supported play, and observed what was happening.

Data came from document analysis, observation, a teacher focus group, and interviews. The observed students were 45 fifth- and sixth-grade students, including students with special educational needs. The researchers conducted 24 observation sessions with groups of four to five players. They also held a focus group with five tutors who had at least two years of GBL experience, interviewed three students, one family volunteer, and one fifth-grade teacher, and analyzed school documents such as the Education Project, Curricular Project, and Annual General Program. The analysis used inductive coding in ATLAS.ti, triangulation across sources, and inter-rater reliability checks.

The findings are organized around three broad themes: educational characteristics of GBL, quality learning, and participation/inclusion. Under educational characteristics, the educational community described games as motivating, emotionally positive, and flexible. Games created challenge, pleasure, confidence, curiosity, and a safer sense of failure than ordinary academic tasks. Teachers and students also described games as adaptable because rules and difficulty could be adjusted to different knowledge levels, learning styles, and special educational needs.

Under quality learning, the study argues that board games can support curricular content, key competencies, cognitive abilities, and executive functions. Participants connected games to oral and written expression, math, estimation, social competence, learning to learn, planning, working memory, emotional regulation, cognitive flexibility, inhibition, initiative, reasoning, creativity, and problem solving. The article is careful about assessment difficulty: teachers believed games improved strategies, memory, attention, and curricular competence, but they also struggled to measure those effects inside conventional subject and evaluation structures.

Under participation and inclusion, the article emphasizes that games made it easier for students with and without special educational needs to stay in the same classroom, interact with peers, cooperate, take turns, manage conflict, and practice social behaviors in a familiar and low-risk environment. Family participation also mattered: volunteers helped sessions run smoothly, improved the family-school connection, gave teachers more room to observe students, and helped families understand classroom methods that could continue at home.

For the larger GBLS project, Pinedo et al. provide useful adjacent evidence about why analog games can support motivation, inclusion, social interaction, and executive-function practice when adults intentionally structure play. Their findings can help librarians think about why board-game programs may need facilitation, family or volunteer support, adaptable rules, inclusive grouping, and reflection on learning goals. The case is especially suggestive for school-library or youth-services programming that partners with teachers or families.

However, the article should not be added to `review.md` because it is not library literature. It studies classroom pedagogy in a Spanish public school, not a library collection, library program, librarian-led instruction, game advisory service, circulating game collection, public-library family program, or school-library collaboration. It does not discuss libraries, librarians, library spaces, cataloging, collections, access, outreach, assessment of library services, or patron outcomes. Because the GBLS review already includes direct library sources on tabletop collections, school-library tabletop gaming, public-library board-game programs, and assessment, this article is best retained as explicitly excluded background rather than cited in the main review.
