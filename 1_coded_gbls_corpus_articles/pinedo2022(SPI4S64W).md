## Purpose
To gain an in-depth understanding of what Game-Based Learning (GBL) means for the educational community (teachers, students, and families) of a public school in Spain and to explore its impact on the teaching-learning process, specifically regarding participation, motivation, quality learning, and reasoning.

## Method

## Population and Data
The study took place in a public school in Castilla y León, Spain. Participants included 45 students (25 from 5th grade, 20 from 6th grade; ages 11–12), 5 teachers (form tutors with at least two years of GBL experience), and 3 interviewees (2 students, 1 teacher, 1 family volunteer). The school had 220 students total. Data included 24 observation sessions, focus group transcripts, interview transcripts, and school documents.

## Findings
Participants conceptualized GBL around three main themes: (1) GBL characteristics (motivation, positive emotions, flexibility), (2) participation (student and family involvement, cooperation, inclusion), and (3) quality learning and reasoning (curricular content acquisition, development of executive functions like working memory and inhibition, and high-level thinking skills). Results indicated that GBL fosters an inclusive environment where students with special educational needs remain in the general classroom, enhances motivation through intrinsic and extrinsic factors, and supports the development of social competencies and executive functions.
Evidence: "The participants conceptualized GBL around three main topics: (1) GBL characteristics, (2) participation, and (3) quality learning and reasoning... GBL encourages participation in school; this produces motivation and contributes to educational inclusion." (Abstract)

## Implications
GBL is presented as a favorable active methodology for twenty-first-century education that supports inclusive practices and the development of executive functions. The authors suggest that educational policies should support teacher training in GBL to facilitate curriculum integration. They note that while GBL benefits learning, assessing specific curricular competencies gained through play remains challenging for teachers.

# Summary

This qualitative case study, conducted by Pinedo et al. (2022), investigates the perceptions and impacts of Game-Based Learning (GBL) using board games within a public primary school in Spain. The research aims to understand how the educational community—comprising teachers, students, and families—interprets GBL and its effects on the teaching-learning process. The study is situated in a context where GBL was progressively introduced over two academic years (2017–2019) following teacher training, with the goal of fostering twenty-first-century skills such as critical thinking, cooperation, and self-regulation.

The methodology employed an exploratory, holistic, and intrinsic case study design. Data collection involved four techniques: document analysis of school projects, observation of 24 game sessions involving 45 students (ages 11–12), a focus group with five form tutors, and semi-structured interviews with two students, one teacher, and one family volunteer. The sample included students with special educational needs (16% in both observed grades). Data were analyzed using constant comparison and thematic coding via ATLAS.ti software until category saturation was reached, with high inter-rater reliability (Cohen’s Kappa = 0.92).

The findings are organized into three primary themes: GBL characteristics, participation and inclusion, and quality learning and reasoning. First, regarding characteristics, participants identified motivation, positive emotions, and flexibility as key attributes of GBL. Teachers and students reported that games foster both intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, creating a "flow state" where failure is perceived as part of play rather than academic incompetence. This environment reduces anxiety and encourages engagement, particularly among shy or struggling students.

Second, the study highlights GBL’s role in promoting participation and educational inclusion. The flexible nature of board games allows for adaptation to diverse learning levels, enabling students with special needs to remain in mainstream classrooms rather than being segregated. Furthermore, GBL facilitates family involvement; volunteers assist in game sessions, which strengthens the home-school connection and provides parents with strategies to support learning at home. Teachers noted that family presence allowed them to observe student interactions more closely and provide individualized attention.

Third, the research addresses quality learning and reasoning. Participants associated GBL with the acquisition of curricular content (e.g., mathematics, language) and the development of executive functions (EFs), such as working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibility, and planning. Teachers observed that games require strategic thinking and problem-solving, thereby enhancing high-level cognitive abilities. However, a significant challenge identified was the difficulty in assessing specific curricular competencies gained through GBL, as traditional evaluation methods do not easily capture improvements in strategy or executive functions.

The authors conclude that GBL is a valuable active methodology for fostering inclusive education and developing essential cognitive and social skills. They recommend further research to generalize these findings and emphasize the need for teacher training to effectively integrate GBL into the curriculum. The study acknowledges limitations related to the single-case design, which restricts generalizability, but suggests the derived category map can serve as a foundation for future longitudinal or quantitative studies.
