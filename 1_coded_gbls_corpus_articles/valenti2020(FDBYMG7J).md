# Summary

Valenti, Lund, and Wang (2020) present an empirical study examining the efficacy of virtual reality (VR) as an orientation tool for new students in distance education Library and Information Science (LIS) programs. The primary objective was to determine whether VR could replicate the benefits of in-person orientations by enhancing student knowledge, reducing anxiety, and fostering engagement compared to traditional static web-based methods. The study is grounded in Game Engagement Theory, which posits that immersive technologies can improve learning outcomes by addressing factors such as challenge, control, immersion, interest, and purpose.

The methodology employed a pre-test/post-test experimental design involving 29 new Master of Library Science (MLS) students from Emporia State University. Participants were drawn from distance education cohorts in four western U.S. states. The sample was divided into an experimental group (67%) who used cardboard VR headsets to navigate an interactive virtual library environment, and a control group (33%) who accessed the same informational content via standard web links. Data collection included pre- and post-tests measuring knowledge of program details and librarianship concepts, as well as surveys assessing anxiety levels and perceptions of the technology. Statistical analysis was conducted using Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA).

The results indicated that while both groups improved their knowledge scores from pre-test to post-test, the final scores were nearly identical (86% for the experimental group and 87% for the control group). Although the experimental group demonstrated a larger gain in score improvement due to lower baseline performance, this difference was not statistically significant (p = .4). Similarly, while the VR group reported slightly lower anxiety levels post-orientation compared to the control group, this reduction did not reach statistical significance at the p < .05 level, though it was significant at p < .1. However, qualitative and behavioral data revealed strong engagement with the VR medium. Nearly 60% of the experimental participants spent more than 25 minutes interacting with the VR experience, challenging assumptions about short attention spans in digital learning environments. Furthermore, the experimental group expressed more favorable attitudes toward the usability and fun factor of the technology compared to the control group.

The authors acknowledge several limitations that may have influenced the results. The small sample size (n=29) likely reduced the statistical power needed to detect significant differences in knowledge gains and anxiety reduction. Additionally, logistical issues, such as delayed delivery of VR headsets and technical difficulties faced by distance students, led to some participant dropout and potentially limited exposure time for others. The geographic homogeneity of the sample, restricted to four western states, also limits the generalizability of the findings.

Despite these constraints, the study suggests that VR holds promise for library instruction and student orientation. The high level of engagement and extended usage times indicate that VR can capture student attention more effectively than static text or links. The authors argue that VR’s immersive nature allows students to exercise control over their learning path, aligning with key tenets of Game Engagement Theory. They recommend that library professionals consider VR as a viable alternative to traditional orientation methods, particularly for distance learners who lack physical access to campus resources. While the study does not assess long-term retention or sustained usage over a semester, it provides preliminary evidence that VR can enhance the immediacy and memorability of introductory library services.
