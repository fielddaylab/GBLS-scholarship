# Summary

The *Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education*, adopted by the Association of College and Research Libraries (ACRL) Board in January 2016, serves as a foundational policy document intended to replace the *Information Literacy Competency Standards for Higher Education* published in 2000. The primary objective of this framework is to shift the conceptualization of information literacy from a prescriptive list of skills and standards to a flexible set of core concepts known as "threshold concepts." These concepts are described as transformative ideas that, once understood, allow learners to progress from novice to expert status within a discipline or knowledge domain. The document argues that the rapidly changing higher education environment and dynamic information ecosystem require this richer, more complex approach to foundational ideas about information.

The development of the framework was conducted by an ACRL Task Force through a rigorous qualitative process. Between 2013 and 2015, the Task Force released multiple drafts, soliciting feedback via online hearings, feedback forms, social media monitoring, and direct outreach to stakeholders including librarians, faculty, deans, and accrediting bodies. The feedback, which included over 562 formal responses and hundreds of additional documents, was analyzed using HyperResearch software to refine the final text. This process underscores the collaborative nature of the document's creation, aiming to ensure broad professional consensus.

The framework is organized around six distinct "frames," each consisting of a central concept, associated knowledge practices (demonstrable abilities), and dispositions (attitudinal tendencies). These frames are:
1.  **Authority Is Constructed and Contextual:** Emphasizes that authority varies by community and context, requiring learners to evaluate sources with informed skepticism.
2.  **Information Creation as a Process:** Highlights that information products reflect the iterative processes of their creation, influencing how they should be evaluated and used.
3.  **Information Has Value:** Addresses the legal, socioeconomic, and ethical dimensions of information, including intellectual property, privacy, and the commodification of data.
4.  **Research as Inquiry:** Portrays research as an iterative process of asking increasingly complex questions, requiring adaptability and persistence.
5.  **Scholarship as Conversation:** Frames scholarly work as an ongoing discourse where ideas are debated and weighed over time, encouraging learners to see themselves as contributors rather than just consumers.
6.  **Searching as Strategic Exploration:** Describes searching as a nonlinear, iterative process requiring mental flexibility and the evaluation of diverse sources.

The document explicitly draws upon the theory of metaliteracy, which expands traditional information skills to include the collaborative production and sharing of information in digital environments. It emphasizes metacognition—critical self-reflection on one’s own thought processes—as crucial for navigating the information ecosystem. The framework asserts that these concepts are not exhaustive nor prescriptive; rather, they provide a lens through which local institutions can design their own learning outcomes and curricula.

The implications of this framework are significant for library services and pedagogical practice. It calls for deeper collaboration between librarians and faculty to integrate information literacy into the broader curriculum, moving beyond isolated instruction sessions. The document encourages institutions to use these frames to redesign assignments, develop assessment instruments, and foster a culture where students actively participate in the creation and negotiation of knowledge. For games-based library services (GBLS), this framework provides a theoretical underpinning for designing learning experiences that emphasize inquiry, collaboration, and the iterative nature of research, aligning game mechanics with the disposition of viewing information seeking as strategic exploration and scholarship as an ongoing conversation. However, the document itself does not mention games or gamification; its relevance to GBLS is derived from its emphasis on active, participatory learning models and threshold concepts that can be operationalized through interactive pedagogical tools.
