The primary objective was to explore the educational and outreach potential of bespoke TTRPG content within a library context, specifically testing whether such materials could enhance library services, foster creativity, and teach library skills.

## Findings
The primary finding is that it is feasible for a small team of librarians to create a comprehensive, custom TTRPG module within a single semester. The resulting artifact included a detailed fantasy map of the library's first floor, quests based on real library tasks (e.g., using microfilm, 3D printing), and simplified character sheets. The authors found that the process was more labor-intensive than anticipated, particularly regarding map creation and module writing. The large scale of the map resulted in an 800 MB file that was difficult to share, suggesting smaller scales may be preferable for future iterations. The use of "zine-style" folding character sheets was well-received by peers at the American Library Association conference. The authors identified several potential pedagogical applications, including using the game to highlight makerspace capabilities and attract novice library users, but noted that efficacy frameworks (e.g., Bloom’s Taxonomy) are needed for future evaluation.

## Implications
The authors imply that bespoke TTRPG modules can serve as effective tools for library outreach and instruction, bridging the gap between community engagement and skill acquisition. They recommend that future projects adopt less aggressive timelines to allow for proper playtesting and consider simplifying module complexity by relying on experienced Game Masters rather than exhaustive written instructions. The study suggests that similar initiatives could be replicated in other academic or public libraries to enhance patron engagement and demonstrate the utility of library resources like makerspaces.

# Summary

This article by Watson et al. (2024) presents a proof-of-concept study detailing the development of a custom tabletop role-playing game (TTRPG) adventure module designed for library outreach and instruction. The authors, five academic librarians from the University of Mississippi, sought to explore the pedagogical potential of bespoke TTRPG content within an academic library setting. While libraries have historically hosted TTRPGs as recreational activities, this project aimed to combine community engagement with explicit library skill instruction, a gap the authors identified in existing literature.

The methodology involved a collaborative design process over one semester. The team, comprising librarians with expertise in game mastering, mapping, and graphic design, held weekly brainstorming sessions to develop a "one-shot" module set in a fantasy version of the J.D. Williams Library’s first floor. Due to licensing controversies surrounding Dungeons & Dragons (D&D) at the time, the team opted for the Pathfinder 1st edition ruleset, which offered an open license and compatibility with D&D mechanics. The development process included creating a detailed fantasy map using DungeonDraft, writing quests based on real-world library tasks (such as locating books via Dewey Decimal or Library of Congress systems, using microfilm, and operating 3D printers), and designing simplified character sheets in a "zine" format. Additionally, the team utilized the library’s IDEALab makerspace to 3D-print miniatures and dice, intending to showcase these resources to potential users.

A critical limitation of this study is the absence of empirical data from end-users. The authors initially planned to playtest the module with the Ole Miss Gamers Association (OMGA), a student organization. However, due to scheduling conflicts, illness among key liaisons, and the end of the academic semester, the playtesting phase was cancelled. Consequently, the "results" section reports on the successful completion of the artifact rather than user outcomes or learning metrics. The authors note that the project was more labor-intensive than anticipated, particularly regarding the creation of a high-resolution map (800 MB file size) and the writing of extensive module text to accommodate novice players.

Despite the lack of playtesting, the authors present several qualitative findings and implications. They argue that the feasibility of creating such modules is established, noting that the artifact attracted positive attention at the American Library Association conference. The study suggests that custom TTRPGs can serve multiple functions: highlighting makerspace capabilities (the "halo effect"), attracting novice library users through gamification, and engaging experienced gamers who may not otherwise utilize library services. The authors recommend that future iterations adopt more modest scopes, simpler maps, and relaxed timelines to facilitate proper playtesting. They also propose using established educational frameworks, such as Bloom’s Taxonomy or Gee’s learning theories, to evaluate the efficacy of such interventions in future research. Ultimately, the article serves as a practical guide for librarians interested in leveraging TTRPGs for instructional purposes, emphasizing the need for careful planning and realistic scope management.
