## Findings
Librarians perceived VR as a unique tool for informal learning due to its immersion and interactivity, believing it could enhance memory retention and allow for safe experimentation (e.g., chemistry experiments). However, post-implementation experiences revealed that patrons primarily learned about the technology itself rather than specific subject matter, largely due to limited educational content. Librarians noted challenges in reaching historically marginalized communities; while financially disadvantaged users accessed free technology, outreach to other marginalized groups was limited by recruitment strategies and waiver requirements. Spatial setup presented a tension between privacy and visibility: private spaces offered comfort but reduced spontaneous participation, whereas public setups encouraged observation and peer coaching but raised privacy concerns. Accessibility issues were noted for patrons with mobility or cognitive disabilities, as standard VR hardware lacked inclusive features. The novelty of VR drove initial interest and social interaction among spectators and users, but engagement often faded once the novelty wore off.
Evidence: "The findings show how VR may be used in the public library as a learning tool... Librarians observed that while VR has a lot of potential... the success also largely depends on the VR content... The majority of the librarians were skeptical as to whether the VR programming had any impact on the marginalized people in their communities... Several librarians discussed tensions between placing the VR equipment in a private or public space... Another unique aspect and potential limitation... was that in order to participate, the user had to put the headset on and use the hand controls... This was challenging for patrons who had mobility issues..."

## Implications
The authors suggest that successful VR programming depends less on the technology itself and more on intentional librarian strategies regarding recruitment, promotion, and spatial arrangement. They recommend connecting VR programs with existing library services (e.g., STEM or art programs) to broaden appeal beyond tech enthusiasts. To address content limitations, the authors advocate for involving users, particularly from marginalized communities, in VR content creation rather than just consumption. Future work should focus on developing guidelines for evaluating interactive technologies and exploring user-generated content opportunities in libraries.
Evidence: "From these findings we learned that often whether VR programming is successful or not is not simply about the technology itself but how each librarian made a careful and intentional decision... Therefore, it is important to think about how to connect the VR program with existing services and resources in the library... # Summary

This article presents a qualitative case study examining librarians' perspectives on implementing virtual reality (VR) programs in public libraries. Conducted in partnership with Washington State Libraries and Oculus, the research focused on seven diverse library sites across urban, regional, and rural settings. The primary data source for this paper consists of 36 semi-structured interviews with 23 librarians, conducted both before and after they facilitated VR programming using Oculus Rift headsets. The study aims to understand how librarians perceive VR as a tool for informal learning and social engagement, and to identify practical implications for successful implementation.

Prior to implementation, librarians held high expectations for VR’s educational potential, citing its immersive and interactive qualities as superior to traditional media. They envisioned VR as a means to enhance memory retention, foster empathy through perspective-taking, and allow for safe experimentation in subjects like science. However, post-implementation findings revealed a disconnect between these expectations and the available content. Librarians reported that patrons primarily learned how to operate the VR hardware rather than acquiring specific knowledge from the applications. The limited library of educational VR content often replicated traditional text-based learning models without leveraging immersive affordances, leading librarians to view the experience more as technological exposure than substantive learning.

A significant finding concerns the challenge of reaching marginalized communities. While VR provided free access to expensive technology for financially disadvantaged patrons, librarians expressed skepticism about its impact on other marginalized groups, such as immigrants or those with different cultural backgrounds. Recruitment hurdles, including complex waiver processes for minors and a lack of targeted outreach, resulted in participation skewed toward existing library users, particularly young males interested in gaming. Librarians noted that older adults and women were less likely to volunteer initially but often engaged positively once they observed others participating.

The physical setup of VR stations emerged as a critical factor in user engagement. Librarians faced a tension between privacy and visibility. Private setups offered comfort for users but reduced spontaneous participation, while public setups in main library areas encouraged observation and "peer coaching," where spectators actively guided users. This social dynamic transformed single-user VR experiences into communal events, fostering interaction among diverse age groups. However, accessibility remained a concern; standard VR hardware excluded patrons with mobility or cognitive disabilities due to the requirement for hand controllers and standing positions, highlighting that technology is not neutral.

The authors conclude that the success of VR programming depends heavily on librarian strategy rather than the technology alone. They recommend integrating VR with existing library programs, such as STEM or arts initiatives, to broaden appeal. Furthermore, they advocate for shifting from passive consumption to active content creation, encouraging libraries to involve community members in designing VR experiences relevant to their local contexts. This approach aims to address content limitations and promote inclusivity, ensuring that VR serves as a meaningful tool for informal learning and social engagement in public libraries.
