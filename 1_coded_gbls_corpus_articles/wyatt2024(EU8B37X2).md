# Summary

Wyatt and Leorke (2024) investigate the transformative role of games and play in public libraries, arguing that these elements are no longer peripheral services but central to the institution's spatial, temporal, and functional evolution. Drawing on qualitative fieldwork conducted between 2015 and 2020, including observations and interviews with 27 library staff and managers across Australia, Finland, Singapore, and the United States, the authors analyze how libraries are adapting to digitization and urban policy imperatives. The study challenges the traditional characterization of libraries as "third places"—neutral zones separate from home and work—by demonstrating how play is increasingly instrumentalized within frameworks of digital capitalism, creative city policies, and smart urban development.

The methodology relies on case studies of diverse library environments, from high-profile flagship developments like Oodi Central Library in Helsinki and the State Library Victoria in Melbourne to suburban branches such as Logan Central Library in Brisbane. The authors categorize the manifestations of play into several domains: gaming zones, children’s spaces, playful architecture, and engagement with external public spaces. Empirical findings indicate that gaming zones are altering library acoustics and spatial flows, often spilling beyond designated areas and creating new social clusters. For instance, at Oodi, transparent console rooms turn gameplay into a spectacle, while at Iso Omena, gaming areas are interspersed with reading shelves, blurring functional boundaries. Temporally, the introduction of games has extended user dwell times and influenced programming schedules, such as evening board game sessions, thereby reshaping the library’s daily rhythms.

A significant finding is the alignment of library services with local economic strategies. In Helsinki, Oodi supports the city’s game development industry through dedicated spaces and literacy programs, reflecting Finland’s strong gaming sector. Similarly, Melbourne libraries provide co-working spaces and mentorship for creative workers, positioning themselves as hubs for the knowledge economy. The authors note that children’s play spaces, such as the "Stories Come Alive Room" in Singapore or the Children’s Quarter in Melbourne, serve dual purposes: fostering early literacy and social skills while also instilling digital competencies valued by a flexible, entrepreneurial labor market. This echoes historical justifications from the 19th century, where libraries used games to provide moral guidance and controlled leisure for working-class populations.

The authors critically assess the implications of these trends, suggesting that the integration of play contributes to the erosion of distinctions between work and leisure. They argue that libraries are being conscripted into neoliberal governance models that prioritize innovation, digital inclusion, and economic productivity. While library staff continue to advocate for the library’s value as a safe, welcoming "third place," the authors contend that this concept obscures the institution's growing role in mediating structural transitions toward post-industrial urbanism. The study concludes that play in libraries is not merely recreational but a significant feature of how public institutions adapt to and facilitate the demands of contemporary digital capitalism and urban redevelopment.
