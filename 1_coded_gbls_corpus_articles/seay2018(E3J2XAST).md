### Summary
# **Seay, J. A. (2018). Considering games and gamification in libraries & associated entities: Surely, you can't be serious: When library folk go game. *Against the Grain, 30*(2), 58-59. https://doi.org/10.7771/2380-176X.8063**

Seay's column is an informal professional essay introducing why games, serious games, escape rooms, and gamification matter for libraries. It is not an empirical study or a systematic literature review, but it is useful as evidence of how library practitioners were framing game-based services in the late 2010s. The column argues that libraries have become "transformed by games" and that games are no longer marginal novelties restricted to children's programming or occasional recreation.

The opening example is the commercial escape room: a timed, multiplayer environment in which players solve puzzles, interpret clues, search the room, and use strategy to reach a goal. Seay immediately connects these practices to library work by noting that escape rooms depend on knowledge, skill with knowledge, information retrieval, clue-finding, and problem solving. He presents library escape rooms as a natural fit for outreach, promotion, and instruction, especially information literacy. He points to contemporary library coverage of BreakoutEDU, research-library escape rooms, youth programming, and library escape-room manuals to show that the form had already become a visible professional trend.

The column then widens from escape rooms to games in libraries more generally. Seay describes public-library game nights, circulating board game collections, board game outreach, ALA tabletop gaming networks, video game events, and board game play around titles such as *Settlers of Catan*, *Ticket to Ride*, and *Pandemic*. The key point for GBLS is that games are framed as part of libraries' community-space function. Seay argues that group video game participation and the board game renaissance helped fuel the idea of libraries as places for gathering, play, and community interaction.

Seay also introduces "serious games," defined broadly as games designed for a purpose beyond entertainment. Serious games let educators and trainers simulate risk and reality without exposing learners to actual risk. This gives GBLS language for connecting library game services to education, training, professional development, socialization, activism, and public engagement without reducing play to frivolity. The column cites the MIT Game Lab's broad claim that play now supports domains ranging from entertainment to education, art, activism, science, and socialization.

The gamification section is especially relevant to the review's conceptual terrain. Seay defines gamification as adding games or game-like elements to a task to encourage participation, but he cautions that it is more than simply making unpleasant tasks palatable. Engagement and participation are the critical terms. He describes gamification as a system of elements and principles that encourage or compel users, then reframes behavior manipulation as user-centered instructional engagement. This ambivalence is useful for GBLS because it keeps gamification distinct from games while also raising questions about design ethics, motivation, and agency.

For academic and school libraries, Seay emphasizes games and gamification as tools for learner engagement. He notes that academic libraries use problem-solving games, badges, incentives, and gamified instruction to introduce research and information literacy skills that might otherwise be taught through bibliographic instruction lectures. The column therefore overlaps with later empirical work on escape rooms and misinformation games, but it functions more as a snapshot of the profession's enthusiasm and vocabulary than as evidence of outcomes.

For the GBLS project, this source supports broad framing. It helps explain why a review must distinguish among games, game-based learning, serious games, gamification, escape rooms, board game collections, and game nights. It also shows how practitioners connected games to outreach, instruction, library promotion, community space, and user engagement. Its limitations are significant: the article is playful, anecdotal, and promotional, with no assessment data. It should therefore be used as contextual practitioner discourse rather than as proof that game services work.
