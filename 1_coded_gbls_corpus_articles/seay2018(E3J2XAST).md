# Summary

Jared Alexander Seay’s 2018 column, "Considering Games and Gamification in Libraries & Associated Entities — Surely, You Can’t Be Serious: When Library Folk Go Game," published in *Against the Grain*, provides a qualitative overview of the growing intersection between library science and gaming culture. The article does not present empirical data or original research findings but rather synthesizes industry trends, anecdotal evidence, and existing literature to argue for the legitimacy and efficacy of games as tools for library instruction and community engagement.

The author begins by describing the mechanics and popularity of escape rooms, characterizing them as immersive experiences that require puzzle-solving, strategy, and information retrieval. Seay draws a direct parallel between these skills and traditional library competencies, noting that players often use knowledge retrieved from primary or secondary sources to solve problems within the game environment. He cites the proliferation of library-based escape rooms in both public and academic settings, referencing specific examples such as the University at Albany, SUNY, where an escape room platform was implemented for information literacy classes. The article posits that this trend is not merely a fad but a significant shift in how libraries approach outreach and instruction, leveraging the "mind stretching" nature of games to teach research skills.

Seay expands the scope beyond escape rooms to include board games and video games, asserting that these mediums have become ubiquitous in library spaces. He references search data indicating millions of results for "board game" and "public library," highlighting the prevalence of circulating collections and game nights. The author argues that this integration transforms libraries into community hubs, comparable to the historical impact of bookmobiles or story times. To support the seriousness of gaming as a sector, Seay includes market data: video games generated over $108 billion in revenue in 2017, and global board game sales reached $9.6 billion in 2016. These figures are used to counter historical perceptions of games as trivial pastimes, instead framing them as substantial economic and cultural forces.

A central theme of the article is the concept of "gamification," which Seay defines as the addition of game-like elements to non-game contexts to encourage participation. He distinguishes this from simple entertainment, describing it as a system of engagement that compels user interaction. In academic libraries, this manifests through the use of badges and incentives to teach information literacy, replacing traditional bibliographic instruction lectures. The author suggests that gamification serves as "user centered instruction engagement," allowing educators to simulate risk and reality without actual consequences. This approach is presented as a modern evolution of library services, aligning with broader trends in education and industry where "serious games" are used for training and learning.

The article concludes by predicting a continued convergence of libraries, education, the game industry, and publishing. Seay suggests that interactive multimedia and book apps are already merging with gaming principles, hinting at a future where these domains may unify into a single interactive entity. While the tone is informal and occasionally humorous, the underlying argument is that libraries must adapt to this "gamification maelstrom" to remain relevant and effective in engaging users. The piece serves as a persuasive endorsement of games-based library services, grounding its claims in observable trends and industry statistics rather than controlled experimental data.
