## Purpose
The article aims to conduct a systematic literature review to bridge the gap in understanding how different elements of learning analytics (LA)—specifically data types, methods, objectives, and stakeholders—contribute to game-based learning (GBL) practices within online higher education environments. It seeks to provide a conceptual framework for using LA to enhance GBL and identify research voids.

## Method
The authors employed a systematic literature review methodology guided by PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analyses). They searched five databases (Web of Science, Scopus, ERIC, IEEE, and LAK Proceedings) using defined keywords. Inclusion criteria restricted studies to peer-reviewed empirical papers published between 2011 and 2021, focusing on online GBL in higher education. A coding scheme based on Chatti et al.’s (2012) LA Reference Model (what, how, why, who) was developed and applied using ATLAS.ti software. Inter-rater reliability was established with a Cohen’s kappa of .83.
Evidence: "In this study, we developed a search strategy based on the PRISMA methodology... A coding scheme was developed... based on Chatti et al.’s (2012) LA framework to analyse selected studies." (Section 3 & 3.6)
Confidence: high

## Findings
The review categorized findings into four elements of LA:
1. **Data Types (What):** Gameplay data was the most dominant type (100% of studies), followed by assessment data (80%), player data (30%), and game metadata (20%). Internal gameplay data (e.g., time, badges) was more common than external data (e.g., eye-tracking).
2. **Methods (How):** Data mining methods were the most frequently coded (56% of codes), including classification and clustering. Visualizations were present in 85% of studies but had fewer distinct codes. Text mining and social network analysis were less prevalent.
3. **Objectives (Why):** Anticipated objectives included assessment (34% of codes), monitoring (19%), and reflection (18%). Additional objectives identified included engagement, motivation, and learning achievement.
4. **Stakeholders (Who):** The text implies stakeholders include learners, educators, and system designers, though specific quantitative breakdowns for this category are truncated in the provided source.
Evidence: "Data analysis for the first research question resulted in a total of 204 codes... Gameplay data... was identified in all included publications... Data mining methods are the most commonly used group of methods... Assessment as an LA objective was found in 35% of studies." (Section 4)

# Summary

This systematic literature review, authored by Banihashem et al. (2024), investigates the role of learning analytics (LA) in online game-based learning (GBL) within higher education contexts. The authors identify a gap in existing literature, noting that prior reviews often focused on serious games broadly or lacked a comprehensive framework detailing how specific LA elements interact to support educational outcomes. Consequently, the study aims to synthesize empirical evidence regarding four core components of LA: data types (what), analytical methods (how), objectives (why), and stakeholders (who).

The methodology adhered to PRISMA guidelines, searching five major databases for peer-reviewed empirical studies published between 2011 and 2021. From an initial pool of 484 records, the authors applied strict inclusion criteria—focusing exclusively on online GBL in higher education—to select 20 final publications. These studies were analyzed using a coding scheme derived from Chatti et al.’s LA Reference Model, with inter-rater reliability confirmed at a high level (κ = .83).

The findings reveal that gameplay data is the predominant data source, present in all included studies, encompassing both internal metrics (e.g., time spent, badges earned) and external metrics (e.g., eye-tracking, emotional reactions). Assessment data was also widely used, appearing in 80% of the studies. Regarding analytical methods, data mining techniques such as classification, clustering, and predictive modeling were the most frequently coded, indicating a strong reliance on algorithmic analysis to uncover patterns in learner behavior. Visualization methods were prevalent across 85% of the studies, primarily serving to present descriptive analytics through dashboards and charts. Text mining and social network analysis were less common but utilized for analyzing interactions and semantic content.

In terms of objectives, the review categorized purposes into anticipated and additional goals. Assessment emerged as a primary objective, with LA used extensively for formative and summative evaluation. Monitoring and reflection were also significant, supporting educators in tracking progress and fostering learner self-regulation. Additional objectives included enhancing engagement, motivation, and problem-solving skills. The study highlights that while LA is frequently used for assessment and monitoring, its potential for adaptive personalization and predictive intervention remains underexplored in the current literature.

The authors conclude by proposing a conceptual framework to guide the integration of LA in online GBL. They emphasize that understanding the interplay between data types, methods, and objectives is crucial for optimizing learning environments. The review suggests that future research should focus on expanding the use of predictive and prescriptive analytics, exploring diverse stakeholder benefits beyond educators and learners, and addressing the specific cognitive demands of higher education GBL contexts. This work provides a structured foundation for researchers and practitioners seeking to leverage data-driven insights to enhance game-based educational practices.
