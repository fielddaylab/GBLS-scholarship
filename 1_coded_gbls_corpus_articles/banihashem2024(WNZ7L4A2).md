### Summary
# **Banihashem, S. K., Dehghanzadeh, H., Clark, D., Noroozi, O., & Biemans, H. J. A. (2024). Learning analytics for online game-based learning: A systematic literature review. *Behaviour & Information Technology, 43*(12), 2689-2716. https://doi.org/10.1080/0144929X.2023.2255301**

Banihashem, Dehghanzadeh, Clark, Noroozi, and Biemans review how learning analytics is used in online game-based learning in higher education. The article is not about libraries, but it is adjacent to GBLS because many library games, escape rooms, online orientations, digital breakouts, and virtual-world programs struggle with the same assessment question: what data should be collected, how should it be analyzed, why is it being collected, and who is supposed to use it?

The review is organized around four core elements of learning analytics: what, how, why, and who. "What" refers to the data collected; "how" refers to analytic methods and levels; "why" refers to the objectives of analytics; and "who" refers to the stakeholders who receive or use the analytic outputs. The authors searched Web of Science, Scopus, ERIC, IEEE, and Learning Analytics and Knowledge conference proceedings using PRISMA-informed procedures, screened 484 records, and included 20 empirical studies published between 2011 and 2021 that addressed online game-based learning in higher education.

For the "what" question, gameplay data dominated. All 20 studies collected internal gameplay data such as time spent, badges, learner-game interactions, points, attempts, or activity traces; some also collected external gameplay data such as eye tracking, emotional reactions, cognitive reactions, or webcam/video data. Assessment data appeared in most studies and included quizzes, tests, interviews, open-ended questions, surveys, and scales. Player data, such as demographics, academic background, profiles, emotions, cognition, and behavior, appeared less often. Game metadata was also part of the overall framework.

For the "how" question, the review found data mining, visualization, text mining, and social network analysis. Data mining was the most coded method category and included classification, regression, clustering, prediction, sequential pattern mining, relationship mining, and related techniques. Visualization appeared in most studies through dashboards, graphs, tables, charts, network diagrams, plots, and other representations. Text mining was used for written interaction data, and social network analysis was used to examine relationships and interaction patterns. The authors also connect methods to descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and prescriptive analytics levels.

For the "why" question, the review distinguishes anticipated objectives from additional objectives. Anticipated learning-analytics objectives included assessment, monitoring, reflection, recommendation, adaptation, and prediction. Additional objectives included engagement, motivation and emotion, learning achievement, performance, decision making, interaction, satisfaction, stress detection, problem solving, language learning, and self-regulation. For the "who" question, learners were the primary stakeholders, followed by educators and system designers. Learners used analytics for reflection, awareness, recommendations, and adaptive support; educators used analytics for assessment, monitoring, feedback, and interventions; designers used analytics to evaluate the game system itself.

The discussion cautions against technological determinism. Learning analytics should not be bolted onto games just because data are available; the authors argue that analytic design should be guided by learning theory, the game-based-learning model, and the intended beneficiary and objective. Their conceptual framework begins with identifying the stakeholder and purpose, then selecting data types, analytics levels, and methods. They also name future-research needs: broader database coverage, inclusion of non-empirical work, deeper analysis of the relationship between game elements and analytics elements, implementation barriers, and more prescriptive models.

For the larger GBLS project, this source is useful as an assessment-adjacent vocabulary. It could help libraries think more carefully about online orientation games, digital breakouts, virtual-world programs, or game-design workshops if they decide to collect data beyond attendance and satisfaction: gameplay traces, assessment data, player data, dashboards, reflection prompts, recommendations, and stakeholder-specific feedback. It also reinforces that assessment design should start with service goals and user needs, not with whatever data the platform happens to log.

However, the article should remain excluded from the main review. Its evidence base is higher education online game-based learning, not library service. It does not study librarians, patrons, library collections, public/school/academic library programs, or GBLS outcomes. It is best retained as background for future assessment design, especially if the project later develops evaluation guidance for digital GBLS formats.
