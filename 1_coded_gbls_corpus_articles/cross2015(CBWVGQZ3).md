## Findings
The authors identify several key findings regarding game collections:
1. **Diversity of Formats:** Libraries must manage distinct challenges for board games (missing pieces, shelf space), console games (high cost, platform exclusivity, security/loss rates), and PC games (DRM issues preventing lending, necessitating lab-based access).
2. **Censorship and Resistance:** Academic libraries face external censorship pressures regarding violence and gender representation in games, as well as internal resistance from staff unfamiliar with the academic value of games. A specific incident involving the game *Rage* prompted a review of challenged materials policies.
3. **Pedagogical Value:** Games support not only game development and studies but also interdisciplinary courses (e.g., digital history, gender studies). The gaming lab facilitates collaborative learning and innovation.
4. **Staff Morale:** Developing game collections improved staff morale and teamwork, leading to the creation of a library games club and professional development opportunities.
5. **Technical Challenges:** Issues include Digital Rights Management (DRM), Downloadable Content (DLC), preservation concerns due to hardware obsolescence, and the need for specialized cataloging practices.
Evidence: "Censorship of video games is an ongoing issue... Internal resistance from within the library... technical and logistical issues around purchasing, preserving, and circulating games." (p. 137); "Another benefit discovered... was that it boosted morale and teamwork for Library staff." (p. 136)

## Implications
The authors argue that academic libraries must adopt comprehensive collection development policies that address the diversity of game formats, including board, console, and PC games. They recommend establishing gaming labs to overcome DRM barriers associated with PC games. Libraries should also prepare for censorship challenges by strengthening challenged materials policies and securing institutional support. Furthermore, the article implies that early adoption of game collections enhances the library’s profile, fosters interdisciplinary collaboration, and provides valuable professional development opportunities for staff.

# Summary

This article by Cross, Mould, and Smith (2015) addresses the evolving landscape of game collections in academic libraries, using Carleton University Library as a primary case study. The authors argue that while earlier literature focused heavily on console video games, the current gaming ecosystem is more complex, requiring libraries to consider board games, PC games, mobile games, and massively multiplayer online (MMO) games. The study aims to examine the challenges and benefits of developing such collections, providing empirical data from Carleton’s collection established in 2008.

The methodology relies on a qualitative analysis of Carleton University Library’s practices, including circulation statistics, policy reviews, and staff experiences. As of late 2014, the library held 119 board games, 550 console games, and 142 PC games. Circulation data from 2013–2014 indicated significant usage, with 1,645 console game checkouts and 521 board game checkouts. The authors distinguish between the logistical challenges of each format: board games offer clear mechanical structures for study but pose shelving and piece-loss issues; console games are popular and easy to circulate but suffer from high costs, platform exclusivity, and security concerns; PC games are hindered by Digital Rights Management (DRM), which prevents traditional lending. To address this, Carleton implemented a dedicated gaming lab where patrons can play PC games on-site rather than borrowing them.

The article highlights several critical challenges. First, censorship remains a significant issue, with faculty and community members occasionally challenging titles due to content related to violence or gender representation. A specific incident involving the game *Rage* led to a review and strengthening of the library’s challenged materials policy, emphasizing academic freedom. Second, internal resistance from library staff unfamiliar with the pedagogical value of games was encountered, particularly regarding the cost and storage of gaming accessories. The authors note that education and clear collection policies helped mitigate this resistance. Third, technical issues such as DRM, downloadable content (DLC), and preservation due to hardware obsolescence require careful planning. The authors suggest that libraries may need to rely on digital distribution platforms like Steam for PC games and consider emulation or institutional repositories for preservation.

Despite these challenges, the authors report substantial benefits. The game collection supports not only game development and studies programs but also interdisciplinary courses in history and women’s studies, fostering innovative teaching methods. The gaming lab serves as a dynamic space for collaborative learning and experimentation. Furthermore, the initiative improved staff morale and teamwork, leading to the formation of a library games club and opportunities for professional presentation at conferences.

The implications for academic libraries are clear: they must develop robust collection development policies that account for the diversity of game formats and the technical constraints of digital distribution. Libraries should anticipate censorship challenges and secure institutional support to uphold academic freedom. Additionally, establishing gaming labs may be necessary to provide access to PC games restricted by DRM. The authors conclude that while managing game collections involves risk and complexity, it positions libraries at the forefront of higher education trends, enhancing their profile and fostering valuable collaborations with faculty and students.
