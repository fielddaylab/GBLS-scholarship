### Summary
# **Cross, E., Mould, D., & Smith, R. (2015). The protean challenge of game collections at academic libraries. *New Review of Academic Librarianship, 21*(2), 129-145. https://doi.org/10.1080/13614533.2015.1043467**

Cross, Mould, and Smith examine the practical problem of academic-library game collections after the early wave of console-game collection building. Their core argument is that the gaming ecosystem had become more complex, so academic libraries supporting game development and game studies could not rely on a simple model of circulating console games. Instead, libraries needed to consider board games, console games, PC games, digital distribution, downloadable content, DRM, mobile games, MMOs, gaming labs, preservation, censorship, staff resistance, and traditional technical-services workflows.

The article is grounded in the Carleton University Library game collection, which began in 2008 after faculty requested games to support game-development courses. By the end of 2014, Carleton's collection included 119 board games, 550 console games, and 142 PC games available through Steam in a dedicated gaming lab. In the 2013-2014 reporting year, board games circulated 521 times and console games 1,645 times, with circulation distributed across much of the collection. Carleton also crowdsourced collection development by seeking input from faculty, students, and library staff.

One major contribution is the distinction among board, console, and PC games. Board games are valuable for aspiring game designers because rules and mechanics are visible, comparatively simple, and open to analysis. Console games are easy to shelve and circulate and include many high-production-value AAA titles that patrons want for course work and leisure. PC games, especially indie games, are important for game studies and design because they are experimental, political, artistic, and often more feasible as models for student projects; however, DRM and non-transferable licenses make ordinary lending difficult. Carleton responded by offering PC games through a bookable gaming lab rather than a circulation model.

The article also identifies several benefits of academic game collections. They support faculty and students in game development and game studies by making a game canon or course texts available on standard library terms. They enable teaching outside game programs, including digital history, gender studies, GIS Day demonstrations, Minecraft, Oculus Rift, and other interdisciplinary uses. The Carleton case also shows internal benefits: staff gaming clubs, conference presentations, improved morale, teamwork, and new professional expertise around an innovative library service.

The challenges are equally important for GBLS planning. Cross et al. describe a censorship challenge around *Rage*, which prompted review and public posting of a challenged-materials policy. They also describe internal resistance around cost, mature-rated content, and cumbersome or allegedly unhygienic accessories such as steering wheels, Wii balance boards, Dance Dance Revolution mats, and Rock Band instruments. Their conclusion is that game collections require institutional support, senior-management backing, collection policies, and communication with staff about how games support teaching and research.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is a strong operations and service-model source. It treats games as collections that require acquisition channels, credit cards for digital subscriptions, MARC records, emerging RDA/OLAC guidance, shelving, browsing, security, replacements, lost-item policies, preservation planning, technical-services capacity, room booking, lab acceptable-use policies, computing support, and ongoing attention to DRM/DLC. It also shows that game collections can be both circulating inventory and in-library infrastructure: the physical game, console, accessory, digital license, lab workstation, and staff expertise all become parts of access.

The article should be used as a practitioner case and planning framework rather than an outcomes assessment. Its evidence is a single academic-library case, circulation data, examples from Carleton courses, and implementation reflection. Still, its value to GBLS is high because it names the operational risks that make game services durable or fragile. Libraries that want to collect games need a collection-development policy that matches local curricula, local patrons, platform realities, preservation needs, and staff capacity; otherwise the collection can become too thin, too hard to access, or too vulnerable to challenge.
