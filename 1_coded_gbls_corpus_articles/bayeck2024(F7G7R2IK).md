## Findings
The analysis reveals that the design of both games is deeply embedded in their local environments ("place"). For Songo, the use of wood for the board and Njansang seeds as playing pieces reflects the forest-dwelling lifestyle of the Ekang/Fang people. The game’s structure (seven holes per player) is interpreted by players as representing the seven days of the week, linking gameplay to local philosophies of labor and time. For Owela, the original design involved digging holes in the ground, reflecting the flat, semiarid plains of Namibia. The playing pieces (marula seeds) are referred to as "cattle," mirroring the Aawambo people’s reliance on animal husbandry and their measurement of wealth through livestock. The authors conclude that these games are not designed in a vacuum but are material expressions of local heritage, social practices, and environmental realities.

# Summary

This article by Rebecca Y. Bayeck and Joseph M. Bayeck investigates the intersection of place and game design through a qualitative analysis of two classic African board games: Songo from Cameroon and Owela from Namibia. The authors aim to expand the scope of game design research, which often focuses on digital or modern commercial games, to include indigenous and historical artifacts. They argue that these games are not merely recreational tools but are cultural texts that embody the values, environments, and philosophies of their creators.

The methodology involves treating the board games as texts for in-depth analysis, supplemented by semi-structured interviews with two players who provided interpretive context regarding the games' historical and cultural significance. The authors explicitly reject the generic term "Mancala" for these games, asserting that such categorization obscures the unique design complexities and specific cultural rules inherent to each community’s version.

The findings highlight a strong correlation between the physical environment ("place") and the material and structural design of the games. For Songo, played by the Ekang/Fang people in Cameroon’s tropical rainforests, the board is crafted from wood and the playing pieces are seeds from the Njansang tree, a vital non-timber forest product. The game’s structure, featuring seven holes per player, is interpreted by players as symbolizing the seven days of the week, reflecting a local philosophy that links strategic gameplay to agricultural labor and time management. Similarly, Owela, played by the Aawambo people in Namibia’s semiarid plains, originally involved digging holes directly into the ground, utilizing the flat terrain. The playing pieces, marula seeds, are referred to as "cattle," mirroring the community’s pastoral lifestyle where wealth is measured by livestock. These design choices demonstrate that the games are deeply situated in their local contexts, drawing on available materials and reflecting social practices.

The authors conclude that game design is never a vacuum but an interaction with local values, heritage, and environmental constraints. They emphasize that these classic games serve as artifacts of material culture that trace social practices and beliefs. The implications for library services and education are significant: the study advocates for culturally responsive approaches to game-based learning. Educators should consider the cultural origins and design contexts of games to ensure they are relevant and meaningful to diverse learners. By recognizing how place informs design, libraries and educators can better leverage indigenous games to foster connections to history, identity, and community values, moving beyond Western-centric definitions of play and learning.
