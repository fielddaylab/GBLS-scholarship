### Summary
# **Bayeck, R. Y., & Bayeck, J. M. (2024). The intersection of place and game design: The case of two classic African board games. *Design Issues, 40*(4), 5-16. https://doi.org/10.1162/desi_a_00776**

Bayeck and Bayeck examine the interaction of place and game design through two classic African board games: Songo, associated here with Ekang/Fang communities in Cameroon, Gabon, and Equatorial Guinea, and Owela, associated with Aawambo communities in Namibia and Angola. The article begins from the observation that game design is often discussed through digital games, modern board games, and Western design vocabulary. The authors argue that historical and Indigenous games also deserve to be understood as designed artifacts, even when their original design process cannot be reconstructed.

A central move in the article is terminological and cultural. The authors resist flattening Songo, Owela, and related games into the generic label "Mancala," because that label hides distinctive names, rules, materials, philosophies, directions of play, and community meanings. They also acknowledge the difficulty of using English and Western concepts such as "board game" and "design" for African games whose playing surfaces may be carved wood, stone, or holes in the ground. They use these terms carefully, defining board games as games with playing pieces and surfaces that facilitate interaction among players, and defining design as a process of creation situated in time, place, situation, culture, materials, values, and local practice.

The theoretical framing connects game design, culture, place, and learning. Drawing on game-design scholarship, critical play, Indigenous game design, architectural and urban-design literature, and theories of place, the authors treat games as cultural artifacts and systems of information. Place is not simply a fictional setting inside a game; it is the physical, social, historical, and cultural where-ness in which the game is made and through which players make meaning. Place includes materials, landscapes, people, memories, symbols, histories, social relations, tangible heritage, and intangible heritage.

Methodologically, the article treats board games as texts and uses textual analysis to read Songo and Owela as cultural objects. The authors combine that textual analysis with semi-structured interviews with two game players, treating those interviews as paratexts that illuminate how players connect the games to history, ancestry, and community meanings. The evidence is therefore interpretive rather than experimental, but it is appropriate to the article's question: how place becomes visible in game aesthetics, materials, names, and player interpretation.

The Songo analysis shows how design draws on local environment and philosophy. Songo is described as a strategic game with a rectangular wooden board, two rows, fourteen holes, and seventy seeds known locally as Ibang. Its board material reflects forest environments, while the playing seeds come from Njansang, a locally important edible tree product used in food and livelihood practices. Player interpretation connects the fourteen holes and seven holes per player to Ekang/Fang ideas about time, work, planning, and life. In this reading, Songo is not only a strategy game; it encodes relationships among forest ecology, food practices, labor, philosophy, and social memory.

The Owela analysis makes a parallel argument for a different place. Owela is described as a strategic Aawambo game with four rows and thirty-two or more holes, originally dug into flat sandy ground and played with marula fruits called Eengobe, or cattle. The authors connect this design to semiarid plains, white sands, marula trees, agriculture, animal husbandry, and cattle as a sign of wealth. Even when Owela shifts from ground-dug holes to stone or wood forms in urban settings, its design remains tied to local materials, names, and lifeways. The article therefore treats analog game technologies as place-informed designs rather than neutral mechanics.

For the larger GBLS project, Bayeck and Bayeck are useful as adjacent game-studies evidence for culturally relevant game selection, advisory, programming, and facilitation. The article does not study libraries, but it challenges libraries to treat games as cultural media whose names, materials, histories, communities, and player meanings matter. A library that collects or programs classic, Indigenous, diasporic, or culturally specific games should avoid presenting them as interchangeable mechanics or generic "Mancala"-style activities. Description, display, facilitation, and teaching should respect local names, contexts, and knowledge, while also considering whether a game design is culturally meaningful for the learners and communities being served.

The limitations are important. The study analyzes two games, focuses largely on visual and material design, and uses only two player interviews. It does not evaluate a library program, circulation collection, game-based learning intervention, or patron outcome. Still, it gives the GBLS review a concise way to connect analog tabletop services with cultural memory, place-based design, and inclusion. It supports the broader claim that library game services need culturally attentive advisory and facilitation, not only access to a shelf of game objects.
