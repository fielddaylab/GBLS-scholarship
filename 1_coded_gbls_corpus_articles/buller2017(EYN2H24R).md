# Summary

Ryan Buller’s 2017 article, "Lending Video Game Consoles in an Academic Library," presents a case study of the University of Denver’s decision to implement a video game console lending service. The initiative was driven by the need to support the Emergent Digital Practices program, an interdepartmental curriculum focused on video game design and creation. Funded partially by a grant awarded to a professor in the program, the project aimed to provide students with access to hardware necessary for analyzing games across different platforms and generations, addressing a gap where traditional library resources failed to meet the specific technical needs of game design students.

The article details the decision-making process that led to choosing circulation over establishing a dedicated gaming space within the library. While a dedicated space would have offered greater control over equipment and eliminated barriers for students lacking televisions, the University of Denver’s limited physical space in its Academic Commons model made this impractical. Furthermore, the academic goal required students to have extended access to hardware for comparative analysis, which circulation facilitated more effectively than restricted in-library use. The library purchased current and previous generation consoles from Sony, Microsoft, and Nintendo, along with GAEMS Personal Gaming Environments to ensure portability and protection, allowing students to use the devices off-campus with built-in screens.

Implementation presented several challenges, primarily concerning patron accountability and software security. To address accountability, the library developed extensive check-in forms with images of all components to ensure complete returns and maintained detailed serial number records for internal hardware parts to prevent component swapping or theft. Software security was managed by creating default library accounts that prevented users from locking out other patrons or storing credit card information on the devices. Staff verified that all user profiles were logged out upon return, a process that slowed circulation but ensured system integrity and patron privacy.

The article also addresses internal resistance from library staff who questioned the appropriateness of lending non-traditional materials. This was mitigated by framing the service as a direct response to demonstrated curricular needs rather than a general attempt to attract patrons. The outcome has been positive: circulation numbers met expectations without formal marketing, and feedback led to the addition of extra controllers. The project is cited as evidence that academic libraries can successfully expand their collections to include hardware, thereby reinforcing their role as active partners in supporting evolving academic disciplines.
