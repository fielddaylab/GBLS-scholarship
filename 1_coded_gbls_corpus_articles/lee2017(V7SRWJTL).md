## Purpose
The article aims to identify the specific "appeals" (elements that draw users in) of video games from empirical user data to support readers' advisory (RA) and recommendation services. It seeks to answer two research questions: what kinds of appeals can be identified from users' descriptions of their favorite games, and how do these appeals relate to established video game genres? The authors argue that understanding these appeals can serve as an additional metadata access point for library catalogs, enabling more diversified recommendations across genres.
Evidence: "In this work, we use the concept of appeals from existing literature in readers’ advisory and media studies to understand what attracts people to play certain games... We aim to answer the following research questions: What kinds of appeals can we identify for video games from people’s description of their favorite games and reasons for liking them? How do appeals relate to currently established video game genres?"

## Method
The study employed a large-scale survey design. The full survey contained twenty-eight questions regarding video game playing experiences, information seeking, and collection behaviors. For this specific analysis, the authors focused on two components: (1) an open-ended question asking respondents to describe their favorite games and why they appealed to them, which was coded by two independent coders using a consensus model; and (2) quantitative questions where respondents selected their favorite genre from a list of eleven genres and identified reasons for that preference from a list of twelve appeals. Descriptive statistics were derived from the quantitative responses.

## Findings
The authors identified sixteen distinct appeals from the open-ended data: Narrative, Challenge, Sensation, Fellowship, Nostalgia, Fantasy, Exploration, Mood, Depth, Creativity/Innovation, Expression, Accomplishment, Competition, Submission, Mastery, and Learning. Narrative was the most frequently cited appeal (628 mentions), followed by Challenge (292) and Sensation (287). The study also analyzed the correlation between these appeals and eleven video game genres. Key correlations included: Exploration being prominent in Action/Adventure games; Narrative in RPGs; Physical Interaction in Music/Dance games; and Fellowship in Shooter, Sports, and Driving/Racing games. The data revealed that appeals provide a different dimension of information than genre alone, allowing for cross-genre recommendations based on shared user motivations (e.g., "Submission" appears in both Puzzle and Simulation genres despite their thematic differences).
Evidence: "From responses to the open-ended question... we identified the following sixteen appeals... Narrative was the most commonly identified appeal... We explored the relationship between the preceding appeals and video game genres... exploration is most important for action and adventure games; narrative for RPGs; and physical interaction for music and dance games."

## Implications
The authors propose that these sixteen appeals can serve as additional metadata fields in library catalogs and recommendation systems to improve readers' advisory services. They suggest methods such as crowdsourcing appeal data or displaying proportional appeal statistics for individual games. The findings support the concept of "whole-collection RA," where video games are integrated into broader media recommendations alongside books, films, and music. Future work will investigate specific appeals like mood and narrative across multiple media types to facilitate cross-media recommendations.
Evidence: "We envision using these appeals as an additional access point for games in catalogs and other organizational systems to help improve user recommendations and RA for video games... # Summary

This article addresses the gap in library readers' advisory (RA) services regarding video games, which are increasingly present in collections but rarely recommended due to a lack of standardized frameworks for understanding user preferences. Building on established RA concepts such as "appeals" and "doorways," the authors define appeals as elements of media that evoke emotional and intellectual experiences. The primary objective is to empirically identify these appeals from user data and determine their relationship with existing video game genres, thereby creating a complementary access point for recommendations that transcends traditional genre classifications.

The methodology involved a large-scale survey distributed through various online and physical channels, including gaming forums and museum exhibitions. Of the 2,163 participants, 1,257 completed the survey. The data collection focused on two areas: an open-ended question asking users to describe their favorite games and the reasons for their appeal, and quantitative questions linking preferred genres to specific appeal categories. The open-ended responses were analyzed using a consensus coding model by two independent coders, while descriptive statistics were used for the genre-appeal correlations.

The study identified sixteen distinct appeals: Narrative, Challenge, Sensation, Fellowship, Nostalgia, Fantasy, Exploration, Mood, Depth, Creativity/Innovation, Expression, Accomplishment, Competition, Submission, Mastery, and Learning. Narrative emerged as the most dominant appeal, cited by 628 respondents, emphasizing the importance of story, character relatability, and integrated gameplay. Challenge was the second most common, encompassing both mechanical difficulty and intellectual problem-solving. Sensation included visual, auditory, and physical aspects, with visual aesthetics being the most frequently mentioned. Social appeals were divided into Fellowship (cooperative play) and Competition (striving against others or oneself). Other significant appeals included Exploration (discovering new worlds), Depth (player agency and consequences), and Submission (relaxation or "turning off the brain").

A critical finding was the correlation between these appeals and eleven standard video game genres. The data revealed that while some genres align with expected appeals (e.g., Narrative in RPGs, Physical Interaction in Music/Dance games), others showed unexpected overlaps. For instance, "Submission" was a top appeal for both Puzzle and Simulation games, despite their differing themes. Similarly, "Fellowship" was prominent in Shooter, Sports, and Driving/Racing games, though secondary appeals differed (Challenge for Shooters, Competition for Racing). These patterns suggest that appeals offer a nuanced layer of metadata that can facilitate cross-genre recommendations, allowing librarians to suggest diverse titles based on shared user motivations rather than superficial genre labels.

The authors conclude that integrating these appeals into library catalogs—potentially through crowdsourced metadata or proportional appeal displays—can enhance the quality of RA services. They advocate for a "whole-collection" approach, where video games are treated as equal partners in media recommendation alongside books and films. Future research will expand this framework to other media types, particularly investigating how appeals like mood and narrative manifest across different formats to support comprehensive, cross-media advisory services. The study provides a robust, user-centered foundation for improving video game discoverability and recommendation in library settings.
