# Summary

This study by Ryan Scicluna (2026) investigates the impact of gamified library orientations on first-year undergraduate students at the University of Malta. Grounded in Social Exchange Theory (SET), the research posits that student engagement is a rational calculation weighing the "costs" (e.g., anxiety, cognitive load) against the "benefits" (e.g., information access, social value). The primary objective was to determine if gamification could lower the psychological cost of library entry and increase the willingness to return, while also assessing whether "Game Literacy" moderates these outcomes.

The methodology employed a quasi-experimental, post-test-only design with 132 first-year students. Intact classes were assigned to either a traditional lecture-and-tour orientation (Control, n=65) or a gamified narrative quest involving scavenger hunt elements (Experimental, n=67). Both interventions covered identical learning outcomes regarding library navigation and resource use. Data were collected immediately post-intervention using an adapted Library Anxiety Scale (LAS), Net Promoter Score (NPS), return intention metrics, and game literacy indicators. Statistical analyses included t-tests, Mann-Whitney U tests, and Two-Way ANOVA to assess main effects and interactions.

The findings demonstrated that gamification significantly reduced library anxiety compared to traditional methods, with a large effect size (Cohen’s d = 1.55). The gamified group reported lower anxiety scores (M = 2.14) than the traditional group (M = 3.42). Furthermore, the gamified intervention yielded higher return intentions and a median NPS of 9, compared to 6 for the control group, suggesting a stronger perceived benefit in the social exchange. However, the study identified a critical moderating variable: Game Literacy. A significant interaction effect revealed that while all students benefited from gamification relative to the lecture format, those with low game literacy experienced higher anxiety levels (M = 2.68) than their high-literacy counterparts (M = 1.85). The authors term this phenomenon a "literacy tax," attributing it to the additional cognitive load required for non-gamers to decipher game mechanics alongside library tasks.

The discussion emphasizes that the reduction in anxiety is partly due to the collaborative, team-based structure of the gamified activity, which distributes psychological risk and fosters peer-to-peer support. However, the authors caution against viewing gamification as a universal solution. The "literacy tax" highlights an equity concern: without careful design, gamification may inadvertently disadvantage users unfamiliar with game logic. Consequently, the study recommends implementing "low-threshold" mechanics to ensure inclusivity.

Several limitations are acknowledged. The post-test-only design precludes the measurement of absolute anxiety reduction from a baseline, as pre-testing was avoided to prevent sensitization effects. Additionally, the immediate timing of data collection raises questions about the longevity of the affective benefits, potentially influenced by novelty effects or demand characteristics. The authors suggest future research employ Solomon four-group designs and longitudinal studies to assess sustained engagement and information-seeking behavior over time. Ultimately, the study concludes that gamification is a valuable tool for humanizing academic libraries and breaking initial barriers, provided it is designed with equitable access in mind.
