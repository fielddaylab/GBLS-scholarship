# Summary

Jon Goddard’s 2020 editorial, "Public Libraries Respond to the COVID-19 Pandemic, Creating a New Service Model," examines the rapid transformation of public library services during the early stages of the pandemic. The article posits that librarians successfully rewrote service protocols to maintain community engagement and educational support through virtual means, thereby demonstrating the enduring value of libraries as technology-driven hubs. The piece serves as both a report on these adaptations and an advocacy tool for preserving the hybrid service model post-pandemic.

The methodology relies on aggregated data from library vendors, specific case studies of library systems, and industry reports rather than original empirical research. Goddard highlights significant surges in electronic resource usage. Data from Overdrive, utilized by nearly 90% of U.S. public libraries, indicates that daily ebook checkouts remained consistently 52% higher than pre-COVID averages, with new user registrations doubling or tripling previous highs. In Suffolk County, New York, new patron registrations to Overdrive increased by 72%. The shift was particularly pronounced among younger demographics; youth and juvenile ebook readership saw an 87% increase. Educational support services also expanded, with Brainfuse online tutoring usage in Suffolk County rising nearly 50%, and Gale’s virtual preschool program, Miss Humblebee's Academy, experiencing a 100% increase in user sessions. Adult learners similarly engaged with digital resources, evidenced by a 50% increase in enrollments for Gale Courses between March and July compared to the previous year.

Community engagement transitioned entirely to virtual platforms. Libraries utilized tools such as Zoom, Discord, Facebook Live, and YouTube to deliver programming. The San José Public Library (SJPL), which had no prior virtual programming, launched over 2,000 virtual events by October 2020, including storytimes and reading initiatives. Similarly, the Oceanside Library in New York scaled its offerings from zero to eight daily programs within a week of closure, eventually opening participation to out-of-state residents due to high demand. Reference services also shifted online; at SJPL, chat reference conversations increased by nearly 400%, rising from approximately 40 to 160 sessions per day, with support provided in multiple languages including Spanish, Vietnamese, and Chinese.

The article addresses the digital divide, noting that approximately 25% of households lack broadband internet. Libraries responded by maintaining outdoor Wi-Fi signals, lending hotspots, and partnering with educational institutions. SJPL collaborated with city departments to distribute approximately 16,000 high-speed hotspots to students and the public. Additionally, libraries implemented contactless curbside pickup services, utilizing tools like LibCal for appointment scheduling and LibAnswers for SMS notifications upon arrival.

Goddard concludes that these adaptations should not be temporary measures but rather a permanent "new normal." He argues that libraries must continue to offer hybrid services, combining physical spaces with robust virtual platforms for reference, programming, and material distribution. The editorial underscores the importance of federal advocacy, noting the success in securing $50 million through the CARES Act and calling for further support via the Library Stabilization Fund Act. Ultimately, the article asserts that the pandemic revealed libraries' critical role in providing equitable access to information and technology, a function that must be sustained through continued investment and strategic planning.
