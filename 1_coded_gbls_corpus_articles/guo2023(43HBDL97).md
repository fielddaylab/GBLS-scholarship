# Summary

This article by Guo and Lan (2023) presents an action research study investigating the efficacy of integrating 3D virtual reality (VR) construction into English as a Foreign Language (EFL) reading programs for young learners in a public library setting. The study addresses the need for innovative language learning strategies in informal environments, particularly given Taiwan’s policy goals for bilingualism and the challenge of maintaining student motivation outside formal schooling.

The research employed a mixed-methods design involving 38 participants aged 10–12 (Grades 4–6), divided into two groups of 19. The first group engaged in a traditional reading project consisting of picture book reading and word games. The second group participated in an enhanced project that combined picture book reading with the creation of 3D virtual stories using the Omni-immersion Vision (OIV) tool. Data were collected through pre- and post-reading tests, questionnaires measuring motivation, anxiety, and technology attitudes, and qualitative analysis of student artifacts including posters and VR constructions.

Quantitative results indicated that both groups improved their English reading scores. The VR-integrated group showed a greater mean improvement (26.84 points) compared to the traditional group (21.58 points), although this difference was not statistically significant (p = .31). However, significant differences emerged in affective measures. The VR group demonstrated a statistically significant increase in extrinsic motivation (p < .05) and reported lower levels of learning anxiety and more positive attitudes toward technology compared to the traditional group. The authors attribute these benefits to the immersive and interactive nature of VR, which reduces the pressure associated with language production and increases engagement through visual richness and autonomy.

Qualitative findings revealed a complex process of transmediation among students in the VR group. Students initially relied heavily on written text and drawings for planning but shifted to multimodal expression in the VR environment. The VR creations were characterized by high object density, extensive use of pre-existing assets, and interactive features such as embedded questions and directional cues. This shift highlights how VR supports semiotic redesign, allowing learners to convey meaning through spatial arrangement and visual intensity rather than solely through text.

The study concludes that VR integration in library settings can effectively support EFL learning by enhancing motivation and reducing anxiety, even if direct gains in reading scores are comparable to traditional methods. The authors acknowledge limitations related to the small sample size and short duration of the interventions, suggesting that future research should employ larger cohorts and longer timeframes to validate these findings. This work contributes to the literature on Games-Based Library Services by demonstrating how virtual construction tasks can serve as engaging, multimodal learning tools in informal educational contexts.
