## Purpose
The study aims to understand how recently housed young people with histories of chronic housing instability would (re)imagine future "third places" (community settings like libraries and parks) to meet their physical, emotional, and relational needs. It seeks to expand Oldenburg and Brissett’s theoretical framework on third places by centering the perspectives of marginalized youth and evaluating how game-based inquiry can facilitate this participatory foresight process.

## Method
The researchers employed a qualitative, participatory design using an original board game titled the "Future of Third Places Game." The method involved an iterative co-design process including brainstorming with residents, prototyping with experts, pilot testing, and final data collection. Data consisted of recorded transcripts from six game sessions where participants designed physical or social elements in response to "community request cards." Analysis utilized qualitative thematic analysis in Dedoose software, guided by an a priori coding scheme based on Oldenburg and Brissett’s tenets, supplemented by inductive subcoding.

## Findings
Participants identified three core tenets necessary for affirming third places: agency and individualization, explicit inclusion, and meeting everyday needs. To actualize these, they imagined spaces with specific physical and relational qualities: multifunctional areas that "meet many needs," portable and adaptable infrastructure (e.g., mobile centers, retractable roofs), freedom to choose modes of play, mechanisms to subvert oppressive social hierarchies (e.g., non-authoritative adult presence), options for privacy or connection, assurance that supportive people are always present (e.g., 24/7 access), and free amenities (e.g., food, resources). These findings diverge from traditional third place theory by emphasizing active inclusion over neutrality and the fulfillment of basic survival needs alongside socialization.
Evidence: "Participants suggested that certain tenets must be present in third places: they must offer opportunities for agency and individualization; they must meet everyday needs; and they must be explicitly inclusive." (Abstract/Findings)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The study suggests that third places like libraries and community centers must evolve to explicitly include marginalized youth by providing free amenities, adaptable spaces, and non-hierarchical adult support. It highlights the utility of game-based inquiry as a method for community planning and participatory design, allowing stakeholders to envision equitable futures. The authors recommend protecting funding for these spaces and shifting public perception regarding youth presence in public areas.

# Summary

This article reports on a qualitative study utilizing game-based inquiry to explore how recently housed young people envision the future of "third places"—community settings such as libraries, parks, and community centers. The authors argue that while third places are theoretically important for social connection, they often fail marginalized youth, particularly those with histories of homelessness, due to exclusionary practices and hostile architecture. To address this gap, the researchers partnered with 21 young adult residents (ages 18–24) from a permanent supportive housing program in Grand Junction, Colorado. The study employed an original board game, the "Future of Third Places Game," designed through an iterative co-creation process involving residents and design experts.

The methodology centered on six game sessions where participants responded to "community request cards" depicting common pain points experienced by unhoused youth (e.g., lack of safe resting spots, exclusion from age-restricted venues). Using craft supplies, participants designed physical or social interventions to address these needs. The researchers analyzed transcripts of these sessions using thematic analysis, guided by Oldenburg and Brissett’s classic tenets of third places but adapted to capture constructivist perspectives on power and inclusion.

The findings revealed that participants prioritized three core tenets for affirming third places: agency and individualization, explicit inclusion, and the meeting of everyday needs. Unlike traditional theory which views third places as neutral social hubs, participants envisioned spaces that actively subvert oppressive hierarchies and provide essential resources. Specific qualities identified included multifunctional spaces that allow for various activities (from dancing to quiet rest), portable and adaptable infrastructure (such as mobile community centers or pods with retractable roofs), and freedom to choose how to engage in play. Participants also emphasized the importance of privacy options, the presence of supportive but non-authoritative adults, and free amenities like food and hygiene items.

The authors discuss these findings as a necessary expansion of third place theory, arguing that for marginalized youth, third places must function as sites of survival and safety, not just socialization. The study highlights the potential of game-based inquiry as a tool for participatory community planning, enabling stakeholders to co-design inclusive environments. Limitations noted include the small sample size (N=21), the lack of systematic demographic data collection which limits intersectional analysis, and the possibility that the game’s design cards may have primed certain responses related to rest and safety. The authors recommend future research explore these tenets across diverse geographic and identity groups and suggest that libraries and community centers adopt these youth-centered principles to better serve vulnerable populations.
