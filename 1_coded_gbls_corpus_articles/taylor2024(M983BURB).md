## Findings
Protagonists are depicted as adventurous outsiders (often young, male, Caucasian/Japanese) who visit libraries to solve short-term problems or improve themselves/relationships. Libraries serve as safe spaces for immersion into local history and culture. Information behavior is streamlined rather than complex; users often rely on guidance from librarians or companions ("insiders") to interpret information. The study identifies a dichotomy where protagonists act as the "body" (active, physical) and librarians/users as the "mind" (intellectual, stationary). Libraries are generally portrayed positively, helping users overcome anxiety and connect with communities, contrasting with some negative stereotypes in other media.
Evidence: "Video games depict game characters visiting libraries to solve short-term problems, to gain knowledge to improve themselves or to bond with others... Protagonists are often depicted as adventurers or outsiders... Overall, these depictions highlight both short- and long-term benefits of library use."
Confidence: high

## Implications
The findings suggest that libraries should design services that support both immediate problem-solving and long-term immersion in local contexts. The depiction of users overcoming initial anxiety through support implies that real-world libraries could benefit from onboarding services for new or "outsider" users. The collaborative nature of library use in games highlights the importance of social interaction and community connection in library spaces.

# Summary

This article, titled "Depiction of library use in video games: a content analysis," addresses a significant gap in information studies literature by examining how libraries and library use are represented in video games. While previous research has focused on depictions of librarians or libraries in film and television, this study specifically analyzes the behavior and experiences of fictional users within game environments. The authors argue that these fictional representations can reflect real-world user attitudes and needs, providing valuable insights for library planning and service design.

The study employs a qualitative content analysis method, grounded in social constructionism. It integrates frameworks from information studies (such as Chatman’s insider-outsider theory) and game studies (including Carr’s textual analysis and Carroll’s character analysis). The sample consists of 15 single-player video games released between 2010 and 2021, selected through purposive sampling to ensure diversity in genre, origin, and depiction depth. The first author engaged in play sessions, recording observations on visual design, dialogue, and gameplay mechanics related to library interactions.

The findings reveal that protagonists in these games are typically characterized as adventurous outsiders—often young males—who enter unfamiliar worlds. Their use of libraries is driven by two primary motivations: solving short-term problems (e.g., finding clues or artifacts) and seeking self-improvement or relationship building. Libraries are depicted as safe, neutral spaces that facilitate immersion into the game’s historical and cultural context. The information behavior observed is generally streamlined; protagonists rarely engage in complex, iterative searching but instead rely on guidance from librarians or companions to interpret information. This dynamic reinforces a dichotomy where the protagonist acts as the active "body" navigating physical challenges, while librarians and other users serve as the intellectual "mind," providing context and knowledge.

The study highlights that despite initial feelings of alienation or anxiety—paralleling real-world library anxiety—protagonists consistently overcome barriers to access information and connect with communities. The authors note that libraries in these games are rarely central to climactic conflicts but serve as crucial intermissions for rest, learning, and social bonding. The implications for real-world library services include the need for supportive onboarding processes for new users and the design of spaces that encourage both individual study and collaborative interaction. The research concludes that video games offer a positive lens on library use, emphasizing the institution's role in fostering community connection and personal growth.
