### Summary
# **Downie, S., & Proulx, S. (2022). Investigating the role of gamification in public libraries' literacy-centered youth programming. *International Journal of Play, 11*(4), 382-404. https://doi.org/10.1080/21594937.2022.2136637**

Downie and Proulx investigate how gamification appears in public libraries' literacy-centered programming for children. The article begins from the observation that public libraries have long supported early literacy through storytime, summer reading, outreach, and children's spaces, but that the professional literature has paid relatively little attention to how gameful design may already be shaping these services. Their central question is not whether libraries are explicitly naming their work "gamification." Instead, they ask whether the public presentation of youth literacy initiatives shows design patterns associated with gamification and with self-determination theory.

The theoretical frame is useful for GBLS because it keeps gamification distinct from simply playing games. Downie and Proulx describe gamification as the application of game elements to non-game contexts in order to increase motivation, engagement, experimentation, exploration, or social participation. They emphasize that gamification works through mechanics, dynamics, emotional responses, feedback, goals, recognition, rewards, and users' sense of meaningful progress. They pair this with self-determination theory, especially autonomy, competence, and social relatedness. That pairing lets the article discuss points, badges, rewards, physical spaces, and playful storytimes without reducing motivation to external prizes.

The method is a public-facing online survey of 16 prominent U.S. public libraries. Because there is no official list of the "best" children's libraries, the authors combined Library Journal Star Library rankings with popular online lists and blog-style recommendations of notable children's library spaces. They then examined websites, social media posts, and other online materials during the COVID-19 closure period to identify literacy-related in-person and virtual offerings. The study therefore analyzes how libraries presented their youth literacy services to children, guardians, and the public; it does not observe programs directly, interview librarians, or measure children's literacy outcomes.

The survey found several common strategies. Every library offered some form of storytime and summer reading challenge, and 81 percent offered virtual storytimes. Half used Beanstack for summer reading, 81 percent designated prizes based on completed reading minutes, and half offered activities that went beyond basic logs to include community exploration or STEAM activities. Most libraries provided children's website sections, and many had substantial children's physical spaces with specialized rooms, materials, technology, or programs. Book kits, workshops, outreach, tutor-oriented programs, reading buddies, and recommended third-party digital resources also appeared.

The discussion is organized around summer reading programs, storytime, and physical spaces. In summer reading, Beanstack is treated as a clear example of gameful infrastructure: it provides progress displays, checklists, activity categories, badges, points, immediate audio/visual feedback, and final rewards. Downie and Proulx read these features ambivalently. Badges and rewards can support goal-setting, feedback, self-efficacy, and habits of reflection when they are meaningful and aligned with users' values, but they can also undermine motivation if perceived as controlling. They note that the Beanstack examples were relatively strong on autonomy and competence, but comparatively weak on social relatedness because they lacked peer comparison, sharing, or discussion features.

Storytime is analyzed as gamified play rather than as passive listening. Modern library storytimes, in their account, are dialogic, embodied, playful, scaffolded, and socially shared. Librarians pause for questions, encourage children to imagine or extend stories, integrate rhymes, music, dance, puppets, pantomime, finger art, yoga, therapy dogs, sensory-aware formats, and multilingual or age-specific sessions. The authors connect these features to autonomy, competence, social belonging, early literacy, identity experimentation, and children's ability to communicate meaning through movement and creative performance before they can fully articulate ideas in speech or writing.

The analysis of physical spaces extends gamification beyond screens and badges. Children's library spaces such as learning gardens, technology rooms, oversized books, rainforest domes, playful book returns, mascots, and hands-on installations are read through autonomy, play, affinity, and space. Downie and Proulx argue that well-designed children's spaces invite exploration, self-directed interaction, and imaginative meaning-making while still providing enough guidance to focus attention. Laramie County's "learning spots," for example, use mascot-marked plaques to explain activities, name their benefits, pose reflection questions, and recommend related books. The point is not that the library becomes a videogame, but that spatial design can create goals, feedback, exploration, and meaningful choice.

For the GBLS project, this article is a strong bridge between conceptual gamification and everyday public-library youth service. It shows that game-based library service does not always announce itself as a game night, videogame tournament, escape room, or TTRPG club. It can also appear in summer reading platforms, storytime facilitation, children's room design, book kits, outreach, and literacy challenges. The article also offers a useful caution for assessment: because it studies public websites and social media, it provides evidence of design patterns and professional framing rather than evidence of literacy gains or patron experience. That limitation makes it valuable for the review's distinction between service design, professional discourse, and demonstrated outcomes.
