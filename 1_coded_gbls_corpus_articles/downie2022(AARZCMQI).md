## Findings
The analysis revealed that gamification elements are prevalent in three main areas: summer reading clubs, storytimes, and physical library spaces. 
1. **Summer Reading Clubs:** 100% of surveyed libraries offered some form of competition or incentive. 50% used the Beanstack app, which utilizes badges, points, and checklists to provide immediate feedback and track progress, aligning with competence and autonomy needs. However, these apps lacked social relatedness features (e.g., leaderboards).
2. **Storytimes:** 100% offered in-person storytime; 81% offered virtual options. Modern storytimes shifted from passive listening to active, dialogic engagement involving movement, music, and tactile play, supporting autonomy and competence.
3. **Physical Spaces:** 63% of libraries provided customized children's spaces beyond basic bookshelves. Designs emphasized "learning through play," offering immersive environments (e.g., themed zones, interactive installations) that encourage autonomous exploration and affinity-based learning.

# Summary

This article investigates the integration of gamification into youth literacy programming within U.S. public libraries. Recognizing that traditional reading engagement is declining among elementary students, the authors explore whether modern library initiatives leverage game-based design principles to foster intrinsic motivation, as defined by Self-Determination Theory (SDT). SDT posits that intrinsic motivation arises when individuals feel autonomy, competence, and social relatedness. The study aims to provide librarians and educators with a framework for understanding how gamification can support these psychological needs in educational contexts.

The methodology involves a qualitative content analysis of public-facing digital materials from 16 highly ranked U.S. public libraries. The sample was selected based on Library Journal star ratings (4–5 stars) and features in popular parenting and travel blogs, ensuring the inclusion of institutions recognized for innovative children's services. Due to the timing of the research during the COVID-19 pandemic, the analysis relied exclusively on websites, social media posts, and online promotional content rather than direct observation or participant surveys. The authors categorized library offerings into three primary domains: summer reading clubs, storytimes, and physical space design.

The findings indicate that gamification elements are implicitly present across these domains, even if not explicitly labeled as such by the libraries. In summer reading programs, 100% of surveyed libraries offered incentives or competitions. Half utilized the Beanstack app, a platform that employs badges, points, and interactive checklists. The authors note that these features support competence by providing clear goals and immediate feedback, and autonomy by allowing users to track their own progress. However, the analysis identified a lack of social relatedness features in these digital tools, such as leaderboards or peer interaction spaces, which are critical for fostering community belonging.

Regarding storytimes, the study found a shift from passive listening to active, dialogic engagement. Modern sessions incorporate movement, music, and tactile play, allowing children to interpret narratives through physical expression. This approach supports autonomy by giving children agency in how they engage with the story and competence by scaffolding their participation. The authors argue that these interactive elements mirror game dynamics where players experiment and receive feedback, thereby enhancing learning outcomes.

Physical library spaces were also analyzed for gamification traits. The majority of libraries featured customized children's areas designed to encourage exploration and play. These spaces often include themed zones, interactive installations, and flexible seating, which support "affinity-based" learning—where interest drives engagement. By removing rigid structures and allowing for self-directed exploration, these environments foster autonomy. However, the authors caution that such freedom must be balanced with clear goals or guidance to maintain a sense of competence and prevent aimlessness.

The implications of this study suggest that gamification is a viable strategy for enhancing youth literacy motivation in libraries. By aligning program design with SDT principles, librarians can create experiences that are not only educational but also intrinsically rewarding. The authors recommend further research into how libraries can address the social relatedness gap in digital platforms and how professionals internally conceptualize gamification. Ultimately, the article serves as a foundational resource for integrating game-based learning strategies into library services to better support early literacy development.
