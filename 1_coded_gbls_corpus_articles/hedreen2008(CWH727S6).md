The article aims to explore the concept of "virtual librarianship" and the definition of a library within the virtual world of Second Life. It seeks to evaluate which traditional library services translate effectively to this environment, assess the information needs of virtual residents, and examine how libraries can remain relevant as business and education activities migrate to virtual spaces. The authors intend to document the early history, services, collections, and community impact of the Second Life Library 2.0 (SLL2) project.

## Findings
The authors report that traditional library services can be adapted to virtual worlds but require significant modification. Reference services were provided via instant messaging and virtual "buzzers," though a trial of OCLC's QuestionPoint chat service saw very little use, while email inquiries were more common. Collections are primarily digital links embedded in virtual objects (books, posters, models) rather than physical texts. Specialized branches demonstrated success: the Medical Library focused on visual/audio formats and health literacy; the Caledon Branch served as a special collection for Steampunk and 19th-century culture, fostering community identity; and the Teen Grid initiative aimed to engage youth where they already socialize. The authors found that virtual libraries serve not only as information repositories but also as "third places" for socialization and community building. Challenges included technical lag, language barriers, and assessing health literacy levels remotely.
Evidence: "Very little use of the chat service was recorded, but some people did use the e-mail service" (p. 172). "The impact of this has been greater than anticipated—it has been a great surprise to see the way that the presence of the library is helping Caledon create itself" (p. 186).
Confidence: high

## Implications
The article implies that libraries must expand their definition beyond physical access to information to maintain relevance in a digital age. Virtual worlds offer opportunities for innovative instruction, interactive collections, and global collaboration unbound by geography. The authors suggest that virtual libraries can support interdisciplinary studies and niche communities that traditional institutions may overlook. They recommend continued exploration of virtual reference tools, the development of teen-specific services, and the integration of virtual presence with real-world library programming to bridge the gap between physical and digital patron needs.
Evidence: "The Second Life Library offers librarians an opportunity to examine existing practices while exploring potential new paradigms beyond the restrictions and limitations that inform libraries in the real world" (p. 182). "We believe that by immersing ourselves in their world and being where they are, we maintain our relevance to their needs and development as creative and interactive learners" (p. 188).

# Summary

This article presents an exploratory case study of the Second Life Library 2.0 (SLL2), a virtual library initiative launched in April 2006 within the online world Second Life. Authored by a collaborative team of librarians from various institutions, the text documents the project's first year, aiming to define the role of libraries in virtual environments and assess the translation of traditional services to digital spaces. The authors frame Second Life as a "third place" for socialization and learning, arguing that libraries must establish a presence there to remain relevant to users who increasingly conduct business and education online.

The methodology is descriptive and participatory, relying on the authors' direct experiences as avatars within SLL2. The project began with the acquisition of "Info Island," which houses the main library, reference desks, and various specialized collections. The article details the adaptation of core library functions. Reference services were delivered through instant messaging and virtual objects like "buzzers" that alerted librarians to user queries. A trial of OCLC’s QuestionPoint chat service yielded minimal usage, whereas email-based reference proved more viable. Instructional services included information literacy sessions and tutorials on navigating Second Life itself.

Collections in SLL2 are predominantly digital, utilizing virtual objects such as books, posters, and 3D models that link to external web resources or internal text files ("note cards"). The authors highlight several specialized branches that demonstrate the flexibility of virtual librarianship. The Medical Library focuses on health information literacy, offering visual and audio formats and collaborating with medical education groups. It addresses challenges such as language barriers and the difficulty of assessing health literacy remotely, often requiring librarians to provide guidance rather than direct access to restricted databases. The Caledon Branch serves a themed community interested in Steampunk and 19th-century culture, functioning as a special collection that supports interdisciplinary interests and fosters community identity. The Teen Grid initiative targets adolescents aged 13–17 in a secure environment, aiming to engage this demographic through peer mentoring, creative workshops, and teen-driven programming.

Key findings indicate that while virtual libraries can replicate traditional services, they also enable new forms of interaction and collection development. The authors note that the presence of a library in Caledon helped shape the community's identity, suggesting that libraries serve as cultural anchors in virtual spaces. However, technical limitations such as server lag and connectivity issues were noted as significant constraints. The article concludes that virtual librarianship offers a paradigm for exploring new service models, emphasizing collaboration, innovation, and the integration of virtual and physical library services to meet evolving user needs.
