### Summary
# **Hedreen, R. C., Johnson, J. L., Lundy, M. A., Burnette, P., Perryman, C., Van Den Brekel, G., Jacobson, J. J., Gullett, M., & Czarnecki, K. (2008). Exploring virtual librarianship: Second Life Library 2.0. *Internet Reference Services Quarterly, 13*(2-3), 167-195. https://doi.org/10.1080/10875300802103833**

Hedreen and colleagues provide a detailed practitioner account of Second Life Library 2.0, one of the clearest early examples of librarians treating a game-adjacent virtual world as a service environment rather than as a novelty website. The article describes a project begun in April 2006 by the Alliance Library System and Online Programming for All Libraries, with Info Island and later Teen Second Life spaces serving as experimental library sites. Second Life is framed as a non-game descendant of massively multiplayer online games: it has no winning condition or quest structure, but it shares avatar presence, spatial navigation, user-created objects, social economies, and immersive worldbuilding with game worlds. The central question is therefore not whether Second Life is a library in the conventional sense, but what library services become when patrons, staff, collections, and programs meet inside a virtual third place.

The article is especially useful because it catalogues concrete services. SLL2 offered ready reference through in-world reference desks, instant-message "buzzers," librarian avatar labels, and an OCLC QuestionPoint trial. It offered instruction in basic Second Life skills, information literacy, and in-world content creation. It built collections through linked "books," note cards, audio books, government information objects, specialty areas such as anime and manga, and educational models such as a rotating Euglena. These examples show that virtual collections were not simply digital shelves. Any in-world object could become an information object: a poster could link to a bibliography, a portrait to a biography, a model to an assignment, or a plant in a medicinal herb garden to quality health information.

Several branch examples extend the service model. Mystery Manor used a gothic genre space for reader's advisory, horror and mystery fiction, interactive guides, book discussions, readings, and literature-based role-play. The Medical Library and Consumer Health Information Library experimented with health reference, PubMed instruction, current-awareness searches, support-group outreach, RSS feeds, self-guided tours, and confidential patron interaction across language and health-literacy differences. The Caledon Branch Library served a steampunk and nineteenth-century role-play community through special collections, local authors, archives, and reference tied to the community's own emerging identity. The Teen Grid work moved youth services into a secure teen-only environment with adult background checks, a teen advisory board, peer mentoring, homework help, machinima, building and scripting classes, book release parties, and partnerships with youth-serving organizations.

For the GBLS project, Hedreen et al. are important because they make visible the operational complexity behind library service in game-like worlds. The project required international volunteers, partner organizations, island spaces, branch identities, training, events, communication channels, background checks for teen work, and constant adaptation as the platform changed. It also raised durable questions for GBLS: which library services translate into a virtual world, which services need to be redesigned around the affordances of avatars and objects, how trust and confidentiality operate when staff and patrons meet through avatars, and how libraries remain relevant when social, educational, and cultural activity moves into networked play spaces.

The article is not an outcome study and does not measure learning, attendance effects, or long-term sustainability. It is also historically situated in the first-wave enthusiasm around Second Life. Even so, its value is substantial. It shows that early virtual-world librarianship linked reference, collections, reader's advisory, health information, youth services, professional collaboration, literary culture, and community identity in one experimental service ecology. For a GBLS review, it helps connect later VR/metaverse work to an earlier library tradition of building services inside persistent, social, game-adjacent worlds.
