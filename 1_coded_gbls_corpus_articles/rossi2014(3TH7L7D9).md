# Summary

This article presents the development and preliminary evaluation of a Controlled Vocabulary (CV) for mood metadata within the domain of video games. Recognizing video games as significant cultural artifacts in digital library collections, the authors argue that existing metadata schemas lack robust mechanisms for representing the emotional tone or "mood" of games, despite its importance to gamers. The primary objective was to create a standardized set of mood terms to facilitate search and collocation functions in library systems.

The methodology began with a review of scholarly literature on mood models from psychology and music information retrieval, specifically citing works by Russell, Thayer, and Watson and Tellegen. These theoretical frameworks provided the structural basis for organizing mood dimensions. The authors then harvested existing mood taxonomies from sources such as AllMusic.com, the Movie Genome Project, gamerDNA.com, and Amazon customer reviews. Through an iterative refinement process, redundant and genre-specific terms were removed, resulting in a CV containing 17 defined mood terms, each with equivalent terms and illustrative game examples. These terms include "Adventurous," "Aggressive," "Cute," "Dark," "Horror," "Humorous," "Imaginative," "Intense," "Light-hearted," "Mysterious," "Peaceful," "Quirky," "Romantic," "Sad," "Sarcastic," "Sensual," and "Solitary."

To evaluate the applicability of this CV, the authors annotated a sample collection of 617 video games. The sample was deliberately selected to ensure diversity in genres and release years, sourced from allgame.com. The empirical findings indicated distinct patterns in term usage. The most frequently assigned mood terms were "adventurous," followed by "aggressive" and "intense." Conversely, the least utilized terms were "sarcastic," "sad," and "sensual." The analysis also revealed potential correlations between specific video game genres and certain mood descriptors, as well as chronological trends in mood representation; for instance, the term "aggressive" showed increased frequency in games released after 1992.

A critical limitation identified during the evaluation was the inherent subjectivity of mood annotation. The authors note that mood perception is influenced by individual user states and fear responses, leading to inconsistent classifications (e.g., one user might classify a game as "horror" while another sees it merely as "dark"). To address this variability, the authors propose allowing multiple mood tags per game and suggest future crowdsourcing efforts to rank terms based on broader user input.

The implications of this work extend to both library science and user experience design. By establishing a standardized mood vocabulary, libraries can offer enhanced search and browse capabilities that align with how gamers naturally conceptualize media. The authors conclude by outlining plans for future research, specifically user studies designed to test the CV’s usability in real-world information-seeking scenarios. This study contributes to the nascent field of video game metadata by providing a structured, empirically tested framework for capturing the emotional dimensions of interactive media.
