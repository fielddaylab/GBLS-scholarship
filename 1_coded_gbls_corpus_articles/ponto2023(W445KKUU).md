## Purpose
The article aims to empirically test the effects of a virtual reality (VR) experience, titled "Waddle," on informal learning and empathy. Specifically, it investigates whether embodying an Adélie penguin in VR can facilitate high-level learning, create a sense of virtual embodiment, and foster positive changes in empathy toward the animal, which is posited as a component for social engagement with ecology.

## Method
The study employed a pre-test/post-test design using a 20-minute VR application called "Waddle." The experience utilized hand-tracking technology on Oculus Quest 2 headsets to achieve visuomotor congruence, allowing users to see and control an Adélie penguin avatar. Data collection involved three primary measures: (1) Empathy, measured via the Dispositional Empathy with Nature scale (9 Likert-scale items); (2) Embodiment, measured via a variation of the Avatar Embodiment Questionnaire (23 Likert-scale items); and (3) Informal learning, assessed through two open-ended questions regarding penguin life events and challenges. Statistical analysis included paired t-tests for pre/post comparisons and Pearson’s product-moment correlation for relationships between variables.
Evidence: "Empathy was measured using the Dispositional Empathy with Nature scale... Embodiment was measured using a variation of the Avatar Embodiment Questionnaire... Informal learning was gathered using two opened ended question... ## Findings
The study reported three main empirical results. First, there was a significant increase in empathy scores from pre-experience (M = 0.85, SD = 1.20) to post-experience (M = 1.65, SD = 1.36), with t(40) = -2.95, p = 0.005. Second, the mean overall embodiment score was positive (M = 0.986, SD = 0.966), indicating successful creation of embodiment. Third, a significant positive correlation was found between embodiment scores and post-experience empathy ratings, r(39) = 0.53, p < .001. Additionally, qualitative data indicated gains in learning regarding penguin life events and challenges.

# Summary

This article presents an empirical study evaluating "Waddle," a virtual reality (VR) application designed to promote informal learning and empathy through the virtual embodiment of an Adélie penguin. The research addresses gaps in previous VR animal embodiment studies, which often failed due to a lack of visuomotor alignment between the user and the avatar. By leveraging hand-tracking technology on Oculus Quest 2 headsets, the developers created an experience with intentional gesture congruence, allowing users to see themselves as penguins—complete with beaks and flippers—and perform species-specific actions such as nest building, mating dances, and defending eggs from predators.

The primary objective was to determine if this embodied VR experience could facilitate informal learning, induce a sense of virtual embodiment, and increase empathy toward the animal. The study hypothesized that users would accept their penguin identity, that embodiment would enhance learning outcomes in informal settings, and that embodiment would correlate with increased empathy.

The methodology involved a pre-test/post-test design with a sample of 43 students aged 8 to 13 (grades 3–8). Participants were recruited from informal learning organizations attending a science festival at a university campus. The VR experience lasted approximately 20 minutes. Data collection utilized three instruments: the Dispositional Empathy with Nature scale for empathy, a modified Avatar Embodiment Questionnaire for embodiment, and two open-ended questions to assess informal learning regarding penguin life cycles and challenges. Statistical analyses included paired t-tests for pre/post comparisons and Pearson correlations to examine relationships between variables.

The findings supported all primary hypotheses. First, the study demonstrated significant gains in informal learning, with participants showing improved ability to identify major life events and challenges faced by Adélie penguins after the experience. Second, embodiment scores were positive (Mean = 0.986), indicating that participants successfully experienced a sense of ownership over the virtual penguin body. Third, and most notably, there was a statistically significant increase in empathy scores from pre-experience (M = 0.85) to post-experience (M = 1.65), with p = 0.005. Furthermore, a strong positive correlation was observed between embodiment scores and post-experience empathy ratings (r = 0.53, p < .001), suggesting that the degree to which users felt embodied as penguins directly influenced their empathetic response.

The authors conclude that direct animal embodiment in VR is not only possible but effective for educational purposes. The significant correlation between embodiment and empathy underscores the potential of VR as a tool for fostering social engagement with ecology and environmental appreciation. The study highlights the importance of visuomotor synchrony in achieving these outcomes, distinguishing it from prior failed attempts at animal embodiment. While the results are promising, the authors note that future research is needed to isolate specific design elements that contribute most to embodiment and empathy, and to further explore how these constructs interact to support informal learning. This work contributes to the broader field of games-based library services by demonstrating how immersive, embodied simulations can serve as effective vehicles for STEM education and affective learning outcomes in informal settings.
