## Purpose
The article aims to document the challenges encountered during a domain analysis and cataloging exercise intended to develop a formal metadata schema for video games. It seeks to demonstrate that traditional organization systems are inadequate for video games and to highlight specific inconsistencies in genre, release dates, features, region/language, and developer/publisher information that necessitate a standardized, user-centered description model.
Evidence: "Through domain analysis and cataloging real-world examples while attempting to develop a formal metadata schema for video games, we encountered challenges in description... confirm the importance of developing a standardized description model for video games." (Abstract)

## Method
The study employed a collaborative domain analysis approach involving document-based and user-based activities. The class then spent several weeks cataloging real-world video game examples to test the usability of this schema and identify descriptive challenges.

## Findings
The authors identified six major categories of challenges in describing video games using the proposed schema:
1. **Genre Labels:** Existing genre labels across commercial websites are inconsistent, vague, and undefined. Controlled vocabularies were difficult to establish due to ambiguity and the complexity of ordering multiple genre tags.
2. **Release Dates:** There is a lack of reliable sources for retail release dates. Copyright dates are often inaccurate for series entries. Commercial websites provide conflicting information (e.g., *Shenmue* release dates varied by day across Wikipedia, GameSpot, and Allgame). Company websites were most reliable but often incomplete.
3. **Features:** Feature descriptions were inconsistent and often marketing-oriented rather than objective (e.g., "mind-blowing spells"). This element was eventually excluded from the core schema due to difficulty in obtaining consistent information.
4. **Region and Language:** Boundaries are unclear, particularly for games released in multiple regions without localization or with mixed language options (e.g., Japanese voice tracks in North American releases).
5. **Developer vs. Publisher:** Distinguishing between developers and publishers is difficult due to overlapping roles, varying company names, and dissolved entities for older games.
6. **Other Issues:** Challenges included mismatching titles across regions, multiple titles for the same game, and difficulty determining series information without prior familiarity.
Evidence: Sections 4.1 through 4.6 detail these specific findings regarding genre, release dates, features, region/language, developer/publisher distinctions, and other descriptive issues.
Confidence: high

## Implications
The study concludes that there is a critical need for a standardized description model for video games, including defined metadata elements, clear instructions, and controlled vocabularies. The authors plan to extend their core schema with a "recommended" set of elements and develop controlled vocabularies for genres and publishers. They also propose future systematic user studies and quantitative analysis of metadata frequencies. The resulting schema is intended to benefit libraries, archives, museums, and commercial enterprises by improving organization, access, and preservation of video game collections.
Evidence: "These confirm the need for a standardized description for games... We plan to further develop our schema... conduct systematic user studies... Improving organization and access will enhance people’s gaming experiences and also have substantial commercial and cultural consequences." (Section 5)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This article by Lee, Tennis, and Clarke (2012) reports on the challenges encountered during a domain analysis aimed at developing a formal metadata schema for video games. The authors argue that as video games become entrenched in economic, cultural, and academic systems, traditional library organization standards and commercial web-based descriptions are insufficient for providing intelligent access to these artifacts. Existing knowledge organization models, such as FRBR and LCSH, fail to adequately describe non-book materials like video games, often focusing on form rather than content or lacking specific genre terms. Commercial websites, while abundant in data, suffer from unstructured, unverified, and inconsistent information.

To address these gaps, the authors collaborated with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) and students in a "Video Game Metadata" course at the University of Washington Information School. The methodology involved both document-based and user-based domain analysis. The team first developed five user personas—Player, Parent, Collector, Academic, and Game Developer/Designer—to identify essential metadata needs. From this analysis, they distilled 16 core metadata elements: Title, Edition, Platform, Format, Developer, Retail Release Date, Number of Players, Online, Special Hardware, Genre, Series/Franchise, Region, Rating, Language, and UPC. The students then spent several weeks cataloging real-world video game examples to test the usability of this schema and identify practical descriptive challenges.

The empirical findings revealed significant difficulties in applying these core elements. First, genre labels were found to be inconsistent and vague across sources; for instance, *Super Mario Bros.* and *Grand Theft Auto* were both classified as "action" on Mobygames despite their differences. Establishing a controlled vocabulary proved difficult due to label ambiguity and the complexity of ordering multiple genres. Second, obtaining reliable retail release dates was problematic. Copyright dates were often inaccurate for series entries, and commercial websites provided conflicting information (e.g., *Shenmue*’s release date varied by days across different sites). Company websites were more reliable but often incomplete due to corporate mergers or dissolution.

Third, the "features" element was excluded from the core schema because descriptions were inconsistent and heavily marketing-oriented rather than objective. Fourth, defining region and language boundaries was complex, particularly for games released in multiple regions without full localization or with mixed language options. Fifth, distinguishing between developers and publishers was difficult due to overlapping corporate roles, varying naming conventions, and lack of historical records for older titles. Additional issues included mismatching titles across regions and difficulty determining series information without prior familiarity.

The authors conclude that these challenges confirm the necessity for a standardized description model for video games, including clear metadata definitions and controlled vocabularies. Future work includes extending the core schema with recommended elements, developing controlled vocabularies for genres and publishers, and conducting systematic user studies to validate element utility. The proposed schema aims to improve access and preservation for libraries, archives, museums, and commercial entities, thereby enhancing both cultural preservation and user experience in gaming contexts.
