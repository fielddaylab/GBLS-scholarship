### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Tennis, J. T., & Clarke, R. I. (2012). Domain analysis for a video game metadata schema: Issues and challenges. In P. Zaphiris et al. (Eds.), *Theory and practice of digital libraries* (pp. 280-285). Springer.**

Lee, Tennis, and Clarke report an early domain-analysis stage in the University of Washington and Seattle Interactive Media Museum effort to build a video game metadata schema. The short conference paper begins with the claim that video games had become economically, culturally, and academically important, but that intelligent access to them lagged behind because traditional knowledge organization systems and commercial game websites did not describe games in standardized, user-centered ways. The paper is best understood as a precursor to the fuller 2013 article by Lee, Cho, Fox, and Perti already summarized in this review.

The project used document- and user-based domain analysis through a University of Washington special topics course on video game metadata. Students, the authors, and SIMM collaborators developed five personas: Player, Parent, Collector, Academic, and Game Developer/Designer. From those personas, the class identified a CORE set of 16 elements judged useful across groups, including Title, Edition, Platform, Format, Developer, Retail Release Date, Number of Players, Online, Special Hardware, Genre, Series/Franchise, Region, Rating, Language, and UPC.

The paper's main contribution is its cataloging-problem inventory. Genre labels were inconsistent, vague, and undefined across commercial sources, making browsing and comparison difficult. Release dates were hard to verify because copyright dates, publisher sites, Wikipedia, GameSpot, Allgame, and other sources could conflict. Feature descriptions were often marketing-oriented rather than objective, and the "features" element risked becoming an uncontrolled notes field. Region and language were difficult to separate because games can be region-locked, multilingual, imported, localized, or released across markets. Developer and publisher data were also unstable because game boxes often show multiple logos, companies merge or dissolve, and organization names vary.

Other issues included title variation, regional title changes, numbering differences across markets, edition/version labels, and series information that may require game-specific expertise. These challenges made clear why game description needed metadata element definitions, cataloging instructions, and controlled vocabularies rather than merely borrowing book-centered standards or commercial website fields. The article also explicitly argues that improved video game organization would matter not only to libraries, archives, and museums but also to game developers, manufacturers, distributors, scholars, educators, parents, and players.

For the larger GBLS project, this source is useful as historical context for the GAMER Group's metadata work, but it should not receive a separate review paragraph because the fuller 2013 publication already captures the mature version of the same project, including domain analysis, personas, interviews, CORE16, and a 46-element recommended schema. This precursor confirms the practical cataloging pain points behind that later work and can be retained in the summaries file for provenance, but the review should avoid duplicating evidence.
