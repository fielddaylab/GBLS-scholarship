### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Cho, H., Fox, V., & Perti, A. (2013). User-centered approach in creating a metadata schema for video games and interactive media. In *Proceedings of the 13th ACM/IEEE-CS Joint Conference on Digital Libraries* (pp. 229-238). ACM. https://doi.org/10.1145/2467696.2467702**

Lee, Cho, Fox, and Perti present a user-centered metadata schema for video games and interactive media, developed through a collaboration between the University of Washington Information School and the Seattle Interactive Media Museum. The article begins from a problem that is central to game-based library services: games are increasingly part of cultural, archival, museum, and digital-library collections, but existing organizational systems often describe them with vague, inconsistent, or book-centered metadata. Traditional library standards such as FRBR, RDA, and LCSH tend to capture basic bibliographic information such as title, platform, publisher, and format, but they do not fully represent how games are played, experienced, discovered, or compared.

The project's goal was to create a standardized metadata schema and encoding scheme that could describe video games and interactive media across organizations and domains. The authors emphasize that a game schema must serve many stakeholders, including players, parents, collectors, academics, developers, curators, librarians, and preservation institutions. This matters for GBLS because a library game collection is not only a shelf or circulation category. It depends on discovery, advisory, preservation, access, and meaning-making systems that match how patrons actually seek and evaluate games.

The study combines domain analysis, in-depth interviews, user-centered design, personas, facet analysis, and cataloging tests. In Phase I, the team collected 61 metadata elements from game websites, WorldCat, Amazon, and other organizational systems, then evaluated them through personas such as a player, parent, collector, academic, game designer, and curator/librarian. That work produced CORE16, a set of 16 core elements. In Phase II, the team expanded the master list to 135 candidate metadata elements by examining more game-related websites and analyzing genre terms. The authors then interviewed 24 gamers, ranging from casual to hardcore, to understand game-related information needs and seeking behaviors. Based on those interviews and their cost-benefit, objectivity, stability, source, and ease-of-acquisition criteria, they proposed a 46-element recommended set.

The interview findings show why ordinary bibliographic description is not enough. Users wanted subject access points that distinguished gameplay genre from theme, setting, plot/narrative, mood, style, and affect. They recognized that genre alone can be overloaded or misleading because the same genre label may contain very different play experiences. Visual information was also highly valued: box art, screenshots, trailers, gameplay videos, characters, visual style, and artistic direction helped users decide whether a game matched their tastes. Users cared about companies and franchises, especially developers and publishers, because production teams and franchises often function as recognizable markers of quality, style, and expected experience.

The article also shows that game information needs change by stage. Some users seek promotional descriptions, images, and videos after announcement; release dates and region information before purchase; screenshots and gameplay videos at the time of purchase; walkthroughs or wikis during play; and similar games after completing a satisfying game. The authors note that this creates a spoiler problem: metadata elements such as plot/narrative, theme, or character information may be valuable for discovery but can also reduce playability if too much is revealed. This is a useful GBLS point because library discovery systems need to support both finding and experiencing games without flattening them into overdescribed records.

The recommended metadata set includes traditional descriptive elements as well as game-specific ones: title, alternative title, edition, format, series, franchise/universe, platform, developer, publisher, distributor, special hardware, online capabilities, system requirements, credits, official website, price, retail release date, controls, packaging, number of players, rating, purpose, customization options, difficulty levels, achievements/awards/trophies, region, language, identifier, box art/cover, screenshots, trailers, gameplay videos, genre/gameplay, style, plot/narrative, theme, setting, mood/affect, temporal aspect, presentation, point of view, character names, character types, links to historical events, type of ending, and visual style. The breadth of this list makes visible how much game discovery depends on audiovisual, experiential, technical, social, and narrative attributes.

For the larger GBLS project, Lee et al. provide an important infrastructure source. Their schema supports circulation, collection development, advisory, preservation, curriculum support, and cultural heritage work by showing what must be described for games to be findable and meaningful. It also foregrounds the practical labor involved: catalogers need sources of information beyond the game box, controlled vocabularies for elements such as visual style and mood, decisions about manifestation-level versus item-level description, strategies for user-generated data versus authoritative accuracy, and enough domain knowledge to describe rapidly changing genres, platforms, online capabilities, and play forms.

The article's limitations are also useful. The authors do not claim the schema is complete, and they present it as a starting point for community iteration. The interview sample was small and heavily male, and the schema required further systematic evaluation through application to sample game collections. Still, it is one of the strongest GBLS sources for arguing that game services require metadata designed around actual user behavior. Libraries that collect or promote games need discovery systems that reflect how patrons search for games, compare them, avoid unwanted content, locate compatible hardware, find similar experiences, and understand games as cultural artifacts rather than generic software objects.
