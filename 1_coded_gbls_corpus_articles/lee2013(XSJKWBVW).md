## Method
The study employed a user-centered design approach combining domain analysis, in-depth interviews, and persona-based evaluation. The methodology was conducted in two phases as part of a graduate-level course. Phase I involved collecting 61 existing metadata elements from various websites and catalog records, evaluating them against six developed personas (Player, Parent, Collector, Academic, Developer, Curator/Librarian) to establish a core set of 16 elements (CORE16). Phase II expanded this list by analyzing additional websites and conducting facet analysis on genre terms, resulting in 135 potential elements. These were then evaluated against data from 24 in-depth interviews with gamers. The final selection criteria included source of information, ease of acquisition, cost-benefit, objectivity, and stability.

## Findings
The study resulted in a recommended metadata schema comprising 46 elements, including the original CORE16. Key findings from user interviews highlighted several specific information needs:
1. **Subject Access Points:** Users found standard genre labels insufficient and misleading. They distinguished between "structural" (gameplay mechanics) and "thematic" (plot/emotional content) categories. Elements such as plot/narrative, theme, setting, and mood/affect were deemed essential for discovery.
2. **Visual Information:** Visual style, box art, screenshots, and gameplay videos were highly valued for determining user preference and immersion, often outweighing textual descriptions.
3. **Company and Franchise Data:** Users placed high importance on developer, publisher, and distributor information, using these as proxies for quality and brand loyalty.
4. **Exclusion of Subjective Data:** Despite some users valuing ratings and reviews, the authors excluded user-generated content (ratings/reviews) from the schema due to subjectivity and lack of authority, preferring objective metadata.
5. **Challenges:** Significant challenges were identified in defining controlled vocabularies for complex elements like "plot/narrative" and "character names," as well as determining reliable sources for metadata given the shift toward digital-only distribution and hardware obsolescence.
Evidence: "As a result, we ended up with a total of 46 metadata elements (including the CORE16) as a recommended set." (Section 3.1); "In our metadata schema, we decided not to include elements that solely rely on user-generated content such as user ratings or reviews." (Section 4.1.2)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The authors argue that this schema serves as a starting point for collaborative development within the game research and library communities. It highlights the need for organizational systems to accommodate multiple stages of information seeking (pre-purchase, during play, post-play) and to balance objective description with user-centric discovery features. The study implies that future work must address the tension between efficiency (user-generated content) and accuracy (authoritative metadata), potentially through verification processes. It also suggests that traditional library standards are insufficient for non-book artifacts like games, necessitating new descriptive frameworks that account for interactive and visual dimensions.
Evidence: "We hope that this will serve as starting point for a discussion in the game research community on how to build a useful organizational system for these artifacts..." (Section 3.2)

# Summary

This article presents the development of a user-centered metadata schema designed specifically for video games and interactive media, addressing the limitations of existing bibliographic standards such as FRBR and LCSH. The authors argue that current systems often rely on vague terms or text-based models that fail to capture the complex, interactive nature of games. To address this, the research team from the University of Washington and the Seattle Interactive Media Museum (SIMM) conducted a two-phase study combining domain analysis, persona-based design, and user interviews.

The methodology began with Phase I, where 61 existing metadata elements were collected from various commercial and library sources. These were evaluated against six personas representing different user groups (e.g., players, parents, curators) to establish a core set of 16 elements (CORE16). Phase II expanded this list to 135 potential elements through further domain analysis and facet analysis of genre terms. Crucially, the team conducted in-depth interviews with 24 gamers (predominantly male, aged 20s–40s) to understand their information needs and seeking behaviors. The final schema was refined by evaluating these elements against criteria such as objectivity, stability, and ease of acquisition, resulting in a recommended set of 46 metadata elements.

Key findings from the user interviews revealed that standard genre labels were often insufficient for discovery. Users distinguished between structural gameplay mechanics and thematic content, requesting detailed access points for plot, narrative, theme, setting, and mood. Visual information, including artistic style, screenshots, and gameplay videos, was highly valued for assessing immersion and aesthetic appeal. Additionally, users placed significant importance on developer, publisher, and franchise information as indicators of quality and brand loyalty. Conversely, despite some users relying on ratings and reviews, the authors excluded these subjective elements from the schema to maintain authority and accuracy, aligning with SIMM’s preservation goals.

The study highlights several challenges in metadata creation for games. Defining controlled vocabularies for complex concepts like "plot/narrative" and "character names" proved difficult due to varying levels of granularity and narrative integration across different game types. Furthermore, the shift toward digital distribution complicates source identification, as physical packaging is often absent, and hardware obsolescence threatens long-term access to authentic metadata sources. The authors acknowledge that constructing a "complete" schema is not feasible in one iteration; rather, this work serves as a foundational framework for ongoing collaboration between libraries, museums, and the gaming community. Future work includes refining the schema’s structure, developing detailed cataloging instructions, and conducting large-scale user surveys to further validate the model. This research underscores the necessity of specialized metadata standards for digital cultural artifacts to ensure effective organization, discovery, and preservation.
