# Summary

Philip Minchin’s 2018 feature article, "In praise of tabletop games: Part 1," published in *Access*, presents a theoretical argument for the integration of tabletop games into library services. The author challenges the historical pedagogical bias that views play as trivial or antithetical to serious learning. Instead, Minchin posits that play is a fundamental human drive intrinsically linked to learning, resilience, and cognitive health. The article serves as the first part of a two-part series, with this section dedicated to establishing the theoretical framework for why tabletop games are uniquely suited to the library environment, specifically through the cultivation of four distinct "literacies."

Minchin argues that while traditional educational models often rely on didactic instruction or rote learning, modern understanding recognizes the need for baseline learning skills and values that foster lifelong engagement. He contends that limiting games to those with explicit didactic goals (such as trivia for fact mastery) drastically limits their potential. Drawing on Johan Huizinga’s concept of the "magic circle" from *Homo ludens*, Minchin asserts that play creates a space separate from broader reality with its own laws, which is essential for genuine learning. He supports this with references to mammalian biology, noting that play acts as "training for the unexpected," and neuroscientific observations suggesting that play is the brain’s preferred method of learning, offering health benefits such as delayed onset of degenerative diseases like Alzheimer’s.

The core of the article defines four literacies that tabletop games effectively teach:
1.  **Psychological Literacy:** Defined as understanding the mind, including neuroscience and empathy. Minchin argues that games requiring players to assess the intentions and knowledge of others foster this literacy more directly than character-based literature due to immediate feedback loops.
2.  **Social Literacy:** This involves awareness of group dynamics, power structures, and social contracts. Minchin notes that every game operates under an implicit or explicit social contract, making them ideal for studying how emergent forces shape individual choices within a group.
3.  **Systems Literacy:** The ability to understand complex systems (such as economies or ecologies) through rule sets that specify interactions, causes, and effects. Minchin suggests that "playing widely" offers exponential value similar to reading widely.
4.  **Procedural Literacy:** A term Minchin proposes to describe the ability to understand and navigate explicit rules governing human behavior, ranging from simple queues to legal codes. He distinguishes this from Ian Bogost’s use of "procedural rhetoric" in digital contexts, arguing that procedural literacy is crucial for navigating real-world power structures and resolving disputes.

Minchin emphasizes that libraries are ideal venues for these activities because they share an ethos of self-directed learning, which is a prerequisite for genuine play. He highlights the cultural resurgence of tabletop games, particularly among young people saturated with screens, as a strategic opportunity for libraries to engage patrons. The article concludes by asserting that librarians should curate playful arts with the same intelligence applied to literature and other media, recognizing them as serious cultural artifacts that facilitate vital learning outcomes. Part 2 of the series promises to provide practical examples of specific games and their applications.
