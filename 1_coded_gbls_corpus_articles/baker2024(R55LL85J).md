# Summary

This document is a research proposal by Christopher J. Baker for a study titled "Games-based Library Services Study & Needs Assessment," submitted to the School of Information Studies at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee in May 2024. The proposal addresses a identified gap in library science literature regarding the implementation, metrics, and community reception of Games-Based Library Services (GBLS) in U.S. public libraries. Despite the growing popularity of gaming among Gen Z and Millennials—53% of whom identify as gamers according to an American Library Association report cited in the text—comprehensive data on how libraries serve this demographic through gaming media is lacking.

The primary objective of the proposed study is to generate data-driven insights to guide advocacy, development, and enhancement of GBLS. The author posits that understanding community interest and usage patterns will allow libraries to optimize service delivery, either by expanding popular offerings or avoiding resource allocation to undesired services. The study defines GBLS broadly to include video games, board games, tabletop role-playing games (TTRPGs), virtual/augmented reality games, and collectible card games, encompassing both circulating collections and programming.

Methodologically, the proposal outlines a quantitative approach using two distinct Qualtrics questionnaires. One instrument targets library staff involved in collection development and program management, while the other targets patrons. The study design identifies two dependent variables: "community interest in GBLS" and "usage of GBLS." These are examined in relation to two independent variables: "libraries’ current GBLS offerings" and "community/patron demographics." The author hypothesizes that many libraries do not currently prioritize GBLS despite gaming's popularity, but those that do offer robust services will report high patron satisfaction. Furthermore, it is hypothesized that younger patrons (ages 18–40) will express significantly higher interest than older adults (41+).

The proposed population includes staff and patrons from public libraries across the United States. The author aims for a large-scale sample to ensure diverse representation regarding region and library size. The target sample size is approximately 1,700 libraries (roughly 10% of U.S. public libraries), with at least one staff respondent per library, and an average of 20 patron respondents per library, totaling an estimated 34,000 patron responses. A minimum threshold of 850 libraries and 17,000 patrons is established. Recruitment strategies involve partnering with the Chief Officers of State Library Agencies (COSLA) and the American Library Association Games & Gaming Round Table to disseminate the call for participation. Data collection will utilize stratified random sampling to account for subgroup attributes such as urban/rural distinctions and demographic variables.

Data analysis plans include the use of Microsoft Excel for organization and IBM SPSS Statistics for rigorous statistical examination. The author intends to employ descriptive statistics to summarize the current state of GBLS, and inferential statistics, including chi-square tests for categorical variables and t-tests for continuous variables, to identify significant relationships between service offerings and community interest/usage. Regression analysis may also be used to predict long-term impacts.

The proposal acknowledges potential limitations, including response bias where respondents may not accurately report interest or usage, and limited generalizability due to the scope of the sample, even if large. The author notes that while stratified random sampling aims for representativeness, findings may not apply to all possible library contexts. Ethical considerations are emphasized, with assurances of voluntary participation, anonymity, secure data storage, and the use of aggregated data in reporting.

The anticipated implications of the study are significant for library practice and policy. The results are expected to provide actionable data for budgeting, collection development, and programming decisions. By clarifying the demand for GBLS, the study aims to help libraries remain relevant to contemporary patrons, particularly younger generations who view libraries as spaces for diverse media engagement beyond traditional books. The proposal concludes that this research will serve as a foundational project for future studies in the intersection of gaming and library services.
