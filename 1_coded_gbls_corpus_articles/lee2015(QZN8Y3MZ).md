## Purpose
The article aims to empirically evaluate a standardized metadata schema designed for video games and interactive media, developed by the Game Metadata Research (GAMER) Group at the University of Washington in collaboration with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum. The primary objectives were to determine if the schema adequately supports user information-seeking activities across diverse user groups and to assess its capability to describe a wide variety of games across different genres, formats, platforms, and time periods.

## Findings
Survey results indicated that <price> and <platform> were the most useful metadata elements (>80% positive response), followed by subject metadata (<genre>, <style>, <plot/narrative>) and relational data (<series>, <franchise>). Visual metadata, particularly <gameplay videos>, was highly valued. Schema testing revealed significant discrepancies in cataloging conceptual levels (work vs. manifestation) and subjective elements like <mood> and <theme>. Challenges were identified in describing digitally distributed games, leading to revisions such as renaming <box art/cover> to <representative art> and adding a <DRM> element. Elements such as <characters>, <controls>, <achievements>, and <credits> were removed from the recommended set due to complexity, inconsistency, or low return on investment for catalogers. The schema was revised to Version 2.0, incorporating these findings.
Evidence: "The elements <price> and <platform> were the most highly rated... Visual information was also highly valued, most notably, <gameplay videos>... ## Implications
The authors conclude that existing bibliographic standards are insufficient for video games and that a specialized, robust metadata schema is necessary to support access for diverse user groups. The revised schema serves as a platform for future advances in video game metadata design. The study highlights the need for clear conceptual models to distinguish between game entities (e.g., work vs. manifestation) and emphasizes the importance of capturing dynamic aspects of gaming, such as online capabilities and digital rights management (DRM).
Evidence: "This work will serve as a platform and catalyst for advances in the design and use of video game metadata... Libraries, archives, and museums need a common, shared metadata standard that covers all aspects of video games..."

# Summary

This article presents an empirical evaluation of a standardized metadata schema for video games and interactive media, developed by the Game Metadata Research (GAMER) Group at the University of Washington in partnership with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum. The study addresses the inadequacy of current descriptive practices in libraries and cultural heritage institutions, which often shoehorn video games into metadata frameworks designed for other media types, thereby hindering effective discovery and access.

The research methodology was multi-phased, with this report focusing on Phase III (2013–2014). The evaluation employed four distinct approaches: collaborative expert review, schema testing through sample cataloging, semi-structured user interviews, and a large-scale online survey. The collaborative review involved iterative feedback from students and domain experts to refine element definitions and controlled vocabularies. Schema testing was conducted by having students create metadata records for 65 sample games, including console titles, digital apps, and flash-based games. Each game was cataloged twice by different individuals to assess inter-cataloger consistency and identify ambiguities in the schema.

User evaluation comprised two streams. First, 32 semi-structured interviews were conducted with a diverse range of stakeholders, including casual and avid gamers, parents, collectors, industry professionals, curators/librarians, and scholars. Participants were recruited via snowball sampling from various communities and professional networks. Second, an online survey was administered to a broader population of gamers. Of 2,163 respondents, 1,257 completed the questionnaire, which assessed the perceived usefulness of specific metadata elements for both currently played games and new game discovery.

Key findings from the survey indicated that <price> and <platform> were the most highly rated elements, with over 80% of respondents finding them useful. Subject metadata, such as <genre>, <style>, <plot/narrative>, and <theme>, was also valued, as was relational data like <series> and <franchise>. Visual metadata, particularly <gameplay videos>, was deemed highly informative for assessing gameplay mechanics and visual appeal, whereas static images like screenshots were viewed with skepticism regarding their veracity.

Schema testing revealed significant challenges in cataloging consistency. Discrepancies frequently arose regarding the conceptual level of description (work vs. manifestation), particularly for games available across multiple platforms. Subjective elements requiring personal play experience, such as <mood> and <theme>, led to inconsistent descriptions when catalogers relied on secondary sources. Consequently, the researchers developed a conceptual model to clarify entity distinctions and established preferred source lists for subjective data.

Several elements were removed or modified from the recommended schema due to complexity or low utility. <Characters>, <controls>, <achievements>, and <credits> were excluded because they introduced excessive workload, inconsistency, or ambiguity without proportional benefit to user discovery. Conversely, new elements were added to address emerging needs in digital distribution. A <DRM> (Digital Rights Management) element was included to capture restrictions relevant to both players and scholars. The <box art/cover> element was renamed <representative art> to accommodate digitally distributed games lacking physical packaging. Additionally, <online capabilities> was split into <networked features> and <connectivity> to distinguish between gameplay interactions and technical requirements.

The study concludes that the revised metadata schema (Version 2.0) better reflects user information needs and the unique characteristics of video games. The authors emphasize the necessity of media-specific standards to support the preservation, organization, and access of video game collections in cultural heritage institutions. The work provides a foundational framework for future developments in video game metadata, highlighting the importance of balancing descriptive richness with cataloging efficiency.
