## Findings
Participants unanimously reported significant challenges in organizing and finding game development artifacts. The authors categorize these challenges into three primary factors:
1.  **Organizational Culture:** The video game industry does not incentivize documentation. Developers often view documentation as unproductive or "not fun," prioritizing product creation over record-keeping. Smaller companies use ad hoc solutions, while larger ones have firmer policies but still struggle with sharing. There is also reluctance to share sensitive information (e.g., budgeting, cut features).
2.  **Technical Work Environment:** The prevalence of agile development principles ("working software over comprehensive documentation") leads to minimal documentation. Rapid changes in project specifics make early design documents obsolete quickly. Open office layouts facilitate verbal communication but reduce the need for written records, which becomes problematic when personnel change or complexity increases. "Living games" with frequent updates further complicate preservation.
3.  **Lack of Standard Vocabulary and Practices:** There is no industry-wide standard for naming conventions or terminology. Developers use various tools (Confluence, Dropbox, wikis) without consistent naming structures, making retrieval difficult. Memory institutions resort to homegrown controlled vocabularies because existing standards (e.g., Argus, WorldCat) are insufficient for game-specific artifacts, leading to siloed and fractured language across institutions.
4.  **Disconnect Between Collections:** Libraries, archives, and special collections operate on different systems without explicit links between related items (e.g., a game in special collections vs. its development artifacts in an archive), hindering comprehensive access.

## Implications
The authors propose two main implications for organizational practice:
1.  **Development of Standard Vocabulary:** There is a critical need for standardized naming conventions and vocabularies to describe video game development artifacts. # Summary

Lee et al. (2020) address the critical gap in the preservation and organization of video game development artifacts, which are often overlooked compared to finished game products. While digital games are increasingly recognized as cultural heritage, the "digital ephemera" generated during their creation—such as game design documents, technical specifications, art bibles, and test builds—remain poorly organized and at risk of loss. The study investigates the challenges faced by stakeholders in organizing and accessing these materials, aiming to inform better preservation practices for libraries, archives, museums, and game developers.

The researchers employed a qualitative, user-centered methodology, conducting semi-structured interviews with 29 participants recruited via snowball sampling. The sample included 12 game industry professionals (developers and producers), 6 game historians, and 11 workers from memory institutions (libraries, archives, and museums). Data were analyzed using inductive coding following a consensus model to ensure reliability.

The findings reveal that stakeholders face significant hurdles in organizing and retrieving development artifacts, driven by three main factors: organizational culture, technical work environments, and a lack of standard vocabulary. First, organizational culture within the game industry often discourages documentation. Developers frequently view documentation as unproductive or tedious, prioritizing rapid product delivery over record-keeping. This is exacerbated by agile development methodologies that value "working software over comprehensive documentation." Consequently, early design documents are rarely maintained as living records, and changes are not systematically tracked. Second, the technical environment contributes to fragmentation. The use of diverse tools (e.g., Confluence, Dropbox, wikis) without consistent naming conventions makes retrieval difficult. Participants noted that while storage is easy, finding specific assets later is akin to "finding a needle in a stack of needles." Third, there is a profound lack of standardized terminology. Both industry professionals and memory institution workers struggle with inconsistent naming practices. Memory institutions often rely on inadequate general-purpose vocabularies or create isolated, homegrown controlled vocabularies, resulting in a fractured linguistic landscape that hinders interoperability.

Additionally, the study highlights a disconnect between different types of collections within memory institutions. Libraries, archives, and special collections operate on separate systems (e.g., WorldCat vs. Argus) without explicit links between related materials. This siloing prevents users from accessing a comprehensive view of a game’s history, as development artifacts may be scattered across unconnected repositories.

The authors conclude that addressing these challenges requires organizational interventions. They emphasize the need for a standardized vocabulary and naming conventions to describe video game development artifacts accurately. To this end, they are developing a taxonomy to extend existing metadata schemas like VGMS and GAMECIP. Furthermore, they advocate for exploring methods to connect related items across different collection types within institutions. The study suggests that these solutions could also benefit other digital media sectors, such as film and animation, which face similar preservation challenges with born-digital artifacts. Ultimately, the authors argue that without industry-wide drives toward documentation and standardized organizational practices, the historical context and development processes of video games will remain critically endangered.
