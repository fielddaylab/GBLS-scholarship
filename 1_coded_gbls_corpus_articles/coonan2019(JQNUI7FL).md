# Summary

This editorial by Coonan (2019) introduces an issue of the *Journal of Information Literacy* dedicated to expanding the conceptual boundaries of information literacy (IL) beyond traditional academic settings. The overarching objective is to demonstrate how IL manifests in diverse contexts, including public libraries, school environments, higher education professional development, and informal digital spaces. The text serves as a curated guide to the issue’s contents, emphasizing a shift toward understanding IL as a practice embedded in everyday life rather than solely a formal educational outcome.

The editorial categorizes the issue’s contributions into several key areas. First, it addresses professional learning and development for library staff, citing reports from the LILAC and ALDinHE conferences. These sections highlight themes such as professional identity, imposter syndrome, and the synergy between learning development and IL. Second, the text outlines pedagogical innovations in higher education. Notable examples include a flipped workshop designed for creative arts students to understand the attribution of non-textual artifacts, and an evaluation of asynchronous online tutorials using learning diaries. The latter method is highlighted for its ability to capture individual reflections and avoid the consensus bias often found in focus groups.

Third, the editorial discusses empirical research on academic perceptions of IL, specifically within nursing and business disciplines at Anglia Ruskin University. This work connects disciplinary practices to IL, framing information evaluation as a means of cultivating social capital for future professional workplaces. Fourth, it examines public library services through the lens of the 'Philosophy for Children' (P4C) approach. This section argues for repositioning libraries as sites of civic discourse and inquiry-based learning, where facilitators help children develop independent critical thinking strategies rather than simply providing information.

Most relevant to games-based library services is the editorial’s discussion of informal learning in videogaming. Coonan highlights a paper by Scolari and Contreras-Espinosa that investigates transmedia literacy among game players. The text notes that this research identifies significant overlaps between gaming practices and IL competencies, including problem-solving, identity performance, navigating complex media environments, and networking. The editorial emphasizes the authors' conclusion that skills often viewed as merely technical or contextual within gaming possess broader social and cognitive applications. This section underscores a critical philosophical point: learning is not merely preparation for living but is an active part of living itself.

The implications drawn in this editorial suggest that library professionals should recognize and integrate these informal literacies into their practice. By acknowledging the IL skills developed through gaming and other everyday interactions, libraries can better support users across all contexts. The text concludes by encouraging practitioners to adopt the innovative ideas presented in the issue, reinforcing the journal’s mission to develop deep, critical understandings of IL that are applicable both within formal institutions and in the broader social sphere. The editorial thus frames games-based learning not as a peripheral activity, but as a significant domain where information literacy is actively constructed and performed.
