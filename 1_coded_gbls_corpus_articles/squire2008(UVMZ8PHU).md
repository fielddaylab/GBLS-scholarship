## Purpose
The article aims to propose a theoretical model for video game-based learning environments, specifically focusing on "open-ended simulation" or "sandbox" games. It seeks to demonstrate how these games function as "possibility spaces" that allow players to develop new identities and productive literacies. The author intends to move the field beyond rhetoric by providing grounded examples of how specific design features operate and how they can be leveraged for 21st-century educational needs.
Evidence: "This paper offers a theoretical model for video game-based learning environments as designed experiences... It concludes with an investigation of how we might use such games to develop players’ productive literacies..." (Pages 3-4)
Confidence: high

## Method
The study employs a qualitative, mixed-methods approach combining cognitive ethnography, case studies, and interview data. The methodology includes: (1) Interviews with three distinct groups of *Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas* (GTA: SA) players to analyze meaning-making regarding race, class, and violence; (2) A classroom-based case study using *Civilization III* in a high school social studies setting to observe student engagement and historical interpretation; and (3) An analysis of Apolyton University, an online community of *Civilization* players, to understand expert player practices.

## Population and Data
The population consists of three distinct groups for the GTA analysis: (1) Honovi, a 12-year-old African-American youth, and his friends; (2) A group of white, working-class adolescents (ages 14-15) who are avid gamers; and (3) A third group of African-American, working-poor high school students. For the *Civilization* study, the population includes high school students in a social studies class and after-school programs, specifically noting disengaged students like "Dwayne." Data sources include semi-structured interviews, classroom observations, student artifacts (game strategies, narratives), and online community interactions from Apolyton.net.
Evidence: "Ben Devane and I (2006) interviewed Honovi, several of his friends, and other groups of GTA: SA players... We conducted a series of interviews with 14-15 year old avid GTA: SA players... I used Civilization III in a high school class and an after school setting..." (Pages 14, 16, 24)
Confidence: high

## Findings
The study finds that open-ended games function as "possibility spaces" where meaning is negotiated through social interaction and player identity rather than fixed textual interpretation. In the GTA analysis, different groups interpreted the game differently based on their social contexts: Honovi used it for car customization and masculine performance; white players focused on racial stereotypes as media critique; and African-American students critiqued structural racism and economic mobility. In the *Civilization* study, students developed robust conceptual models of history through geographical materialism (e.g., understanding how resources and geography influence political power). Disengaged students showed increased participation and success when leveraging their gaming expertise. However, the study also found that school structures (time limits, standardized outcomes) constrained the full potential of these games, and some students retained naive ethical beliefs about history despite gaining systemic understanding.
Evidence: "For Honovi, the game was as much a 'car detailing simulation'... white students focused on racial representations while a group of working poor African-American students focused on structural issues... Students interpreted from the game model that civilizations conquered others due to particular geographic properties..." (Pages 14-15, 22, 27)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The author argues that educators should design learning environments that leverage the open-ended nature of sandbox games to support diverse learner interests and identities. The implications suggest moving away from targeted, linear educational games toward "possibility spaces" that allow for creative expression and identity development. The study highlights the need for social structures (like interpretive communities) to support learning and suggests that after-school or extended programs may be more effective than traditional 45-minute classroom blocks for deep game-based learning. It also implies that games can serve as bridging mechanisms for disengaged students by validating their existing expertise.
Evidence: "Open-ended sandbox games appear to be a productive space for studying and designing game-based learning environments... The designed experience of school... greatly shapes the learning that ultimately takes place." (Pages 21, 33)
Confidence: high

# Summary

Kurt Squire’s 2008 article, "Possibility Spaces," presents a theoretical framework for understanding open-ended simulation games, or "sandbox" games, as effective models for educational design. The primary objective is to move beyond the rhetoric of game-based learning by providing empirical evidence of how players interact with and derive meaning from complex game systems like *Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas* (GTA: SA) and *Civilization III*. Squire argues that these games function as "possibility spaces"—ideological worlds where players experiment, perform identities, and negotiate meanings within interpretive communities.

The methodology combines qualitative interviews, classroom case studies, and cognitive ethnography. The study first examines three distinct groups of GTA: SA players to understand how social context influences interpretation. The first group, including a 12-year-old African-American youth named Honovi, utilized the game primarily for car customization and as a space to perform masculinity, largely ignoring the narrative or violent aspects. A second group of white, working-class adolescents interpreted the game through a lens of media literacy, critiquing racial stereotypes while viewing violence as a mechanical feedback system. A third group of African-American high school students focused on structural issues, using the game to discuss real-world economic mobility and systemic racism, finding the game’s depiction of segregation realistic but its portrayal of upward mobility unrealistic. These findings suggest that there is no single "game" experience; rather, meaning is emergent and tied to players' identities and social contexts.

The second part of the study investigates *Civilization III* in a high school social studies setting. The goal was to help students understand history through geographical materialism—how resources, geography, and trade influence political outcomes. The data revealed that students, particularly those previously disengaged from school, became highly motivated when they could apply their gaming expertise. Students developed sophisticated theories about historical patterns, such as the relationship between resource access and military power. For example, one student, Tony, articulated how geography dictates economic strength and political stability. However, the study also noted limitations: while students gained systemic understanding, their ethical beliefs about war and colonization did not necessarily change. Furthermore, the rigid structure of traditional schooling (45-minute periods, standardized objectives) conflicted with the open-ended, exploratory nature of the game, limiting its full educational potential.

Finally, Squire analyzes Apolyton University, an online community of *Civilization* players, to illustrate how expert players develop deep design expertise and systemic understanding through social collaboration. The implications for library and educational services are significant. Squire suggests that libraries and schools should consider creating "possibility spaces" that allow for open-ended exploration and identity performance, rather than prescribing linear learning paths. He argues for the importance of supporting interpretive communities where learners can negotiate meaning and develop productive literacies. The study concludes that while open-ended games offer powerful tools for engaging diverse learners and fostering complex thinking, their implementation requires flexible pedagogical structures that accommodate varied play styles and learning trajectories.
