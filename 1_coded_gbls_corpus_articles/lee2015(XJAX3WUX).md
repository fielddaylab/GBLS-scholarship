## Findings
The authors identify that additional content challenges traditional bibliographic notions of a "work" because it can significantly alter gameplay and narrative, creating ambiguity about whether modified games constitute new works. Practically, they find that the lack of standard controlled vocabulary, varying distribution methods (physical vs. digital), platform-specific dependencies, and technical barriers (encryption, copy protection) hinder acquisition, description, and preservation. The proposed conceptual model addresses these issues by treating additional content as a separate entity with specific attributes (title, type, version requirements), allowing for clearer delineation between mods, DLC, and expansions. However, the authors note that this model abandons the FRBR framework, which they argue is ill-suited for non-text media and serial-like objects.

# Summary

This article addresses the growing complexity of managing video game collections in cultural heritage institutions, specifically focusing on the challenges posed by "additional content" such as downloadable content (DLC) and modifications (mods). The authors define additional content as material that expands or supplements a game beyond its initial point of purchase, explicitly excluding ordinary software patches and bug fixes. They distinguish between mods, typically developed by fan communities, and DLC, produced by corporate entities, noting that both require the original game to function but differ in origin and often in cost.

The authors identify significant philosophical challenges related to the bibliographic concept of a "work." Traditional definitions view a work as a distinct intellectual creation, but additional content can substantially alter gameplay mechanics and narrative structures. This raises questions about whether a game with extensive mods or DLC remains the same work or constitutes a new one. The optional nature of this content further complicates matters, as different players may experience vastly different versions of the same title. The authors note that there are no established criteria for determining when a modification becomes a distinct work, suggesting that community usage and popularity might serve as informal benchmarks.

Practically, the article outlines hurdles in acquisition, characterization, description, access, and preservation. Acquisition is complicated by the ephemeral nature of digital distribution platforms, the dependency on original game copies, and budget constraints given the volume of available content. Description is hindered by the lack of standardized controlled vocabulary within the gaming industry and the difficulty of attributing creation to diverse sources, ranging from large corporations to individual fans. Furthermore, describing system requirements, compatibility, and inter-mod dependencies is critical for usability but currently lacks standardization. Access and preservation face technical barriers due to varied distribution methods, platform-specific architectures, copy protection measures on physical media, and encryption in digital files that obscure the underlying code.

To address these issues, the authors propose a new conceptual model that treats additional content as a separate entity from the base game. This model assigns specific attributes to each piece of additional content, including title, type (mod, DLC, expansion), and version/configuration requirements. By doing so, it facilitates clearer bibliographic control and allows for the creation of formal metadata records. The authors illustrate this with examples from *Civilization V*, showing how the model can capture detailed information about expansions and mods, including their dependencies on other content.

The authors acknowledge that this approach diverges from the Functional Requirements for Bibliographic Records (FRBR) framework, arguing that FRBR is poorly suited for non-text media and objects with multiple versions, such as serials. They posit that video games are increasingly analogous to serials due to their continuous evolution. A significant limitation of the proposed model is that it does not resolve the philosophical question of when a mod becomes a new work; this remains a matter for community best practices. Additionally, the model introduces the challenge of "perpetual cataloging," as the constant creation of new content requires ongoing maintenance of bibliographic records. The authors suggest future research should focus on refining the model through practical application and exploring linked data ontologies like BIBFRAME or schema.org as alternative frameworks for representing video game collections.
