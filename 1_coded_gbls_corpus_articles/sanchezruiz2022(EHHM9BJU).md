# Structured Extraction

## Purpose
The study aims to analyze the emotional footprint and student perceptions resulting from the implementation of ten short-duration digital escape rooms (dEERs) in a first-year Mathematics course for Aerospace Engineering students. Specifically, it seeks to determine if these game-based learning activities foster an environment of positive emotions, enhance motivation, and support knowledge acquisition and soft skill development.
Evidence: "This article focuses on the consequences, beliefs, and feelings that several dEERs... have had on the students who have participated in them." (Section 1)
Confidence: high

## Method
The researchers employed a mixed-methods case study design. They designed and implemented ten digital escape rooms using Genial.ly and RPG Maker MZ software. Data collection involved an ad hoc questionnaire validated by subject experts (Cronbach’s α = 0.97; KMO = 0.834) to measure student feelings, satisfaction, and perceptions of specific game elements (teamwork, storytelling, tests). Additionally, semi-structured interviews were conducted with a volunteer subset of students. Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS, employing non-parametric tests (Mann–Whitney U, Kruskal–Wallis) due to non-normal data distribution.
Evidence: "A feelings/satisfaction questionnaire has been conducted... After the questionnaire, some students were interviewed..." (Abstract); "The analysis and treatment of the data were carried out with SPSS software." (Section 2.4)
Confidence: high

## Population and Data
The sample consisted of 296 undergraduate students (135 female, 161 male), aged 18–19, enrolled in the Mathematics I course at the Universitat Politècnica de València during the 2019/2020, 2020/2021, and 2021/2022 academic years. Students participated in teams of 3–5 members (mean team size 4.05). A separate sample of 387 students was used to analyze final grades relative to dEER participation. Data included survey responses on emotions (primary/secondary feelings and intensity), Likert-scale ratings of game elements, and self-reported final course grades.

Confidence: high

## Findings
**Emotional Impact:** Positive emotions predominated. "Motivated" was the most frequent primary feeling (18.9%) and secondary feeling (20.9%), with motivation selected by 39.8% of students overall. Other positive feelings included happiness and confidence. While "Stressed" was reported (7.4%), interviews indicated this stress was lower in intensity than traditional exams, mitigated by teamwork and the game environment.
**Perception of Elements:** Storytelling was highly valued for enhancing motivation (85% agreement) and creating a positive emotional environment (90% agreement). Teamwork was seen as beneficial for motivation (70%) but less consistently for generating positive emotions (50%). Students reported that dEERs helped reinforce knowledge (Mean = 3.83/5) and develop soft skills like critical thinking (Mean = 3.90/5).
**Academic Performance:** Students who participated in dEERs had significantly higher final grades (Mean = 7.27) compared to non-participants (Mean = 6.69; p = 0.009). However, the authors caution that participation may indicate pre-existing motivation.
**Gender Differences:** Female students rated the usefulness of dEERs for reinforcing knowledge and the impact of teamwork on motivation significantly higher than male students. No significant gender difference was found in final grades.
Evidence: "Results show a high incidence of positive emotions... The final grade for the students participating in the dEERs... is significantly greater..." (Abstract, Results, Table 4)
Confidence: high

## Implications
The study suggests that digital escape rooms are effective tools for fostering positive emotional environments and motivation in STEM education, particularly when storytelling is integrated. They support knowledge reinforcement and soft skill development. The authors recommend considering the balance of difficulty to maintain flow and noting that while stress occurs, it can be managed through collaborative design. Further research is needed to correlate self-reported feelings with objective learning outcomes more rigorously.
Evidence: "It can be concluded that the dEERs used as GBL elements improve student motivation and help generate an environment of positive emotions." (Section 4)
Confidence: high

# Summary

This article presents a case study evaluating the impact of digital escape rooms (dEERs) on student emotions, motivation, and perceived learning outcomes within a first-year Mathematics course for Aerospace Engineering students at the Universitat Politècnica de València. The primary objective was to determine whether implementing game-based learning (GBL) via dEERs fosters a positive emotional environment and enhances educational effectiveness in a STEM context.

The methodology involved the design and implementation of ten short-duration dEERs (20–30 minutes each) using Genial.ly and RPG Maker MZ software. These activities were integrated into the "Mathematics I" syllabus, covering calculus and series topics. The study utilized a mixed-methods approach: an ad hoc questionnaire completed by 296 students (18–19 years old) assessed their primary and secondary feelings, intensity of emotions, and perceptions of specific game elements such as teamwork, storytelling, and quiz formats. Additionally, semi-structured interviews were conducted with 25 volunteers. A separate analysis compared the final grades of 387 students based on their participation in dEERs. Statistical validity was confirmed with a Cronbach’s α of 0.97 and KMO of 0.834.

Key findings indicate a strong prevalence of positive emotions. "Motivation" was the most frequently cited feeling, selected by nearly 40% of students as either their primary or secondary emotion. Other dominant feelings included happiness and confidence. Although stress was reported by 7.4% of participants, qualitative data suggested this was less intense than traditional exam stress, largely due to the collaborative nature of the activity. Students highly valued the narrative elements; over 85% agreed that storytelling enhanced motivation, and 90% believed it contributed to a positive emotional atmosphere. Teamwork was also viewed positively for boosting motivation (70% agreement), though its impact on generating positive emotions was slightly lower (50%).

Regarding academic utility, students reported that dEERs effectively reinforced theoretical knowledge (Mean = 3.83/5) and developed soft skills like critical thinking (Mean = 3.90/5). The overall satisfaction with the activities was high (Mean = 4.44/5). Empirically, students who participated in dEERs achieved significantly higher final course grades (Mean = 7.27) compared to non-participants (Mean = 6.69), with a p-value of 0.009. However, the authors note that participation may be confounded by pre-existing student motivation. Gender analysis revealed that female students rated the educational value and motivational impact of teamwork higher than male students, though no significant gender gap was observed in final grades.

The study concludes that dEERs are a viable pedagogical tool for creating engaging, positive learning environments in STEM disciplines. The integration of storytelling and collaborative problem-solving appears critical to this success. While the results are promising, the authors acknowledge limitations, including the reliance on self-reported data for learning effectiveness and the potential bias in grade comparisons due to voluntary participation. They recommend further research to objectively measure the correlation between emotional states and academic performance.
