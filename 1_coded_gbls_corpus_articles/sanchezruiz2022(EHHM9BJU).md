### Summary
# **Sánchez-Ruiz, L. M., López-Alfonso, S., Moll-López, S., Moraño-Fernández, J. A., & Vega-Fleitas, E. (2022). Educational digital escape rooms footprint on students' feelings: A case study within aerospace engineering. *Information, 13*(10), 478. https://doi.org/10.3390/info13100478**

Sánchez-Ruiz and colleagues study the emotional footprint of short digital educational escape rooms in a first-year Mathematics I course for aerospace engineering students at the Universitat Politècnica de València. The article is framed around game-based learning, positive emotions, motivation, and STEM education rather than library services. The authors argue that educational escape rooms can combine student-centered learning, problem solving, time pressure, teamwork, narrative, and game mechanics to create an active learning environment, particularly when face-to-face escape rooms are difficult to stage or when digital formats fit institutional infrastructure.

The project implemented ten digital educational escape rooms, each approximately 20 to 30 minutes long, across two blocks of the Mathematics I syllabus: multivariable calculus and series. Five escape rooms were designed for each block. The escape rooms were built with Genial.ly and RPG Maker MZ, originally for remote play during COVID-19 restrictions and later retained in digital form after students returned to face-to-face classes. Students generally played in teams of four or five, though the rooms remained available for individual or group replay. Activities used linear puzzle structures, hidden symbols, mathematical quizzes, distractor clues, countdown pressure, attempts counters, optional clues, and in some RPG Maker rooms avatars with life, strength, and intelligence attributes that could be penalized or rewarded.

The study focused on feelings, perceptions, and design elements rather than a controlled learning-outcome experiment. A total of 296 students completed anonymous questionnaires during the 2019/2020, 2020/2021, and 2021/2022 academic years; 25 students also participated in follow-up interviews. The questionnaire asked students to name primary and secondary feelings, rate their intensity, and evaluate whether escape-room components such as teamwork, storytelling, tests/quizzes, difficulty, and duration supported motivation, positive emotions, knowledge reinforcement, and soft-skill development. The questionnaire showed high reliability, with Cronbach's alpha of 0.97 and KMO of 0.834.

The results emphasize generally positive affect. The most common primary feeling was motivation, named by 18.9% of students, followed by happiness at 11.1%; when primary and secondary feelings were combined, motivation was selected by 39.8% of students. Stress also appeared, but interview responses suggested that team play and the game environment made it feel less like ordinary exam stress. Students reported that the escape rooms helped reinforce knowledge, that storytelling strongly enhanced motivation and positive emotion, and that teamwork supported motivation while sometimes creating group-dynamic problems. The overall opinion of the activity was very positive, with a mean of 4.44 on a five-point scale, and difficulty was perceived as appropriately moderate.

The authors also compare final grades for students who participated in digital escape rooms and those who did not. Participating students had a higher mean final grade (7.27) than nonparticipants (6.69), with a statistically significant Mann-Whitney U result, but the authors caution that participation may reflect prior motivation and that the groups were not paired. They also find some gender differences in perceptions of knowledge reinforcement, teamwork, tests, positive emotion, and difficulty. The conclusion is cautious: digital educational escape rooms appear to foster motivation and positive emotions, and students perceive gains in knowledge and soft skills, but the relationship between self-reported feeling and actual learning effectiveness requires further study.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is helpful as indirect design background for digital escape-room programming, especially around narrative, teamwork, puzzle difficulty, stress, positive emotion, and the use of accessible platforms such as Genial.ly and RPG Maker. Those considerations could inform library-run escape rooms, particularly digital or hybrid instruction programs. However, the study itself is not about libraries, librarians, information literacy, public programming, collections, or community access. Its setting is an aerospace engineering mathematics course, its evidence base is mostly student self-report, and its primary service system is university teaching. It should therefore remain outside the main GBLS review except as broad educational escape-room context.
