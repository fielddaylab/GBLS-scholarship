Interweaving of self‑regulated learning and game‑based learning in higher education: a review of academic publications from 2009 to 2020
Xiao‑Li Zheng1 · Yun‑Fang Tu3 · Gwo‑Jen Hwang2,3,4  · Jue Yu6 · Yuan‑Bo Huang5,6
Accepted: 8 June 2024 / Published online: 19 June 2024 © Association for Educational Communications and Technology 2024

Abstract
Researchers have indicated the importance of engaging learners in self-regulated learning (SRL) states when situated in game-based learning contexts; however, it remains a challenge for both educational and educational technology researchers to effectively integrate both. To this end, this study investigated how SRL strategies are interwoven with gamebased learning in higher education by searching the web of science database to systematically review the papers published between 2009 and 2020 in academic journals. The encoded dimensions ranged from the primary research purpose to research issues, including application domains, research methods, duration of the studies, SRL strategies, game types, and game genres. It was found that since 2015, the research purposes have become increasingly diverse, with skills acquisition in game-based learning being regarded as the most important goal, followed by knowledge acquisition and behavior change. Such games took goal orientation, peer learning, and regulating as the main SRL strategies, which exerted a positive effect on learning performance, self-efficacy/confidence, attitudes/ effort, satisfaction/interest, and learning behavior. Meanwhile, these SRL strategies were well embedded into problem-solving, simulation, multi-type, and RPG game types against the setting of the real-life-related storyline as the main game genre. Since previous studies lacked the systematic application of all SRL strategies within a game-based learning environment, they could not uncover the dynamic and cyclic processes of SRL in game-based learning environments. Hence, this study proposed corresponding suggestions for future research issues as a reference for researchers, teachers, and decision-makers.
Keywords  Game-based learning · Higher education · Self-regulated learning (SRL) strategies · Self-regulated learning

As games are playful, learners easily engage in entertainment rather than learning when playing games if they lack self-regulated learning (SRL) competence. Self-regulated learning focuses on the interaction among cognitive, motivational and contextual factors. According to previous studies, game-based learning is regarded as a promising way to promote students’ motivation and engagement in higher education (Hamari et al., 2016; Troussas et al., 2020), where engagement consists of cognitive, affective and behavioral aspects (Wang et al., 2016). In higher education, university students are required to self-direct their own learning and hence SRL is especially important for them (Broadbent, 2017; Broadbent & Poon, 2015). According to a literature review synthesized by Cibrian et al. (2022), serious games can implicitly support the development of academic and life skills in home and school settings by encouraging improved self-regulation and collaboration with others. Moreover, Wang et al. (2020) indicated that self-regulation has a negative correlation with addictive behavior. In other words, it is crucial to foster students’ self-regulation competence in case of game addiction in game-based learning contexts. Wouters and van Oostendorp (2013) used a meta-analysis method to analyze effects of learning support on learning outcomes, where such learning supports as advice, cues, collaboration, elaboration, and so on were included without referring to SRL strategies. Compared to the SRL framework given by Panadero (2017), most of the research only explored some aspects of SRL strategies in game-based learning, while few covered all aspects of SRL strategies. Literature review
Self‑regulated learning strategies in higher education
Self-regulated learning was originally explored five decades ago by the social learning community. There are six models of SRL (Panadero, 2017), of which the Metacognitive and affective model of self-regulated learning (MASRL) presented by Efklides (2011) has been widely adopted. This model involves self-efficacy as one component of motivational aspects in SRL. In 1986, Zimmerman and Martinez-Pons’s taxonomy of self-regulated strategies was proposed, consisting of 14 categories. That is (1) self-evaluation, (2) organizing & transforming, (3) goal setting & planning, (4) seeking information, (5) keeping records & monitoring, (6) environmental structuring, (7) self-consequences, (8) rehearsing & memorizing, (9) seeking social assistance from peers, (10) seeking social assistance from teachers, (11) seeking social assistance from adults, (12) rereading tests, (13) rereading notes, and (14) rereading textbooks (Garcia et al., 2018; Zimmerman & Martinez-Pons, 1986). With the evolution of learning environments, self-regulated learning strategies are variously defined by different scholars. Pintrich (2004) defined self-regulated learning strategies as metacognitive, motivational, and behavioral strategies which engage students in monitoring and regulating their own behavior in learning situations in a goal directed way in general. In contrast, Anthonysamy et al. (2020) contended that SRL strategies can be classified into four domains: (1) cognitive engagement, (2) metacognitive knowledge (3) resource management (Zimmerman & Martinez-Pons, 1986) and (4) motivational beliefs (Pintrich, 1999), where cognitive engagement includes rehearsal, elaboration, organization and critical thinking; metacognitive knowledge refers to planning, monitoring and regulating; resource management embraces time and study environment, effort regulation, peer learning, and help seeking; and motivational beliefs consist of self-efficacy, task value and goal orientation, which sum up to 14 categories. With reference to the connotation of regulation in the metacognition domain (Efklides, 2008), it embraces regulation of cognition, emotion and willpower (or effort). Seeking information is necessary for individuals to engage themselves in initiating the assigned task towards a targeted goal, which also belongs to individual cognitive engagement, so in this study 14 categories were adjusted to (1) rehearsal, (2) seeking information, (3) elaboration, (4) organization, (5) critical thinking, (6) planning, (7) monitoring, (8) regulating, (9) time and study environment, (10) peer learning, (11) help-seeking, (12) self-efficacy, (13) task value, and (14) goal orientation; these are slightly different from the 14 categories addressed by Anthonysamy et al. (2020).
These strategies are often assessed with self-report instruments (Roth et al., 2015) such as the Motivational Strategies for Learning Questionnaire (MSLQ; Pintrich et al., 1993), the Learning and Study Strategies Inventory (LASSI; Weinstein et al., 1987), the Inventory of Learning Styles (ILS; Vermunt, 1998), the Academic Self-Regulated Learning Scale (A-SRL-S; Magno, 2009), the Online Self-Regulated Learning Questionnaire (OSLQ; Barnard et al., 2008), the Self-Efficacy for Learning Form (SELF; Zimmerman & Kitsantas, 2007), the Self-Efficacy for Self-Regulated Learning Scale (SESRL; Garavalia &

These instruments involve motivation (e.g., value, expectancy, and affect), metacognition (e.g., rehearsal, elaboration, organization, critical thinking, and metacognitive self-regulation), and behavioral aspects (e.g., time and study environment regulation, effort regulation, peer learning, help seeking, and time management). Game‑based learning in higher education

Game-based learning can foster learners’ cognitive, behavioral, affective, and sociocultural engagement with the subject matter (Castillo et al., 2023; Plass et al., 2015, 2020). Elements of game design for learning consist of game mechanics (i.e., learning mechanics and assessment mechanics), visual aesthetic design, narrative design, incentive system, musical score, and content & skills. The ways that games can facilitate cognitive processing for learners include situatedness of learning, transfer of learning, scaffolding and relevant feedback, dynamic assessment, information design (i.e., representation of information), interaction design (i.e., learning mechanics), and gestures & movement (Swacha et al., 2023). As for scaffolding, it should be dynamically adaptive in game-based learning, which requires ongoing evaluation of the learner, and fading as learners acquire skills and knowledge. With the sophistication of learning analytics techniques, data mining technology embedded into serious games can be used to assess learning and predict performance, analyze in-game players’ behaviors, profile students’ characteristics, and analyze the study effect of in-game interventions (Alonso-Fernández et al., 2019). Whether game-based learning has a positive effect on learning outcomes has not reached a consensus (Qin & Clark, 2016). Some studies (Boyle et al., 2014; Nitisakunwut & Hwang, 2023; Vogel et al., 2006) indicated that game-based learning might be superior to traditional classroom instruction as it could increase students’ motivation for learning and provide them with opportunities to explore and acquire new knowledge and skills. Nonetheless, other studies (Emes, 1997) found no strong evidence that game-based learning was associated with students’ high academic achievements or psychological development. These inconsistent findings regarding the effectiveness of game-based learning might be mediated by learners’ engagement. Hamari et al. (2016) explored the impact of flow, engagement and immersion on learning in game-based learning environments and indicated that engagement in the game has a clear positive effect on learning, while immersion in the game did not exert a significant effect on learning. In addition, the challenge of the game has a positive effect on learning both directly and via increasing the engagement, but skill in the game did not affect learning directly but by increasing engagement in the game. Both the challenge of the game and being skilled in the game had positive effects on being both engaged and immersed in the game. In other words, engagement mediated the effect of challenge or skill in the game on learning. Many studies have indicated that some SRL strategies such as self-efficacy (Heo

Self‑regulated learning strategies and game‑based learning
According to Zimmerman’s (1989) sociocognitive model of self-regulation, social context or environment is an important part of students’ SRL. In contrast, game-based learning just aims to afford a playful context to motivate learners to engage in active learning, when SRL easily occurs because contemporary SRL perspectives regard the effects of social and contextual influences on SRL (Corno & Mandinach, 2004). In recent years, a few studies have investigated effects of self-regulated learning strategies on learning performance or achievement in game-based learning environments. Zumbach et al. (2020) indicated that in a serious game, metacognitive and cognitive prompting as one strategy of self-regulated learning proved effective, whereas direct metacognitive training only had a minor effect on knowledge acquisition for high school students. Chen and Hsu (2020) found that self-efficacy, intrinsic value and test anxiety significantly influenced game engagement and game experience when undergraduates used a VR game-based English mobile learning application for learning English. During game-based English learning, self-efficacy affected selfregulation, and self-regulation in the learning environment was at a moderate level. Syal and Nietfeld (2020) utilized self-report measures including achievement goals, situational interest and trace data measuring in-game engagement and disengagement behaviors to verify which approach measuring self-regulated learning could effectively predict performance and content learning. They unveiled that self-reported achievement goals and situational interest were not as effective as in-game trace behaviors in predicting performance and content learning. Taub et al. (2018) used sequence mining to analyze how undergraduates’ scientific reasoning along with their metacognitive monitoring of self-regulated learning processes impacted their STEM learning effectiveness in a game-based learning environment. Compared to the self-regulated learning framework given by Panadero (2017), most available research only explored some aspects of self-regulated learning strategies in game-based learning, and few studies covered all aspects of self-regulated learning strategies. Research methods
This systematic review and content analysis were conducted according to the rigorous protocols suggested by Moher et al. (2009) and Wu et al. (2012b). In addition, the parts on data sources and data coding and analysis are dissected later.

The web of science database (WOS) was searched due to its authority and reliability with reference to the previous systematic review papers (Chen et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022; Lai, 2020). On September 30, 2021, the WOS database was searched for research related to the adoption of game-based learning to facilitate SRL in higher education published until 2020. Research results
Primary research purpose
As reported in Fig. 3, games to acquire skills account for the largest percentage (50.91%) of published articles from 2009 to 2020, followed by games to acquire knowledge (18.18%), mixed (16.36%), and games to change behaviors (10.91%). During the former 6 years (2009–2014), only seven papers were published for using games to acquire knowledge, skills, and both. Moreover, studies on acquiring knowledge, skills, or both were evenly distributed. In contrast, acquiring skills in game-based learning was of increasing interest, and the number of published papers (25) was the greatest during the latter 6 years (2015–2020). Meanwhile, changing behavior in game-based learning also attracted scholars’ research interest, with the published papers increasing to six from zero. The number of studies on games to acquire knowledge also increased by six. Studies with more than two purposes (mixed) similarly increased by five articles. In other words, a growing number of studies showed that gamebased learning was adopted to gain more skills than knowledge, behavior, or others. Moreover, game-based learning is helpful for comprehensive competence development. For instance, Biswas et al. (2020) used the UrbanSim game to train complex decision-making competence. In addition, Fu et al. (2019) employed a mind mapping-based contextual game approach to significantly improve English writing performance.

Most studies focused their interest on mixed strategies (23 articles, 47.92%) with more than two tactics, where the greatest number of studies involved goal orientation (13 articles, 27.08%), followed by peer learning (9 articles, 18.75%), regulating (9 articles, 18.75%), planning (7 articles, 14.58%), seeking information (7 articles, 14.58%), critical thinking (6 articles, 12.5%), self-efficacy (6 articles, 12.5%), monitoring (5 articles, 10.42%), task value (4 articles, 8.33%), elaboration (2 articles, 4.17%), organization (2 articles, 4.17%), time and study environment (2 articles, 4.17%), rehearsal (1 article, 2.08%), and help-seeking (1 article, 2.08%).
According to categories of SRL strategies (Pintrich, 2004), in the 25 studies other than the mixed strategies studies, the distribution of SRL strategies mainly covered motivational beliefs (i.e., goal orientation, self-efficacy, and task value), metacognitive knowledge (i.e., regulating, planning & monitoring) and cognition engagement (i.e., rehearsal, seeking information, elaboration, organization, critical thinking), and behavioral aspects (i.e., peer learning, time and study environment, help-seeking). In terms of motivational beliefs of SRL strategies, seven studies focused their interest on self-efficacy, five on goal orientation, and two on task value. As far as cognitive & metacognitive strategies are concerned, only three studies attached importance to just critical thinking, there were two studies on rehearsal, and only one for each of seeking information, elaboration, planning, monitoring, and regulating. With behavioral strategies, help-seeking was investigated by only one study. Judging from the holistic distribution in the period of 2015 to 2020 along with the detailed mixed strategies as above, most attention was paid to cognitive & metacognitive strategies (e.g., Lee, 2019; van Ginkel et al., 2019), while motivational strategies (e.g., Rohlfsen et al., 2020; Sanchez et al., 2020) ranked second, and behavioral strategies (e.g., Ibrahim, 2019; Mavridis & Tsiatsos, 2017) ranked third. Although the total number of studies on cognitive & metacognitive strategies was greater than those on motivational and behavioral strategies, sub-facets of cognitive & metacognitive strategies were not studied evenly by researchers. More attention was paid to regulating, planning, and monitoring than to elaboration, organization, seeking information, and rehearsal. For example, Chang et al. (2020) used a problem-based game called “Programmer Adventure Land” to enhance learners’ knowledge of computer programming. While playing the game, students experienced peer interaction and gaming context & mission design where they set their own goals to achieve self-directed learning. Meanwhile, the Programmer Adventure Land game provided feedback for students along with tutor’s comments, and students’ activities were recorded in the game, through which instructors and students could monitor their progress. In such a game-based learning environment, three kinds of SRL strategies, namely goal orientation, peer learning, and monitoring, were embedded. Taub et al. (2020) exploited Crystal Island, a game-based learning environment for microbiology, to engage students in assuming the role of a medical detective to investigate an unidentified outbreak that has spread among a team of scientists at the virtual research station. Discussion
This study intended to (1) map trends in research on SRL interwoven with game-based learning environments for higher education, including primary research purpose, SRL strategies, game type and game genre, application domains, research methods and duration of these studies, research issues; (2) identify SRL strategies situated in game-based learning environments as a reference for game developers, teachers and researchers. Self-regulated learning is highly stressed in the sophisticated digital technology era. Game-based learning environments as a context to induce learners’ self-regulation have been widely studied over the years. Although many studies have respectively reviewed game-based learning and self-regulated learning, few have been conducted on SRL strategies interwoven with gamebased learning. Hence, this research conducted a systematic review of empirical studies that examined SRL situated in game-based learning from the WOS database during 2009 and 2020. In this section, the main findings, limitations and suggestions for future research are discussed as follows.
In general, from 2009 to 2020, the total number of published articles on acquiring skills in game-based learning interwoven with SRL strategies was 2.8 times that of knowledge acquisition and 4 times that of behavior change. From 2009 to 2014, few articles were published on knowledge acquisition, skills acquisition, behavior change, mixed, and others. However, from 2015 to 2020, the total number of published articles on such goals in game-based learning was close to seven times that of from 2009 to 2014. It was also found that SRL strategies embedded in game-based learning exerted a positive effect on learning performance, learning behavior, learning attitudes/effort, self-efficacy/confidence, and satisfaction/interest. The main findings are summarized below.
Primary research purposes
The earliest research purpose of SRL strategies integrated into game-based learning is to gain skills (Sancho et al., 2009), with an increasing number of studies on interweaving SRL strategies with game-based learning for knowledge acquisition (Liu & Chen, 2012; Rodríguez Corral et al., 2014), skills acquisition (Chen & Hsu, 2020; Lee, 2019; Stolaki & Economides, 2018), and behavior change (Hardy & Totman, 2017; Hsu et al., 2018; Tsai et al., 2016). These results are consistent with the argument contended by Zhonggen (2019) that players cultivate their knowledge and practice their skills through overcoming numerous hindrances during gaming. In addition, the research purposes reviewed by this study are akin to the conclusions drawn by Clark et al. (2016) in their meta-analysis of

game-based learning which found that digital games are helpful for conceptual and epistemological understanding (i.e., knowledge acquisition), process skills and practices (i.e., skills acquisition), and player’s engagement (i.e., behavior change).

SRL strategies
Most studies focused their attention on mixed strategies with more than two tactics (Chang et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2019), where the greatest number of studies involved goal orientation (Tan et al., 2017; Yeh et al., 2015). Especially, goal setting or goal orientation results in successful goal attainment, which is congruent with the finding concluded by Kizilcec et al. (2017). Such SRL strategies as peer learning (Gonzalez et al., 2014), regulating (Chen et al., 2019b; Ding et al., 2018), planning (Khatri et al., 2019; Topalli & Cagiltay, 2018), seeking information (Biswas et al., 2020; Mavridis & Tsiatsos, 2017), critical thinking (Bawa et al., 2018; Chang et al., 2020), self-efficacy (Buil et al., 2019), and monitoring (Hsu et al., 2018) were moderately attended to by researchers. In some sense, this finding coincides with the argument that metacognition, effort regulation and critical thinking were positively correlated with academic outcomes, and peer learning had a moderate effect on academic outcomes in online higher education learning environments (Broadbent & Poon, 2015). In contrast, task value, elaboration, organization, time and study environment, rehearsal, and help-seeking were less studied by scholars. In other words, cognitive strategies (i.e., rehearsal, organization and elaboration), resource management (i.e., time and study environment, and help seeking) and task value as one of the motivational beliefs were scarcely studied in game-based learning environments for higher education.

Game types and game genres
In terms of SRL strategies interwoven with game-based learning, problem-solving games were the most popular game type. The second most popular game type was simulation, which aligns with the finding of Noroozi et al. (2020). These choices might have been affected by active learning and experiential learning theories as well as learners’ preference for games (Acquah & Katz, 2020) since these types of games provide gratification for learners (Sjöblom et al., 2017). Moreover, the majority of problem-solving games and simulation games were designed based on real-life related storylines, followed by not-specified game genres. In such game genres, mixed SRL strategies were most adopted, which showed that learning is a dynamic and cyclic system involving motivational, cognitive or metacognitive, and behavioral processes.

Application domains
Engineering is regarded as the first application domain of integrating SRL strategies into game-based learning, followed by language during the years of 2009 to 2014. Up to 2015, SRL strategies interwoven with game-based learning were firstly exploited in health, medical and physical education domains, business & management, and social studies. In 2016, SRL strategies were adopted in the science domain for game-based learning. In 2018, SRL strategies were firstly adopted in the STEM learning domain for game-based learning. Such an evolution of application domains of SRL in game-based learning aligns with the arguments that game-based learning mainly acquires complex skills and problem-solving

competence in STEM, and promotes transferring skills to authentic learning tasks (Plass et al., 2020).

Research methods and duration
The first greatest number of adopted research methods is experimental design, which is consistent with the findings of Gul and Sozbilir’s (2016) research. They mainly examined learning performance, including evaluation of artifacts and test/examination. The second greatest number was mixed methods involving more than two methods, in which qualitative methods, surveys, learning analytic technology, sequential analysis, system development, and experimental design were used. Most studies lasted less than 8 weeks. This finding is congruent with Xia and Zhong (2018). Considering the internal validity of experimental design, in order to control the growth effect, researchers generally prefer to conduct an experiment during a short-term period for observing behavior change, motivational outcomes or effects of treatments on cognition or metacognition. Only one third of the studies published during 2009 to 2020 lasted for over 2 months.

Research issues
The available previous studies explored affect, cognition, and skills. Such a classification is congruent with that in these studies (Tang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2018). However, more attention was paid to attitudes/efforts, self-efficacy/confidence, and satisfaction/interest in terms of affective aspects, which coincides with other systematic reviews where affective aspects were also focused on by the majority of studies (Tang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2018), because new media or methods could change affective aspects more easily than cognitive aspects in the short term. In terms of cognitive aspects, learning performance was the first attended focus. As for skills, learning behavior received increasing attention from researchers. However, fewer studies explored anxiety, collaboration or communication, and skills. Since game-based learning aims to help learners to acquire skills and collaboration & communication competences, more studies need to be investigated on effects of GBL on skill acquisition and collaboration & communication.
Despite its useful findings, there are some limitations in this study. Firstly, this review focused primarily on the published articles pertinent to SRL strategies interwoven with game-based learning in higher education, and conducted analyses based on the pre-established coding scheme. Hence, some results might not be highlighted in this analysis. Secondly, since this study used the categorization of SRL strategies given by Anthonysamy et al. (2020) based on Efklides’ (2011) and Pintrich’s (2000) models rather than on that of Hadwin et al. (2011), socially shared regulation in SRL, which is important for complex GBL, has not been taken into account; thus, the results might not cover all the strategies in complex GBL. Thirdly, since game types and game genres are evolving along with the development of the game industry, this study could not specify current new game types and game genres. Fourthly, considering the authority and reliability of the WOS database, this systematic review only used one database rather than all databases, which might have affected the distribution of papers by year.

According to the findings, this study offers some suggestions for future studies when integrating SRL strategies into game-based learning in higher education.
(1). There are large openings for future studies to conduct behavior change verification through game-based learning, along with enhancing studies on effects of SRL strategies on learning behavior.
(2). To date, there have been no related studies covering all strategies of SRL. It is therefore essential to explore effects of all SRL strategies together in a study on research issues such as learning performance, learning behavior, self-efficacy/confidence, satisfaction/interest, attitudes/effort, and so on. In addition, this study has not found that such SRL strategies as organization and time & study environment were applied to game-based learning in higher education. Therefore, in the future it is inevitable to integrate organization or time and study environments into game-based learning in higher education.
(3). Since the action game type has not been found to be used in game-based learning for higher education, it is suggested that in the future, how to design action games integrating SRL strategies for higher education would be worth exploring. Besides, competitive and strategy games are developed to engage students in SRL. Meanwhile, games developed on the basis of space exploration, civilization/history, and fantasy/ fiction are expected to improve students’ participation in SRL in higher education.
(4). With respect to application domains of higher education, it is suggested that in the future, researchers working in the areas of science, mathematics, arts, and across disciplines (including STEM) should attempt to embed SRL strategies into game-based learning. In other words, these domains have many gaps to be filled by applying SRL strategies to game-based learning.
(5). According to the existing studies, experimental design was the main research method for most studies, with few qualitative studies including interviews being used for deep analysis of learning processes. Hence, it is suggested that future studies should expand research methods to qualitative methods to deeply understand the fine-grained learning process. Besides, since most studies were conducted for less than 6 months, it is suggested that future studies should prolong the experimental duration to two semesters, that is, less than 12 months, in order to carefully investigate the learning growth path.
(6). With regard to research issues, studies should be conducted on cognitive load, anxiety, the technology acceptance model, higher-order thinking skills, and collaboration & communication affected by SRL strategies in game-based learning in higher education.

Conclusion
This study aimed to propose potential directions for future research by reviewing the articles on SRL strategies interwoven with game-based learning in higher education published from 2009 to 2020. Although previous studies have discussed the benefits and research trends of game-based learning, this study advances the research by

3206

X.-L. Zheng et al.

offering SRL strategies integrated into game-based learning for future research. This review found that researchers focus more on effects of different SRL strategies on gamebased learning outcomes with the intention to find more effective game-based learning approaches. It was also found that some seldom-discussed issues for game-based learning might deserve a fresh look. According to the previous studies, mixed strategies involving more than two tactics were mostly adopted in game-based learning, in which goal orientation in the mixed strategies was widely involved, whereas task value, elaboration, organization, time and study environment, rehearsal, and help-seeking were less attended by researchers. In addition, this study found that mixed SRL strategies were widely interwoven with problem-solving and simulation games rather than action games or competitive and strategic games. Meanwhile, the experimental research methods were mostly utilized in the extant studies. The number of studies has also increased on SRL strategies interwoven with game-based learning for higher education.
According to the results, some suggestions are proposed for novice researchers or for those researchers who intend to improve their game-based learning studies. The research of SRL strategies interwoven with game-based learning has shown rapid development, indicating that future studies need to focus more attention on how to effectively use SRL strategies in game-based learning in higher education. Studies comparing game-based learning with other conventional learning approaches are not encouraged. On the other hand, some rarely discussed disciplines using SRL integrated with game-based learning (e.g., science, mathematics, arts, STEM), topical educational issues (e.g., Higher-order thinking, collaboration & communication) or qualitative research methods should be encouraged. In addition, comprehensive analysis methods can be used to analyze learners’ performance from different perspectives (e.g., skill performance, self-regulated learning process patterns, affect domains, etc.).
