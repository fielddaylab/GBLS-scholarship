# GBLS Literature Review Evaluation Rubric

## Purpose and Basis

This rubric evaluates whether the GBLS review functions as a publishable scholarly review rather than an annotated bibliography. 

It adapts current ALA and ALA-affiliated guidance rather than reproducing a single journal checklist. *College & Research Libraries* asks reviewers whether a manuscript makes a contribution, uses an appropriate method, supports claims with evidence, communicates clearly to an educated nonspecialist audience, and places research in perspective. ALA's Core Competences emphasize multidisciplinary research synthesis, ethical use of methods, assessment of services with community input, cultural humility, equitable partnerships, and evaluation of technology for accessibility, practicality, sustainability, and effectiveness. ALA division research agendas add impact, staff preparation, equity of access, cultural competence, community engagement, media and technology use, and benefit to library practice.

## Scoring Scale

- **Excellent (9-10):** Ready for peer review with only copyediting or venue-specific changes.
- **Strong (7-8):** Substantively sound; targeted revision is still needed.
- **Developing (5-6):** Important material is present, but argument, method, or synthesis remains uneven.
- **Weak (0-4):** Major conceptual, evidentiary, or organizational revision is required.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Excellent Performance |
|---|---:|---|
| Contribution and significance | 10 | Defines a consequential field-level problem, establishes why it matters to libraries, and makes an original conceptual or practical contribution. |
| Scope and review transparency | 10 | Defines GBLS and review boundaries; explains corpus construction, coding, synthesis method, evidence weighting, and limitations without implying systematic comprehensiveness that was not achieved. |
| Thematic synthesis | 15 | Organizes around concepts, purposes, models, patterns, and tensions; paragraphs make claims supported by multiple sources; individual studies serve the argument rather than determine the structure. |
| Evidence calibration | 15 | Distinguishes experiments, surveys, interviews, observations, reviews, case reports, practitioner reflections, proposals, and historical evidence; aligns the strength of claims with study design and acknowledges null findings. |
| Cross-context analysis | 10 | Compares library types, audiences, formats, and evidence types; identifies convergence, divergence, outliers, and limits on transfer across settings. |
| Equity, access, and cultural humility | 10 | Treats access as material, technical, social, cultural, and spatial; examines exclusion and nonparticipation; avoids universalizing dominant hobby cultures or flattening culturally specific games. |
| Relevance to library practice | 10 | Derives actionable implications for collections, advisory, facilitation, partnerships, staffing, policy, sustainability, preservation, and evaluation without turning the review into a handbook. |
| Research agenda | 5 | Identifies specific, field-level gaps and feasible directions for comparative, longitudinal, implementation, equity, and needs-assessment research. |
| Organization and narrative flow | 5 | Uses a coherent progression, synthesis-driven openings, informative headings, section conclusions, and transitions that build a cumulative argument. |
| Scholarly communication and citation integrity | 10 | Uses clear, concise, bias-aware prose; defines specialized terms; accurately represents sources; uses consistent author-date citations and a complete matched reference list. |
