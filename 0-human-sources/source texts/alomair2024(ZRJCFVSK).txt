Gamiﬁcation and card games have gained increased popularity across diverse educational domains, particularly in the ﬁeld of foreign language learning (FLL). They oﬀer potent tools that can transform the learning process into an engaging, interactive, and enjoyable experience. Currently, numerous instances of gamiﬁed or card game–based language learning methods have demonstrated their signiﬁcant impact on sustaining learners’ engagement and advancing their proﬁciency levels. However, it is noteworthy that these approaches often prioritize the motivational aspect while neglecting the imperative need to enhance student learning outcomes (SLOs). The latter holds pivotal importance not only in determining the success of academic courses but also in evaluating the eﬀectiveness of e-content. To address this gap, the primary objective of this work is to enhance the achievement of SLOs within the context of foreign language courses. A novel framework is proposed for learning foreign languages, harnessing the potential of gamiﬁcation and card games. This framework, developed through the integration of contemporary technologies used in e-learning systems and foreign language applications, encompasses agent technology and the microlearning strategy. A case study was conducted to evaluate the eﬀectiveness of the proposed framework. The results revealed a statistically signiﬁcant improvement in SLOs. This study contributes signiﬁcantly to the advancement of both gamiﬁcation and game-based learning, presenting a promising pedagogical paradigm for language teaching and learning.
Keywords: card games; gamiﬁcation; language learning; learning outcomes; student achievement

1. Introduction
Owing to technology’s capacity to transcend geographical boundaries and facilitate universal access to information and interpersonal connections, there exists an unprecedented demand for acquiring proﬁciency in foreign languages [1]. Over time, this demand has transformed into a substantial and fundamental skill for individuals. The acquisition of foreign languages oﬀers a myriad of advantages, including the enhancement of skills, expansion of cognitive capacities, facilitation of global communication, creation of new career opportunities, improvement of self-esteem, and other noteworthy beneﬁts [2]. In the contemporary era, digital education has become an indispensable component of the educational landscape. Modern learners, deeply immersed in technology, regard it as an essential tool for learning [3]. Evidently, advanced technology exerts a sub-

stantial positive inﬂuence on language learning. One such advancement is the utilization of gamiﬁcation [3] and games [4]. Gamiﬁcation involves integrating game mechanics into nongaming contexts. It is primarily focused on motivating learners and engaging them by incorporating game elements such as points, leaderboards, immediate feedback, badges, and progressing through game levels [5].
Notably, there is a growing body of research on the implementation of gamiﬁcation in language learning, gaining popularity for motivating students, enhancing performance, and fostering enjoyment in computer-mediated and nongaming settings [1]. Furthermore, scholarly research underscores the eﬃcacy of games as impactful learning instruments across diverse educational levels [6]. This eﬀectiveness stems from the creation of a “play and learn” environment that heightens learners’ interest in mastering the target language [6]. Card games are an example of successful

These games provide a tactile and interactive learning experience, thereby augmenting the enjoyment and memorability of the language learning process [4]. Moreover, the incorporation of card games in language learning signiﬁes an inventive and engaging approach capable of enriching various facets of language acquisition [7].
However, the majority of language learning approaches that have been proposed based on gamiﬁcation or card games emphasize motivational aspects, often overlooking the essential enhancement of student learning outcomes (SLOs) [8]. Interestingly, SLOs are integral components of the educational framework, providing a structured approach to measuring, enhancing, and ensuring the quality of language education. They not only guide the learning process but also contribute to the overall success and satisfaction of students in their learning endeavors [9]. The sense of accomplishment derived from meeting speciﬁc learning outcomes serves as a powerful motivator, signifying student progress and growth over the course of their education. 2. Related Work
This section encompasses various salient themes, namely, SLOs, agent technology, microlearning, gamiﬁcation in the context of FLL, and card games in the context of FLL.
2.1. SLOs. In the current educational landscape, there is a noticeable shift toward emphasizing SLOs. The SLOs represent articulated statements outlining the anticipated achievements of a successful student or learner upon completing a speciﬁc program module, course unit, or qualiﬁcation [10].

The core objectives of SLOs can be deﬁned in key dimensions [12]:
1. Informed student progression: SLOs serve to inform students about the knowledge and skills they are expected to gain through a speciﬁc course or program of study.
2. Holistic mapping of educational trajectories: SLOs contribute to mapping the intricate relationships between various courses, programs of study, and academic degrees, oﬀering a comprehensive view of educational trajectories.
3. Curriculum development and progression: SLOs play a crucial role in mapping the development of knowledge and skills at each curriculum level, ensuring a coherent and progressive learning experience.
4. Structural framework for assessment: SLOs oﬀer a structured framework for evaluating teaching and learning processes, providing a foundation for meaningful assessment strategies.
5. Guidance for curriculum design and teaching practices: SLOs inform curriculum design and pedagogic practices, guiding educators in creating eﬀective and relevant educational experiences.
6. Communication of performance standards: SLOs serve as a means of communicating clear and speciﬁc standards of performance expected from students, setting a benchmark for achievement.
In the realm of language learning, the realization of SLOs holds paramount signiﬁcance, serving as an indication of the triumphant acquisition and mastery of indispensable language skills and competencies. These outcomes, meticulously delineated by educational institutions, encompass a spectrum of linguistic abilities, including proﬁciency in speaking (s), listening (l), reading (r), and writing (w). The accomplishment of these learning objectives ensures that students not only comprehend the foundational principles of the language but also cultivate the practical skills essential for proﬁcient communication [13].
Moreover, attaining these objectives demonstrates a student’s preparedness to navigate real-world situations in the target language, augmenting their comprehensive language proﬁciency and cultural acumen. Ultimately, the emphasis on the fulﬁllment of SLOs contributes substantively to the eﬃcacy and triumph of foreign language education [14]. In light of this, the primary objective of this paper is to improve the achievement of SLOs among individuals engaged in FLL.

An agent is a software entity endowed with autonomy, capable of performing activities on behalf of a user or another program while holding knowledge or representations of the user’s goals or intentions [15]. 2.3. Microlearning. The concept of microlearning has garnered considerable attention in the realm of language learning, contributing to an engaging and fulﬁlling educational experience. It presents information in bite-sized, easily digestible units, promoting eﬀective retention and recall. Microlearning demonstrates its eﬀectiveness in fostering the development of language skills, encompassing areas such as vocabulary and grammar, concurrently facilitating the acquisition and retention of instructional content [18].
Beyond improved memory retention, this approach exhibits adaptability to individuals with diverse proﬁciency levels, as it does not necessitate prolonged periods of concentrated focus. Moreover, it provides a satisfactory level of accessibility, thereby mitigating the perceived challenges of learning a new language. The incorporation of microlearning often involves the dissemination of information through online platforms, empowering learners to partake in selfdirected study [19]. Such an approach not only augments learning outcomes but also contributes to time and cost eﬃciency by diminishing reliance on conventional teaching methods and physical classrooms [20]. Considering these advantages, the framework presented in this study aligns with the principles of the microlearning approach for the delivery of educational content.
2.4. Gamiﬁcation in the Context of FLL. In the ever-evolving landscape of education, traditional methods are constantly being reimagined to meet the needs of modern learners. One such transformative approach gaining momentum is

gamiﬁcation [3]. Gamiﬁcation refers to the incorporation of game elements into nongame contexts [21]. In the domain of FLL, the utilization of gamiﬁcation has become a subject of increasing interest and exploration in recent years, as it brings about numerous beneﬁts that make the learning process more engaging and enjoyable. One fundamental beneﬁt is the enhancement of motivation. By introducing elements of challenge, competition, and reward, gamiﬁcation taps into learners’ motivation, making language acquisition a more interactive and purposeful endeavor [22]. Additionally, gamiﬁcation fosters a sense of achievement and progress. As learners accomplish tasks, unlock levels, or earn rewards, they experience a tangible sense of accomplishment. This not only boosts their conﬁdence but also encourages a positive attitude toward language learning. The element of enjoyment is another key beneﬁt. Gamiﬁed language learning platforms often create a more dynamic and entertaining environment, transforming potentially mundane language exercises into engaging activities. This shift in tone contributes to a more positive and enthusiastic approach to learning. Gamiﬁcation also promotes a sense of community. Whether through collaborative challenges, group activities, or shared achievements, learners can connect with each other, fostering a supportive learning environment. Furthermore, gamiﬁcation facilitates personalized learning experiences. Learners can choose activities, challenges, or themes that align with their interests and preferences, allowing for a more tailored approach to language acquisition. This personalization increases engagement and makes the learning journey more relevant to individual learners [3].
Several studies have embraced gamiﬁcation as a valuable approach to enriching the language learning experience. For example, the authors in [23] designed a digital resource tool to reinforce theoretical knowledge acquired within a virtual language learning environment. The tool included various tasks, gamiﬁcation elements, and competitive features. According to the assessment, it exhibited substantial promise as a valuable asset for enhancing the language learning environment. The authors in [24] conducted an empirical study to examine the inﬂuence of gamiﬁcation on the time-limited w performance of English majors in Chinese colleges. They utilized a gamiﬁed platform with diverse game components like rankings, points, levels, and leaderboards. Results indicated that integrating game elements had the potential to captivate learners and enhance their proﬁciency in English w. The authors in [25] conducted a thorough analysis of three websites aimed at incorporating new elements within a conventional classroom environment. The results of this analysis revealed that, despite the distinct features inherent in these tools, all three demonstrated the capacity to elevate levels of engagement and motivation.
Additionally, gamiﬁcation is widely implemented in mobile applications, aiding vocabulary memorization and language skill acquisition. These apps not only provide an engaging user experience but also enhance ﬁnancial gains for developers [26]. 2.5. Card Games in the Context of FLL. The ﬁeld of FLL has undergone a notable shift in pedagogical approaches, with growing recognition among educators of the value of incorporating innovative and engaging methods [29]. Among these methods, the integration of card games into language learning environments has gained attention for its potential to enhance linguistic proﬁciency, cultural understanding, and overall learning experiences [30]. Card games, beyond their recreational value, serve as eﬀective learning tools by engaging learners in meaningful communication and fostering language production and comprehension skills. Their interactive nature not only enhances motivation and engagement but also stimulates strategic thinking, problem solving, and memory recall, creating a conducive environment for language learning [31].
The evolution in foreign language education has prompted researchers and educators to investigate the eﬃcacy of integrating card games into language learning curricula. For example, but not limited to, the authors in [7] explored the U-NO-ME card game’s pedagogical utility in improving English vocabulary acquisition among primary school students. This study enhanced U-NO-ME cards through visuals and power cards, demonstrating positive impacts on vocabulary retention and motivation. The authors in [4] introduced the Vocabulary Connect 4 Circles (VC4C) educational tool, drawing inspiration from twoplayer connection games. VC4C positively inﬂuenced students’ vocabulary skills, fostering improvements in coopera-

tion, communication, creativity, and critical thinking. The authors in [30] presented a pedagogical application rooted in card game dynamics, aiming to enhance children’s English vocabulary proﬁciency. This app, tailored to distinct proﬁciency levels, evidenced marked improvements in children’s vocabulary skills. Moreover, the authors in [32] oﬀered inclusive insights into how learners’ use of word cards contributes to vocabulary learning. Overall, their study favored paperwork cards over digital ones and ready-made cards over self-constructed ones, with a tendency for more substantial eﬀects in intentional and massed learning conditions.
In the realm of grammar skills acquisition, the authors in [31] conducted a study involving a language card game inspired by Donkey and Joker games. This game, consisting of 53 cards, was aimed at assisting students in learning and applying adjectives while reducing anxiety in English learning. This game demonstrated eﬃcacy in enhancing students’ interest in grammar intricacies. Furthermore, the authors in [33] investigated the impact of a card game intervention on improving English verbal communication skills for nursing students from diverse backgrounds. Findings revealed three key themes: education content, skills development, and fun and creativity, emphasizing the importance of creative practice in a safe learning environment.
While previous research has predominantly focused on the motivational aspects of card game integration, elucidating how these games can engage and stimulate students in the learning process, this focus on motivation is undeniably valuable. However, there appears to be a discernible gap in the literature concerning the strategies and methodologies employed to enhance the achievement of SLOs. Addressing this gap, the study introduces a framework utilizing card games to enhance the achievement of SLOs in language learning.
3. Proposed Framework
For the time being, the educational process is moving in the direction of a learning outcome-based approach [10]. Hence, all learning activities should be closely aligned with learning outcomes. This work is aimed at enhancing the student’s achievement of learning outcomes related to FLL. To meet this aim, we combine the idea of educational CGs with point-game elements and propose a framework for learning foreign languages. As illustrated in Section 2.5, card games play a prominent role in motivating learning. In addition, several studies have shown that points are one of the most commonly used game elements in the learning domain [34]. Figure 1 shows the proposed framework. It consists of the card drafting agent (CDA) and the card set database. The description of the CDA and the card set database is provided in Sections 3.1 and 3.2, respectively. Interestingly, the framework can be used as a standalone language learning tool or integrated to support existing language learning platforms.

4. Implementation and Evaluation of the Proposed Framework
4.1. Implementation. The proposed framework was implemented to fulﬁll the evaluation purpose. The Java Agent

Development Framework (JADE) [38] was utilized for the implementation of the CDA. JADE is a robust software framework designed for developing intelligent agents using the Java programming language. It provides essential tools for constructing and programming agent systems. The choice of JADE was motivated by its simpliﬁcation of the primitives necessary for programming the multiagent system within the Java programming language, oﬀering a resilient and robust environment conducive to applications based on object-oriented design. Additionally, JADE adheres to the speciﬁcations set forth by the Foundation for Intelligent Physical Agents (FIPA), facilitating communication between agents and enabling service detection within the system. FIPA has formulated speciﬁcations for interaction protocols, communicative acts, and content messages for agent communication, thereby supporting the potential extensibility of our proposed framework to incorporate additional functionalities in the future. Moreover, the web pages for our framework were developed using Java Server Pages (JSP) technology [39], and the card set database was implemented using MySQL Server [40].

4.2.1. Research Methodology. This section delineates the study conducted to assess the eﬃcacy of the proposed framework. The study adopted a quasiexperimental design [41], which facilitated the analysis of the inﬂuence of an independent variable (the FLL framework) on dependent variables (achievement of SLOs). Within this methodological framework, one of the experimental approaches was employed, entailing the establishment of both a control group and an experimental group. The sample size comprised 120 selected students, who were subsequently divided into two distinct groups. The experimental group was exposed to the independent variable (FLL framework), while this variable was intentionally withheld from the control group.

The advisor meticulously identiﬁed students who possessed the requisite computer and internet skills, which were deemed essential for the successful completion of the anticipated tasks. Additionally, eﬀorts were made to include students from diverse dialect communities within the participant pool. This deliberate inclusion was aimed at capturing a nuanced understanding of the proposed framework’s eﬃcacy across various linguistic backgrounds and signiﬁcantly contributed to enhancing the ecological validity of the study. Furthermore, the control group, which did not receive the FLL system, was also selected based on the recommendations of the student advisor. Consequently, the sample was divided into an experimental group and a control group, each comprising 60 students.
4.2.3. Instruments. The instruments utilized in this study encompass subject-completed assessments, namely, achievement tests and surveys. Achievement tests serve the purpose of evaluating an individual’s level of knowledge or proﬁciency within a speciﬁc subject area. They are traditionally administered in educational institutions to measure learning or the eﬀectiveness of instructional practices [42]. Our study employed quizzes and an evaluation test as achievement tests. Quizzes gauge students’ understanding during the learning process. For the experimental group, quizzes were integrated into the proposed system. Each card, excluding the prize card, provides learning material and a pertinent quiz involving a single multiple-choice question. Meanwhile, the control group underwent a parallel assessment process. Similar single multiple-choice questions were administered to students in the control group, but these assessments were delivered through the traditional method of paper-based quizzes. In addition, the evaluation of students’ overall achievement of learning outcomes was conducted at the culmination of each academic period. This evaluation consisted of a standardized test comprising 20 multiple-choice questions, which were thoughtfully crafted by the course instructor. Students were required to respond to these questions within a speciﬁed 30-min timeframe. Importantly, the mode of delivery for these achievement tests diverged for the two groups under investigation. Speciﬁcally, the experimental group underwent online testing, facilitated by the integration of the e-learning system, while the control group engaged in paper-based testing.
Another pivotal instrument involves the utilization of surveys. Surveys provide a structured and systematic methodology for gathering data from a broad and diverse range

of participants, thereby facilitating the simultaneous acquisition of quantitative and qualitative information. Thus, upon the culmination of the study, participants belonging to the experimental group were invited to complete an online survey employing the conventional 5-level Likert scale, which spans from strongly agree to strongly disagree. This survey encompasses two inquiries, each capturing students’ perspectives on the proposed framework from distinct vantage points: motivation and dialect variation. Recognizing the signiﬁcant role of motivation in FLL success [43], participants were queried regarding whether the framework positively inﬂuenced their motivation to enhance their linguistic proﬁciencies. Additionally, acknowledging the multifaceted nature of dialects within communities and the ongoing debate surrounding dialect inclusion in language learning systems [44], the second inquiry delves into the potential impact of integrating students’ native dialects within the proposed framework. Meanwhile, participants from the control group were presented with identical questions, albeit in a paper-based format, to ensure consistency in data collection procedures.
Moreover, at the study’s conclusion, an online survey was administered to six English subject instructors who served as coordinators for the experiment. The survey featured a single question aimed at evaluating their perspectives on the applicability and reliability of the proposed solution. This feedback from instructors complements the studentcentric evaluations, providing a comprehensive view of the framework’s eﬃcacy.
4.2.4. Data Collection Settings. The experiment took place during the second trimester of 2023, speciﬁcally divided into two periods: t1 and t2, each lasting 6 weeks and comprising ﬁve 50-min learning sessions. The ﬁrst period (t1) commenced at the beginning of the trimester on November 26th, while the second period (t2) started in the middle of the trimester on January 28th. The framework was tailored to deliver learning material and quizzes aligned with the English textbook taught during this trimester, utilizing the microlearning strategy. Throughout each period, close attention was given to monitoring the students’ achievement of two designated learning outcomes:
• Knowledge learning outcome (LO1): deﬁnition of basic features and terminology relevant to language skills.
• Skill learning outcome (LO2): development of proﬁciency in the four language skills for eﬀective communication.
The attainment of these outcomes played an integral role within the context of the English curriculum delivered during the trimester. During each period, participants underwent a series of quizzes and an evaluation test tailored to the speciﬁc content covered during that period. Additionally, during the 13th week of the study, participants in both groups engaged in the completion of a survey, encompassing dimensions such as motivation levels and considerations related to dialect variations. 4.2.5. Data Analysis. The progress of students in achieving learning outcomes LO1 and LO2 was assessed for both groups using the Continuous e-Assessment Method (CAM) algorithm, introduced in [10]. Primarily, CAM tracks and calculates students’ achievement of learning outcomes, identifying areas of weakness, and suggesting corrective measures to enhance attainment. In this study, CAM was exclusively employed to calculate students’ achievement of LO1 and LO2 during t1 and t2. The utilization of CAM aligns seamlessly with the primary objective of this study, which is the enhancement of the achievement of SLOs. Additionally, the quantitative data obtained from surveys underwent thorough analysis using Microsoft Excel [45]. The widespread availability and basic statistical analysis capabilities of Excel contribute to its prevalent use for diverse data analysis tasks. In addition, Excel’s charting and graphing tools were employed to create visual representations, aiding in the interpretation of trends and patterns. This analytical process involved meticulous examination and tabulation of participant responses.
4.2.6. Results. As a result of this experiment, our attention has been directed toward the following aspects:
1. The evaluation of students’ enhancement in achieving learning outcomes (direct assessment) in the experimental group and control group
2. The perceptions of participants regarding the proposed framework, with emphasis on motivation and dialectal variation
3. The opinions of instructors about the applicability and reliability of the framework
In the context of learning outcome achievement, the CAM algorithm was employed to compute individual students’ achievement throughout the experiment. Instructors provided comprehensive reports delineating students’ achievements in LO1 and LO2. Presented in Table 1 are the cumulative achievements of LO1 for each student in both the control and experimental groups during time periods t1 and t2. Notably, 72% of the participants in the experimental group exhibited an enhancement in their achievement of LO1 (positive increased rate [IR]), while 55% of the participants in the control group improved their achievement of LO1. Table 2 displays students’ achievements in LO2 during t1 and t2, revealing that 90% of the participants in the experimental group improved their attainment of LO2, while 53% of the participants in the control group enhanced their achievement of LO2.

The ﬁndings suggest that a majority of participants in the experimental group concurred that the framework acted as a catalyst for enhancing their motivation to learn a foreign language, thereby fostering increased enthusiasm. Conversely, participants in the control group, who utilized the traditional learning method, exhibited lower levels of motivation, as evidenced by their performance in Tables 1 and 2. However, dialectal variation emerged as a challenge for participants in the experimental group.
In order to gauge the applicability and reliability of the framework, an online survey was administered to a cohort of eight English subject instructors, encompassing a single inquiry. Table 4 encapsulates a concise overview of the outcomes. Approximately 86% of the instructors expressed satisfaction with the framework, attributing their positive sentiment to its integration of learning outcomes and the incorporation of a point-based gamiﬁcation aspect. This feature holds the potential to elevate students’ motivation and engagement within the educational environment. However, 14% of instructors voiced reservations, advocating against the sole reliance on this framework for language instruction and proposing its integration with traditional teaching methodologies. From the student perspective, instructors highlighted concerns about certain students lacking access to devices, internet connectivity, or technical proﬁciency, potentially hindering their ability to engage with e-learning systems. From the instructor’s viewpoint, it was emphasized that educators also require adequate training to navigate the technical intricacies of online learning. Moreover, dependency on such a framework for knowledge acquisition raises apprehensions about diminishing the primary role of teachers in imparting knowledge.
5. Discussion
This paper introduces a novel framework for learning foreign languages based on gamiﬁcation and card games. In contrast to existing approaches that primarily emphasize elevating student motivation while neglecting the enhancement of SLOs, our primary objective was to improve the achievement of SLOs. Drawing on the ﬁndings of the case study presented in Section 4.2.6, the proposed solution demonstrated a favorable inﬂuence on the attainment of SLOs. Furthermore, it has been instrumental in augmenting student motivation to persevere through the educational journey, thereby oﬀering a promising trajectory for the reﬁnement of language instruction. However, the path to eﬃcacious implementation necessitates a nuanced comprehension of the variations in dialectal representation. Moreover, instructors have expressed their endorsement of the use of our solution as a supportive adjunct to foreign language courses rather than exclusive reliance on it as the sole pedagogical tool for language acquisition. 6. Conclusion
Gamiﬁcation and card games are integral components of foreign language education, infusing the learning process with interactive and enjoyable elements. These educational tools not only serve to enhance student motivation but also oﬀer practical applications for language skills, thereby fostering a dynamic and engaging language learning experience. However, current gamiﬁed or card game–based language learning approaches often prioritize motivating students while overlooking the achievement of SLOs. This gap is addressed in this paper, presenting a novel framework for learning a foreign language based on gamiﬁcation and card games, aimed at enhancing the achievement of SLOs. The framework is developed based on agent technology and the microlearning strategy. The eﬃcacy of the framework underwent evaluation through a case study. The primary focus was on assessing the impact of the proposed solution on students’ achievement of learning outcomes. Additionally, a comprehensive evaluation of the framework was conducted, considering aspects such as motivation, dialect diversity, and applicability.
