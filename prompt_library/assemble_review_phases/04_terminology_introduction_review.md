# Review First Use of Concepts, Terms, and Acronyms

This prompt can run without conversation history. Resolve `PROJECT_ROOT` and
read the live baseline, runtime manifest, shared contract,
`stage_3_heading_framing_draft.md`, metadata schema, explicit values, working ledgers, and
current audits.

Its purpose is to ensure that a library-science reader encounters every
specialized term with enough explanation to understand its later use.

## Blocking Structural Rule

Before and after editing, compare every H1-H6 heading with the live baseline.
Do not change heading wording, level, count, or order.

## Runtime Terminology Inventory

Scan the manuscript in reading order and build a first-use inventory. Include:

- acronyms and initialisms;
- named organizations, standards, frameworks, and professional bodies;
- specialized library, game-studies, education, technology, and research
  terminology;
- concepts whose ordinary-language meaning differs from their scholarly use;
- program forms or technologies likely to be unfamiliar outside their
  subfield.

Examples include, but are not limited to, GBLS, ACRL, LIS, TTRPG, LARP, VR,
AR, DRM, RDA, MARC, OLAC, DCRM, CPD, “third place,” “affinity space,”
“procedural rhetoric,” “gamification,” “participatory culture,” and
“preparation for future learning.” Discover the actual inventory at runtime;
do not treat this list as exhaustive.

## First-Use Rules

1. Spell out an acronym or initialism at first use and place the abbreviation
   in parentheses if it will recur.
2. Define a specialized concept immediately before or at first substantive use.
3. Identify an organization or standard by full name and briefly state why it
   matters when that relevance is not self-evident.
4. Distinguish related terms that the manuscript later compares, such as game,
   play, and gamification or virtual reality and augmented reality.
5. Do not define common terms unnecessarily or overload the manuscript with
   glossary-style asides.
6. Do not introduce an abbreviation used only once unless it improves clarity.
7. Preserve historically or theoretically distinctive attribution where the
   concept is associated with a particular source.

Search from the beginning of the manuscript for each term. A later definition
does not cure an unexplained earlier use. Headings count as first use.
