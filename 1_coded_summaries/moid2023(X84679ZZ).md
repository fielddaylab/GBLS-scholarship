### Summary
# **Moid, A., Raza, M. M., Javed, M., & Jahan, K. (2023). Role of digital humanities in libraries: Mapping gamification gimmicks. *DESIDOC Journal of Library & Information Technology, 43*(4), 249-257. https://doi.org/10.14429/djlit.43.4.19233**

Moid, Raza, Javed, and Jahan provide a bibliometric overview of gamification research, with a smaller discussion of its relevance to libraries and digital humanities. The article defines gamification in familiar terms as the incorporation of game mechanics, game thinking, feedback, goals, badges, point systems, leaderboards, and user levels into non-game activities. Its library claim is broad: gamification can attract users' attention, engage them in game-like library activities, influence user behavior, and make learning more engaging. The article also links gamification to digital humanities tools and to gamified e-library services, though the digital humanities connection remains more contextual than deeply analyzed.

The study uses Web of Science data from 2013 through February 25, 2023. The authors searched the topic field for "Gamification*" and, after applying an English-language filter, analyzed 4,778 records with R, RStudio, Bibliometrix, and Biblioshiny. Their indicators include relevant sources, authors, globally cited documents, affiliations, country scientific production, three-field plots, word growth, keyword plus, word clouds, and co-occurrence networks. In other words, the article maps the research field around gamification rather than studying a specific library program, user group, or learning outcome.

The broad bibliometric findings show a rapidly expanded research area. The dataset includes 1,564 sources, 13,861 authors, and more than 136,000 references. The highest annual record count was in 2021, followed closely by 2022, while 2013 had the smallest count. The most productive sources are outside librarianship, with journals such as *Sustainability*, *Computers in Human Behavior*, *JMIR Serious Games*, and *Frontiers in Psychology* prominent in the results. Hamari appears as the most productive author, and the most globally cited documents include work by Seaborn, Dominguez, Hanus, and Koivisto. The United States, Spain, and China lead in country production, while the University of Pennsylvania, University of Granada, and other large research institutions appear among the most relevant affiliations.

For the LIS slice, Moid et al. report approximately 200 documents related to library and information science within the broader gamification dataset. They identify an increasing publication pattern since 2013, with a peak around 2017 in their LIS subset, and note sources such as the *International Journal of Information Management*, *Information Technology & People*, *European Journal of Information Systems*, and *Information & Management*. The article concludes that gamification became especially visible during the pandemic period and that tools such as interactive quizzes, digital badges, and virtual escape rooms can help attract patrons and popularize information products.

The source's usefulness for GBLS is mostly contextual. It supports the claim that gamification is a large interdisciplinary research area rather than a niche library fad, and it shows that LIS scholarship has engaged the term, though on a much smaller scale than education, psychology, business, health, and information systems. It also names common library-facing examples: orientation, websites, databases, education, virtual escape rooms, coding clubs, badges, quizzes, and interactive storytelling. These examples align with other sources in the review that treat gamification as a design logic for engagement, progress, motivation, and instruction.

At the same time, the article should be used cautiously. It sometimes treats gamification, game-based learning, digital humanities, and gamified e-library services as overlapping concepts without fully separating them. Its statements about libraries are largely drawn from prior literature and broad claims rather than from new empirical evidence about library patrons or programs. The bibliometric search is also broad enough that most of the analyzed records are not library-specific. For GBLS, then, Moid et al. are best used as a field-mapping source: evidence that gamification discourse has grown, that LIS is participating in that discourse, and that library-oriented gamification needs more precise definitions and stronger program-level assessment.
