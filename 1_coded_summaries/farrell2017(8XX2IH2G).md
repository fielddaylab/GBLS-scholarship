### Summary
# **Farrell, S. L., Neeser, A. E., & Bishoff, C. (2017). Academic uses of video games: A qualitative assessment of research and teaching needs at a large research university. *College & Research Libraries, 78*(5), 675-700. https://doi.org/10.5860/crl.78.5.675**

Farrell, Neeser, and Bishoff provide one of the strongest empirical needs assessments for academic-library video game services. Working at the University of Minnesota Twin Cities, a large doctoral research university with no dedicated game design department, the authors asked how scholars across disciplines used video games in research and teaching and what information and technology needs the library might support. They identified 92 faculty, staff, and graduate students who appeared to use video games or game technology academically, then conducted 30 semistructured interviews. The interview sample included arts and humanities, social sciences, STEM, and health sciences.

The study found that video games were distributed across the university rather than concentrated in one department. Twenty-eight of 30 interviewees used games in research, and 23 used games in teaching; 21 used games in both. Research uses fell into five categories: development of games or game technology, games as instrumentation, games as objects of study, games as influences on people or society, and games as educational technology. Teaching uses included designing games for classes, teaching game design, using games as course material, and discussing games, game mechanics, or the instructor's game-related research in class. Every disciplinary group used games as course material or as discussion material.

The article is especially valuable because it complicates assumptions about what an academic game collection should contain. Existing library game collections often emphasize console games and commercially successful titles, but the interview data showed that PC games were nearly as important as all other game types combined and that PCs, software, peripherals, programming languages, web applications, servers, and game design tools also mattered. Arts and humanities and social sciences researchers had stronger needs for games as primary sources or course materials; STEM and health sciences participants reported stronger software and peripheral needs. Health sciences uses could involve Wii balance boards or other peripherals as low-cost instrumentation rather than play in the usual sense.

Information needs were also broader than standard library resources. Interviewees used journals, Google Scholar, web sources, colleagues, books, datasets, interviews, game manuals, trade magazines, streaming video, and other nontraditional sources. Colleagues were the single most common information source, particularly in STEM and health sciences, and many interviewees collaborated across departments. The authors argue that libraries can act as connectors across disciplinary boundaries through events, experimental technology spaces, shared collections, guides, and liaison coordination.

Access and equity are central to the findings. Graduate students often relied on personal collections, borrowed technology, free tools, or out-of-pocket purchases, while faculty and staff more often used grants, departmental funds, startup funds, or other institutional resources. Arts and humanities and social sciences graduate students were especially likely to pay personally for games or related materials needed for research. The authors therefore argue that library access to games and game technology could reduce disciplinary funding barriers and support graduate students whose work treats games as primary research materials analogous to texts.

For GBLS, this article shifts the academic-library game conversation from "Should libraries have games?" to "Which local scholarly practices should game services support?" Its recommendations are needs-based and institutional: conduct local assessment, recognize cross-disciplinary networks, build collections and services around actual research and teaching uses, collect PC games as well as console games, consider peripherals and software, support students in classes that require games, and help graduate students locate funding. The study is limited to one campus and does not include students taking game-related courses, but it provides a strong model for needs assessment and evidence-based academic game collection development.
