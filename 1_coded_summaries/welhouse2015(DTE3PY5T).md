### Summary
# **Welhouse, Z., Lee, J. H., & Bancroft, J. (2015). "What am I fighting for?": Creating a controlled vocabulary for video game plot metadata. *Cataloging & Classification Quarterly, 53*(2), 157-189. https://doi.org/10.1080/01639374.2014.963776**

Welhouse, Lee, and Bancroft develop a controlled vocabulary for video game plot metadata as part of the University of Washington and Seattle Interactive Media Museum video game metadata schema work. The article begins from a cataloging problem that is directly relevant to libraries: video games are cultural and research objects, but existing organizational systems often describe them through platform, title, publisher, price, and broad genre while giving little reliable access to what a game is "really about." Commercial sites, fan summaries, Steam tags, WorldCat summaries, and Library of Congress Subject Headings may supply useful fragments, but the authors argue that none provide consistent, standardized, plot-level access for discovery, browsing, collocation, advisory, or scholarship.

The article is grounded in user warrant. Earlier GAMER/SIMM work identified personas including game players, parents, collectors, scholars, developers, librarians/curators, teachers, and industry professionals, and this article uses that broader research to justify plot/narrative as a metadata field. In interviews with 56 users, 21 mentioned story, plot, or narrative as important, often as one of their top factors, while also noting that there was no effective way to search games by it. A later survey of 1,257 participants ranked "Plot/Narrative" as the seventh most useful metadata element, with positive responses from 71 percent of participants. For libraries, this user evidence matters because plot access could support readers' advisory-like game advisory, parent mediation, curricular matching, thematic displays, exhibits, and research into game stories over time.

The method combines domain analysis and card sorting. The authors collected candidate terms from RPGs, instruction books, in-game text, package descriptions, fan-created descriptions, game websites, wikis, encyclopedias, and other game-related sources. They then reduced and organized a large term list using criteria of accuracy, understandability, and comprehensiveness. The article also reviews literary plot classification systems, including appeal elements, folktale motifs, dramatic situations, and "basic plots," but finds that these systems are often too broad, too fine-grained, too literary, or too difficult for catalogers to apply consistently to games.

Their main design contribution is a pair structure inspired by a faceted approach. Each plot statement combines a controlled VERB and OBJECT, with an implied SUBJECT of the player-character or characters. Examples include seeking treasure, escaping a dystopia, restoring order, developing friendship, confronting self, preventing disaster, discovering intrigue, and saving the world. Limiting records to three plot pairs discourages cataloging every side quest while pushing catalogers beyond a single generic statement such as "characters save world." In the sample records, the structure allows games within the same genre, especially RPGs, to be collocated by plot elements that would otherwise be hidden by broad genre labels.

For GBLS, the article strengthens the review's cataloging and discovery thread. It shows that game collections need metadata that supports how patrons actually seek, compare, and interpret games, not only how vendors package them. Plot metadata can help a librarian connect a game to novels, films, nonfiction, class topics, or patron interests; it can help a teacher pair a game with literary themes; and it can help researchers ask cultural questions about recurring stories in games. In this sense, game-based library service includes not only events and play programs but also the infrastructure that makes games findable, recommendable, and intellectually legible.

The limitations are also important for the GBLS review. Plot metadata introduces hard cataloging questions about granularity, optional side quests, nonlinear narratives, moral choices, games with thin or no plot, and the amount of game knowledge required from catalogers. The article also names practical concerns around spoilers, the need for free-text summary fields, and possible crowdsourced description to supplement professional cataloging. These problems are useful because they show that game metadata is not a simple matter of adding more tags; it requires local policy, interface choices, user testing, domain expertise, and perhaps collaboration with game communities.

Overall, Welhouse et al. are best used as foundational evidence for video game cataloging, metadata, and advisory rather than as a program outcome study. The article does not evaluate a library service intervention, but it gives the GBLS project a concrete vocabulary for why discovery systems matter: richer game metadata can turn video games from loosely described entertainment objects into searchable cultural materials that support access, interpretation, curriculum, preservation, and library-mediated recommendation.
