### Summary
# **Dahya, N., Lee, J. H., Lee, K. J., King, W. E., Goel, M., & Yassin, H. (2019). *Virtual reality in public libraries*. University of Washington Information School. https://ischool.uw.edu/vrinlibraries**

Dahya, Lee, Lee, King, Goel, and Yassin's report presents the broader Washington State public-library VR project that later informs Lee, King, Dahya, and Lee (2020). It is worth summarizing separately because the report includes patron survey and interview findings, site observations, demographics, and practice recommendations that are only partly visible in the later article. The project was funded by Washington State Libraries, the Office of the Secretary of State, and Oculus, and studied VR programming at seven public-library sites in Washington, including urban, regional, and rural locations.

The study asked what happens when public libraries offer access to VR as a tool for informal learning and community engagement. Each library designed its own programming, while Oculus provided a suggested list of freely available educational games and experiences. The research team interviewed 23 librarians at the start and end of the project, producing 36 librarian interviews; observed library programs from March through June 2018; collected 185 patron surveys; and interviewed 39 patrons. Approximately 400 patrons experienced VR during the study period, with some returning up to five times.

The report distinguishes between expectations about VR and actual library experiences. Patrons and librarians imagined VR as a tool for learning, empathy, engagement, accessibility, schoolwork, therapy, job training, and safe practice. Yet many of these aspirations were not directly tested through the short drop-in VR experiences actually available in the libraries. Patrons reported intense immersion, interactivity, curiosity, and excitement, but the report cautions that patrons often learned more about VR technology itself than about the content of a particular VR experience. If content learning is the goal, the authors argue, librarians need clear learning outcomes, structured teaching processes, and more deliberate curriculum or pedagogy.

The patron findings complicate simple access narratives. Survey respondents overwhelmingly enjoyed VR, with 99% reporting enjoyment and 84% saying they really enjoyed it; 69% were first-time users; 97% reported learning about VR technology; and 78% reported learning about the experience's content. However, 81.6% of survey respondents reported playing video games, and boys, men, and patrons already comfortable with games were often more likely to try VR. Girls who tried VR enjoyed it, but fewer expressed initial interest. Most patrons using VR were White, though the Tukwila site showed strong participation from its non-White regular patron base. The report therefore treats equitable access as a design problem, not an automatic consequence of putting expensive hardware in a library.

The implementation findings are highly practical. Librarians needed significant lead time to learn the hardware, explore software, plan programming, update equipment, staff events, and manage waivers, especially for youth. The Oculus age restriction disappointed tweens and required alternate activities while siblings or friends used VR. Space mattered: public setups could recruit curious patrons because people saw others using VR, while private rooms reduced self-consciousness and allowed wraparound activities such as Ozobots, Perler beads, slime, 3D pens, and coloring. Promotion also mattered. Posters and web announcements were less effective than themed events, fairs, personal invitations, and clear explanations of why VR might matter locally.

For the larger GBLS project, the report is a strong reminder that VR is not just a technology-access service. It is also a social, spatial, pedagogical, equity, and facilitation problem. Even single-user VR became social when spectators watched the mirrored screen, coached the participant, reacted together, or began conversations with librarians. At the same time, some patrons felt watched or isolated while wearing the headset, and some experienced dizziness, nausea, fear, or disorientation. The report's strongest advice is to connect VR to broader library programs and local interests, build wraparound activities, offer a mix of educational and entertaining content, and move toward creation in VR rather than only consumption.

The report's limitations are useful to state. The study did not show strong evidence that short VR drop-ins produced empathy, deep content learning, or broad social-equity outcomes, despite participants' hopes. Its evidence is strongest for early access, enjoyment, social interaction around the headset, operational lessons, and questions for future programming. That makes it a foundational GBLS source for VR implementation because it resists both hype and dismissal: public libraries can make VR available in meaningful ways, but only with intentional design, outreach, staffing, space, accessibility support, and definitions of value beyond novelty.
