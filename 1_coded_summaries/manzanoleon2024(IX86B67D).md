### Summary
# **Manzano-León, A., Rodríguez-Rivera, P., Raposo-Rivas, M., & Rodríguez-Ferrer, J. M. (2024). Digital game-based learning for transgender identity awareness: A qualitative study in Spanish social education degree. *Current Psychology, 43*(20), 18065-18073. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12144-023-05556-3**

Manzano-León and colleagues examine digital game-based learning as a way to promote transgender and gender diverse (TGD) identity awareness among undergraduate social education students in Spain. The article is situated in higher education, social education, gender diversity, and educational technology rather than library services. Its premise is that social education students need experiential, reflective ways to understand social realities such as discrimination, family rejection, gender identity, and social justice, and that a narrative video game can make those issues more concrete than lecture-based instruction alone.

The intervention used *A Normal Lost Phone*, a puzzle game presented through the interface of a found mobile phone. Players explore messages, email, web history, and hidden applications to reconstruct the story of Sam/Samira, a transgender and bisexual young person negotiating family conflict, romantic relationships, friendship, identity, secrecy, and the decision to leave home for a safer life in another city. The authors selected the game because of its trans-friendly perspective, low cost, accessibility, and capacity to place players inside a first-person investigation of another person's social world.

The study took place in a mandatory second-year Social Skills in Social Education course during the 2022/2023 academic year. Thirty-five students participated voluntarily out of a class of 42, most of them women and most around age 20. The activity occupied two two-hour practical class sessions. Students played cooperatively in teams of four to six, prepared a critical essay, and then participated in five voluntary focus groups lasting about 40 minutes each. Two external observers also conducted participant observation. The authors analyzed focus-group and observation data thematically in ATLAS.ti, using independent coding by two researchers and a third researcher to resolve disagreements.

Results are organized around three main themes: narrative and gameplay elements within digital game-based learning, training through the video game, and raising awareness toward TGD people. Students described the game as motivating, fun, creative, and useful for learning; 97.3% of focus-group participants expressed high interest in learning and willingness to participate actively. They also connected the activity to professional social education training, especially the need to understand prejudice, communicate about difficult social realities, and design engaging interventions for affected communities. The game appeared especially strong as an empathy and awareness mechanism: students reported that it helped them experience, internalize, and discuss the social reality of TGD people, including family conflict, concealment, harassment, and the importance of support networks.

The authors conclude that video games may be attractive and effective tools for fostering learning, reflection, sensitivity, empathy, and critical thinking about complex issues such as gender identity and sexual diversity. They also emphasize important limits. The sample was small, drawn from one university course, mostly women in a narrow age range, and self-selected into a voluntary workshop. The study did not collect pre/post quantitative measures, did not test long-term effects, and cannot show whether the workshop changed attitudes beyond students' retrospective accounts. The findings are therefore best read as exploratory qualitative evidence about student receptivity and perceived learning.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful as indirect design background for inclusive game programming but not as direct GBLS evidence. A library could plausibly adapt similar narrative games for social justice programming, student affairs partnerships, staff development, book/game discussion groups, or community conversations about identity and belonging. The study also reinforces a GBLS design principle: game selection matters, because narrative perspective, cooperative play, facilitated reflection, and post-play discussion can turn a commercial game into a structured learning encounter. However, the article does not study libraries, librarians, library users, collections, circulation, information literacy, or public programming. It should therefore remain outside the main review except as broader educational-game context for inclusive and empathy-oriented design.
