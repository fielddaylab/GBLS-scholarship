### Summary
# **Baltezarević, R., & Baltezarević, I. (2025). Digital game-based learning's (DGBL) effect on students' academic performance. *International Journal of Cognitive Research in Science, Engineering and Education (IJCRSEE), 13*(1), 127-140. https://doi.org/10.23947/2334-8496-2025-13-1-127-140**

Baltezarević and Baltezarević examine participants' attitudes about digital game-based learning (DGBL), academic performance, artificial intelligence, personalization, motivation, rewards, feedback, and competition. The article is framed as educational technology research rather than library and information science. Its central claim is that DGBL can make learning more complex, adaptive, motivating, and aligned with how students engage contemporary digital environments, especially when combined with AI-supported personalization and immersive technologies.

Much of the article is a literature review of DGBL affordances and risks. The authors describe digital educational games as environments that can combine course objectives with simulation, feedback, reward systems, progression, autonomy, experimentation, problem solving, competition, collaboration, and multisensory interaction. They also discuss possible drawbacks, including screen time, distraction, technical cost, lack of pedagogical fit, addiction concerns, cybersecurity threats, privacy questions, and AI bias. The future-facing portions of the article emphasize AI-driven adaptation, player analytics, automated feedback, game difficulty balancing, chatbots, virtual worlds, augmented reality, and virtual reality.

The empirical component used an emailed questionnaire at Megatrend University in Belgrade. The authors sent the questionnaire to 350 addresses belonging to students at all three levels of study as well as professors and administrative staff; 328 complete responses were analyzed. Respondents included 186 men and 142 women, with ages distributed from 18 to 65 and educational levels ranging from elementary school to doctoral study. The instrument collected demographic information and included 15 Likert-type statements about DGBL in educational settings. Eight variables were selected for the core subscale, which had high internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = .913). Analyses were conducted in SPSS using descriptive statistics, correlation analysis, scale reliability analysis, Spearman's rho, Pearson's r, Gamma, and chi-square tests.

The study tested four hypotheses about perceived relationships among DGBL statements. The authors report strong positive correlations between beliefs that digital gaming should be part of learning content and beliefs that digital games contribute to learning effectiveness; between dynamic DGBL environments and motivation; and between AI-supported personalization and dynamic adaptation to each student's performance. They report a moderate-to-strong positive correlation between rewards/feedback/competition and perceived improvement in learning outcomes. All four hypotheses were treated as confirmed. The conclusion states that participants believe DGBL can increase learning efficiency, motivation, participation, and outcomes, especially when supported by rewards, feedback, competition, and AI personalization.

The article's main limitation for evidence use is that it measures attitudes about DGBL rather than learning outcomes produced by a specific intervention. Although the title refers to academic performance, the study does not compare grades, test results, completion rates, or observed performance before and after a DGBL experience. It also combines students, faculty, and administrative personnel in the respondent pool, making it difficult to isolate learner experience. The findings are therefore best understood as a snapshot of favorable perceptions about DGBL and AI personalization, not as direct evidence that DGBL improved achievement.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful only as broad design background. Its discussion of feedback, rewards, competition, personalization, social interaction, cybersecurity, privacy, cost, and pedagogical alignment can inform how libraries evaluate game-based learning tools or digital programming partnerships. It also reinforces the need to distinguish enthusiasm for DGBL from demonstrated service outcomes. However, the article does not study libraries, librarians, library users, collections, public programming, information literacy, access services, or community learning. It should therefore remain outside the main GBLS review except as general educational-game context.
