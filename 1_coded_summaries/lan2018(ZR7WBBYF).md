### Summary
# **Lan, Y.-J., Botha, A., Shang, J., & Jong, M. S.-Y. (2018). Guest editorial: Technology enhanced contextual game-based language learning. *Educational Technology & Society, 21*(3), 86-89.**

Lan, Botha, Shang, and Jong introduce a special issue on technology-enhanced contextual game-based language learning. The editorial argues that games can support second-language learning when they require language input and output across linguistic form, semantic meaning, and pragmatic use. It situates game-based language learning within contextual, experiential, and sociocultural learning traditions, emphasizing authentic environments, meaningful social interaction, scaffolding, and low-stress opportunities to practice a second language.

The editorial's main function is to frame the special issue rather than report an original study. It describes how technology can extend language-learning contexts beyond conventional classrooms through computer-mediated communication, mobile devices, augmented reality, and 3D virtual environments. It then summarizes ten accepted papers on mobile language games, gamified interactive response systems, flipped contextual game-based learning, augmented paper interfaces for Chinese character learning, competitive vocabulary games, Kinect-supported English learning, MMORPG-based English role play, a contextual game-based system called PlanetAdventure, game translation as fandom literacy, and 3D virtual environments for special education students with language delay.

The source is relevant to game-based learning broadly, especially where a later project might need background on digital games, virtual worlds, contextual learning, language learning, or transfer between formal learning and real-life application. It also shows how game-based learning research often examines motivation, anxiety, self-efficacy, learning performance, learner behavior, and design principles across specific educational domains.

For the GBLS literature review, however, the source should be explicitly excluded. It does not discuss libraries, librarians, library instruction, information literacy, library collections, public programming, cultural heritage, access to games, game advisory, metadata, or any library service context. Although language learning and literacy can matter to libraries, this editorial is centered on classroom and educational technology research rather than game-based library services. Including it would broaden the review from GBLS into general educational game-based learning without adding library-specific evidence.
