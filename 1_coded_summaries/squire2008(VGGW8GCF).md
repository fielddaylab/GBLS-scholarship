## Summary

Squire reviews early video-game research to define game literacy as a form of expertise rather than a simple ability to decode a fixed text. He organizes the chapter around three connected objects of study: games as designed artifacts, game playing as a social practice, and the implications of both for learning and schooling. Across these domains, he argues that video games are experiential and interactive media in which players participate in and partly co-create simulated worlds. Game literacy is the developing capacity to design rewarding experiences for oneself within a game's semiotic and rule systems, with expertise judged through culturally situated practices rather than a single universal rubric.

The artifact-focused discussion emphasizes that a game does not exist as a stable experience apart from player action. Rules, representations, narrative, space, time, and player choice interact to generate changing game states. This makes conventional assumptions about authorship and interpretation inadequate: two players may inhabit very different versions of the same designed system. Squire treats the narratology-ludology divide as unhelpfully reductive because games join story, symbols, agency, play, and rule-governed action. Games can be understood as spaces that players explore and contest, dynamic systems that evolve over time, and cycles of choices and consequences through which players form goals, act, receive feedback, and revise their strategies.

The chapter then moves from games to gaming cultures. Participation includes mentoring, strategy discussion, collaborative problem solving, producing guides and modifications, circulating multimodal texts, and negotiating what counts as legitimate expertise. Communities such as Apolyton University illustrate how players can move from consumers to producers and even influence game design. In these self-organizing environments, information gains authority through its usefulness for action rather than through institutional distance. Squire argues that game literacy therefore includes not only interpreting representations but also navigating social systems, producing meanings in multiple forms, using and critiquing digital spaces, and following trajectories into journalism, criticism, design, or other cultural work.

For schooling, the contrast is sharp. Game cultures often permit risk, failure, divergent pathways, peer teaching, and meaningful production, while formal classrooms commonly position texts and teachers as authorities and student work as disposable after grading. Squire reviews design-based educational projects in which students play, critique, and design games, including work with historical role-playing games and competitive design pitches. These projects can connect game play to historical inquiry, media criticism, careers, and production, but he warns that school programs have generally not recreated the participatory cultures surrounding games. Treating games as motivational wrappers or isolated software misses the social practices that make them powerful.

For the larger GBLS project, the chapter supports treating games as cultural media that require distinct literacies, advisory knowledge, and interpretive support. Libraries can provide access not only to game objects but also to communities, documentation, criticism, design tools, and pathways from play to production. A library game-literacy program would therefore combine playing, critical interpretation, discussion of rules and representations, and opportunities to make or modify media. The chapter also strengthens the case for facilitation: newcomers need help reading systems, locating relevant texts and communities, and recognizing possible identities within game culture. At the same time, libraries should avoid presuming that participation itself guarantees critical literacy or equitable access; which forms of expertise are legitimized, and whose interpretations and production pathways are supported, remain institutional choices.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

**Category mismatch:** The metadata schema has terms for literacy, community, play, and game creation, but it cannot directly represent `participatory_culture`, `affinity_space`, `expertise`, `identity_trajectory`, or the movement from media consumption to production that structures Squire's argument. **Proposal:** consider adding `participatory_culture`, `identity`, and `expertise` to `Theme`, while reserving `game_creation` for the format of an actual intervention rather than using it to code a theoretical account of production.

**Conceptual disagreement:** The source defines literacy as situated performance within rule systems and communities, not primarily as critical reading, information evaluation, or technical competence. This exceeds the current outline's separate treatments of information literacy, games as cultural media, and participatory creation. **Proposal:** preserve the distinction among these service areas but explicitly describe game literacy as spanning system interpretation, social participation, and production.

**Outline tension:** The chapter crosses `Game Studies Debates Relevant to Libraries`, `Instruction, Orientation, and Information Literacy`, and `Game Design Workshops, Jams, and Participatory Creation`. Treating any one as its sole home would lose the argument that artifact, practice, and production are interdependent. **Proposal:** use cross-references among these sections rather than creating an isolated definition of game literacy.

## Evidence Cautions and Limitations

This is a broad theoretical literature review, not a systematic review or a new empirical study. Its examples come from an early period of online games and educational game research, and several claims depend on illustrative studies whose methods and populations must be checked in their original publications. The transfer from school-based game-literacy projects to libraries is an interpretation for GBLS, not a finding of this chapter. The attached manuscript and Zotero record are dated 2007 and use a running-head title, while the identifiable published chapter appeared in 2008 under the title cited above.
