### Summary
# **Al-Hafdi, F. S., & Alhalafawy, W. S. (2024). Ten years of gamification-based learning: A bibliometric analysis and systematic review. *International Journal of Interactive Mobile Technologies (iJIM), 18*(7), 188-212. https://doi.org/10.3991/ijim.v18i07.45335**

Al-Hafdi and Alhalafawy present a two-part mapping of gamification-based learning in education: a bibliometric analysis of Web of Science records from 2012 to 2021 and a systematic review of 52 selected studies. Their purpose is to identify the dominant terms, countries, journals, authors, growth patterns, educational stages, game elements, and measured outcomes in gamification-based learning. The article treats gamification as the use of game elements and game design technology in non-game educational contexts, especially e-learning environments.

The bibliometric portion frames gamification as a rapidly expanding educational technology field. The authors report that the most common keywords include gamification, game-based learning, motivation, engagement, education, games, serious games, virtual reality, Moodle, Kahoot, social media platforms, students, e-learning, teaching, technology, and learning systems. They identify the United States, Spain, Britain, China, Germany, Australia, Italy, and Canada as major publication centers, with the United States leading by volume. They also name *Computers in Human Behavior* and *Computers & Education* as especially influential venues and identify Juho Hamari, Mitesh Patel, and Richard Landers as prominent authors in the mapped literature.

The article's growth finding is one of its clearest contributions. Within the search frame, gamification-based learning records increased from seven studies in 2012 to 917 studies in 2021. Al-Hafdi and Alhalafawy interpret this growth as evidence that gamification has become a vital and rapidly developing research area, especially because of its promise for performance, motivation, engagement, and active participation. Their discussion repeatedly connects gamification with educational theories such as goal-setting theory, self-efficacy theory, self-determination theory, social comparison theory, flow theory, and operant conditioning.

The systematic-review portion is more focused on instructional design. The authors report that university and primary education are the most common stages in the selected studies, while secondary and intermediate education appear less frequently. The game elements most often used are points, leaderboards, and badges, followed by rewards, levels, challenges, progress bars, goals, narration, selection, avatars or virtual characters, forums, time, stars, and other digital stimuli. The measured learning outcomes most often addressed are achievement, performance, engagement, and motivation, with satisfaction, attitudes, competence, stimulation, awareness, psychological well-being, readiness, decision-making, cognitive load, independence, and related outcomes appearing less often.

For the larger GBLS project, the article offers useful background vocabulary for distinguishing gamification from fuller game-based library services. It helps explain why points, badges, leaderboards, levels, feedback, progress tracking, rewards, competition, and challenge structures appear frequently in educational platform design. It also reinforces an important caution already present in the GBLS review: evidence about gamified e-learning can support general claims about motivation and engagement, but it should not be treated as direct evidence for library gaming programs, tabletop play, circulating game collections, TTRPG services, VR labs, game jams, or other library-specific service models.

The article is therefore adjacent rather than directly applicable. It does not study libraries, librarians, public-library programming, school-library instruction, academic-library information literacy, game collections, library game metadata, community gaming, virtual library worlds, or patrons' experiences with game-based library services. Its bibliometric dataset is education-wide, and its systematic review focuses on experimental or quasi-experimental educational gamification studies, not library practice. Because `review.md` already includes more directly relevant library sources on gamification, including Seay, Downie and Proulx, Maranchak and Maranchak, Capdarest-Arest et al., and Moid et al., adding this article would likely duplicate broad gamification background without strengthening the GBLS evidence base.
