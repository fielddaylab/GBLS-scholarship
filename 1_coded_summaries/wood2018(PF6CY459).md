### Summary
# **Wood, K., & Carter, D. S. (2018). Art and technology: Archiving video games for humanities research in university libraries. *Art Libraries Journal, 43*(4), 185-195. https://doi.org/10.1017/alj.2018.29**

Wood and Carter argue that video games have become significant cultural, technological, and artistic objects that require collection, curation, preservation, and access in university libraries. The article combines a literature review on academic video game collections with a case study of the University of Michigan's Computer & Video Game Archive (CVGA), one of the largest academic game archives of its kind. For GBLS, its value lies in showing how game collections can support humanities research, pedagogy, visual analysis, art history, film/media studies, game studies, and interdisciplinary scholarship, not only recreation.

The article opens historically by linking early computer program libraries and the circulation of *SPACEWAR* in 1962. The point is that games and library-like systems for storing, testing, cataloging, and sharing software co-evolved from the beginning of computing culture. Wood and Carter then situate contemporary video games as transmedial objects: games can move across boards, cartridges, disks, consoles, computers, handheld devices, servers, downloads, phones, tablets, and virtual reality environments. This transmedial state makes game collection and cataloging difficult because the "same" game may depend on specific hardware, software, interface, network, carrier, and control conditions.

The literature review emphasizes that university libraries collect games for several overlapping reasons: faculty research, classroom instruction, student curricular needs, entertainment, game design programs, historical preservation, and access to culturally important media. The authors cite selection criteria that include technical merit, aesthetic quality, gameplay, educational value, maintenance, ease of use, cost, language, teaching relevance, real-world skills, historical value, and cultural value. They also note persistent issues such as violent or sexual content, censorship concerns, digital rights management, online games, company partnerships, and the need for collaboration across libraries when access barriers exceed one institution's capacity.

The University of Michigan CVGA case gives the article its concrete service model. Opened in 2008, the CVGA housed nearly 8,000 games from more than seventy systems by 2018, along with board and card games, tabletop role-playing games, books, player guides, game novelizations, graphic novels, gaming magazines, secondary sources, and ephemera. In 2017 it welcomed nearly 5,000 visitors and recorded use of more than 16,000 collection items. Users can browse games on shelves and play them at gaming stations, including space for motion and VR gaming and tables for board games. Because the CVGA maintains both software and hardware, the collection is limited to in-house use and is staffed during open hours.

The preservation discussion is especially useful for GBLS infrastructure. A representative game archive must manage cartridges, cassette tapes, magnetic disks, optical discs, digital downloads, consoles, handhelds, computers, controllers, and other access equipment. Some items are difficult to acquire in good condition, especially microcomputer games from the 1970s and early 1980s that circulated through flea markets, plastic bags, magazines, or hand-typed code. Some media are more fragile than others: cartridges may be robust, while each cassette load may risk failure. The CVGA chose not to take a "white glove" approach; instead, it deliberately allows games to be played and experienced by users, accepting wear as the cost of access. Mitigation strategies include archival sleeves, basic repair skills, and caches of replacement systems and controllers.

The mission and use cases show why university game archives matter for research. The CVGA supports teaching, class-directed research, independent research, and open recreational use. Courses and projects draw from art, music, history, literature, education, psychology, computer science, engineering, language instruction, and game design. Examples include studies of race and ethnicity in the *FIFA* series, race options in character generation across role-playing games, time in *The Legend of Zelda*, games about historical trauma and the American Civil War, video game music for non-music majors, samurai representation in games, programming inspiration for game-creation courses, engineering research using racing games, and board games for instructional English practice.

Wood and Carter also make a humanities argument for game libraries. They position game studies as a multidisciplinary field shaped by questions from anthropology, history, textual studies, computer science, sociology, media studies, cinema studies, design studies, visual and material culture, and art history. The article connects library collections to debates over ludology and narratology, race and representation, aesthetics, politics, avant-garde games, contemporary art, museum exhibitions, and game images as research objects. They argue that libraries can shape the field by providing subject guides, co-housing games with exhibition catalogs and game-studies journals, and partnering with visual resource centers to include still and video captures from games in image databases with consistent metadata.

For the larger GBLS project, Wood and Carter strengthen the review's claim that games are cultural media and research infrastructure. A game archive is not only a storage room or recreation lab; it is a space where hardware, software, staff expertise, cataloging, repair, preservation, visual resources, related scholarship, classroom support, and public access converge. The article also clarifies the tension at the center of game preservation: preserving playable experience requires allowing use, but use creates wear. Libraries must therefore balance access and preservation while keeping enough contextual and technical infrastructure to let future researchers understand games as aesthetic, technological, historical, and social artifacts.

The article is a case study and conceptual essay rather than an assessment study. It does not provide patron survey data or long-term preservation metrics. Its strongest evidence comes from the University of Michigan CVGA description and the broader literature on academic game collections. Even so, it is a strong GBLS source because it explains why academic libraries are essential to game studies and why game labs and archives should be considered service models for research, scholarship, and creative production.
