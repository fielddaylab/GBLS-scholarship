### Summary
# **Rossi, S., Lee, J. H., & Clarke, R. I. (2014). Mood metadata for video games and interactive media. In *2014 IEEE/ACM Joint Conference on Digital Libraries* (pp. 475-476). IEEE. https://doi.org/10.1109/JCDL.2014.6970232**

Rossi, Lee, and Clarke present a short but useful poster on mood metadata for video games and interactive media. The paper begins from the broader GAMER Group argument that video games are becoming important digital library collection objects and cultural artifacts, so libraries need metadata schemas that support robust search, browse, collocation, and discovery. Mood is one element in the Video Game Metadata Schema, where it is defined as the pervading atmosphere or tone of a video game that evokes or recalls an emotion or state of mind.

The article's contribution is to make that mood element operational through a controlled vocabulary. The authors note that video game mood research was still in its infancy and that most prior mood-taxonomy work came from psychology and music information retrieval. They therefore drew on Russell's circumplex model, Thayer's work on mood and arousal, Watson and Tellegen's two-factor structure of affect, and music mood research. They also harvested mood terms from AllMusic.com, the Movie Genome Project, gamerDNA.com, and Amazon.com customer reviews, removing redundant and genre-related terms through iterative review.

The resulting controlled vocabulary contains 17 mood terms with definitions, equivalent terms, and game examples: Adventurous, Aggressive, Cute, Dark, Horror, Humorous, Imaginative, Intense, Light-hearted, Mysterious, Peaceful, Quirky, Romantic, Sad, Sarcastic, Sensual, and Solitary. The list is valuable because it treats mood as more than genre or subject. A game might be adventurous because it emphasizes exploration and risk-taking, dark because it has a grim atmosphere, imaginative because it foregrounds world-building or creative problem-solving, or solitary because the player feels isolated or alone. These descriptors could help users browse for experiential qualities that platform, genre, and title metadata do not capture.

To test applicability, the authors annotated a sample collection of 617 video game titles selected for variety in genre and release year. Adventurous, aggressive, and intense were the most frequently assigned mood terms, while sarcastic, sad, and sensual were least frequent. The annotation process also suggested possible correlations between genre and mood and possible chronological shifts, such as aggressive appearing more often after 1992. The poster does not present the full visualizations or detailed statistics, but it establishes that a mood vocabulary can be applied to a large sample and can reveal patterns across game collections.

The authors are careful about mood's subjectivity. Mood may vary by player, state of mind, prior experience, and interpretation of the game's prevailing atmosphere. One player may label *Bioshock* horror because of fear responses to Rapture and its residents, while another may reserve horror for games like *Silent Hill*. Rossi et al. therefore suggest allowing multiple mood annotations per game and possibly crowdsourcing mood annotation so user input can rank or weight terms. This caveat is important for GBLS because mood metadata is useful precisely where it is also unstable: it connects to user experience, affect, and advisory language rather than only to fixed bibliographic facts.

For the larger GBLS project, the source strengthens the cataloging, metadata, and discovery thread. Library game services need more than shelves, circulation, and events; they need ways for patrons, students, teachers, researchers, and staff to find games by the qualities that matter to play and learning. Mood metadata could support readers' advisory-style game advisory, thematic displays, curricular matching, wellness-oriented recommendations, collection browsing, and preservation description. It also complements related metadata sources in the review: Lee et al. (2013) establish the broader user-centered schema, Lee, Clarke, Sacchi, and Jett (2014) address relationships, Welhouse et al. (2015) address plot/narrative, and Keating et al. (2017) address visual style.

The limitations are those of a poster paper. It offers a proposed controlled vocabulary and a preliminary annotation exercise, not a patron study of mood-based searching or a deployed library catalog interface. The authors themselves call for future user studies asking players to annotate familiar games and describe how they would use mood in game-related information seeking. Even with those limits, the article belongs in the review because it gives GBLS a concrete example of how game metadata can move from generic description toward experiential discovery.
