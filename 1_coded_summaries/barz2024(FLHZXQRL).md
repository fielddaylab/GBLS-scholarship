### Summary
# **Barz, N., Benick, M., Dörrenbächer-Ulrich, L., & Perels, F. (2024). The effect of digital game-based learning interventions on cognitive, metacognitive, and affective-motivational learning outcomes in school: A meta-analysis. *Review of Educational Research, 94*(2), 193-227. https://doi.org/10.3102/00346543231167795**

Barz, Benick, Dörrenbächer-Ulrich, and Perels offer a theory-based meta-analysis of digital game-based learning (DGBL) interventions in school settings. Their starting point is that previous meta-analyses covered broad time spans and many target groups, making it difficult to transfer findings to pupils in contemporary schools. The authors therefore focus on studies published between 2015 and 2020, a period when digital devices, game development tools, memory, graphics, and school technology infrastructure had changed enough to warrant a more current synthesis.

The article defines DGBL as the use of complete digital games or simulations on computers or mobile devices for teaching and learning. This definition is intentionally narrower than much GBLS work. The authors excluded analog games, role play, card games, pure gamification with only a single game element, Kinect-style embodiment, augmented reality, and virtual reality because those formats involve different qualities of interaction or immersion. They also limited the target population to pupils in grades 1-13, ages 6-18, and excluded university students, adult learners, clinical settings, workplaces, and special subpopulations.

The inclusion criteria were strict. Studies had to use at least a quasi-experimental pre-post-control design, include a traditional-instruction or no-intervention control group, occur in a school setting, measure at least one cognitive, metacognitive, or affective-motivational outcome, use quantitative methods, report enough statistics to calculate effect sizes, and appear as peer-reviewed journal articles or conference proceedings in English or German. The search drew from PubMed, ProQuest, IEEE, ACM, Web of Science, EBSCOhost, and Google Scholar. After title, abstract, and full-text screening, the final synthesis included 35 studies after excluding one outlier.

The final sample represented 7,139 participants with an average age of 10.89 years and an average grade level around sixth grade. The authors coded study year, location, sample characteristics, grade, number of sessions, additional non-game instruction, intervention duration, game type, playing mode, competition, dimensionality, visual realism, narration, content domain, avatars, digital agents, and statistical data. Seven educational-science experts categorized outcomes as cognitive, metacognitive, or affective-motivational. Effect sizes were converted to Hedges' g, and random-effects meta-analyses and meta-regression models were used because DGBL interventions varied substantially.

The findings partially support DGBL in schools. The overall learning effect was significant and medium-sized, g = .54, with 91 percent of observed estimates positive. Cognitive learning outcomes showed a significant medium effect, g = .67, and all observed cognitive estimates were positive. Affective-motivational learning outcomes showed a significant but small effect, g = .32. Metacognitive outcomes, also g = .32, were not statistically significant, largely because only five studies measured metacognitive learning. The authors found no evidence of publication bias after using fail-safe N, funnel plots, Egger's regression test, and selection models.

The moderator results are also useful. The authors did not find significant moderator effects for age, number of sessions, competition, dimensionality, visual realism, narrative, avatars, digital agents, additional non-game instruction, game type, playing mode, learning domain, publication year, or country of data collection. They caution that this may reflect very high heterogeneity, small subgroup sizes, and incomplete reporting rather than proof that design features do not matter. The discussion emphasizes that DGBL only motivates effectively when learning content and engaging game mechanics are thoughtfully balanced.

For the larger GBLS project, Barz et al. should be used as background evidence rather than direct library evidence. It supports the general claim that well-designed digital game-based learning can improve cognitive learning and modestly improve affective-motivational outcomes for school-aged learners compared with traditional instruction. It also reinforces several GBLS cautions: game format should be matched to outcomes, cognitive gains should be separated from motivation and metacognition, implementation requires device access and staff or teacher gaming literacy, and heterogeneity makes it risky to say that games work uniformly across contexts.

The article's limits matter for placement. It does not examine libraries, public programming, collections, informal learning, teen services, tabletop games, gamification, VR, AR, or virtual-world programs. Its results cannot be treated as evidence that any library game program will improve learning. However, it gives the review a credible educational-research anchor for why libraries might take game-based learning seriously, while also clarifying that GBLS needs its own field-specific evidence on audiences, formats, implementation, equity, and outcomes.
