### Summary
# **Giles, K., Shuyler, K. S., Evans, A., & Reed, J. (2019). Creating a library orientation card game to reach new transfer students. *Public Services Quarterly, 15*(3), 236-245. https://doi.org/10.1080/15228959.2018.1488643**

Giles, Shuyler, Evans, and Reed describe the creation, implementation, and assessment of a library orientation card game for new transfer students at James Madison University. The article begins from a specific service problem: transfer students have distinct academic and social transition needs, but transfer orientation at JMU offered only short, concurrent, optional sessions with uncertain attendance, little setup time, limited staff availability, and no guarantee that students would arrive at the library with much time to spare. The library wanted to replace a short lecture about resources and services with something more interactive, welcoming, and social.

The transfer-student focus is important. The authors note that transfer students may face increased academic demands, uneven information literacy preparation, difficulty finding community, work-life-school pressures, and a need to learn a new institution quickly. Some transfer students miss first-year library instruction normally embedded in introductory or core courses, yet are still expected to navigate upper-level research and campus systems. The library orientation therefore needed to introduce spaces, services, policies, subject librarians, remote database access, and basic library resources while also helping students feel welcome.

The chosen format was a traditional tabletop card game modeled after *Apples to Apples*. JMU librarians already had experience with games, including online citation and database-search games, an Information Literacy Game adaptation, an alternate reality game for first-year engineering majors, and game-based training for student employees. An ARG was rejected for transfer orientation because the session was too short and the group too large; a videogame was impractical because the available study area lacked public computers. A familiar card game could be played at tables, reset quickly, scale to variable attendance, and work without much staff facilitation.

The game itself adapted a student-employee training card game. It used question cards about JMU Libraries and answer cards containing a mix of factual library information, campus references, and humorous or popular-culture content. The project team revised cards to remove internal staff procedures and add patron-facing transfer-student content, such as subject specialist librarians and off-campus database access. During testing, the team discovered a design problem: in the original training game, moderators could explain correct answers, but orientation did not have enough staff for a moderator at every table. The solution was to print the correct answer on the back of each question card so the round judge could reveal it after choosing the winning answer.

The revised game included 27 question cards and 144 answer cards and debuted at transfer orientation in 2015, with about 100 total attendees across two sessions. Facilitators gave a brief greeting and rules explanation, then circulated to answer questions. The cards prompted conversation and laughter; some students asked facilitators about library services and resources, and at least one table exchanged contact information after the session. The library also distributed a handout of the game questions with correct answers, bookmarks, and branded water glasses. Over the next year, the game was reused as a freshman-level icebreaker, at student-athlete orientation, and during new faculty orientation.

The 2016 assessment was modest but useful. Ninety-nine students attended the transfer orientation sessions, and 85 completed an IRB-approved survey. Responses were strongly positive: 93% said the game was enjoyable, 92% said it helped them learn about the library, 86% said it helped them feel more comfortable in the library, and 89% recommended using it in future orientations. Comments were few but generally favorable, describing the game as informative, entertaining, and preferable to reading a pamphlet. Some students wanted more humorous answer cards, while others preferred a more informative session, suggesting a tension between orientation as affective welcome and orientation as information delivery.

For the GBLS project, this article is useful because it shows how game form can be matched to local constraints and audience needs. The *Apples to Apples* structure worked because many students already knew the rules, the game could stop at any point without needing a special endgame, groups could play at different speeds, and students with uneven prior library knowledge could still participate. In fact, the authors argue that a game in which knowing the correct answer was not required to win was well suited to reducing library anxiety and welcoming transfer students. The game also served as a structured social icebreaker, which matters because transfer students may need peer connection and a sense of belonging as much as library facts.

The limitations are clear. The study relies on immediate self-report, facilitator observation, and a simple survey rather than testing learning retention, later library use, or transfer-student social outcomes. It also reports a local, low-cost implementation rather than a generalizable intervention. Still, it offers a transferable design model: adapt a familiar game, build correct answers into the materials when facilitation capacity is limited, use humor and social play to reduce anxiety, provide take-away information after play, and revise cards annually as services, spaces, and cultural references change.
