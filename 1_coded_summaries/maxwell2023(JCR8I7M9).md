Maxwell presents *Let's Roll* as a practical guide for school and public librarians who want to run tabletop role-playing game (TTRPG) programs, especially Dungeons & Dragons and related systems. The introductory framing is deliberately beginner-friendly: the guide is aimed at librarians, teachers, and other youth-serving professionals who may be intimidated by rules, Dungeon Master/Game Master responsibilities, preparation time, or the feeling that they must emulate expert online performers. Maxwell repeatedly stresses that the point is not rules mastery but creating a fun, welcoming, collaborative experience.

The excerpt establishes several practitioner principles that matter for GBLS. First, Maxwell treats TTRPGs as unusually sticky library programming. He contrasts D&D sessions with manga club, book club, film club, mock trial, makerspace, and other library clubs, noting that the same core students kept attending over a long period and rarely missed sessions. Second, he frames TTRPG play as connected to mental health and well-being, while carefully noting that he is not a psychologist. The introduction describes TTRPGs as personally therapeutic and as a cathartic space for youth, and the table of contents indicates that the first chapter argues for benefits to literacy, mental health and well-being, and autistic players.

The excerpt is also useful for its implementation details. Maxwell describes starting through student interest generated partly by *Stranger Things*, surveying students, beginning with a small budget, and initially allowing older, more experienced students to act as Game Masters when staff capacity was limited. During COVID lockdowns, he moved sessions online through Microsoft Teams and found that frequent virtual play helped sustain connection and reduce anxiety. These details reinforce a GBLS lesson already visible in other sources: successful play programs often begin with local enthusiasm, modest resources, flexible facilitation, and willingness to let patrons or students supply some expertise.

The glossary and table of contents make the guide's expected infrastructure visible. The book defines TTRPG terms such as Game Master, Session Zero, theatre of the mind, battle mat/map, one shot, non-player character, rule of cool, rule zero, sandbox, and total party kill. The chapter outline covers convincing decision-makers, case studies, program proposals, what materials are needed, running online sessions, Roll20, Zoom, promotion, zero-preparation games, Session Zero, before-session preparation, Game Master tips, teen Game Masters, a list of TTRPGs for libraries, online resources, recommended reading, and fiction for ages 9 and up. Even from the excerpt, TTRPG service work appears as facilitation, advocacy, safety-setting, collection development, and reader/player advisory rather than simply hosting a game.

For the GBLS project, Maxwell is best used as a practitioner guide rather than empirical evidence. It adds a concrete public/school-library TTRPG voice that emphasizes accessibility, beginner confidence, student leadership, low-cost startup, online adaptability, and session design. It also aligns TTRPGs with literacy, reading interests, youth belonging, social play, and well-being.
