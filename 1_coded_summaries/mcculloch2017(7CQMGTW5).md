### Summary
# **McCulloch, A. (2017). Cataloguing and classifying board and tabletop games. *Catalogue and Index, 189*, 20-24.**

McCulloch offers a concise practitioner guide to creating MARC records for board and tabletop games. The article begins from the observation that board and tabletop games are increasingly common in library collections and may be collected for historical, pedagogical, or recreational value, but that very little literature addresses their cataloguing directly. Most guidance concerns video games, realia, kits, or visual materials, leaving cataloguers to translate general standards into workable local practice for boxed games.

The article uses *Settlers of Catan* as its running example and focuses on parts of a record that differ from ordinary books, serials, or online resources. For fixed fields, McCulloch recommends Leader/06 type `r` for realia, Leader/07 `m` for monograph/item, and the Visual Materials 008 specifications, especially 008/33 `g` for game. Identifiers such as ISBNs or publisher/distributor numbers can be recorded where available. Main entry will usually be title, although named designers can be recorded as personal-name access points when appropriate, and publishers should receive corporate added entries because users often search tabletop games by publisher.

The descriptive guidance is especially relevant to library access. McCulloch recommends detailed piece inventories in the 300 field, both to inform users and to help staff manage loss and replacement. A 520 summary note can help patrons decide whether a game is worth playing, and a 521 target-audience note can record age recommendations. Player count and duration of play are important board-game selection data, but MARC often pushes them into uncontrolled 500 general notes. McCulloch explicitly identifies this as a discovery problem: if player count and duration are uncontrolled note text, integrated library systems cannot easily support facets for two-player games, short games, or other practical patron questions.

The article also translates RDA and subject access decisions for tabletop games. It recommends the 336/337/338 combination of three-dimensional form, unmediated, and object, while noting some disagreement over tactile versus visual interpretation and carrier terminology. Genre/form access through LCGFT terms such as Board games, Puzzles and games, and Jigsaw puzzles is treated as essential. McCulloch also discusses franchise or intellectual-property headings, the lack of a useful form subdivision for physical games, and the limits of using topical headings when a game is the thing itself rather than a work about the topic.

Classification is handled pragmatically. If games are meant to circulate and be played, McCulloch suggests that local shelving such as a separate "Board games" section arranged alphabetically may serve users better than interfiling. When interfiling is required, DDC 794 and LCC GV ranges provide possible homes for indoor games, card games, board games, and puzzles. The concluding advice is local and systems-oriented: cataloguers should know how their ILS indexes and displays records so that records work for local users, not only for OCLC or a union catalogue.

For the larger GBLS project, McCulloch adds a compact bridge between tabletop game collections and discoverability. The article shows that tabletop games need description of creators, publishers, components, target age, player count, play duration, genre/form, franchise relationships, physical size, and local shelf logic. It also names a persistent service-design problem: game metadata that is technically present in notes may still fail patrons if it cannot be searched, faceted, or displayed where selection decisions happen. This makes the article a useful companion to enhanced tabletop discovery projects and to broader arguments about game metadata as public-service infrastructure.
