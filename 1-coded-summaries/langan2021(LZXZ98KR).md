### Summary
# **Langan, K. A. (2021). The library language game: Information literacy through the lens of Wittgenstein's language games. *Communications in Information Literacy, 15*(1), 104-118. https://doi.org/10.15760/comminfolit.2021.15.1.6**

Langan's article is a philosophical and critical information literacy essay about labeling, meaning, cataloging, power, and knowledge building. It uses Wittgenstein's idea of "language games" to argue that labels are not neutral handles attached to information. Instead, labels participate in meaning making: they shape how users understand, retrieve, group, and build knowledge from information. The article's central example is the relabeling of semi-automatic rifles from "assault weapons" to "weapons of war" in a newspaper headline, which Langan treats as an epistemological shift rather than a merely stylistic edit.

The article explains Wittgenstein's language games as contexts in which words have meaning only because participants understand the rules and uses of those words. Langan distinguishes naming from labeling: naming invents or bestows a term, while labeling adheres to an established concept. If participants do not understand the rules, contexts, implied meanings, or labels, the language game breaks down and "language goes on holiday." Langan brings this framework into library and information science by arguing that information seeking depends on the successful labeling of information.

The essay then links Wittgenstein to philosophy of information, especially Floridi's idea of the infosphere. In the library context, information has the potential to become knowledge only when it has been named and labeled in ways that carry meaning for participants in the library language game. Catalogers, interfaces, search algorithms, call numbers, subject headings, and metadata all help set the rules and contexts through which information seekers interact with information. Langan therefore treats cataloging broadly as an epistemological bridge between labels, information systems, and user meaning making.

A major emphasis is critical librarianship. Langan argues that cataloging and information literacy are shaped by perspective, power, bias, social position, and privilege. The article discusses the Library of Congress subject heading "Illegal Aliens" as an example of how a label can encode a political and colonialist worldview. Drawing on Olson, Noble, critical race theory, critical librarianship, and related scholarship, the article asks instruction librarians to teach students to be mindful not only of information sources but also of the labels and systems that organize those sources.

For GBLS, the article is adjacent but not directly applicable. It is useful background for thinking about game cataloging, player vocabularies, genre labels, and critical information literacy because it foregrounds how labels shape access and interpretation. It could help explain why game metadata terms such as genre, platform, style, mood, or subject should be taught and questioned rather than treated as transparent. However, the article does not discuss library games, game-based learning, game collections, game programming, gamification, tabletop or video games, or playful library services. Its "language game" is Wittgenstein's philosophical concept, not a GBLS format.

Because the GBLS review already has stronger, directly relevant metadata and information-literacy sources, this article should not be added to `review.md`. It is best treated as explicitly excluded literature with a note that it may be useful only as broader critical cataloging or information literacy theory if the project later adds a theoretical appendix on naming, controlled vocabularies, and bias.
