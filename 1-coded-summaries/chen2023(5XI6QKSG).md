### Summary
# **Chen, C.-H., Law, V., & Huang, K. (2023). Adaptive scaffolding and engagement in digital game-based learning. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 71*(4), 1785-1798. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-023-10244-x**

Chen, Law, and Huang study adaptive scaffolding in digital game-based learning (DGBL), focusing on whether scaffolds triggered by learners' game behavior improve science learning and engagement more effectively than fixed scaffolds. The article is situated in educational technology and secondary science education rather than libraries. Its starting point is that DGBL can support fantasy, story, curiosity, feedback, competition, modality, and interaction, but that evidence for science and math learning is mixed because students may not automatically connect game action to abstract disciplinary concepts.

The authors distinguish fixed or hard scaffolds from adaptive or soft scaffolds. Fixed scaffolds appear in a planned sequence regardless of a learner's current need. Adaptive scaffolds respond to learner status, such as game progress, tool use, answer results, and behavioral logs. The study argues that adaptive scaffolds may reduce unnecessary prompts, avoid interrupting game flow, lower cognitive load, and better support individual learning needs. It also treats engagement as multidimensional: cognitive engagement includes self-regulation and deep strategy use; emotional engagement includes interest, enjoyment, or boredom; and behavioral engagement includes participation, attention, effort, and persistence.

The empirical study used a quasi-experimental design with 61 seventh-grade students from a secondary school in Taiwan. Students learned Newton's laws through a computer-based game called *Summon of Magicrystal*, in which a village is threatened by a dragon and players must solve physics problems related to time, distance, position, velocity, path length, and speed to save the food supply. Students were randomly assigned by class to a fixed-scaffold group (n = 30) or an adaptive-scaffold group (n = 31). The learning experience took place in a computer classroom, lasted about six hours over two weeks, and included a pretest, individual gameplay, a posttest, and engagement questionnaires.

Both groups had access to the same conceptual scaffolds, but the delivery differed. The fixed-scaffold group received the preplanned scaffolds at each challenge level in sequence. The adaptive-scaffold group received scaffolds only when the game system diagnosed need based on logs, game progress, answers, tool use, behavioral frequencies, and predefined trigger conditions. The adaptive system collected data, estimated cognitive level and obstacles, and then provided scaffolding content intended to address misconceptions or support progress.

Results favored adaptive scaffolding. On learning performance, ANCOVA showed a significant scaffolding effect, with the adaptive group outperforming the fixed group on the posttest, F(1,59) = 17.847, p = .00, eta squared = .263. Descriptively, the fixed group improved from a pretest mean of 13.07 to a posttest mean of 14.93, while the adaptive group improved from 13.77 to 19.19. On engagement, MANOVA showed a significant overall group difference. The adaptive group reported stronger self-regulation, deep strategy use, and emotional engagement, but not significantly stronger behavioral engagement. Engagement did not moderate the relationship between scaffolding type and learning performance.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is relevant only as design-adjacent background. If libraries create or adopt digital learning games for orientation, information literacy, science outreach, or youth programming, the study suggests that support should be timed to actual learner need rather than delivered as a fixed stream of hints. It also reinforces the importance of measuring different forms of engagement rather than treating engagement as a single outcome. However, the study does not involve libraries, librarians, patrons, information literacy, public programming, collections, or community learning spaces. It should therefore remain outside the main review except as broader educational-game design context.
