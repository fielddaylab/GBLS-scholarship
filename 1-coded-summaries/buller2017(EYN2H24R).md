### Summary
# **Buller, R. (2017). Lending video game consoles in an academic library. *portal: Libraries and the Academy, 17*(2), 337-346. https://doi.org/10.1353/pla.2017.0020**

Buller describes how the University of Denver's University Libraries created a video game console lending service to support an emerging interdisciplinary video game design curriculum in the Emergent Digital Practices program. The article is a practical academic-library case study rather than an outcomes study, but it is valuable because it treats consoles as curricular access infrastructure. The library did not frame the service primarily as outreach, entertainment, or a way to attract a demographic group. Instead, the rationale was that students and faculty needed hardware in order to study, compare, critique, and create video games across platforms.

The project began when a faculty grant supported development of a video game collection for courses involving programming, design, writing, and music. After meeting with the grant recipient, library faculty and staff decided that their contribution would be to acquire and circulate the hardware needed to use the game collection. Buller situates this decision in a broader shift in academic libraries: as game design programs grow, libraries may need to support not only books and journals about games but also the games, platforms, peripherals, and use conditions that make game study possible. This is similar to libraries providing microfilm readers, record players, VHS equipment, laptops, or tablets when access to content depends on nonbook hardware.

The article's central decision point was whether to build a dedicated library gaming space or circulate consoles. A dedicated space would have offered more control over equipment care and would have helped students without televisions. But the University of Denver library occupied a newly renovated academic commons where group study rooms were heavily used and space was scarce. A dedicated room would have supported only one console at a time and reduced access to other popular services. Circulating consoles, by contrast, gave patrons more time with hardware in the setting where consoles were designed to be used and better supported detailed study of platform affordances. The library chose circulation as a pilot because the service goal was curricular support rather than drawing patrons into the building.

The hardware choices followed the curriculum. The library purchased the current and prior generations of the three major console families at the time: Sony PlayStation 3 and 4, Microsoft Xbox 360 and Xbox One, and Nintendo Wii and Wii U. This enabled students to compare games across platforms and generations. To reduce barriers for students without televisions and to protect the equipment, the library purchased GAEMS Personal Gaming Environments for most consoles, providing portable screens, speakers, and durable hard cases. Additional carrying cases held controllers, cords, and peripherals.

Buller's circulation details are especially useful for GBLS operations. The consoles circulated for one week, matching the loan period for the video games purchased with the grant, and allowed two renewals unless another patron placed a hold. Holds were important because the library did not know how popular the service would be and wanted to prevent one borrower from monopolizing equipment. The library also created extensive check-in procedures. Staff used forms with pictures of every component, maintained serial numbers for console parts, verified hardware after each circulation, and checked software accounts before recirculation. Because users could log in with personal gamer profiles and store payment information, staff had to make sure accounts were logged out to protect patron privacy and prevent fraudulent charges.

The article also documents internal resistance. Some staff questioned whether console lending was an appropriate library service and worried about start-up costs and movement away from traditional materials. Library leaders responded by tying the purchases to demonstrated curricular need, likely circulation, and the library's mission to support teaching, research, scholarship, and creative work. This internal discussion became an opportunity to revisit the changing role of the academic library and to build staff understanding of nontraditional collections.

For the GBLS project, Buller adds a concrete console-lending model to the literature on circulating and in-library collections. It shows that access to games may require access to hardware, screens, controllers, accounts, security practices, and staff workflows. It also links game collections to academic programs and creative production, not only recreation. The service was reported as successful: it met circulation expectations without formal advertising, relied on word of mouth from faculty, students, and library staff, kept consoles in good condition through double-checking procedures, expanded to additional controllers based on student feedback, and helped secure further collection support. The limitations are also clear: there is no formal patron survey, learning assessment, cost-benefit analysis, or long-term circulation data in the article. Still, it is a highly transferable operational case for academic libraries deciding whether console hardware belongs in the collection and how to lend it responsibly.
