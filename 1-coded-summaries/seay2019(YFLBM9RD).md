### Summary
# **Seay, J. A. (2019). Considering games in libraries and such: The ultimate library instruction. *Against the Grain, 31*(6), 65.**

Seay's "The Ultimate Library Instruction" is a short professional column written as a narrative reenactment of a gamified library instruction scenario. It is not an empirical report, and it does not provide implementation data, assessment results, or a formal lesson plan. Its value for the GBLS project is as a practitioner design vignette: it dramatizes how a database-searching session could be reframed as a team-based, time-limited, immersive challenge with story, props, progress tracking, competition, and escalating stakes.

The scenario places students in a library classroom transformed into a nuclear-plant emergency. The lights are low, screens display a "Nuclear Core," warning symbols pulse, a temperature gauge tracks danger, and a set of glowing tube props represents unstable fuel rods. The librarian enters in a lab coat, announces that the core is approaching meltdown, and divides students into teams. Each team receives instructions that lead them into an application requiring them to solve problems using Web of Science search results.

The column's instructional design is gameful rather than merely decorative. Students must interpret sparse instructions, work through levels, answer questions, solve database-related problems, use clues from printed articles, open a locked box, decode information, and combine their data to shut down the fuel rods. Public progress indicators on the classroom screen show each team's movement toward the core, turning the activity into a visible race. Alarms, lights, countdown pressure, animated doors, physical props, and team coordination all support the scenario.

For GBLS, the column demonstrates how gamification and escape-room-like design can be applied to a familiar library instruction problem: students often experience database demonstrations as abstract, passive, or boring. Seay imagines instruction as an embodied problem space in which search tasks have immediate narrative consequences. The activity makes database use purposeful inside the fiction of the game, while also introducing collaboration, competition, tactile puzzles, and print/digital source use.

The most useful lesson is not that every library should build a nuclear-core classroom game. Rather, the source illustrates several reusable design principles: create a scenario with a clear goal, divide learners into teams, make progress visible, connect each puzzle to a real information task, use time pressure carefully, combine digital and analog clues, and let students discover procedures through play. It also shows how librarian facilitation can maintain the fiction without overexplaining the learning objective.

The limitations are substantial. Because the column is a narrative sketch, the reader cannot know whether the described game was fully implemented, how students performed, what learning outcomes were measured, whether the activity improved search skills, or how much staff time and equipment were required. Its place in the literature review should therefore be illustrative rather than evidentiary. It belongs alongside stronger studies of library escape rooms and game-based information literacy as a practitioner example of what a gamified database lesson might look and feel like.
