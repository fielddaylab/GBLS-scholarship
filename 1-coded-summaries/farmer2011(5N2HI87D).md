### Summary
# **Farmer, L. S. J. (2011). Are girls game? *Knowledge Quest, 40*(1), 14-17.**

Farmer argues that school librarians who add electronic gaming to library programs need to address gender equity deliberately. The article begins from the premise that gaming has become part of school-library resources and services because youth are interested in games and because games can support information and digital literacies. Farmer distinguishes long-standing board-game participation from newer e-gaming, including video, console, and computer games, and notes that e-games have historically appeared to engage boys more than girls. The core question is how school librarians can make e-gaming positive and accessible for all students.

The article summarizes gendered patterns in youth e-gaming. Farmer notes that girls and women make up a substantial share of the gaming population, especially because of Wii and casual cell-phone games, but boys tend to play more often and for longer periods. Teenage girls' gaming activity may decline because of gendered perceptions of gaming, discomfort with mechanical or violent game styles, difficulty identifying with male, nonhuman, or sexualized default characters, and negative first experiences with complex games. Farmer emphasizes that gender differences may fade with experience and age, which makes early access and supportive design especially important.

The equity argument is linked to technology futures. If girls generalize negative e-gaming experiences into avoidance of technology courses, professional development, or technology-rich learning environments, school-library gaming can unintentionally contribute to broader opportunity gaps. Farmer warns that technology avoidance may narrow college, career, and professional options. Conversely, thoughtful e-gaming can introduce technology through motivating activity, improve problem solving and collaboration, enhance spatial skills, and increase self-efficacy.

Farmer's learning rationale is built around activity theory and intentional instructional design. E-games may support learning when they provide fixed and equitable rules, meaningful contexts, clear goals, identity exploration, cognitive and affective engagement, timely feedback, control, and personal investment. But games do not guarantee learning by themselves. Farmer stresses that e-games need academic context, conceptual purpose, skill-building design, and a rationale. Merely assigning a game without context may alienate girls in particular.

For GBLS, Farmer is useful as an early school-library equity lens for digital gaming. The source supports the idea that game-based library services should not assume that a game collection or event is automatically inclusive because it is available to everyone. Selection, characters, genres, difficulty curves, first experiences, social context, instructional framing, and staff support all shape who feels invited. The article also helps connect game services to digital-literacy equity: inclusive gaming may support technology confidence, while poorly designed gaming may reinforce exclusion.

The limitations are clear. This is a short professional article that synthesizes prior research and offers guidance rather than reporting new data or a specific library program. Some statistics and market conditions are historically situated in 2011. Even so, it gives GBLS a useful gender-equity caution and vocabulary for thinking about e-gaming as technology access, not just entertainment.
