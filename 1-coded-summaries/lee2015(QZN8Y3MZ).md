### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Clarke, R. I., & Perti, A. (2015). Empirical evaluation of metadata for video games and interactive media. *Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology, 66*(12), 2609-2625. https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.23357**

Lee, Clarke, and Perti report the empirical evaluation and revision of the GAMER Group's video game metadata schema, developed with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum. The article begins from the problem that libraries, archives, museums, and commercial sources describe games inconsistently, often forcing games into book-, film-, or software-oriented standards that do not support how players, parents, collectors, curators, scholars, and other users actually search and browse. The project therefore evaluates whether a media-specific schema can describe diverse video games across genres, formats, platforms, and time periods while reflecting user needs and language.

The article situates the work across three phases. Phase I used domain analysis, existing game websites and catalogs, personas, and use scenarios to identify 16 core elements. Phase II expanded that work through additional domain analysis, 24 user interviews, facet analysis, and controlled vocabulary development, resulting in 46 recommended elements. Phase III, the focus of this article, revised and tested the schema through collaborative expert review, double cataloging of 65 sample games, 32 additional interviews with casual gamers, avid gamers, parents, collectors, industry professionals, curators/librarians, and scholars, and a large online survey with 1,257 completed responses.

The evaluation showed that users valued many kinds of game information that ordinary catalog records do not handle well. In the survey, price and platform were rated useful by more than 80% of respondents, followed closely by genre, series, style, gameplay videos, plot/narrative, franchise/universe, theme, mood/affect, system requirements, developer, setting, format, trailers, number of players, distributor, release date, presentation, visual style, online capabilities, and point of view. The authors also found that usefulness varies by task: some elements matter more when a user is already playing, while others matter more when seeking a new game.

The cataloging tests exposed persistent description problems. Catalogers disagreed about whether to describe games at work-like or manifestation-like levels, especially when the same game appeared across platforms, regions, editions, and digital stores. Subjective elements such as plot, theme, mood/affect, and visual style required either playing experience or carefully chosen secondary sources. Elements such as achievements, customization options, credits, and characters became too labor-intensive or too unstable for the recommended set. The evaluation also showed that digitally distributed games require attention to download, streaming, flash, representative art, DRM, region codes, connectivity, networked features, and additional content such as DLC, mods, and patches.

The revised version 2.0 schema separates and refines elements that are especially important for game discovery. Series, franchise, and other relationships are treated as meaningful access points rather than incidental notes. Online capabilities are split into networked features and connectivity. The schema adds or revises elements for additional content, DRM, price/MSRP, summary, setting, pacing, representative art, visual style, gameplay videos, screenshots, trailers, dimension, point of view, and estimated time of completion. A crosswalk to Dublin Core, MARC, and RDA shows that many game-specific elements have no direct equivalent in general cultural-heritage standards.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is a central infrastructure source. It shows that metadata is not a neutral back-office issue: game-based library services depend on whether users can identify, evaluate, compare, access, recommend, preserve, and reconstruct playable works. The study gives libraries a concrete evidence base for adding game-specific access points to catalogs, guides, discovery layers, collection-development records, and advisory tools. It also explains why local game collections need metadata policies for source authority, granularity, digital distribution, versioning, network dependency, visual evidence, user-facing subject description, and relationships among games.
