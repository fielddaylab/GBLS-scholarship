### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Jett, J., & Perti, A. (2015). The problem of "additional content" in video games. In *Proceedings of the 15th ACM/IEEE-CS Joint Conference on Digital Libraries* (pp. 237-240). ACM. https://doi.org/10.1145/2756406.2756949**

Lee, Jett, and Perti address a specific but increasingly central problem for video game collections: additional content such as mods and downloadable content (DLC). They argue that additional content creates philosophical and practical challenges for cultural heritage institutions on acquisition, description, access/use, and preservation. The issue is not limited to games, since serial publications, e-publications with datasets, and other digital or multimedia objects can also have supplementary or evolving content, but video games make the problem especially visible.

The article defines additional content carefully. Ordinary patches and bug fixes are excluded because they correct malfunctioning software rather than expand content. Additional content, in their usage, means content that improves or extends the player's experience beyond the game as initially purchased: new characters, dungeons, stories, maps, play modes, cosmetic assets, or other additions. The authors focus especially on mods, usually developed by fan communities, and DLC, usually produced and distributed by corporate entities. Both require the original game to be present, but they differ in responsibility, distribution, cost, and relation to community practice.

The philosophical challenge is the boundary of the work. In traditional bibliographic terms, a work is a distinct intellectual or artistic creation, but mods and DLC can substantially change gameplay and narrative. Optional additional content means two players may experience different versions of what is nominally the same game. In some cases, a mod can become better known than its source game, as with *Counter-Strike* and *Dota*. This raises a cataloging question that GBLS cannot ignore: when is additional content merely supplemental, and when does it become a new game or work?

The practical challenges are equally consequential. Acquisition is difficult because additional content may be time-limited, digitally distributed through platform-specific services, withdrawn, poorly documented by official distributors, incomplete in fan documentation, and dependent on possession of the original game. Selection is also difficult because fully acquiring all additional content for a game may be impossible or budget-breaking. Description is difficult because communities lack stable controlled vocabularies for different types of additional content, and mods or DLC may have different creators, genres, titles, system requirements, compatibility constraints, dependencies, and conflicts from their base games.

Access, use, and preservation become complicated because additional content can be delivered through physical media, separate folders, user profiles, encrypted console storage, platform accounts, or digital storefronts. Institutions may struggle to identify which files are the additional content, restore them to usable form, migrate them past copy protection, or preserve files that users never directly receive. The authors emphasize that a one-size-fits-all preservation framework is unlikely because platform architectures and access mechanisms vary so much.

Their proposed solution is a video game conceptual model that treats Additional Content as a separate entity published after the initial game. That entity can have attributes such as name/title, type (mod, DLC, expansion), and version/configuration requirements. The model could record, for example, that *Civilization V: Gods & Kings* is an expansion requiring the base game, while the *Camera Rotation* mod requires the base game, the expansion, and another mod. The authors acknowledge that this game-specific model departs from FRBR, but they argue that FRBR has difficulty with non-text media, serials, and multiple-version objects.

For the larger GBLS project, this paper strengthens the metadata, access, and preservation strand by showing that collecting games means collecting relationships, dependencies, versions, and contexts, not just discs or titles. DLC and mods affect what patrons can play, what scholars can cite, what preservationists can reconstruct, and what libraries can legally or technically provide. The article also warns that additional content creates perpetual cataloging: as games evolve, records and relationships must be maintained over time, making some games more analogous to serials than monographs.
