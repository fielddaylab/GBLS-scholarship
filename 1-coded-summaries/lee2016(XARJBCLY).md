### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Clarke, R. I., & Rossi, S. (2016). A qualitative investigation of users' discovery, access, and organization of video games as information objects. *Journal of Information Science, 42*(6), 833-850. https://doi.org/10.1177/0165551515618594**

Lee, Clarke, and Rossi investigate how people find, access, select, purchase, collect, recommend, and organize video games as information objects. The article is part of the broader University of Washington video game metadata project, but it shifts attention from schema construction to empirical user behavior. The authors conducted 56 semi-structured interviews with gamers, parents, collectors, game industry professionals, librarians, educators, and scholars, then coded the interviews for gaming experience, collection and organization behavior, information needs, metadata types, appeal factors, and user characteristics.

The first major finding is that user roles are fluid. Participants often occupied multiple positions at once, such as gamer and collector, scholar and game designer, or parent and player. The authors distinguish direct users from proxy users: direct users seek games for themselves, while proxy users such as parents, librarians, and educators select games for children, patrons, students, or collections. Proxy users had especially strong needs for information about cost, age appropriateness, ESRB rating, violence, profanity, gore, platform availability, system requirements, number of players, popularity, and whether a game could fit a program, collection policy, or instructional goal.

The metadata findings are directly relevant to libraries. Genre was the most discussed metadata element, but users often found genre labels confusing, limiting, and overloaded. Theme was also important, especially for content such as violence, gender, race, sexuality, cyberpunk, and regional or cultural traditions. Technical metadata mattered across groups: platform, number of players, hardware requirements, digital/physical format, system compatibility, and corporate bodies such as developers, publishers, franchises, and brands all shaped discovery and acquisition. Relationship metadata was also desirable, including series, franchises, sequels, prequels, mods, additional content, remakes, influence relationships, related media, soundtracks, strategy guides, concept art, and design documentation.

The article also highlights the importance of reviews, ratings, rankings, recommendations, and visual metadata. Participants often triangulated among user reviews, professional reviews, Amazon rankings, patron requests, word of mouth, and listserv recommendations. Librarians specifically wanted more reliable professional game reviews that addressed library-relevant concerns such as DRM, privacy, collection suitability, and programming use. Visual metadata, including screenshots, trailers, gameplay videos, YouTube, and Twitch-like spectating, was crucial because users wanted to "test drive" a game and see controls, difficulty, visual style, and gameplay rather than rely only on textual descriptions.

The authors' appeal findings extend readers' advisory logic to games. When title or genre was insufficient, users searched through similarity, relationships, and appeals. Common appeal factors included fellowship, competition, sensation, narrative, challenge, accomplishment, mastery, nostalgia, mood, submission, fantasy, expression, exploration, learning, creativity, cost, accessibility, and limited time. The authors argue that systems should support queries such as games with similar visual style, networked features, stories, or appeal factors rather than recommending only by broad genre.

For the larger GBLS project, this is a foundational game-discovery source. It shows that a library game collection or guide must serve multiple audiences and modes: casual browsing, collection development, parental advisory, classroom selection, scholarly research, personal collection management, and program planning. It also shows why game metadata should include objective access fields, subjective and visual information, appeals, relationship data, user-generated content, and multiple sorting modes for physical and digital collections. The study is not about a library program outcome, but it strongly supports metadata, advisory, and collection-access design for game-based library services.
