### Summary
# **Valenti, S., Lund, B., & Wang, T. (2020). Virtual reality as a tool for student orientation in distance education programs: A study of new library and information science students. *Information Technology and Libraries, 39*(2), 1-12. https://doi.org/10.6017/ital.v39i2.11937**

Valenti, Lund, and Wang study whether virtual reality can support new-student orientation for distance library and information science students. The article is especially relevant to GBLS because it connects VR, game engagement theory, library-school education, orientation, and distance learning in an experimental design. The authors frame VR as a technology associated with gaming and learning and ask whether its immersive qualities can help students learn about a program, a profession, faculty, library services, and academic spaces when they cannot regularly access the physical campus.

The setting was Emporia State University's School of Library and Information Management, a hybrid MLIS program with cohorts across multiple distance locations in the western United States. New students normally attend a one-day, in-person orientation, but distance learners may still miss the ambient information and sense of presence that campus-based students get from physical spaces, faculty contact, and institutional familiarity. An initial survey of new MLS students showed that 61% had elevated anxiety about their first course, 55% believed that knowing more about faculty, course structure, and program purpose would reduce anxiety, and students wanted information about curriculum, faculty teaching areas, information access, librarianship, library services, and program spaces.

The experimental phase used a pre-test/post-test design with 29 participating students from South Dakota, Idaho, Nevada, and Oregon. Students were assigned by state to either an experimental group or a control group. The experimental group received a cardboard VR headset and instructions for viewing a researcher-created VR orientation. The control group received links containing the same information in a static, non-immersive format. The VR experience was built in Veer VR using 360-degree images captured with a Samsung Gear 360 camera, plus interactive text boxes, video, audio, and transitions. In both conditions, students completed pre-test and post-test measures on orientation knowledge, program anxiety, and perceptions of VR.

The results are mixed but useful. Nearly 60% of the VR group spent more than 25 minutes in the virtual orientation, and only 10% of the experimental group declined to use the headset. Participants in the VR group generally rated the experience as more fun, helpful, engaging, and informative than the text-and-links group, and they felt somewhat more connected to the school. Both groups improved from pre-test to post-test. The VR group had a larger gain because it began with lower pre-test scores, but the difference between groups was not statistically significant. The VR group also reported less post-experiment anxiety than the control group, but that difference was not significant at the conventional .05 level.

The discussion ties the findings to game engagement theory. VR may increase interest, control, immersion, and perceived purpose because users can move through an environment, choose what to inspect, and experience the orientation more like presence than sequential reading. For GBLS design, this is a useful reminder that immersive technology may help with engagement and orientation even when measurable learning gains are modest. The article also cautions that information overload can create information anxiety, especially in immersive environments; VR orientations may work best when they introduce broad concepts, offer freedom and flexibility, and avoid cramming too much new information into one experience.

For the larger GBLS project, Valenti et al. add an experimental orientation model that complements physical library games such as mystery tours and card games. It suggests that VR can be used not only for public technology access but also for institution-specific orientation, remote connection, and professional identity formation. The same design logic could apply to library orientation for online students, branch campuses, adult learners, remote patrons, or anyone who needs to understand library spaces and services before or without visiting in person.

The limitations are substantial. The sample was small, state assignment was not random at the individual level, some headsets arrived late, troubleshooting was difficult for distance students, and several participants dropped out when technology access became frustrating. The study does not show long-term learning, later library use, or sustained anxiety reduction. Still, it provides a rare comparative study in the GBLS-adjacent VR literature and gives libraries a concrete model for asking whether immersive orientation adds value beyond ordinary text-and-link modules.
