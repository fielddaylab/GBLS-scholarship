### Summary
# **Tahir, R., & Wang, A. I. (2024). Evaluating the effectiveness of game-based learning for teaching refugee children Arabic using the integrated LEAGUE-GQM approach. *Behaviour & Information Technology, 43*(1), 110-138. https://doi.org/10.1080/0144929X.2022.2156386**

Tahir and Wang evaluate a digital game-based learning approach for teaching Arabic reading skills to migrant refugee children. The article has two linked purposes: it proposes an integrated LEAGUE-GQM approach for planning game-based learning evaluation studies, and it demonstrates that approach through a study of *Feed the Monster*, an Arabic language learning game created for refugee children as part of the EduAppSyria initiative. The study is motivated by gaps in empirical GBL evaluation, gaps in Arabic digital game-based language learning, and the educational disruption experienced by Syrian refugee children.

The methodological contribution is the integrated LEAGUE-GQM guide. LEAGUE stands for Learning, Environment, Affective-cognitive reactions, Game factors, Usability, and User; GQM stands for Goal, Question, Metric. The authors combine these into a process for defining the evaluation type and data, then developing a LEAGUE tree and evaluation plan across conceptual, operational, and measurement levels. The approach guides evaluators from broad goals to dimensions, factors, relations, data sources, measures, and analysis methods.

The empirical study used a quasi-experiment with a one-group pre/post-test design. Thirty children aged 5 to 10 from migrant refugee backgrounds in Trondheim, Norway participated through a weekend class program at Muslim Society Trondheim. Participants spoke Arabic but did not have Arabic reading or writing skills and had no prior experience with *Feed the Monster*. The study included a playtest session, one week of at-home gameplay, and a post-test. Data sources included an Early Grade Reading Assessment adapted to selected Arabic letters and syllables, game log data, observation checklists, demographic and mobile-use questionnaires, a VARK learning-modality questionnaire, observation notes, and short interviews.

The main result was a statistically significant positive change in Arabic reading assessment scores after one week of gameplay. The study also found that age group mattered: younger children had higher learning gain, while older children had better usability scores and played more levels. Learning modality preference did not affect learning gain, but read/write and aural preferences were associated with usability or gameplay measures in ways tied to the game's audio and text emphasis. Mobile-use experience also did not affect learning gain, but mobile expertise and years of use were associated with usability and some gameplay performance measures.

The qualitative usability findings are detailed. Younger children struggled with icon recognition, concepts, and gestures, while older children often found the material too easy and wanted more challenge. Some children skipped textual instructions, had difficulty with dialect-specific audio, did not notice hints, time bars, or scores, and wanted more customization of characters, colors, difficulty levels, and scenarios. Some lost interest because the storyline did not hold attention or the game lacked variation. The authors translate these findings into design recommendations: age-appropriate design, customization, adaptivity, meaningful feedback, adequate help and tutorials, pedagogic feedback for self-correction, varied tasks and learning activities, empathy and connection through characters, effective multimedia for multimodal learning, and an intriguing storyline.

For the larger GBLS project, the article is useful only as adjacent design and evaluation evidence. Its emphasis on user characteristics, multimodal instruction, accessibility for children with different mobile experience, and mixed-methods evaluation could inform library game design in a very general way. However, the study is not about libraries, librarians, library instruction, library collections, library spaces, public programming, information literacy, or library users as such. It evaluates a language-learning game in an informal community/religious setting for refugee children, so its findings cannot serve as direct evidence for game-based library services.

The source should therefore remain outside the main literature review. It may be worth retaining as background for future work on inclusive GBL evaluation, refugee-serving learning games, child-centered usability, or multilingual informal learning, but it should not be cited as GBLS literature unless a later section explicitly discusses non-library adjacent evidence.
