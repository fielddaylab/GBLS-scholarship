### Summary
# **Windleharth, T. W., Jett, J., Schmalz, M., & Lee, J. H. (2016). Full Steam ahead: A conceptual analysis of user-supplied tags on Steam. *Cataloging & Classification Quarterly, 54*(7), 418-441. https://doi.org/10.1080/01639374.2016.1190951**

Windleharth, Jett, Schmalz, and Lee analyze user-generated tags on Steam to understand what game players themselves treat as useful descriptive metadata. The study begins from a familiar problem in game discovery: existing search and browse systems are fragmented, while formal metadata often does not match the language and dimensions users want when choosing games. Steam offers an unusually large folksonomic data source because users can attach free-form tags to digitally distributed games, supplementing Steam's official catalog data with player language.

The authors collected all tags applied to publicly accessible Steam games on March 11, 2015, using custom Ruby scripts and HTML/XPath queries. The final dataset included 294 unique tags applied to 4,495 games. The team then used conceptual analysis and card sorting in Trello to organize those tags into 29 categories, often checking the games associated with specific tags to infer how users were applying ambiguous terms. Most categories mapped to existing elements in the GAMER Group's Video Game Metadata Schema (VGMS), but some pointed to gaps in the schema.

Gameplay genre was the largest category, with 96 tags. Steam users used tags to refine broad official categories, introduce emergent genre terms such as MOBA, Parkour, and Physics, and name prototype-based genres such as Lemmings-like or Metroidvania. Narrative genre, theme, setting, mood, tropes, visual style, point of view, pacing, platform, additional content, price, production, estimated time, and relationship indicators also appeared. The analysis shows why game genre cannot be treated as a single flat label: player tags mix mechanics, narrative genre, setting, visual presentation, mood, media relationships, and evaluative appeal.

The article is especially useful because it treats user tags as both evidence and problem. Tags revealed user-relevant concepts not fully represented in VGMS, including mechanics, user interactions, and evaluation. Mechanics included rules, operations, movement systems, puzzle structures, resource economies, progression structures, and input modes. User interaction included PvP, PvE, co-op, local multiplayer, online co-op, team-based play, asynchronous multiplayer, mouse-only input, and controller use. Evaluation captured player judgments such as Story Rich, Lore-Rich, Great Soundtrack, Experimental, and other terms tied to replay value, novelty, appeal, and core aesthetics.

At the same time, the authors show that folksonomies require interpretation and curation. Many Steam tags are ambiguous, duplicated, inconsistent, emergent, or context-dependent. A tag such as Abstract may describe gameplay genre, visual style, or theme; America may work as setting, national theme, gun culture, hunting, or other loosely associated meanings; Anime may refer to visual style, media relationship, or both. The authors therefore recommend not simply importing tags, but using them to revise metadata elements and controlled vocabularies, ideally with linked-data practices that can distinguish different senses of the same term.

For the larger GBLS project, this source strengthens the metadata and advisory argument. Libraries that collect or recommend games need vocabularies that reflect how players choose games: mechanics, interaction modes, pacing, mood, visual style, narrative genre, theme, appeal, local/online multiplayer, and emergent community terms. The study is not about a library program, but it is directly relevant to game collection discovery, readers' advisory, catalog enrichment, and patron-centered metadata. It also helps explain why commercial tags, fan language, and library vocabularies should be brought into conversation rather than treated as interchangeable.
