### Summary
# **Adams, M. J., Finch, M., & Gamble, B. (2023). More than a game: Scenario planning, imagination, and the public library's future. *Public Library Quarterly, 42*(6), 665-687. https://doi.org/10.1080/01616846.2022.2154068**

Adams, Finch, and Gamble examine scenario planning as a strategic-planning method for public libraries facing turbulence, uncertainty, novelty, and ambiguity. The article is centered on Reading Public Library in Pennsylvania and the process it used to plan amid COVID-19, U.S. political polarization, local economic and demographic change, and leadership transition. The authors argue that strategic planning should not focus only on present organizational goals; it should also imagine the contextual future world in which those goals might have to operate.

The paper defines scenarios as storytelling tools derived from current-day realities that present plausible glimpses into future contexts. In the Oxford Scenario Planning Approach used at Reading Public Library, scenarios are not predictions or preferred futures. They are imagined future contexts designed to contrast with current assumptions, stretch perception, expose blind spots, and help decision-makers ask what is happening, what it means, and what they can do. The article therefore treats scenario planning as a form of strategic reframing.

Reading Public Library's context matters to the case. The library serves Reading, Pennsylvania, a post-industrial city with a long library history, financial challenges, poverty, local political complexity, and pandemic disruptions. During the pandemic, RPL expanded digital services, streaming media, online programming, pickup services, and Go Packs distributed through school and recreation partnerships. The article positions these pressures as the setting for a planning process that could not simply extrapolate from stable past conditions.

The scenario process began with virtual workshops and a mapping exercise in which staff and trustees explored the library's business environment, flows of value, partner relationships, and uncertainties affecting the future. The team generated scenarios for 2040, eventually working with three future contexts: Life in the Clouds, The Wild Frontier, and Paris on the Schuylkill. These futures varied in their assumptions about digital life, cooperation, fragmentation, civic flourishing, arts, education, and community relationships. Staff and trustees then used the scenarios to look back at present choices, identifying signs that each future might already be emerging and considering what strategic actions would remain robust across possible futures.

The process produced four strategic tracks: Library Staff Reflecting the Community, Co-designed Programs, Digital Opportunities, and Collection Management. The language of tracks intentionally evoked Reading's railroad history. The article's first-person reflections emphasize that scenario planning helped staff look outward, talk about partners and community forces, imagine RPL serving residents in new ways, and avoid a conventional SWOT process that might have felt too narrow in a turbulent moment. The scenarios also shaped later decisions, including workspace planning, art display policy, digital access efforts, local history digitization, staffing, community art partnerships, and teen internship programs.

The article's title and discussion explicitly connect scenario planning to play. The authors draw on Leorke and Wyatt's idea of the library as playground, then argue that the play of mind in scenario work can extend beyond makerspaces, video gaming events, gamification, and live-action role-play. The scenario workshop becomes a playful heterotopia: a space separated from everyday expectations in which participants can suspend ordinary assumptions, play with future identities, and test strategic actions without real-world consequences. Strategy is described as a form of serious play and conceptual simulation.

For the GBLS project, this source is adjacent but not a direct literature-review addition. It is about using playful scenario methods for internal library strategy, not about delivering game-based library services to patrons, building game collections, designing game programs, teaching through games, or evaluating patron-facing play. Its most relevant insight is conceptual: games and play can shape library organizations not only through public programs but also through planning, imagination, and institutional identity work. However, including it in the main GBLS review would broaden the scope toward general strategic foresight and organizational play rather than the project's focus on game-based library services.

The source is therefore best kept as explicitly excluded literature with a note. It may become useful if the project later adds a section on staff-facing serious play, scenario planning, or gameful strategic design, but it should not currently be used as evidence for GBLS programming or services. Its limitations for the current review are not flaws in the article itself; they are scope limitations. The case is rich, reflective, and library-specific, but its audience and intervention are internal planning stakeholders rather than library patrons or learners.
