### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Björling, E., McLean, R., Moreno, A., & Lee, K. J. (2024). Supporting youth mental health through co-designing a virtual reality experience: A recipe-based co-design model. *The Library Quarterly: Information, Community, Policy, 94*(2), 134-158. https://doi.org/10.1086/729230**

Lee, Björling, McLean, Moreno, and Lee examine a public-library project that combined teen mental-health support, participatory co-design, and virtual reality (VR). The article begins from the COVID-19-era intensification of teen anxiety, depression, isolation, and service gaps, arguing that public libraries may serve as accessible community hubs for wellness-oriented support. The project, launched by the Seattle Public Library and the University of Washington with IMLS support, engaged teens ages 14-18 in designing VR environments intended to support mental health and well-being. Rather than evaluating the finished VR environments, this article focuses on the roles, perceptions, opportunities, and tensions experienced by librarians and library staff who helped facilitate the program.

The study was implemented at three public-library sites: Seattle Public Library, DC Public Library, and Fayette Public Library, Museum and Archives in La Grange, Texas. At each site, librarians or library staff partnered with 10 to 12 teens in co-design sessions that involved reflecting on mental health, imagining supportive VR spaces, and providing feedback on iterative VR prototypes created by undergraduate developer interns. The authors interviewed eight librarians and library staff before and after the program, for 16 interviews total, and also analyzed a community-of-practice discussion using a shared MIRO board. Their modified collaborative thematic analysis focused on staff experience, anticipated and actual challenges, and implications for libraries that want to implement similar programs.

One major finding is that mental health initially felt intimidating to library workers. Many participants wanted to support teen patrons but worried that they lacked training, might say the wrong thing, or might harm participants by mishandling sensitive topics. Over the course of the project, however, staff often shifted from thinking about mental health only in crisis terms to thinking about everyday wellness, stigma reduction, belonging, and safe conversation. The program helped librarians see that library programming can normalize mental-health discussion without making librarians into clinicians. This distinction is important for GBLS: game-based and immersive programs may support care, reflection, and connection while still requiring clear boundaries around professional expertise and referral.

The second major finding concerns co-design. Librarians generally valued youth ownership, collaboration, creative thinking, and the opportunity for teens to see their ideas reflected in a shared product. Some participants even suggested that designing together was itself beneficial for mental health because it required openness, shared goals, and interpersonal connection. At the same time, staff who lacked prior design experience wanted enough structure to facilitate confidently while still needing flexibility to adapt activities, tools, and expectations to local conditions. The authors therefore propose a "recipe-based co-design model" that distinguishes core ingredients that should be preserved from substitutes and local ingredients that can vary. In the model, the main ingredients include the core spirit of co-design, youth participants, and design sprints; substitutes can include different design activities; local ingredients can include community partners or local staff strengths; and tools can include required or optional technologies.

The third major finding is that VR was useful but uneven. VR drew some teens in, supported technology literacy, and offered an immersive way to imagine relaxation spaces, learning spaces, and social spaces. Some staff described social VR as more natural and engaging than ordinary online interaction during pandemic conditions. Yet VR also created barriers: motion sickness, hardware logistics, online technical support, Facebook/Oculus account requirements, variable teen interest, privacy concerns, and the possibility that some marginalized or non-"techie" youth would feel excluded. The authors emphasize that enthusiasm for VR was sometimes overshadowed by enthusiasm for co-design and wellness-oriented programming. This is a crucial caution for GBLS: technology should serve the service goal, not become the goal.

For the GBLS project, the article is useful because it treats immersive technology programming as a staff, service, and community-design problem. It adds evidence to the review's emerging wellness and care section, showing that game-adjacent technologies and design processes can support teen mental-health conversations when framed around everyday well-being rather than clinical intervention. It also belongs in the VR and immersive technology section because it shows both the appeal and the limits of VR in public libraries. Finally, the recipe-based co-design model is broadly useful for game-based library services: it gives libraries a way to preserve core program values while adapting formats, tools, and partnerships to local needs.

The study's limitations are clear. It reports librarian and staff perspectives, not teen outcome measures, long-term mental-health effects, or a formal evaluation of the VR environments. The program was also resource-intensive, involving university partners, developer interns, multiple sites, and substantial technical support. Even so, the article gives GBLS a strong framework for thinking about participatory design, teen services, wellness, staff confidence, and technology adoption. It argues, in effect, that the most transferable element may not be VR itself but the combination of youth voice, flexible facilitation, local adaptation, and reflective program design.
