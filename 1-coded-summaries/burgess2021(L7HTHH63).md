### Summary
# **Burgess, J., & Wallace, A. (2021). Using investigative video games to teach clinical intuition and metacognition in reference transactions. *Journal of Education for Library and Information Science, 62*(4), 477-491. https://doi.org/10.3138/jelis.62-4-2020-0099**

Burgess and Wallace argue that investigative video games can supplement reference and user-services education by giving MLIS students practice with clinical intuition, metacognition, and reasoning skills adjacent to reference work. The article begins from a problem in online LIS education: as MLIS instruction becomes more widespread online, especially asynchronously, reference instructors may have fewer opportunities to model live reference transactions, provide immediate feedback, and let students practice the real-time uncertainty of reference work. The authors treat the reference transaction as a form of information literacy work, but one that differs from planned instruction because the goals, patron need, resources, and search path may not be clear at the beginning of the encounter.

The conceptual core of the article is clinical intuition. Drawing on nursing and clinical judgment literature, Burgess and Wallace define intuition not as an unexamined gut feeling but as a hypothesis or premise deliberately sought in situations where a practitioner must choose an action. In reference work, clinical intuition develops as workers form and test ad hoc rules about reference interviews, resource selection, and search-string construction. That intuition is useful only if reference workers can reflect on it and explain it. The authors therefore pair clinical intuition with metacognition: the ability to think about one's own thinking, manage tasks strategically, recognize what resources and approaches are available, and refine practice based on outcomes.

The article uses concept analysis to clarify this phenomenon for LIS pedagogy. Burgess and Wallace argue that good reference work is not only a set of procedural skills. It is a complex practice that combines cognitive competencies, behavioral competencies, and social competencies. Reference workers must know how information systems work, use metadata and search tools, select sources, ask open and closed questions, listen actively, avoid jargon, notice sensitive topics, share screens or demonstrate tools, check patron satisfaction, and adapt as new evidence emerges. The problem for online teaching is that some of the most important pieces of this work depend on rule testing and timely feedback, which are harder to reproduce when students only complete readings, watch videos, or search from prewritten questions.

To make the teachable pieces more concrete, the authors reduce reference reasoning to three major types: deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning. Deductive reasoning applies fixed principles to infer what kind of source or search strategy is likely to fit a patron's need. Inductive reasoning evaluates accumulated evidence, such as search results, and revises assumptions based on what is observed. Abductive reasoning forms and tests hypotheses, such as the likely tacit question behind a patron's initial request. The authors connect these reasoning types to experiential learning theory, especially action/reflection and experience/abstraction cycles, arguing that students need opportunities to act, receive feedback, articulate what happened, and refine their rules.

Investigative video games enter the argument as commercially available practice environments. Burgess and Wallace define investigative games as games whose mechanics let players solve a mystery by finding and evaluating clues. Such games can require players to discover rules, test hypotheses, revise assumptions, persist through uncertainty, and use immediate feedback from the game world. The authors analyze *Her Story*, *Return of the Obra Dinn*, and *Subserial Network* as examples. *Her Story* requires players to build search terms in a database of interview clips and learn through trial and error when keywords retrieve useful evidence. *Return of the Obra Dinn* asks players to infer identities and causes of death from incomplete evidence. *Subserial Network* asks players to identify suspect communities by applying decision rules to messages, links, and online traces.

For the larger GBLS project, this source is valuable because it treats commercial games as adjacent simulations for library professional judgment rather than as library-skills games in a narrow sense. The authors do not claim that playing *Her Story* is the same as conducting a reference interview. Instead, they suggest that investigative games can give students supplemental practice with the reasoning habits that reference work requires: abductive inference about tacit needs, deductive source selection, inductive search revision, rule testing, metacognitive reflection, and perseverance through ambiguity. This is especially useful for thinking about GBLS in LIS education, staff training, and professional development.

The article should be cited as conceptual rather than evaluative evidence. The authors do not report a classroom implementation or assess whether students who play investigative games become better reference workers. They also identify limitations: instructors need student buy-in, the distinction between reasoning types may need explicit scaffolding, some mystery games include violent or upsetting content, and games rooted in dominant-culture tropes may not support critical information literacy without careful selection and reflection. Still, the article gives the review a strong vocabulary for one branch of GBLS instruction: using games not merely to teach facts about library tools, but to cultivate the judgment, metacognition, and interpretive flexibility required for library service.
