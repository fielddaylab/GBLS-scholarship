### Summary
# **Anderson, K. (2022). Libraries & lizards: Playing D&D at public libraries program framework. *YA Hotline*, (116).**

Anderson's "Libraries & Lizards" is a practical program framework for running Dungeons & Dragons with young adults in a public library, with possible adaptation for school libraries. The source is not an empirical study, but it is a detailed professional-planning document: it specifies audience, staffing, developmental objectives, materials, session timing, publicity, budget, remote delivery tools, and evaluation practices. Its main GBLS value is that it translates a popular tabletop role-playing game into a library program design that can be copied, adapted, and assessed locally.

The framework describes D&D as a tabletop role-playing game built from improvisation, storytelling, dice rolling, character action, and Dungeon Master facilitation. Anderson begins from an access problem: the game is increasingly popular, but young players may not know how to find a group or how to start. The library is presented as a familiar, safe, resourced space where teens can learn the hobby and find a supervised place to play. The design is intended for three to five players ages 15 to 18, run by one staff member acting as Dungeon Master, with possible expansion to multiple groups when more staff are available.

A notable feature is the explicit connection to the Search Institute's 40 Developmental Assets for Adolescents. Anderson maps the program to adult relationships, safety, positive peer influence, creative activities, youth programs, reading for pleasure, responsibility, planning and decision making, interpersonal competence, peaceful conflict resolution, personal power, and self-esteem. This gives the program a youth-development rationale rather than treating D&D as entertainment alone. The rationale is also operational: library staff supervise emotional and physical safety, participants cooperate in a shared story, and breaks can direct teens toward related fantasy books, role-playing resources, library cards, and displays.

The program timetable is unusually concrete. Session 1 is a four- to five-hour introduction and character-creation session with welcome, introductions, character creation, a break, rules instruction, a no-consequences combat encounter, and closing. Session 2 and later sessions use a similar four- to five-hour structure, moving more quickly into play, refreshing table etiquette, recapping the story, taking a break, continuing the game, and ending at a narratively appropriate moment or cliffhanger. Anderson also notes that the second-session template can be adapted for one-shots by using prewritten characters from the D&D Starter Set or Wizards of the Coast.

The materials and infrastructure sections make the program's hidden labor visible. Anderson lists campaign and rules books, the Player's Handbook, Dungeon Master's Guide, Monster Manual, Curse of Strahd, optional Starter Set, DM screen, character sheets, pencils, notebooks, dice, snacks, water, D&D Beyond, tablets, fantasy books, library-card forms, display cases, chairs, printer access, and remote tools such as Discord, Roll20, or other video-conferencing software. The budget ranges from a low-cost Starter Set model to a more complete setup, with a reminder that many purchases are reusable across months or years of play. The publicity plan includes posters, handouts, conversations, local game shops or cafes, school librarians, the library website, newsletters, Instagram, and TikTok.

For the larger GBLS project, Anderson is useful because it shows how TTRPG programming can be treated as a replicable service model. It connects play to developmental assets, reading, social connection, safety, and decision making, but it also attends to program logistics: staff expertise, group size, recurring scheduling, snacks, physical/digital tools, remote participation, starter materials, partnerships, and post-session debriefing. It also reinforces that evaluation for social play may be informal and formative: staff should check whether players are having fun, debrief after emotional or stressful encounters, ask what players enjoyed or disliked, and improve the experience session by session.

The limitation is evidentiary. Anderson provides a framework, not measured outcomes from an implemented program. It should therefore be used in the review as a practitioner model and service-design example rather than as proof of developmental impact. Its strongest contribution is to show what public-library D&D planning looks like when staff connect youth-development goals to concrete table practice.
