### Summary
# **Vanden Elzen, A. M., & Roush, J. (2013). Brawling in the library: Gaming programs for impactful outreach and instruction at an academic library. *Library Trends, 61*(4), 802-813.**

Vanden Elzen and Roush describe how Lawrence University's Seeley G. Mudd Library used game programs to support outreach, instruction, and campus community. The article is framed around a practical academic-library problem: libraries need to teach students about resources, but they also need to reach students who might not independently seek out the library or ask librarians for help. Games are presented as a way to connect the library to a familiar part of undergraduate culture, bring students into the building for social reasons, and make library staff, collections, and spaces more visible.

The article reviews several academic-library uses of games, including orientation games, information-literacy games, alternate reality games, open gaming nights, LAN parties, console collections, gaming stations, and gaming events in learning commons. Its main contribution, however, is the Lawrence University case. Mudd Library had long used board games and puzzles, but beginning in 2009 it expanded into video game tournaments, open gaming, welcome-week programs, and collaborations with student gamers. Because Lawrence is a residential liberal arts campus, the library understood gaming programs as a way to strengthen the campus's sense of community while increasing library awareness.

The authors emphasize partnerships as a core planning principle. Library staff collaborated with student organizations, student workers, and knowledgeable players because game expertise matters. Serious participants care about rules, tournament structure, unlocked characters, equipment, timing, and game-specific norms. At Lawrence, a student worker helped staff avoid outdated choices such as *Rock Band* or *Guitar Hero* and identify *Super Smash Bros. Brawl* as a better tournament game for the local campus culture. Later tournaments were planned with the Lawrence University Gaming Club, and a Magic: The Gathering event was co-planned with the campus Magic Club using club-owned cards in a special-collections room whose atmosphere enhanced the event.

The planning advice is concrete. Libraries should consult the academic calendar so gaming does not collide with midterms or finals unless intentionally framed as a low-volume study break. They need adaptable spaces with enough room, light, power, and tolerance for noise, even if that means creating temporary setups with carts, folding tables, movable chairs, power strips, and borrowed televisions. They should think carefully about whether to buy equipment, borrow from other libraries, rely on partners, or use staff and student systems. The authors also warn that tournament play can exclude students who are less skilled or have less time, so open gaming, board games, retro systems, puzzles, and casual alternatives can make events more welcoming.

The article also treats marketing as part of service design. The authors found that library Facebook followers were mostly older than the traditional undergraduate audience, so social media alone was insufficient. Student workers helped staff learn that the university calendar was printed and posted in residence halls, making it an unexpectedly effective channel. Paper flyers also mattered, especially outside the library. The marketing lesson for GBLS is practical: game programs often aim to reach students who are not already library insiders, so promotion has to move through the channels those students actually notice.

The impact evidence is anecdotal and survey-based, but it is still useful. After the first tournament in 2009, 50 percent of respondents reported meeting new people with similar interests. In a 2012 survey of gaming-event participants and Gaming Club email-list members, the most frequently selected response was that gaming events had added to respondents' sense of belonging in the Lawrence University community, with a 76.9 percent selection rate; well over half said library gaming events were moderately, very, or extremely important to their overall Lawrence experience. The events also helped catalyze the Lawrence University Gaming Club, whose growth eventually included dedicated student housing for gaming.

For GBLS, this source is valuable because it connects academic-library game programming to outreach, belonging, collections, marketing, and partnerships rather than treating tournaments as isolated entertainment. Gaming events brought students with shared interests together, made the library more visible, helped students become familiar with staff and spaces, and created opportunities to display books on games, education, programming, new media, creative writing, and the art of video games. The library also developed a gaming LibGuide and added related books to the collection, showing how programs can feed collection development and advisory.

The limitations are straightforward. The article is a practitioner report, not a rigorous assessment study. Survey methods, response counts, and sampling are not fully reported, and the findings are local to a small residential campus with an active gaming culture. Still, the source offers a rich operational account of academic-library gaming programs, especially tournaments and open gaming, and it supplies language for the GBLS review's themes of community, outreach, facilitation, partnership, and sustainable program planning.
