### Summary
# **de Groat, G. (2015). A history of video game cataloging in U.S. libraries. *Cataloging & Classification Quarterly, 53*(2), 135-156. https://doi.org/10.1080/01639374.2014.954297**

de Groat provides a technical history of video game cataloging in U.S. libraries, tracing how descriptive rules, MARC coding, subject/genre practice, and title authority work struggled to accommodate games from the late 1970s through the transition from AACR2 to RDA. The article is not about public-facing gaming programs, but it is central to GBLS because collections, advisory, access, preservation, and scholarship all depend on whether games can be described, collocated, searched, limited, and distinguished in library catalogs.

The article begins from a blunt premise: video games have always been an awkward fit in regular cataloging workflows. There have never been specific descriptive cataloging rules for video games, so they were usually treated under broader and constantly changing rules for machine-readable data files, computer files, electronic resources, computer programs, interactive multimedia, moving images, or realia. de Groat explains the institutional structure behind this problem: descriptive cataloging rules, Library of Congress practices, subject and genre vocabularies, name/title authority files, and MARC encoding all developed through systems built primarily for books and later adapted to nonbook materials.

The descriptive-cataloging history shows repeated lag between technology and standards. Early AACR2 rules for "machine-readable data files" focused on data and programs rather than interactive consumer software. Early guidance even suggested that cartridge-based video games might be treated as realia, while later ALA guidelines placed them under computer-file rules. Across AACR2 revisions, terminology shifted from "machine-readable data file" to "computer file" to "electronic resource," and physical descriptions shifted from program-file statements to computer cartridges, computer disks, optical discs, CD-ROMs, DVDs, and conventional terminology. These changes made it difficult to collocate like resources or reliably identify particular carriers and platforms.

The article is especially useful on MARC workarounds. MARC created fields for computer files, type of computer file, physical description fixed fields, file characteristics, and system details, but the values and fields were not designed around the complexity of video games. Platform information was often trapped in a system requirements note, a 538 field, or even improvised into an edition statement. The 500 general note became a place where catalogers put useful information that had no better structured field. RDA and later MARC 21 fields added more granular content, media, carrier, sound, video, and digital-file elements, but de Groat argues that these fields still describe simpler electronic resources better than interactive, multi-component games.

The subject and genre discussion is the article's strongest GBLS contribution. de Groat shows that Library of Congress Subject Headings and genre practices were built around books and works about games, not the cataloging of games themselves. Game-genre headings existed as topical headings for books about games rather than as genre/form headings for games. The distinction between "computer games," "video games," "electronic games," "simulation games," "software," and "interactive multimedia" remained unstable. Some headings could be used through workarounds; others could not. Platform or brand headings such as Sega Dreamcast video games were sometimes used even though platform is not a genre. de Groat concludes that the entire field of game genre headings and subdivisions needs overhaul.

Title authority practice creates another layer of difficulty. Library catalogs need titles not only to describe an item in hand but also to collocate versions, translations, adaptations, franchises, and works about games. de Groat explains that computer program titles moved awkwardly between subject and name authority files, while rules for preferred titles under RDA did not give specific guidance for video games. Catalogers had to check authority files and bibliographic files for conflicts, decide whether a game title conflicted with a film, television program, subject heading, or other game, and choose qualifiers such as "Computer game," "Video game," or "Game" without clear rules. This is especially important for games tied to franchises, ports, editions, sequels, and cross-media works.

For the larger GBLS project, de Groat provides historical grounding for the claim that game collections require infrastructure, not merely enthusiasm. If catalogs cannot reliably express platform, carrier, interactivity, genre, subject, franchise, preferred title, version, or relationship to other media, patrons and staff cannot easily discover, compare, recommend, preserve, or study games. This matters for public access, collection development, circulation, curriculum support, game studies scholarship, archives, and future digital preservation. The article pairs well with Welhouse et al. (2015) on narrative/plot metadata and with later artifact-metadata work by Lee et al. and McDonald et al.

The limitations are those of a standards-history article. de Groat does not evaluate patron search behavior, local catalog interfaces, or the effect of catalog records on circulation or use. It is also necessarily technical. Still, it is one of the most important infrastructure sources for GBLS because it shows that the metadata problem is not incidental; it is rooted in decades of rule changes, MARC constraints, Library of Congress policy gaps, and the mismatch between book-centered cataloging traditions and complex interactive media.
