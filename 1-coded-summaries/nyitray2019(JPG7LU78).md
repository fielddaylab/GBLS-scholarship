### Summary
# **Nyitray, K. J. (2019). Game on to game after: Sources for video game history. *Reference & User Services Quarterly, 59*(1), 7-11.**

Nyitray's Alert Collector column is a curated guide to sources for video game history. It is not a study of a library program, but it is directly relevant to academic and special-collections GBLS because it explains why video games require research support, preservation attention, and historically informed collecting. Nyitray writes from experience founding the William A. Higinbotham Game Studies Collection and frames video games as dominant entertainment, complex cultural artifacts, and increasingly legitimate objects of historical and critical inquiry.

The opening argument is definitional and historiographic. Nyitray notes that even defining "video game" can involve technical and philosophical debate, using *Tennis for Two*, *Computer Space*, and *Pong* to distinguish analog computer games, commercial arcade video games, and broader interactive screen-based game experiences. She argues that early writing on games often presented chronological facts for younger audiences, while recent work has moved toward critical video game historiography. Video games bring together computer science, arts, humanities, social sciences, design, industry, and popular culture, making them transdisciplinary objects rather than simple entertainment products.

The article emphasizes material and archival complexity. Video games can be studied through source code, ROM cartridges, platforms, controllers, circuits, corporate records, developers' papers, box art, manuals, and other ephemera. Arcade machines, consoles, joysticks, cartridges, and packaging provide tangible evidence of shared play experiences, while online games, app-based games, emulation, and digital distribution complicate preservation and access. Nyitray connects this to the growth of archives and documentation initiatives at institutions such as The Strong National Museum of Play, the Library of Congress, the Smithsonian, the Museum of the Moving Image, Stanford, the University of Texas, the University of Illinois, and the University of Michigan.

Most of the column is an annotated bibliography organized by reference works, visual histories, histories, culture and critical studies, industry, art, music, and digital collections. It highlights works such as *The Video Games Guide*, *Phoenix IV*, Brett Weiss's classic home-video-game guides, *The Game Console*, *Supercade*, *History of Video Games in 64 Objects*, *Replay*, *The Ultimate History of Video Games*, *Debugging Game History*, *Game After*, *Coin-Operated Americans*, *Racing the Beam*, *Console Wars*, *Masters of Doom*, *Blood, Sweat, and Pixels*, *Art of Atari*, *The Art of Video Games*, and *Music Video Games*. It also points to the Learning Games Initiative Research Archive and the Internet Archive Console Living Room.

For GBLS, Nyitray is useful as a research-support and collection-development source. It gives librarians vocabulary for explaining why game collections are not only recreational collections: games are primary sources, technological artifacts, art objects, industrial products, music/media forms, and cultural records. It also supports reference work for students and researchers entering game history, game studies, media archaeology, preservation, industry history, platform studies, and critical studies. In a GBLS review, it helps connect game services to special collections, archives, bibliographic instruction, and scholarly discovery.

The source's limitation is that it is a curated professional bibliography, not empirical evidence. It does not assess use of these sources, evaluate a game collection, or report patron outcomes. Its value lies in mapping the scholarly and reference terrain for video game history and in showing why academic and research libraries need robust source pathways, preservation partnerships, and descriptive practices for games and game-related ephemera.
