### Summary
# **Wang, D.-C., & Huang, Y.-M. (2023). Exploring the influence of competition and collaboration on learning performance in digital game-based learning. *Educational Technology Research and Development, 71*, 1547-1565. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11423-023-10247-8**

Wang and Huang examine how competition and collaboration interact in digital game-based learning. The article is not a library study, but it is relevant to GBLS as adjacent design evidence because many library game formats, including tournaments, escape rooms, team challenges, reading competitions, and gamified orientations, combine competitive and collaborative structures. The authors begin from the premise that competition can be a "double-edged sword": it may motivate engagement and improve performance, but it can also create stress, frustration, anxiety, or a focus on winning rather than learning. Collaboration may buffer those negative effects by providing social support, shared goals, discussion, negotiation, and mutual assistance.

The study developed an intergroup competition with intragroup collaboration game for question-bank practice. Students competed in two-person teams while also depending on teammates to complete different parts of the task. The authors built a research model linking perceived competition, perceived collaboration, perceived enjoyment, perceived usefulness, attitude toward using, behavioral intention, and self-reported learning performance. Data came from 108 students in one department in Taiwan, and the analysis used partial least squares structural equation modeling.

The findings emphasize the motivational power of competition within this particular design. Perceived competition had a stronger total effect on learning performance than perceived collaboration. Competition influenced behavioral intention directly and indirectly through perceived enjoyment, while collaboration influenced behavioral intention indirectly through perceived usefulness. Perceived competition also directly affected perceived collaboration, suggesting that intergroup competition can stimulate intragroup collaboration when team members share a common goal and have interdependent roles.

For GBLS, the article's most useful contribution is its design caution. Competition should not be treated as inherently good or bad. It can prompt excitement, acceptance, persistence, and team coordination, but it can also distort attention if the competition mechanism is too complex or if winning is disconnected from the learning or service goal. Wang and Huang argue that game designers should keep competition mechanisms simple and clear, link victory conditions to learning performance, divide roles fairly, and make the educational usefulness of the game explicit rather than relying only on entertainment value.

The limitations matter. Learning performance was self-reported rather than measured with an exam or performance artifact, the sample was drawn from one department in Taiwan, the game was a specific question-bank/Tetris-style system, and the authors explicitly caution that competition may not be more important than collaboration in all digital game-based learning contexts. The study should not be used as evidence that competitive library programs produce learning outcomes. Its proper use in GBLS is conceptual and methodological: it helps libraries think carefully about when competition supports a service goal, when collaboration should be built into the structure, and how assessment should distinguish enjoyment, intention to participate, collaboration, and actual learning or transfer.
