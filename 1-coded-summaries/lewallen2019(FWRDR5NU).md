### Summary
# **Lewallen, K. (2019). Curating breakout boxes. *Knowledge Quest, 48*(2), 42-46.**

Lewallen offers a practitioner introduction to breakout boxes as school-library resources for inquiry, collaboration, curriculum support, and student-centered play. The article is grounded in the AASL Standards Framework for Learners, especially the belief that learners should be prepared for college, career, and life and the Shared Foundations of Inquire and Collaborate. Lewallen argues that soft skills such as communication, collaboration, resilience, perseverance, critical thinking, and problem solving can be difficult to teach through ordinary classroom routines, but breakout boxes make them visible through playful, time-bound challenges.

The article defines breakout boxes as portable cousins of escape rooms. Instead of escaping from a room, learners work together to solve puzzles, open locks, and complete a scenario before time runs out. A typical kit can include a large box, small box, hasp, invisible-ink pen, UV flashlight, red lens viewers, reflection cards, and several reprogrammable locks. Because the locks can be reset, one kit can support many games, difficulty levels, subjects, and grade bands. Lewallen notes that games can be used to introduce a unit, review content, test comprehension at the end of a unit, support team building, or bring physical movement into instruction.

The school librarian's role is important but deliberately restrained. Lewallen describes the librarian as a "guide on the side" who offers hints as needed while letting students control gameplay. Students must search the room, share clues, communicate what they have found, connect evidence, recognize peers' different talents, and apply prior knowledge in a new context. Reflection questions included with games can help students examine their experience, peer feedback, and future strategies. In AASL terms, breakout boxes give learners opportunities to build new knowledge, work toward shared goals, think critically, and persist when they fail to solve a puzzle or complete the whole game.

The collection-development argument is also useful for GBLS. Lewallen treats breakout boxes as curatable school-library resources rather than one-off classroom activities. The school library can purchase or build kits, lend them to teachers, help match games to curriculum, create games for library orientation, host lunch or after-school breakout programs, run breakout clubs, and support students in designing their own games. Lewallen's own school used four kits in classrooms, sixth- and ninth-grade library orientations, informal library programs, and a weeklong summer breakout camp in which students both played and created games.

The article includes pragmatic implementation advice. BreakoutEDU supplies ready-made physical and digital games, including user-created games searchable by subject, grade, and format; some require subscriptions, while many are free. Teachers Pay Teachers, Pinterest, Facebook groups, librarian communities, and digital Google Sites breakouts provide additional resources. Lewallen recommends starting with an easy game, playing before designing, joining a professional community, and recognizing that older students may need time to learn this style of thinking.

For the GBLS project, Lewallen's value is practical and service-oriented. The article shows how breakout boxes can live at the intersection of collection development, instruction, teacher collaboration, orientation, programming, and student creation. It also clarifies why breakout boxes are attractive for school libraries: they are reusable, adaptable, collaborative, budget-scalable, and aligned with library inquiry goals. The limitations are equally clear. This is a professional guidance article, not a research study, and it does not report formal assessment data. It should be used as evidence of practitioner rationale and implementation patterns, not as proof of learning outcomes.
