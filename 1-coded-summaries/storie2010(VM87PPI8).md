### Summary
# **Storie, D. (2010). [Review of the book *Everyone plays at the library: Creating great gaming experiences for all ages*, by S. Nicholson]. *Journal of the Canadian Health Libraries Association / Journal de l'Association des bibliothèques de la santé du Canada, 31*, 123.**

Metadata note: the Zotero parent item is for Scott Nicholson's 2010 book *Everyone Plays at the Library*, but the attached PDF is Dale Storie's one-page book review. This entry summarizes the attached review rather than the full book.

Storie reviews Scott Nicholson's *Everyone Plays at the Library: Creating Great Gaming Experiences for All Ages*, positioning Nicholson as a leading figure in games and libraries and noting his roles at Syracuse University's School of Information Studies, the Library Game Lab of Syracuse, and ALA's Games and Gaming Member Initiatives Group. The review's central observation is that the book distinguishes itself by treating games as social experiences. Storie quotes Nicholson's emphasis that the important question is not the game itself but how games can be integrated into library programming and how different gaming experiences can meet different library goals.

The review describes the book as broadly relevant to many library types, with school and public libraries likely to benefit most. Public libraries are singled out because gaming programs can reach underserved patrons, provide recreational programs, and help the library function as a community hub. Storie also notes that creative librarians in other settings may find the book useful when designing classes or programs that use games. For GBLS, this matters because it frames game-based service around library goals and user experience rather than around a narrow format or technology.

Storie outlines the book's three-part structure. The first part introduces gaming and libraries and connects gaming programs to common library mission-statement goals. The second part functions like a readers' advisory tool for librarians looking to incorporate games into programs and services. It organizes game recommendations around five "gaming experience archetypes": knowledge, strategy, action, narrative, and social gaming. The third part covers logistical issues such as facilitation, marketing, and evaluation. Even though the review is brief, this structure is directly relevant to the GBLS review because it links selection, program design, service goals, facilitation, and assessment.

The review emphasizes inclusivity across game formats. Nicholson covers word games, board games, card games, live-action role-playing games, and "big games" such as murder mystery party games, rather than equating gaming with videogames. Storie praises Nicholson for pushing back against videogame marketing hype and for arguing that libraries do not need the newest or most heavily advertised games to run successful programs. Quality of play and enthusiastic staff are presented as more important than expensive technology. This point supports the GBLS project's recurring distinction between game form and service goal.

Storie also highlights the practical value of Nicholson's game reviews. Each review is categorized by gaming experience archetype and tied to common library goals. The reviews also include cost, number of players, complexity, recommended age group, time required, and skills used, such as strategic planning or teamwork. This is especially useful for GBLS because it describes the kind of advisory and program-planning metadata libraries need in order to select games responsibly for different audiences and settings.

For the larger GBLS project, this source should be used cautiously because it is a review of a book rather than the book itself. It does not provide independent empirical evidence, and it should not substitute for reading Nicholson's full framework if the book is later available. Still, it confirms that *Everyone Plays at the Library* was understood by contemporary reviewers as a practical, inclusive, mission-oriented guide to library gaming programs across formats and ages. It also reinforces several themes already present in the review: games as social experiences, games matched to library goals, broad analog/digital inclusion, the importance of facilitation, and the need for evaluation.
