### Summary
# **Maranchak, M., & Maranchak, N. (2023). Gamification mechanics usage for UGC promotion in online library marketing. *Ukrainian Journal on Library and Information Science*, (11), 21-33. https://doi.org/10.31866/2616-7654.11.2023.282652**

Maranchak and Maranchak examine how gamified mechanics can stimulate user-generated content (UGC) in the internet marketing of libraries. The article is directly relevant to GBLS because it extends gamification beyond instruction, escape rooms, orientations, and reading challenges into the everyday social-media work of library visibility, trust-building, and community interaction. Its premise is that libraries now compete for user attention in Web 2.0 environments and can benefit when users voluntarily create, tag, share, review, photograph, film, remix, or otherwise circulate content about library spaces, materials, services, and events.

The authors define UGC broadly as content created and distributed by users rather than by marketers: posts, comments, reviews, photos, videos, podcasts, ratings, comparisons, wiki-style entries, creative designs, and other social-media formats. They argue that UGC matters because it functions as a form of social proof and word-of-mouth marketing. When patrons post about a library, its spaces, its books, or its services, other users may perceive the institution as more trustworthy than they would if they encountered only direct advertising from the library itself.

Methodologically, the article uses descriptive, analytical, comparative, bibliographic, selective observation, grouping, and content-analysis methods. It analyzes the work of internet marketers for Ukrainian libraries and other brands, relevant scholarly and professional literature, and examples of social media mechanics. It is therefore not an outcomes study and does not measure user behavior before and after a library intervention. Its contribution is practical and conceptual: it identifies UGC formats, gamified activation strategies, and design components that library marketers can adapt.

The article's practical recommendations are useful for GBLS service design. Maranchak and Maranchak recommend that libraries begin by identifying what motivates their target audience and what UGC formats are comfortable for that audience on the relevant platform. Possible library-facing prompts include asking users to choose a book character for a Story, make a short Reels or TikTok video with a favorite book in the library, post a short review with a book photograph, tag the library account, use a proposed hashtag, or create visual content around a library space. The authors also discuss library-relevant examples such as user photographs of Kyiv libraries and tagged book-related Instagram content.

The authors propose several design components for gamified UGC activation: an idea aligned with the audience's interests, an accessible content format, a carefully chosen reward, a call to action, and a platform suited to the desired content. Rewards do not have to be material; users may value being reposted by the library account, appearing in Stories, gaining organic reach through collaboration features, or receiving emotional satisfaction from interaction with a favored institution. The authors also stress moderation and selection: libraries should choose content that fits relevance, reputation, legality, and minimum quality criteria, and if winners are selected, the process must be transparent to avoid reputational damage.

For the GBLS review, this source broadens the scope of gamification in libraries. A gamified service need not be a full game or a scheduled program; it can be a lightweight social-media challenge, creative prompt, visual installation, hashtag campaign, poll, quiz, status reward, AR filter, sticker, or participatory content mechanic. The article also connects online and offline design: art installations, photo zones, decorated interiors, readable signs, lighting, platform affordances, and social-media prompts can work together to invite patrons into voluntary creative participation.

The source's limitations are important. It relies on conceptual analysis, examples from marketing, and selective observation rather than rigorous library assessment. It also frames users partly through marketing language, which may sit uneasily with library values if implemented without care. Still, its strongest GBLS contribution is to show that gamification can support library-public relationships, user voice, trust, and participatory promotion when libraries design clear, voluntary, platform-appropriate activities and evaluate them through engagement, reactions, subscriptions, leads, number of participants, and quality of created content.
