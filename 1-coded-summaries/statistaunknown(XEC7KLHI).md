### Summary
# **Statista. (n.d.). *Industries & markets: Video games*.**

This Statista report is a market and audience compendium on video gaming worldwide. The PDF itself is titled "Video gaming worldwide" and is organized as a chart-based report across overview, gaming audiences, user engagement, devices, mobile gaming, gaming video content, purchasing behavior and spending, and esports. The Zotero record has no publication date, but the charts draw heavily on 2023 and 2024 data and include forecasts through 2029. The report is not an argument-driven article; it is a set of descriptive market indicators with brief explanatory notes, source notes, and chart captions.

The overview section frames video games as a large and growing media market. Statista estimates global video game market revenue at 475.04 billion U.S. dollars in 2024, with projected growth to 691.31 billion in 2029. It breaks this market into segments such as gaming hardware, in-game advertising, mobile games, online games, download games, cloud gaming, gaming live streaming, physically sold video games, and gaming networks. Other charts compare digital-game revenue across selected countries, market revenue by device, market share by segment, and market share by region. These charts position video games as a major global media industry rather than a niche entertainment form.

The audience and engagement sections are the most useful parts for GBLS background. Statista forecasts the number of video game users in the games segment rising to 3.02 billion by 2029. A Q2 2024 global internet-user survey reports that 84.1 percent of internet users played video games on some device, with especially high participation among respondents aged 16 to 24. The age-and-gender chart reports similar levels of play among young women and men globally, and continued participation among older groups, though at lower rates. The genre charts show shooter and action-adventure games as broadly popular, while also listing sports, MOBA, racing, puzzle platform, simulation, strategy, action RPG, role-playing, MMO, and party games.

Several charts connect gaming to everyday life and wellbeing claims. A 2023 survey of weekly gamers in selected countries found substantial shares saying games helped them feel less stressed, less anxious, less isolated, happier, or better able to get through difficult times. The report does not test those claims or establish causal effects, but it does show that many players describe video games in emotional, social, and coping terms rather than only as entertainment purchases. Another chart on pre-purchase research channels suggests that video reviews, friends and family, print reviews, brand websites, and expert advice all matter to players deciding what to buy.

The devices section is important for libraries because it shows how distributed video-game access has become. Globally, smartphones were the leading device for play in Q2 2024, with 70.1 percent of internet users reporting play on smartphones, compared with 35.8 percent on laptop or desktop computers, 23.7 percent on game consoles, 17.8 percent on tablets, 11.2 percent on handheld gaming devices, 8.7 percent on media streaming devices, and 6.5 percent on VR headsets. U.S. survey data similarly placed smartphones ahead of consoles, PCs/laptops, tablets, smart TVs, mobile game consoles, and streaming devices. Later sections extend the market picture through mobile-game revenue and downloads, gaming video content, Twitch and YouTube gaming audiences, store-brand purchasing, and esports.

For the larger GBLS project, the report is useful as contextual evidence that video games are mainstream, multi-platform cultural media. It helps justify why libraries might treat video games as part of contemporary media literacy, collection development, programming, research support, and public culture. It also cautions against equating video gaming only with consoles or teen programs: patrons may encounter games through phones, PCs, streaming platforms, esports, mobile apps, online video, marketplaces, and social research channels.

The report should be cited carefully. It is proprietary market intelligence rather than peer-reviewed library research, and many figures are estimates, forecasts, or survey results drawn from secondary sources such as DataReportal, GWI, We Are Social, Newzoo, YouGov, Sony, AppMagic, TwitchTracker, Stream Hatchet, and others. It offers no evidence about library services, patron outcomes, instruction, circulation, access equity, or program design. Its best placement is a brief contextual sentence in the introduction, where it can update older general claims about video games as mainstream cultural practice without carrying weight in the review's evidence claims.
