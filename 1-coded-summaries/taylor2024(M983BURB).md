### Summary
# **Taylor, E., & Liew, C. L. (2024). Depiction of library use in video games: A content analysis. *Journal of Documentation, 80*(1), 158-179. https://doi.org/10.1108/JD-03-2023-0046**

Taylor and Liew examine how video games depict characters using libraries to seek and use information. The article is not a study of a library program, but it is directly relevant to GBLS because it treats video games as culturally significant texts through which real users' expectations, desires, and anxieties about libraries may be reflected and shaped. The authors position the study against a literature that has focused more often on fictional libraries and librarians than on fictional library users and their information behavior.

The article defines library use as interaction with library spaces or services in support of a user-defined process or need, drawing on Fleming-May's process-oriented account of library use. It also frames information behavior broadly, including seeking, use, sharing, serendipity, avoidance, thoughts, emotions, and context. The analysis combines information studies and game studies: Tancheva's semiotic analysis of fictional libraries, Carr's textual approach to video games, Carroll's character analysis, and Chatman's insider-outsider theory all shape the coding and interpretation.

The authors used qualitative content analysis on a purposive sample of 15 single-player video games released since 2010 and available on platforms accessible to the researchers. The sample crossed genres and production contexts, including *Assassin's Creed Origins*, *Dragon Age: Inquisition*, *Dragon Quest XI S*, *Eastshade*, *Final Fantasy VIII Remastered*, *Fran Bow*, *Monkey Island 2 Special Edition*, *NieR Replicant*, *Night in the Woods*, *OneShot*, *Persona 5*, *Psychonauts 2*, *Skyrim*, *Stardew Valley*, and *The Sinking City*. The first author played from the beginning of each game to the library-use segments, replayed those segments using varied playstyles, viewed footage of endings to contextualize library scenes, and recorded notes and screenshots. The unit of analysis was any element related to library use and users' information behavior, including dialogue, visual design, mechanics, cutscenes, actions before library visits, activity inside libraries, and outcomes afterward.

The findings show two broad patterns of library use. Some protagonists are problem solvers who visit libraries because they need a clue, document, object, person, or method to overcome a specific obstacle. Others are improvement seekers who use libraries to study, bring artifacts for analysis, gain bonuses, deepen relationships, or pursue optional self-development. Across the sample, libraries provide documents, tools, quiet spaces, experts, helpful intellectuals, and opportunities for companionship. They also frequently appear as safe intermissions within hostile or unstable worlds, places where players can slow down, read, talk, decode local histories, and connect the protagonist's personal journey to broader community context.

Taylor and Liew also find that game protagonists are often outsiders: travelers, returnees, prisoners, adventurers, or strangers learning unfamiliar worlds. Library use helps them shift from outsider status toward partial belonging. Librarians, companions, and other users direct protagonists to resources, explain unfamiliar information, or create barriers that the protagonist eventually overcomes. Information seeking is usually more streamlined than real-world library use because games give clear objectives and satisfying progress structures, but the depictions remain socially embedded: protagonists share information, rely on guides, bond with companions, encounter local histories, and sometimes uncover difficult truths about their worlds.

For the larger GBLS project, this article supports the claim that games belong in library collections and scholarship not only as popular entertainment but as cultural media that represent libraries, users, information behavior, archives, local memory, and belonging. It also gives service-design implications. If games repeatedly imagine libraries as places where outsiders become oriented, where local histories become legible, and where users both receive help and contribute back, real GBLS programs can build around those expectations through onboarding, advisory, displays, local-history game discussions, playable archives, and reflective programming. The article's limitations are also important: it analyzes a small, purposive sample; interpretation depends on one primary researcher-player; and fictional depictions cannot be treated as direct evidence of user outcomes. Its value for GBLS is conceptual and cultural rather than evaluative.
