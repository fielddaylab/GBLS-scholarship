### Summary
# **Seay, J. A. (2018). Considering games and gamification in libraries & associated entities: In praise of real people. *Against the Grain, 30*(5), 86-87.**

Seay's column is a short professional essay rather than a research study, but it captures a useful argument within library gaming discourse: analog, immersive, co-present games can support learning in ways that many individual screen-based educational games do not. The piece appears in a column on games and gamification in libraries and associated organizations, and it continues Seay's interest in analog games, escape rooms, and educational gaming. Its tone is informal, but the argument is clear enough to be useful for the GBLS review as a conceptual and practitioner-facing source.

The column begins with a learning problem: people forget a large proportion of newly presented information, and attention in lecture settings is limited. Seay invokes the forgetting curve and classroom attention-span discussions to argue that conventional information delivery struggles to produce durable learning. He then lists characteristics of effective learning experiences: they are impactful, relevant, engaging, motivating, inspiring, and playful. Games are attractive because they can combine these qualities, but Seay cautions that simply calling something an educational game is not enough.

The main distinction in the piece is between digital learning games and analog, socially immersive games. Seay acknowledges the power of digital technologies, including virtual reality, and notes that digital games dominate much of the educational games market. At the same time, he argues that many routine digital educational games are used for practice, rote learning, dexterity, or memorization, and that they often create one-person-one-screen experiences even when learners occupy the same room. For the GBLS project, this critique is useful because it helps explain why library game services should not automatically privilege digital formats as the most innovative or educationally meaningful.

Seay proposes a category he calls "collaborative multi-player immersive games." By this he means analog activities that put groups of players into a problem, issue, or scenario and give them mechanics for cooperative or contested interaction. The core features are real-time, group, social interaction in an immersive environment. Escape rooms are his most familiar example, described as a form of problem-based learning in a room. He also briefly introduces educational live action role-playing games and megagames as related forms that use physical presence, role-play, factional goals, research, writing, and large-scale social dynamics.

The column's discussion of Edu-LARP is especially relevant to library instruction and outreach. Seay describes educational live action role-playing as experiential learning that engages cognitive, affective, and behavioral dimensions. He points to historical and civic scenarios and to Barnard College's Reacting to the Past model, where students supplement role-play with research and writing. This overlaps with other GBLS sources on TTRPGs, LARPs, and library escape rooms because it highlights role assumption, scenario design, embodied interaction, and debriefable experience rather than only puzzle completion.

The piece also ties analog play to the "high tech high touch" idea: as technology becomes more pervasive, people may value slower, chatty, face-to-face, co-present interaction. Seay argues that analog game experiences make verbal and nonverbal communication central. This helps GBLS articulate why board games, escape rooms, LARPs, and megagames are not merely lower-tech substitutes for digital games. They can be deliberately chosen when the service goal is social interaction, collaborative problem solving, peer communication, motivation, or embodied engagement.

For the GBLS project, this source should be used cautiously. It is not empirical evidence, does not report a library case study, and uses broad claims about attention, retention, and learning. Still, it offers useful practitioner language for distinguishing game forms by affordance: digital games may excel at simulation, visualization, and individual practice, while analog immersive games may excel at co-presence, group dynamics, communication, and social learning. It therefore supports review sections that ask libraries to match game form to service goal and to treat facilitation and social design as central rather than incidental.
