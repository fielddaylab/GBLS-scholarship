### Summary
# **Ponto, K., Tredinnick, R., Verbeke, M., Kopp, K., Swanson, L., & Gagnon, D. (2023). Waddle: Using virtual penguin embodiment as a vehicle for empathy and informal learning. In *Proceedings of the 29th ACM Symposium on Virtual Reality Software and Technology (VRST 2023)*. ACM. https://doi.org/10.1145/3611659.3617211**

Ponto, Tredinnick, Verbeke, Kopp, Swanson, and Gagnon present *Waddle*, a 20-minute virtual reality experience designed to support informal learning and empathy by embodying the user as an Adélie penguin. The paper is a short ACM extended abstract rather than a full-length library article, and it does not study a library program. Its relevance to GBLS is therefore transferable: it offers evidence about how an immersive, game-like, narrative VR experience can be designed and assessed for informal learning outcomes that libraries might care about when they host VR, STEM, ecology, or experiential learning programs.

The design centers on virtual embodiment. The authors situate the work in research on embodied learning, empathy, perspective-taking, and animal embodiment in VR. They note that earlier animal embodiment experiences have sometimes struggled because the learner's movements do not align well with the animal body. *Waddle* responds by emphasizing gesture congruence and visuomotor synchrony. The learner enters a snowy Antarctic scene from a low penguin-height perspective, sees a beak where their nose would be, sees a penguin body and flippers when looking down, and can view their penguin avatar in an icy mirror. Quest 2 hand tracking is used so users can act without controllers.

The experience takes users through major events in an Adélie penguin's life. They build a nest by picking up rocks with their beak, find a mate, perform a mating dance, and defend an egg from a Skua attack. The experience was developed with educational game designers, VR researchers, and research scientists. This collaborative design process matters for GBLS because it shows how playful informal learning experiences can combine scientific content, interaction design, narrative structure, and embodied mechanics rather than relying on passive display or novelty alone.

The study involved 43 students in grades 3 through 8, ages 8 through 13, recruited in groups from informal learning organizations during a science festival on a major university campus. The authors measured empathy with the Dispositional Empathy with Nature scale, embodiment with a variation of the Avatar Embodiment Questionnaire, and informal learning with open-ended questions about life events and challenges for Adélie penguins. Scores for empathy and embodiment were averaged on a scale from -4 to 4, pre/post differences were tested with paired t-tests, and relationships were examined with Pearson correlations.

Results suggest that *Waddle* supported learning, embodiment, and empathy. The article reports learning gains, an overall positive embodiment score, and a statistically significant increase in empathy from pre-experience to post-experience. It also reports a significant positive correlation between embodiment and post-experience empathy. The authors conclude that direct embodiment of a penguin may be possible in VR and that non-human virtual embodiment may be a useful tool for education and appreciation of other creatures.

For the GBLS project, the source is not evidence about library implementation, staffing, collection policy, or program assessment. It should not be used to claim that libraries have successfully run *Waddle* or that VR programs automatically produce empathy. Its contribution is narrower: it provides a design-and-assessment example for immersive, playful informal learning that could inform library VR programming. It reinforces lessons from library VR sources that content quality, embodiment, interaction, audience, accessibility, and learning goals matter more than putting patrons into a headset for novelty's sake.

The limitations are substantial but clear. The paper is brief, the sample is small, the setting is a science festival rather than a library, and the article provides only compressed detail about the learning measures. Still, for a GBLS review that includes VR, AR, and immersive technology, *Waddle* is a useful adjacent example of how a game-like VR experience can be tied to empathy, informal STEM learning, ecology, and embodied perspective-taking.
