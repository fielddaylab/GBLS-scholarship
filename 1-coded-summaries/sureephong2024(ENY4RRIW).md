### Summary
# **Sureephong, P., Chernbumroong, S., Niemsup, S., Homla, P., Intawong, K., & Puritat, K. (2024). Exploring the impact of the gamified metaverse on knowledge acquisition and library anxiety in academic libraries. *Information Technology and Libraries, 43*(1). https://doi.org/10.5860/ital.v43i1.16651**

Sureephong, Chernbumroong, Niemsup, Homla, Intawong, and Puritat examine whether a gamified metaverse platform can promote academic library services, increase first-year students' knowledge of library services, and reduce library anxiety. The article was published in *Information Technology and Libraries* with an editorial note dated October 21, 2024, stating that works cited in one endnote were fake and that the remaining citations had been verified. That note should temper how the literature review is used, but the study's reported design, intervention, and findings remain directly relevant to GBLS because they compare a metaverse-based library program with a traditional library program.

The project took place at Chiang Mai University and centered on an academic library in the Faculty of Humanities. The authors developed a digital twin of the library using photogrammetry, Meshroom, and the Unity game engine, then delivered it through Meta Quest 2 virtual reality devices. The virtual library represented the physical space and included interaction with books, computers, online resources, waypoints, virtual desks, and a user interface for navigation and search. Gamification elements included quests, challenges, points, levels, badges, and a leaderboard.

The metaverse intervention included four gamified services: Library Orientation, Research Support, Book Services, and Technology Services. In Library Orientation, students explored the virtual library, followed waypoints, and read signs or information. In Research Support and Technology Services, students interacted with virtual librarians and completed questions or tasks. In Book Services, students navigated shelves and used the Dewey Decimal Classification system to find books. The comparison group completed the same basic content in the physical library through a traditional tour, checkpoints, librarian explanations, questions, and assigned tasks.

Methodologically, the study used an explanatory sequential mixed-methods design with a randomized controlled trial. Eighty first-year students who had never used the university library were randomly assigned to a traditional library program group or a metaverse-based library program group, with 40 participants in each group. Both groups completed pre- and post-tests measuring knowledge acquisition and library anxiety. The knowledge questionnaire contained 30 questions adapted from an earlier gamified mobile orientation study. The anxiety measure was adapted from Kannegiser's modified version of Bostick's Library Anxiety Scale, covering barriers with library staff, affective barriers, comfort with the library, and knowledge of the library. The metaverse group also completed open-ended questions about the platform.

The knowledge findings are clear but modest. Both groups began with similar pre-test scores and improved substantially after the intervention. The traditional group moved from a mean of 2.37 to 17.50, and the metaverse group moved from 2.45 to 18.10. However, the difference in pre-post gains between the two groups was not statistically significant, and the effect size was small. In GBLS terms, the gamified metaverse worked as an orientation and library-services learning environment, but it did not outperform a traditional librarian-led program on knowledge acquisition.

The library anxiety findings are more cautionary. The traditional program produced significant improvements on four of five anxiety questions: comfort asking librarians for help, seeing libraries as welcoming places, feeling less overwhelmed, and knowing how to use services and resources. The metaverse program produced significant improvements only for feeling less overwhelmed and knowing how to use services and resources. It did not significantly improve comfort asking librarians for help, sense that libraries are welcoming, or perceptions that librarians are available for research and technology support. Qualitative comments help explain why: some students found the virtual librarian robotic, unapproachable, unable to communicate adequately, and lacking the personal touch and empathy of a real librarian.

Students nevertheless responded positively to gamification. Open-ended responses described the platform as interesting, engaging, fun, and useful for learning about library resources. Students appreciated quests, points, levels, leaderboards, competition, and the novelty of exploring the virtual library. Some also reported that the virtual environment helped them understand how the library worked without feeling lost or overwhelmed. At the same time, participants raised concerns about expensive VR equipment, accessibility, nausea, dizziness, and loss of balance.

For the GBLS project, this article is valuable because it separates several outcomes that are often collapsed in discussion of immersive library technology. A metaverse platform can be engaging, motivating, and effective for factual knowledge acquisition without being better than traditional instruction. It can reduce some spatial or procedural anxiety without reducing anxiety tied to human help-seeking and staff relationships. It also shows that game elements may improve perceived engagement even when the larger technology introduces cost, expertise, accessibility, and physical-comfort barriers. The source therefore supports a cautious service-design principle: match immersive and gamified forms to the parts of library learning they are good at, and preserve human facilitation where trust, empathy, and help-seeking matter.

The limitations are significant. The study is local to one university context, uses short-term post-tests rather than retention or later behavior, and does not fully explore prior VR experience, which could affect both engagement and side effects. The metaverse used NPC-style virtual librarians rather than human avatars or live chat, so its weak performance on librarian interaction should not be generalized to all social VR designs. Still, the article is a useful empirical companion to other GBLS sources on mobile orientation, AR tours, VR programming, and assessment beyond enjoyment.
