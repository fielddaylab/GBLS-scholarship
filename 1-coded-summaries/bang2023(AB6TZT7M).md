### Summary
# **Bang, H. J., Li, L., & Flynn, K. (2023). Efficacy of an adaptive game-based math learning app to support personalized learning and improve early elementary school students' learning. *Early Childhood Education Journal, 51*(4), 717-732. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10643-022-01332-3**

Bang, Li, and Flynn evaluate *My Math Academy*, a supplemental adaptive game-based math learning app for kindergarten and first-grade students. The article is situated in early childhood education rather than library services. Its central problem is the difficulty teachers face when classrooms include students with widely varying math knowledge, especially in high-needs schools where early opportunity gaps can become persistent achievement gaps. The authors frame adaptive digital games as one possible way to support personalized learning, embedded assessment, feedback, and practice aligned to students' zones of proximal development.

The intervention is a mastery-based Personalized Mastery Learning System for early number sense and operations. *My Math Academy* includes more than 130 game-based activities across 96 concepts and skills from pre-kindergarten through second grade. The system uses in-game preassessments, learning trajectories, evidence-centered design, adaptive algorithms, scaffolding, formative feedback, and teacher dashboards. Students move through teaching activities, difficulty levels, and boss activities; mastery evidence from play determines which activity and difficulty level the system recommends next. The authors emphasize developmentally appropriate interface design, story contexts for math problems, low cognitive load, audio and visual support, and tap/drag/drop interactions suited to young learners.

The study used a blocked cluster randomized design in 41 classrooms across 11 high-needs elementary schools in two Southern California districts. Teachers were randomly assigned within school and grade to implement *My Math Academy* or continue with business-as-usual math curricula and interventions. The analytic sample included 886 kindergarten and first-grade students who completed both pre- and post-assessments. Treatment teachers were asked to use the app for at least 60 minutes per week as a supplement to their core math curriculum; actual usage averaged about 14.79 hours over 12 to 13 weeks. Data sources included a researcher-developed math assessment, in-app usage data, teacher surveys, teacher interviews, and classroom observations.

The results favored the treatment condition, though modestly. Students using *My Math Academy* outperformed control-group peers on the posttest with a small but statistically significant effect size of 0.11. Effects were stronger for kindergarten students and for some of the most advanced skills assessed. Within the treatment group, the cumulative number of skills mastered in the app correlated strongly with posttest performance, while time spent in the app correlated only weakly. Teachers described the app as easy to implement, useful for differentiation, motivating for students, and valuable because children could work independently while teachers supported other students. They especially valued embedded assessments, adaptiveness to students' current level, and feedback that helped students persist through challenging concepts.

For the larger GBLS project, the article is useful mainly as a contrast case rather than as direct evidence. It demonstrates a mature educational-technology model for adaptive game-based learning, including embedded assessment, learning trajectories, dashboards, and sustained use in authentic classrooms. Those design ideas may inspire library instruction games or youth programming, especially when libraries want games to offer individualized feedback rather than one-size-fits-all play. However, the study does not involve libraries, librarians, library collections, information literacy, public programming, game circulation, or community-based library services. Its service system is the elementary classroom, with teachers as implementers and math achievement as the outcome. For that reason, it should not be used as evidence for GBLS impact except when briefly discussing the broader educational-game literature that GBLS borrows from.
