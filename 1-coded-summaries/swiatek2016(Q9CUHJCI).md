### Summary
# **Swiatek, C., & Gorsse, M. (2016). Playing games at the library: Seriously? *LIBER Quarterly, 26*(2), 83-101. https://doi.org/10.18352/lq.10161**

Swiatek and Gorsse provide a substantial overview of gaming initiatives and management issues in French academic libraries. The article begins from the observation that academic libraries had historically treated games as minor, youthful, or more appropriate to public libraries, but that French academic libraries increasingly use games for documentary research training, student welcome activities, public engagement, cultural programming, and organizational innovation. The authors focus on non-virtual and public-oriented games already organized in and by libraries, including board games, mystery games, alternate reality games, live-action role playing, training games, and playful research challenges.

The article's examples are especially useful for GBLS. The University Pierre et Marie Curie library received an innovation award for murder-party events during first-year welcome week. INSA Lyon ran a two-month alternate reality game that began virally and virtually and ended in a real library event. The University of Haute-Alsace ran a Cluedo-like rally that became part of students' curriculum. Other examples include Zombibli, a motion-recognition game about library rules; BnF/Gallica's weekly document-discovery puzzle; Montpellier's OSNI Hunter, which invited users to identify unusual scientific objects both online and in the library; and law-library training built around transforming a new student avatar into a fully equipped first-year law student through documentary research.

The authors argue that gaming activities need more than enthusiasm. In many academic libraries, gaming initiatives rely on individual staff skill and personal involvement while lacking explicit service definition, budget, external communication, publicity, knowledge management, and a formal place in organizational charts or job descriptions. They recommend that libraries establish a gaming strategy and calendar, connect games to institutional goals, gather user needs, learn from colleagues and gamers, collaborate with publics where appropriate, and decide whether games are a means, an end, or both.

One of the article's strongest GBLS contributions is its management vocabulary. Swiatek and Gorsse distinguish a strategic game manager from an operational game designer. The game manager does not need to be an experienced gamer; that role advocates, translates, coordinates, builds policy, manages communication, supports knowledge management, and connects collaborators and partners. The game designer or game expert creates, facilitates, adapts, and evaluates the activity, and may come from outside librarianship. This distinction helps libraries avoid assuming that every librarian must become a game designer while also showing that games require real professional labor.

The article also treats staff training and internal communication as core infrastructure. Staff resistance can be stronger than administrative resistance when workers cannot identify with the activity, so the authors recommend formal support from leadership, internal communication, staff awareness, and training through play. Examples include staff team-building puzzles, classification-based treasure hunts, decision-making games, and game-like design competitions for training creators. The point is not simply morale; staff need to understand how games connect to library missions, user mediation, and professional skills.

For GBLS, the article is valuable because it frames games as services that require organizational change. The authors emphasize knowledge management, reports, reusable documentation, web publication, conference presentations, posters, professional networking, and feedback loops. Visibility can help secure budgets, stabilize games as services, and justify job-description changes. They also warn that students and users may resist poorly designed or poorly mediated games, especially when games feel disconnected from disciplinary or practical goals. This reinforces the review's broader argument that game-based library services need purpose, fit, facilitation, assessment, and sustainability planning.

The limitations are those of an overview and professional essay rather than a formal empirical study. The article does not provide systematic evaluation data across the French examples. Still, it is one of the stronger operational sources for GBLS because it moves beyond whether libraries can host games toward how games become legitimate, managed, communicated, staffed, and sustained parts of academic-library service.
