### Summary
# **Horban, Y., & Skachenko, O. (2019). A library in the game: Teaching information literacy and preventing plagiarism. *Bibliotechnyi Visnyk, 5*(253), 26-33. https://doi.org/10.15407/bv2019.05.026**

Horban and Skachenko provide a Ukrainian-language survey of how foreign university libraries use games and gamification to teach information literacy, citation practice, academic integrity, and plagiarism prevention. The article is written from the perspective of the Scientific Library of Kyiv National University of Culture and Arts and explicitly notes that, in Ukrainian library practice, gamification of library activity, information literacy formation, and anti-plagiarism instruction through games had not yet received much specialized study. The authors therefore set out to describe international examples and their potential relevance for Ukrainian academic libraries.

The article uses content analysis, analytical-synthetic processing, and generalization rather than a local implementation study. It reviews library and education literature on gamification, games for learning, and information literacy. James Paul Gee, Maura Smale, Mary Broussard, Jessica Urick Oberlin, Karen Markey, Jennifer McCabe, Steven Wise, and other authors appear as key points of reference. The article also cites the 2018 Horizon Report and game-industry data to argue that games are increasingly important educational technologies and that game-based approaches are especially familiar to students who grew up in digital environments.

The most useful part of the article is its annotated account of digital games and tutorials used by academic libraries in the United States, Canada, and Portugal. Horban and Skachenko discuss the University of Florida's *Gaming Against Plagiarism* project, including its three mini-games: *Cheats and Geeks*, *Frenetic Filing*, and *Murky Misconduct*. In their description, these games ask students to recognize plagiarism, falsification, fabrication, poor citation, insufficient paraphrase, self-plagiarism, and other research-misconduct problems through board-game, sorting, timed, and detective mechanics. The authors also summarize *Gamàgio*, a Portuguese platform game for plagiarism prevention; Lycoming College's *Goblin Threat Plagiarism Game*; Rutgers-Camden's *The Cite is Right!* anti-plagiarism game show; Indiana University's plagiarism tutorials and tests; James Madison University's *Tic-Tac-Toe Citation Game* and *Magnetic Keyword*; the University of Washington's APA and MLA Citation Game; and Acadia University's *You Quote It, You Note It!* citation tutorial.

Across these examples, the authors identify recurring affordances of game-based library activity: challenge, curiosity, risk, competition, and feedback. Many of the games use multiple-choice questions, hidden or timed tasks, points, levels, certificates, progress tracking, avatars, drag-and-drop citation ordering, immediate feedback, repeat attempts, and increasingly difficult questions. The authors argue that these mechanics can motivate students to improve information literacy, choose correct citation types, use reliable research tools, understand academic integrity, and avoid plagiarism in research writing.

For the GBLS project, the article is useful in three ways. First, it shows that game-based information literacy and anti-plagiarism instruction had become visible enough internationally by 2019 to be synthesized for Ukrainian academic librarians, extending the review's geographic frame beyond North American and Anglophone implementation reports. Second, it brings together a set of anti-plagiarism and citation games that can be cross-referenced with more detailed primary sources already represented in the review, especially Broussard and Oberlin, McCabe and Wise, Smale, and Markey et al. Third, it reinforces a practical design point: library games for information literacy tend to work through feedback, repetition, clear goals, short duration, and alignment between game actions and instructional purpose.

The source should be used cautiously. It is a narrative review and descriptive scan, not an empirical assessment of patron learning, and many examples are drawn from public web resources rather than direct observation. The English abstract also summarizes the claims more clearly than the extracted Ukrainian text permits for some details. Still, the article belongs in the GBLS review because it shows how anti-plagiarism and citation games circulate internationally as a recognizable academic-library service model and because it explicitly frames gamified information-literacy instruction as a response to academic dishonesty in higher education.
