### Summary
# **Janik, J. (2021). Intra-acting bio-object: A posthuman approach to the player-game relation. *Journal of Gaming & Virtual Worlds, 13*(1), 21-39. https://doi.org/10.1386/jgvw_00026_1**

Janik develops a theoretical framework for understanding the relation between a video game and a player as a posthuman, meaning-making process. The article argues that game studies has often centered the human player through concepts such as avatar, agency, immersion, implied player, and player experience. Even when scholars recognize that player freedom is constrained by rules and systems, analysis still often begins from the player. Janik instead asks what happens if the game object is treated as an equal partner in play rather than as a secondary container for human agency.

The conceptual framework joins Tadeusz Kantor's theatrical idea of the bio-object with Karen Barad's new-materialist concept of intra-action. Kantor's bio-object describes an indivisible but tense stage relation between actor and object: the actor animates the object, but the object also frames, limits, and shapes the actor's movement and meaning. Barad's intra-action helps Janik describe how entities do not simply preexist their relations; their boundaries, properties, and meanings emerge through relational material-discursive practices. Janik applies these ideas to gameplay, arguing that player and game form a temporary bio-object during play.

The article uses examples from video game play, glitches, subversive player practices, and games that resist player control to show how meaning emerges through tension. The player animates the game by playing, interpreting, exploring, modifying, or exploiting it, but the game also structures action through software, hardware, audiovisual form, rules, limits, saved states, anti-piracy mechanisms, glitches, and unexpected behaviors. When a player encounters a glitch, a resistant system, or an apparently autonomous game object, the game can become visible as more than a tool. The player and game then co-produce meanings that belong to neither one alone.

Janik's larger claim is that agency and meaning-making should be understood as distributed across the player-game relation. The player does not simply impose meaning on the game, and the game does not simply deliver designed meaning to the player. The player and game intra-actively shape each other: the player becomes a specific player through the encounter, and the game becomes a specific played object through the player's actions, data, interpretations, and traces. The bio-object remains tense because the game and player stay linked but distinct; this difference is what allows meaning to emerge.

For the GBLS project, this article is only indirectly useful. It offers a vocabulary for thinking about games as active cultural and technological objects rather than neutral containers for library programming. That could matter when discussing why game-based services should attend to rules, systems, interfaces, glitches, platform constraints, and player interpretation. It may also help explain why patrons' experiences of the same game can diverge depending on their bodies, histories, skills, expectations, and interactions with the game object.

However, this source should remain outside the main literature review. It is not library literature, not an empirical study of learning or service, and not a discussion of collections, programming, outreach, access, or advisory. Its theoretical vocabulary is more specialized than the review currently needs. It can be retained as excluded adjacent game-studies theory, useful if the project later develops a deeper conceptual section on player-game ontology, but it should not be used as evidence for GBLS practice or outcomes.
