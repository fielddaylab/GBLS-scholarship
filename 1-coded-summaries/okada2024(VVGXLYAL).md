# **Okada, T. (2024). *LSHM: TTRPG To The Bitter End*. Google Docs.**

This short Low Stress High Mayhem resource document supports a streamed session of Takuma Okada's *To the Bitter End!* It identifies the game, links to the Twitch VOD, points to Okada's social and itch.io pages, and notes that the game appears in *The Ultimate Micro-RPG Book*, edited by James D'Amato. The handout then gathers play-alikes, safety tools, GameRT links, a Twitch chat policy, and a brief stream outline.

The play-alike section frames *To the Bitter End!* around "versus vibes," with recommendations such as *Halo 'n' Horns*, *First Dig Two Graves*, *Swords as Subtext*, *High Noon*, *A Duel with Shadows*, and *Gunslingin' in Space*. The safety section points viewers toward *Script Change*, *The Door Is Open*, and the TTRPG Safety Toolkit. The Twitch policy emphasizes kindness, respect, no hate speech, moderator intervention, harassment reporting, and private communication options.

The stream outline is the most program-relevant part of the document. It lays out a practical sequence: tech check, introduction and rules, character creation, explanation of play flow, actual play, emotional check-in, discussion of rules and playtesting, favorite moments, programming potential, safety-tool considerations, and chat questions. This makes the item useful as a trace of how GameRT translated a small TTRPG into a facilitated online event.

For the larger GBLS project, the document may be useful as a practitioner example of game advisory and program scaffolding. It shows how a streamed tabletop program can combine creator attribution, discovery pathways, play-alike recommendations, safety resources, community moderation policy, and a structured post-play discussion. These elements could inform practical guidance for libraries designing low-preparation TTRPG streams or online game demonstrations.

However, this source should remain outside the main literature review. It is not scholarship, does not report a study, and does not describe a library program in enough detail to support claims about GBLS outcomes or implementation. Its best use is as an excluded primary artifact that may later support an appendix on advisory metadata, safety tooling, or streamed game-program planning.
