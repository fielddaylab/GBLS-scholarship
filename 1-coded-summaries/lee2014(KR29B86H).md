### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Karlova, N., Clarke, R. I., Thornton, K., & Perti, A. (2014). Facet analysis of video game genres. In *iConference 2014 Proceedings* (pp. 125-139). https://doi.org/10.9776/14057**

Lee, Karlova, Clarke, Thornton, and Perti address one of the hardest game-discovery problems: video game "genre" is important for access, but existing genre labels are unstandardized, undefined, and overloaded with multiple kinds of information. The paper is part of the University of Washington and Seattle Interactive Media Museum metadata-schema work, but it narrows in on genre as a faceted classification problem. The authors argue that video games cannot be adequately described by one flat genre term because labels often mix gameplay, narrative, theme, mood, setting, point of view, target audience, presentation, artistic style, purpose, and endings.

The study used facet analysis. The authors gathered 804 instances of genre labels from game websites, online directories, encyclopedias, commercial and fan systems, and game studies literature, including Allgame, GameFAQs, GameSpot, MobyGames, IGN, GiantBomb, DMOZ, Ranker, Wikipedia, Amazon, and prior scholarly genre systems. After removing duplicates, conceptual overlaps, and labels outside the scope of games, they used card sorting to organize labels into mutually exclusive categories and then named facets according to Spiteri's principles of differentiation, relevance, ascertainability, permanence, homogeneity, mutual exclusivity, and fit to the domain.

The resulting scheme contains 12 facets and 358 foci. The facets are Gameplay, Style, Purpose, Target Audience, Presentation, Artistic Style, Temporal Aspect, Point of View, Theme, Setting, Mood/Affect, and Type of Ending. Some facets are hierarchical: Style functions as a Gameplay subfacet, Theme includes parent and child terms, and Setting is divided into spatial and temporal subtypes. The examples show why a faceted approach matters: RPG, platformer, education, party, 2D, cel-shaded, turn-based, first person, dinosaurs, restaurant, spaceship, medieval, horror, peaceful, finite, and branching describe different dimensions that ordinary genre labels often collapse together.

The paper also surfaces conceptual problems that matter to catalogers and advisory staff. Gameplay is treated as the most fundamental facet, but familiar terms such as Action can become catch-all categories. Style describes distinctive gameplay modes such as platformer, rhythm, tower defense, or run-and-gun. Presentation and Artistic Style had to be separated so display manner, such as 2D or side scrolling, would not be confused with aesthetic look, such as abstract, cel-shaded, or retro. Theme and Setting also required careful separation because game labels often mix concepts, places, historical periods, and story worlds.

For the larger GBLS project, this article supports the argument that game discovery and advisory need multidimensional description. Patrons rarely choose games only by platform or broad genre; they may want cooperative, turn-based, horror, peaceful, short, first-person, educational, medieval, cel-shaded, branching, or party-oriented experiences. A faceted genre scheme can help libraries design catalogs, guides, displays, advisory tools, and discovery interfaces that match how games are actually differentiated. The article is not a library program study, but it is directly relevant to game collections, cataloging, metadata, readers' advisory, and research access.
