### Summary
# **Coonan, E. (2019). 'The best way to learn is just to play'. *Journal of Information Literacy, 13*(1), 1-2. https://doi.org/10.11645/13.1.2648**

Coonan's short editorial introduces a broad issue of the *Journal of Information Literacy* by positioning information literacy as a field that must move beyond academic and formal educational settings into "everyday contexts." The editorial is not itself an empirical study of game-based library service, but it is useful because it frames videogaming, informal learning, and transmedia literacy as legitimate information-literacy terrain. Coonan describes the issue's mission as pushing the boundaries of information-literacy thinking in theory, practice, and method, with contributions from public libraries, school libraries, higher education, professional learning, and informal gaming cultures.

The game-relevant passage appears near the end of the editorial, where Coonan introduces Carlos Scolari and Ruth Contreras-Espinosa's peer-reviewed article on informal learning practices in videogaming. She describes their topic as transmedia literacy: the evolving media and information practices of game players outside formal institutions. Coonan highlights the ways those practices overlap with information literacy, including problem-solving, identity performance, navigating, networking, and negotiating. The editorial's title comes from a gamer interviewee's claim that "the best way to learn is just to play," which Coonan uses to underscore the idea that learning is not merely preparation for later action but is embedded in living, doing, interacting, and making meaning.

For the larger GBLS project, this source helps justify an expansive account of information literacy in game-based library services. It supports the claim that games should not be treated only as hooks for preexisting library lessons or as rewards for participation. Instead, games and game communities can be sites where people already practice media navigation, peer learning, problem solving, performance, collaboration, and knowledge co-creation. That framing is especially relevant to videogame clubs, game design workshops, streaming or actual-play cultures, and programs where patrons learn through participation rather than through direct instruction.

The source should be used modestly. It is an editorial overview, not a library program case study or assessment article, and it summarizes another article rather than presenting original gaming findings. Its value is conceptual and source-base oriented: it shows that a major information-literacy venue explicitly recognized videogaming and transmedia literacy as part of the field's everyday-learning agenda. In the review, it can function as a bridge between library-specific videogame literacy work and broader information-literacy scholarship on informal gaming practices.
