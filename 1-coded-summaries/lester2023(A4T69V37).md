### Summary
# **Lester, D., Skulmoski, G. J., Fisher, D. P., Mehrotra, V., Lim, I., Lang, A., & Keogh, J. W. L. (2023). Drivers and barriers to the utilisation of gamification and game-based learning in universities: A systematic review of educators' perspectives. *British Journal of Educational Technology, 54*(6), 1748-1770. https://doi.org/10.1111/bjet.13311**

Lester and colleagues systematically review university educators' drivers and barriers for using gamification and game-based learning (GBL). The article is concerned with higher-education teaching practice rather than library services. It distinguishes gamification, defined as the use of game elements to motivate engagement in tasks, from GBL, defined as learning environments in which game content and play support knowledge or skill acquisition. The authors treat the two as points on a continuum of educational game use and ask why university educators do or do not adopt them.

The review followed PRISMA guidelines and was registered through the Open Science Framework. The authors searched Web of Science, Scopus, and EBSCO databases, including Business Source Complete, ERIC, and Library, Information Science & Technology Abstracts. Search terms combined higher-education settings, educator populations, gamification/game-based concepts, and adoption-related outcomes. Eligible studies had to report university educators' perceptions of factors that positively or negatively influenced use of gamification or GBL, be peer-reviewed, be in English, and be published from 2011 onward. Student-only perception studies were excluded.

The search identified 1,330 articles, of which 1,096 remained after duplicate removal. Seventeen studies met the inclusion criteria: 11 quantitative, two mixed-methods, and four qualitative. Ten focused on gamification, five on GBL, and two on both. The quantitative studies generally used surveys, often with limited reporting of response rates and sample representativeness. The mixed-methods and qualitative studies were small and methodologically varied. The authors therefore emphasize that the synthesis identifies recurring perceptions rather than definitive causal evidence.

Across the included studies, the primary drivers were educators' beliefs that gamification and GBL encourage student interaction and collaborative learning, provide fun, improve engagement, are easy for students to use, and can support motivation or competitiveness. Regression studies suggested that educators' attitudes were the strongest predictors of intention to use gamification, with attitudes influenced by perceived usefulness, ease of use, social influence, and social recognition. Qualitative studies also reported drivers such as student engagement, attention, motivation, and increased teaching confidence.

The main barriers were lack of time to develop gamification or GBL approaches, lack of proven benefits, classroom setting issues, financial considerations, technical/design demands, student attitudes, and institutional or subject constraints. Barriers appeared especially pronounced for full GBL compared with lighter gamification approaches, because GBL can require more design expertise, explanation, technical development, and implementation support. The authors group the findings into three categories: attitudinal factors, design-related factors, and administrative factors. They recommend that teaching support staff, administrators, experienced educators, and possibly external partners help create evidence-sharing, training, resources, and flexible support for educators who want to adopt these methods.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is useful as organizational background rather than direct evidence. Its driver/barrier framework maps well onto library implementation questions: staff confidence, perceived usefulness, ease of use for patrons, time to design programs, proof of benefit, classroom or room constraints, cost, technical expertise, and administrative support are all likely to shape library adoption of games and gamification. However, the article does not study libraries as service providers, librarians as educators, library users, collections, programming, or information-literacy interventions. It should therefore remain outside the main GBLS review except as broad higher-education adoption context.
