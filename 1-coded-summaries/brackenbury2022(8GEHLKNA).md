### Summary
# **Brackenbury, T., & Kopf, L. (2022). Serious games and gamification: Game-based learning in communication sciences and disorders. *Perspectives of the ASHA Special Interest Groups, 7*(2), 482-498. https://doi.org/10.1044/2021_PERSP-21-00284**

This Zotero record should be treated with caution because the attached PDF does not appear to match the parent item. The parent metadata identifies Brackenbury and Kopf's *Perspectives of the ASHA Special Interest Groups* tutorial on serious games and gamification in communication sciences and disorders (CSD), but the attached PDF extracts as Claudia Schrader's handbook chapter, "Serious Games and Game-Based Learning," from the *Handbook of Open, Distance and Digital Education*. Because the Brackenbury and Kopf article text is not actually present in the Zotero attachment, the record cannot be used as full article evidence for the GBLS review.

Based on the Zotero metadata and external abstract-level indexing, the intended Brackenbury and Kopf article is a CSD education tutorial. It defines serious games as activities that directly incorporate educational content into games and gamification as the integration of game characteristics into nongame activities. Its stated purpose is to help readers understand and use serious games and gamification in classroom and clinical CSD settings. It includes models of motivation and engagement, research on serious games and gamification, in-depth examples from two CSD courses, and implementation suggestions.

The article's abstract-level conclusions emphasize that serious games and gamification can improve motivation, engagement, and learning, and that self-determination theory is a useful model because it distinguishes learners' needs and multiple levels of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. The recommendations named in the record are to design activities that balance motivation and learning, target specific forms of motivation in meaningful ways, and reconsider the assumption that intrinsic motivation should always be the ultimate goal of learning.

The mismatched attached PDF, while not the Brackenbury and Kopf article, reinforces similar broad educational design themes. It distinguishes serious games from game-based learning, reviews motivational, affective, sociocultural, and cognitive foundations for learning through games, and summarizes mixed evidence from meta-analyses: games can support motivation, self-efficacy, retention, cognitive skills, and learning outcomes, but effects vary by game type, instructional design, learner characteristics, and learning activities. It also emphasizes that designers must balance motivational features with cognitive load, and attend to collaboration, competition, learner control, aesthetic and narrative design, feedback, and adaptive support.

For the larger GBLS project, both the intended article and the attached-but-mismatched PDF are adjacent rather than directly applicable. They may be useful background for thinking about why motivation, engagement, autonomy, competence, relatedness, feedback, challenge, and support matter in serious games and gamified learning. However, neither the available attachment nor the intended article provides evidence about libraries, librarians, patrons, collections, library instruction, public programs, game collections, TTRPG services, virtual library spaces, or library-specific assessment.

Because the review already includes stronger and more directly relevant sources on library gamification, game-based instruction, serious games, and broad educational evidence, this record should not be added to `review.md`. It is best retained as explicitly excluded literature with a note that the Zotero attachment should be corrected if the Brackenbury and Kopf article is needed for a future health, clinical education, or CSD-specific sidebar.
