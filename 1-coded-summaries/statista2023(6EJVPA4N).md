### Summary
# **Statista. (n.d.). *Industries & markets: Board games and card games*.**

This Statista report is a short chart-based market and player compendium on board games and card games. The Zotero record has no publication date, but the PDF includes 2023 and 2024 figures and forecasts through 2028. It is organized into four sections: overview, leading games, global players, and U.S. players. Like the companion video-games report, it is not a peer-reviewed study or a library-service source. Its value for the GBLS project is contextual: it documents the continuing cultural and market visibility of analog tabletop play.

The overview section gives the strongest background figures. Statista reports that global board-game market revenue reached 8.3 billion U.S. dollars in 2023, with forecast growth to 9.9 billion by 2028. The United States led the selected board-game markets in 2023, with 2.611 billion U.S. dollars in revenue, followed by China, Japan, Germany, and the United Kingdom. For card games, the report estimates global revenue at 2.75 billion U.S. dollars in 2023, forecast to reach 3.27 billion by 2028. In the selected card-game markets, the United States again led in 2023, with 817.7 million U.S. dollars in revenue, followed by China, Japan, Indonesia, and India.

The leading-games section draws from hobby and crowdfunding platforms rather than ordinary consumer surveys. It lists highly rated and heavily played board games from BoardGameGeek, including titles such as *Brass: Birmingham*, *Pandemic Legacy: Season 1*, *Gloomhaven*, *Ark Nova*, *Azul*, and *Wingspan*. It also lists highly funded and highly backed tabletop Kickstarter projects, including *Frosthaven*, *Kingdom Death: Monster 1.5*, *Avatar Legends: The Roleplaying Game*, *Exploding Kittens*, and *Bears vs Babies*. These charts are useful reminders that tabletop gaming is not only a mass-market retail category; it also has active hobbyist, crowdfunding, and community-data ecosystems.

The player sections are especially relevant to library service assumptions. A 2023 YouGov survey across selected countries found that 23 percent of U.S. adults said they enjoyed playing board games or cards, second among the listed countries after Belgium. Statista also reports that board-game enthusiasts globally were split nearly evenly by gender, with 51 percent women and 49 percent men, and that 47 percent of enthusiasts were ages 18 to 34, 35 percent were ages 35 to 54, and 18 percent were 55 or older. For U.S. players, Statista Consumer Insights reported that the share of adults age 18 to 64 who considered board and card games one of their hobbies rose slightly from 23 percent in early 2022 to 24 percent in Q1 2024.

The U.S. player charts add practical texture. A Q3 2023 YouGov survey found that 72 percent of women and 59 percent of men in the United States said they liked board games, with Baby Boomers, Gen X, and Millennials all in the mid-to-high 60 percent range. The card-game charts show both popularity and uneven frequency: Solitaire, Go Fish, Blackjack, Old Maid, War, Poker, Crazy Eights, Hearts, Gin Rummy, and Spades appeared as commonly played games, but only 7 percent of respondents said they played in-person card games very often, while 22 percent played somewhat often and 49 percent not very often.

For the larger GBLS project, this report supports the claim that tabletop games remain a mainstream and intergenerational cultural form, not merely a niche hobby. Its figures are useful for introduction-level framing, collection justification, and audience planning. They also caution against assuming that board and card games are only youth services, only male-coded hobby spaces, or only legacy family games. Libraries designing tabletop collections, casual programs, game days, and intergenerational events can use this kind of market context to argue that analog play sits inside current public culture.

The limitations are important. The report does not discuss libraries, learning outcomes, circulation, programming, facilitation, access, or community impact. Some charts rely on hobbyist platform data such as BoardGameGeek and Kickstarter, which overrepresent active hobby communities. Others are survey-based market snapshots or forecasts. This means the report should not be treated as evidence that library tabletop programs work. Its best role is to source the review's broad contextual claim that board and card games are a visible contemporary media and hobby category.
