### Summary
# **Huber, S. E., Kiili, K., Nebel, S., Ryan, R. M., Sailer, M., & Ninaus, M. (2024). Leveraging the potential of large language models in education through playful and game-based learning. *Educational Psychology Review, 36*(1), Article 25. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-024-09868-z**

Huber, Kiili, Nebel, Ryan, Sailer, and Ninaus write a perspective article about large language models in education and the possibility that playful and game-based learning can help address the risks those systems introduce. The article is not about libraries, information services, collections, youth programming, or public access to games. It is nevertheless an adjacent education/game-based-learning source because it connects generative AI, playful experimentation, prompt engineering, game-based learning, and expertise development.

The problem the authors identify is over-reliance. LLMs can help teachers and learners generate teaching units, curricula, study plans, assessments, explanations, feedback, writing support, literature reviews, and personalized learning materials. Yet the authors emphasize that present LLMs generate text through stochastic processes, so they can produce fabricated information, bias, false citations, misleading answers, and authoritative-sounding errors. Responsible use therefore requires domain expertise, critical evaluation, and human judgment. The educational danger is that learners may outsource the very practice through which expertise is developed.

The first proposed response is a playful approach to LLM use. Drawing on Papert's constructionist use of Logo, Piaget, Vygotsky, and self-determination theory, the article argues that new technology can be treated as a tool for meaningful, exploratory activity rather than as a shortcut for task completion. Prompt engineering becomes a form of playful interaction with LLMs when users iteratively adjust prompts, test outputs, use role or persona prompting, request alternative approaches, and critically revise results. In that framing, the value lies not only in the generated text but in the user's repeated practice of judgment, domain knowledge, and reflective control.

The second response is game-based learning. Huber et al. argue that well-designed games can foster persistence, autonomy, competence, relatedness, safe failure, double-loop reflection, and sustained practice. Their account draws on self-determination theory, the problem-based gaming model, and sociocultural accounts of game communities, affinity groups, theorycrafting, modding, peer apprenticeship, and spontaneous expert roles. Games matter because they can make practice intrinsically engaging, reducing the temptation to delegate cognitive effort to AI when the activity itself is meaningful.

The article then connects LLMs to two game-based-learning scenarios. First, students can learn by generating games: generative AI may lower technical barriers so learners can design games, reformulate academic content, prototype, playtest, share, reflect, and iterate. The authors call for a "prompting pedagogy" that sequences AI-supported game-making through idea generation, core design, prototyping, assessment, peer testing, and reflective discussion. Second, teachers can use LLMs and other generative AI tools to gamify learning materials or create learning-game prototypes by generating artwork, code, levels, mechanics, scenarios, and instructional-design options. In both cases, the authors stress that prompting and critical evaluation remain central.

For the GBLS project, this source is useful mainly as a horizon marker. It suggests possible future library-adjacent directions: AI-supported game design workshops, patron-created learning games, staff-created playful learning materials, and digital-literacy programs about evaluating LLM outputs through playful experimentation. It also reinforces a design caution that applies to GBLS: games should not merely decorate instruction; they should create meaningful practice, reflection, and motivation. However, the article provides no library evidence and no direct service model for public, school, or academic libraries.

This source should therefore remain excluded from the main review text. It can be retained as background for future-thinking notes about AI-assisted game design or playful AI literacy, but it should not be cited as evidence that libraries are implementing or evaluating such programs. Its proper use is conceptual adjacency, not GBLS literature review support.
