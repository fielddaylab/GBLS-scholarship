### Summary
# **Littman, D. M., Milligan, T., Berry, R., Holloway, B., & Scott, M. L. (2024). What do recently housed young people imagine for the future of third places? Using game-based inquiry to (re)imagine affirming, youth-centered third places. *American Journal of Community Psychology, 74*(1-2), 127-141. https://doi.org/10.1002/ajcp.12750**

Littman, Milligan, Berry, Holloway, and Scott use game-based inquiry to ask recently housed young people with experiences of chronic housing instability how they would (re)imagine future third places. The article is not a library program evaluation in a narrow sense, but it is highly relevant to GBLS because libraries are explicitly named as third places, and because the method treats play as a way to surface design knowledge from young people who have often been excluded, surveilled, or displaced in public and community settings. The authors frame play as both a human right and a portal into imagining new ways of being when ordinary social hierarchies can be suspended.

The study took place with residents at Laurel House Apartments, a permanent supportive housing program for young adults in Grand Junction, Colorado. The sample included 21 residents across six game sessions. Rather than using a survey or interview alone, the researchers designed the *Future of Third Places Game*, an original board game in which players entered a future world, filled out worldbuilding and personal expertise cards, responded to community request cards, and built physical or social design elements from craft materials. The request cards were grounded in literature and prior conversations with residents about barriers in present-day third places, such as being stared at while eating, lacking safe places to rest, or needing places to gather without spending money. Sessions lasted roughly an hour to an hour and a half, were recorded and transcribed, and were analyzed through thematic analysis using Oldenburg and Brissett's third-place framework as an initial lens.

The central findings revise conventional third-place theory for young people who have experienced housing instability. Participants argued, through their designs and explanations, that affirming third places must offer agency and individualization, meet everyday needs, and be explicitly inclusive. These were not abstract values. Participants imagined places that meet many needs and do many things; portable and adaptable physical spaces; freedom to choose how to play; choice for privacy or connection; practices that attend to and subvert oppressive social hierarchies; the assurance that people will be around; and free amenities. Designs included mobile community centers, sleeping pods, multipurpose community-center rooms, under-21 social spaces, adult parks, hang pods that could be open or closed, drop-in centers where adults support youth without being in charge, always-open places, free food, barter systems, indoor games, craft areas, and sober community spaces.

For libraries, the article's strongest contribution is its insistence that inclusion cannot be presumed from openness or neutrality. Participants challenged the idea that third places automatically flatten hierarchy simply because they are public. They wanted spaces where adult power, classism, ageism, ableism, hostile architecture, anti-loitering norms, and cost barriers are actively addressed. They also complicated the assumption that third places are primarily social. For young people whose first and second places have failed to provide safety, shelter, food, rest, or belonging, third places may need to meet everyday physical and relational needs as well as offer play.

The methodological contribution is equally important for GBLS. The game was not used merely to make research fun; it created an inquiry structure in which participants could work from fictionalized community requests, make artifacts, narrate design decisions, collaborate or play solo, and keep one imagined design they wished existed in the present world. This suggests a practical use for libraries: game-based inquiry can become a community planning and co-design tool for teen services, youth spaces, makerspaces, game rooms, VR labs, outreach programs, or any service that claims to be youth-centered. A library could adapt the game to invite patrons to design third-place features, then translate imagined elements into policies, furniture choices, staffing models, hours, amenities, privacy options, and program formats.

The article should be used carefully. It is based on a small qualitative sample in one Western Colorado supportive-housing site, and the game cards were intentionally shaped by prior knowledge of participants' experiences, which may have elevated some needs over others. The researchers also chose not to collect individualized demographic information for ethical and relational reasons, limiting intersectional analysis. Even with those limits, the source is valuable because it gives GBLS a design-justice vocabulary for thinking about youth-centered library play spaces: agency, everyday needs, explicit inclusion, adaptable space, free access, privacy, connection, and anti-oppressive facilitation.
