### Summary
# **Keating, S., Lee, W.-C., Windleharth, T., & Lee, J. H. (2017). The style of Tetris is... possibly Tetris?: Creative professionals' description of video game visual styles. In *Proceedings of the 50th Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences* (pp. 2046-2055). https://doi.org/10.24251/HICSS.2017.248**

Keating, Lee, Windleharth, and Lee study video game visual style as an access point for game retrieval, cataloging, and cultural preservation. The article begins from a familiar problem in game metadata: users often can browse games by platform or genre, but those access points do not capture how games look, how their visual design is recognized, or how image-based qualities shape discovery. Existing library standards such as RDA, Dublin Core, and other bibliographic models were not built to describe game visual information in sufficient detail. Visual style is therefore treated as an overlooked but important part of game metadata.

The article is part of the University of Washington GAMER Group's larger work on the Video Game Metadata Schema (VGMS). Earlier VGMS work had developed a visual style taxonomy based largely on general game users. Keating et al. ask whether that taxonomy conforms to the language and cognitive descriptors used by people closer to game creation: video game art and design students at DigiPen Institute of Technology. This matters because metadata schemas must serve different user groups. A public-facing game catalog, a classroom collection, a game-studies research archive, and a game art/design program may need different degrees of specificity.

The method was a think-aloud card-sort activity with eight participants who were undergraduate or master's students in game art, design, or programming and active members of teams developing games. Participants sorted 48 screenshots representing a broad range of visual styles, created labels for their piles using their own language, and explained their sorting decisions aloud. The researchers recorded the activity, coded the language in NVIVO, and compared the resulting terms with the existing visual style taxonomy. The study produced 121 terms, including a long tail of unique descriptors used by only one participant.

Four prominent visual-style groups emerged: Abstract, Realistic, Stylized, and Text. Participants also used more specific subcategories of Stylized, including terms such as anime, cartoon, handicraft, Lego, clay/claymation, knitted/stitched, and yarn. A key finding is that expert or creator-adjacent users generated more Technique terms than general game players. They described visual styles through cel-shading, low-poly design, pixel art, 8-bit, 16-bit, 32-bit, and pre-rendering. Because the more cataloger-friendly version of the taxonomy had removed a Technique facet for simplicity, Keating et al. recommend restoring Technique as an optional facet for organizations serving expert users.

The study also shows that visual style does not stay neatly inside one metadata element. Participants frequently used terms that belonged to related VGMS elements such as dimension, gameplay genre, mood, point of view, and subject. They also used specific games or series as proxy terms for visual style, such as Tetris, Mirror's Edge, and Street Fighter. The title's Tetris example captures the problem: a participant could identify the image as distinct but struggled to name its style except by referring to the game itself. Visual style is therefore both useful and ambiguous; it can involve technique, visual mood, mechanics, motifs, dimensionality, cultural reference, and exemplar titles.

For the larger GBLS project, Keating et al. strengthens the cataloging and discovery argument. Game collections need metadata that reflects how patrons, students, artists, designers, scholars, and preservationists actually search and talk about games. Visual style could support advisory, curricular matching, exhibits, research collections, and preservation description, especially when users are looking for games that resemble a style, era, technique, or aesthetic rather than a platform or genre. The article also cautions that metadata depth should vary by context: public library catalogs may need broad visual-style terms, while academic game archives or design programs may benefit from deeper hierarchies and optional expert facets.

The limitations are clear. This is a small specialized-user study with eight DigiPen participants, based on screenshots rather than full play experiences, and it evaluates taxonomy language rather than testing a live library catalog. Still, its contribution to GBLS is strong because it translates abstract claims about game metadata into concrete design choices: controlled vocabularies need preferred and lead-in terms, near-synonym management, hierarchy, optional depth, and ways to handle temporal change. The proposed Intention facet, with values such as Retro, Futuristic, or Homage, is especially useful because game aesthetics change meaning over time; what was once technical limitation can later become intentional style.
