### Summary
# **McDonald, C., Schmalz, M., Monheim, A., Keating, S., Lewin, K., Cifaldi, F., & Lee, J. H. (2021). Describing, organizing, and maintaining video game development artifacts. *Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology, 72*(5), 540-553. https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.24432**

McDonald, Schmalz, Monheim, Keating, Lewin, Cifaldi, and Lee provide a fuller treatment of the game development artifact problem introduced in the shorter Lee et al. (2020) conference paper. The article argues that finished games alone cannot preserve the cultural, technical, and creative history of games. Development artifacts such as design documents, style guides, test builds, prototypes, source code, art files, marketing materials, financial documents, quality-assurance records, and company ephemera reveal how games were made, why design choices happened, how production cultures worked, and what creators intended. Organizing and preserving these materials can enrich game history and inspire future creators, but the materials are fragile, scattered, heterogeneous, and often poorly described.

The study asks three questions: what types of documents and artifacts are created during game design and development; what challenges creators, information professionals, and game researchers face in organizing and accessing them; and what best-practice recommendations can address those challenges. The authors use a multimethod design. They conducted 29 semi-structured interviews with 12 creators, 11 information professionals, and six game researchers, and they examined several collections at Living Computers: Museum + Labs and The Strong National Museum of Play, including the Doug Sharp Collection, Brøderbund Software, Inc. Collection, Her Interactive, Inc. Collection, Minnesota Educational Computing Corporation Collection, and Jordan Mechner Papers. This combination of stakeholder interviews and direct collection analysis gives the article much more evidentiary weight than a purely conceptual metadata proposal.

One major contribution is the article's description of the game development artifact lifecycle. Games often move from proof of concept to production, builds, testing, milestone versions, release, patches, and later archival or institutional accession. Contemporary development also includes minimum viable products, live updates, and frequent patches, while older packaged games had very different constraints. After release, development artifacts often lose priority as teams shift to new products. When collections are accessioned by cultural heritage institutions, they are assigned identifiers, legal and administrative files, locations, inventories, archival housing, descriptions, and finding aids. The article therefore makes clear that development artifacts move through different regimes of value: production utility, company memory, legal risk, scholarly evidence, and cultural heritage.

The findings identify several recurring challenges. Creators, information professionals, and researchers all valued preservation, but that value rarely translated into sustained investment. Creators faced unclear responsibilities, restructured teams, fluid roles, intellectual-property and nondisclosure concerns, weak file-naming practices, inconsistent storage locations, and pressure to prioritize production over documentation. Information professionals faced missing context, mixed physical and digital formats, obsolete media, copyright restrictions, and uncertainty over which standards to apply. Game researchers struggled with collection-level finding aids that were too broad to reveal whether a trip to an archive would be worthwhile, catalog systems that returned too much irrelevant material, and access restrictions that prevented online consultation of copyrighted or proprietary files.

The article's central practical contribution is a taxonomy of 123 game development artifact types. The taxonomy is organized into three broad sections: development, organization-related material, and marketing. Development includes design, engineering, quality assurance and control, art, audio, writing, and production; marketing includes advertising, public relations, and promotional materials. The taxonomy was built from interview data and artifact examination, then reviewed collaboratively by advisory board members, stakeholders, and students in a graduate metadata course. The authors discuss several taxonomy-design challenges that matter for GBLS metadata work: deciding whether to include company culture documents and ephemera, distinguishing artifact type from format, handling vague terms such as "design document" and "prototype," and choosing a level of granularity that supports search and browsing without over-categorizing materials.

The best-practice recommendations are equally important. The authors call for consistent controlled vocabulary, naming conventions, documentation standards, central locations for files, and regularly maintained practices that help creators and later information professionals. They also recommend stronger communication between game companies and cultural heritage institutions, arguing that creators may not know existing descriptive frameworks while information professionals may lack domain knowledge. Participatory archival processing could help bridge this gap by allowing creators to explain connections, context, and significance during accessioning or processing. For access, the authors discuss onsite terminals, restricted copying, donor agreements, rights-holder referrals, and the need to check copyright and NDA issues before accepting donations.

For the GBLS project, this article is a key infrastructure source. It expands the scope of game-based library services beyond acquisition, circulation, and programming into long-term cultural stewardship. It shows that games require specialized metadata, relationship modeling, preservation planning, rights management, domain vocabulary, and collaboration with creators, researchers, museums, and archives. Public libraries may not process game development archives themselves, but academic libraries, special collections, and library-adjacent cultural institutions increasingly confront these materials. The article also supports the review's argument that games are cultural media: treating them seriously means preserving development context, not only boxed products or playable files.

The study's limits are mainly scope limits. The collections and stakeholders were North American, and the authors acknowledge that some parts of the taxonomy may need refinement for particular roles, newer digital collections, and other media contexts. The taxonomy had also not yet undergone all future evaluation phases described in the paper. Still, this source gives GBLS one of its strongest examples of applied game metadata research. It turns the abstract claim that "games are cultural heritage" into a concrete set of artifacts, systems, standards, roles, and practices.
