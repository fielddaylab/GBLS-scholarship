### Summary
# **Smith, T. S. (2024). A dicey situation: A study of how controlled vocabularies describe tabletop roleplaying games. *Cataloging & Classification Quarterly*, 1-25. https://doi.org/10.1080/01639374.2024.2308117**

Smith examines how controlled vocabularies describe tabletop role-playing games (TTRPGs) and argues that existing headings fail to support accurate discovery. The article begins from the premise that TTRPGs are increasingly relevant to academic collections because they function as primary sources for game studies, literature, history, sociology, psychology, and popular-culture research, yet they are often still difficult to distinguish from computer role-playing games, board games, live-action role-playing games, fantasy fiction, and secondary sources about games. Smith defines TTRPGs as collaborative, rule-governed narrative games in which players assume characters and resolve conflicts through human adjudication and narration, while also noting that even this definition is imperfect because many TTRPGs lack a traditional game master.

The study evaluates several vocabularies that include headings relevant to TTRPGs: Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH), Getty's Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT), Wikidata, Genre Terms for Tabletop Games (GTTG), and Library of Congress Genre/Form Terms (LCGFT). Smith evaluates each vocabulary across three dimensions: whether its definition can distinguish TTRPGs from other forms of roleplay, whether its structure supports that distinction, and whether it disambiguates subject from genre/form. This matters because a work can be about TTRPGs, can be a TTRPG, or can be a different role-playing form; catalog records that collapse those distinctions make discovery much harder.

Smith is especially critical of LCSH. The relevant heading, "Fantasy games," has no scope note, artificially associates TTRPGs with fantasy as a genre, and contains narrower terms that mix TTRPGs, wargames, trading-card games, videogames, choose-your-own-adventure books, broad properties, and individual titles. LCSH also remains primarily a subject vocabulary, so even form subdivisions such as "Rules" or "Handbooks, manuals, etc." cannot fully solve the problem: *Dungeons & Dragons* is not a work about role-playing games but a role-playing game itself. AAT also lacks a strong definition and places role-playing games under an object/equipment hierarchy that does not clearly fit rulebooks and mixed-format TTRPG materials.

Wikidata performs better on definition because its "tabletop role-playing game" entry includes variant terms such as pen-and-paper role-playing game, TRPG, and TTRPG and distinguishes leisure games from roleplay used in education or business training. Its linked-data structure also relates TTRPGs to tabletop games, role-playing games, publication, and game genre. However, Smith notes that Wikidata still has errors and does not clearly mark whether an entry is a subject term, a genre/form term, or both. LCGFT is explicitly genre/form, but its "Role-playing games" scope note is broad enough to include computer RPGs and larps. GTTG comes closest because it was built specifically for tabletop game collections, but its narrow local application makes it hard to recommend as a general solution for academic libraries.

The article's practical conclusion is that no current vocabulary fully satisfies all three criteria. Smith argues that local consistency may be the best short-term option, even when imperfect, because consistent description within a collection is more discoverable than many local variations. In the long term, one or more widely used controlled vocabularies should amend their headings so TTRPGs can be described as genre/form materials distinct from computer RPGs, larps, board games, and works about TTRPGs. Smith also suggests that automated approaches might help identify and update records, though TTRPGs are harder than audio materials because they often appear as text and therefore cannot be separated from scholarship by fixed fields alone.

For the larger GBLS project, Smith strengthens the argument that game-based library service depends on infrastructure as much as programming. TTRPG collections will remain hard to use if patrons cannot reliably find core books, supplements, adventures, settings, or game systems in catalogs and discovery layers. The article also connects controlled vocabulary to patron choice: users often need game-specific facets such as age range, number of players, duration of play, and distinctions between core books and supplements. This is a key source for the review's cataloging, metadata, and discovery section, especially because it extends the videogame metadata literature into tabletop role-playing games.
