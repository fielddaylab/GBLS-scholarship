### Summary
# **Dong, H., Moreno, P., Rodrigues, T., & Stone, J. (2025). Enhancing discoverability: Transitioning a board game collection from a LibGuide to a CollectionBuilder site. *Information Technology and Libraries, 44*(1). https://doi.org/10.5860/ital.v44i1.17075**

Source-access note: the Zotero PDF attachment has no local storage path, but the publisher's open PDF was available and used for processing.

Dong, Moreno, Rodrigues, and Stone present a University of Idaho Library case study about making an academic board game collection more discoverable by moving its online presence from a LibGuide to a custom CollectionBuilder site. The local collection began in January 2019 and grew by more than fifteen games each year; by the time the project began, the library had about 110 games and the LibGuide had received more than 22,000 views. The LibGuide included title pages, images, descriptions, player counts, play time, catalog links, themes, mechanics, rules, how-to-play videos, recommendation links, event information, and community resources, but its dropdown organization became cumbersome as records reached triple digits.

The article frames discoverability as both a front-end service issue and a metadata infrastructure issue. Physical shelving and library catalogs can support discovery, but board games require access points that ordinary bibliographic records and public catalog interfaces may not handle well, such as recommended player count, age, play time, mechanics, themes, awards, designer, and publisher. The authors note that many tabletop-game collections lack bibliographic records at all, in part because technical services staff may be unfamiliar with games, original cataloging is laborious, records are hard to obtain, and standard interfaces do not always expose game-specific fields usefully.

CollectionBuilder offered the team a more flexible alternative because it is an open-source framework for digital collection and exhibit websites driven by metadata and powered by static web technology. The project was interdepartmental, involving instructional technology, digital infrastructure, student engagement, digital scholarship, digital labs, and other library staff. The team collaborated through email, Zoom, and shared documents; scraped the LibGuide with OpenRefine; converted unstructured web pages into a structured Google Sheet; renamed book-oriented fields such as "author" to game-relevant fields such as "designer"; and added metadata for awards, recommended player count, recommended age, play time, small images, and thumbnails.

The finished site improved browsing and searching in ways that matter directly for GBLS. Users could browse all games through interactive cards, sort by theme and mechanics, use word-cloud pages for themes, mechanics, and awards, and search across title, description, mechanics, theme, awards, year, designer, and publisher. The team also refined the user interface by moving overwhelming award lists lower on item pages, adding an award-winner indicator, creating more consistent game images through local photography, generating image derivatives, and placing the catalog button more visibly.

The lessons learned are especially useful for libraries planning game collections. The project surfaced copyright and image-quality issues, file-naming conventions for the web, missing metadata, the need to gather information from boxes, rulebooks, and BoardGameGeek, link rot in instructional videos, the need for an audit or maintenance schedule, and the importance of a data dictionary when personnel change. The authors also argue that the site can support collection development by revealing holes in the collection by mechanic, play length, or number of players.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is a strong infrastructure case. It shows that a game collection is not made accessible simply by purchasing games or listing titles; patrons need discovery tools that reflect how people choose games for time, group size, theme, mechanics, age, awards, and local availability. It also connects discovery to sustainability: a flexible collection interface, structured metadata, documented workflows, image standards, and maintenance planning help a growing game collection remain usable. The source should be cited in the review's metadata/discovery section and can also inform collections/access recommendations.
