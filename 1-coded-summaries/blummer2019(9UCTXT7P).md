### Summary
# **Blummer, B., & Kenton, J. M. (2019). Academic libraries' outreach efforts: Identifying themes in the literature. *Public Services Quarterly, 15*(3), 179-204. https://doi.org/10.1080/15228959.2019.1592058**

Blummer and Kenton review 174 peer-reviewed papers on academic-library outreach published from 2008 through January 2019. The article is not primarily about games, but it is useful for GBLS because it places gaming within the broader modernization of academic-library outreach. The authors define outreach through instruction, cooperation or community-based work, and special programming, then code the literature through qualitative comparison and simple counts. Their five main themes are outreach to targeted groups, specialized library outreach programs, collaboration, management of outreach efforts, and social media outreach.

The targeted-groups section shows how broad academic-library outreach had become during the decade. Libraries reached community organizations and area residents, middle and high school students, students by class standing, international students, veterans, athletes, college staff, adult learners, transfer students, distance students, diverse student bodies, faculty, and academic majors. For GBLS, this matters because game-based services often appear as outreach to particular audiences rather than as standalone "gaming" services. The review reinforces that outreach design should begin with the audience and local institutional context.

The specialized-programs section is the clearest point of contact with GBLS. Blummer and Kenton identify 47 papers describing outreach programs, with subthemes including instruction and information, roaming services, orientations and other activities, gaming, exhibits, makerspaces, and collections. Gaming appears in six papers. The authors summarize this small gaming subset as including video-game events used to promote library space and resources, low-cost and popular programming, planning and marketing concerns, assessment, and other games used for information-literacy instruction or library/resource orientation. The cited gaming literature includes Bishoff, Farrell, and Neeser (2015), Vanden Elzen and Roush (2013), Courtier and DeLooper (2017), Martin and Martin (2015), Miller (2014), and Spencer (2015).

The article's discussion identifies four cross-cutting outreach qualities that are useful for GBLS: relevance, collaboration, cultural artifacts, and flexibility. Relevance means designing outreach around user groups and needs. Collaboration includes work with faculty, departments, campus groups, community organizations, public libraries, schools, cultural groups, businesses, and state organizations. Cultural artifacts include social media, video games, makerspaces, films, and graphic novels as attention- and interest-building materials. Flexibility includes meeting users where they are, moving services into popular campus and community spaces, and adapting programs to local interests.

Blummer and Kenton are also useful for their caution about assessment. Although many authors described outreach and often reported positive responses, only a handful detailed evaluation. Stronger assessment used both qualitative and quantitative data to improve future programs, while weaker accounts relied on usage statistics, comments, or anecdotal evidence. This echoes a recurring GBLS problem: outreach success is often inferred from popularity, attendance, or enthusiasm, but durable claims require clearer measures and reporting.

For the GBLS project, this source should be used as adjacent context. It supports the claim that gaming was already visible in academic-library outreach discourse by the late 2010s, but it does not evaluate game programs directly. Its main contribution is framing: games are one kind of cultural artifact and special program within larger outreach systems that also depend on audience relevance, collaboration, management, marketing, flexibility, and assessment. The article is therefore best cited in the source-base or review-approach discussion rather than in a section making claims about game-based learning outcomes.
