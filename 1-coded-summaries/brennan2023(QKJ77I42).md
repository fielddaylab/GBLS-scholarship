### Summary
# **Brennan, R. (2023). Wellbeing in the school library. *School Librarian, 71*(2), 10-11.**

Brennan's short practitioner feature describes a school library redevelopment at Stonyhurst College with wellbeing, purpose, and community as its organizing terms. The article is written from the perspective of a newly appointed college librarian who arrived after the library had gone several years without a dedicated librarian. The More Library was architecturally impressive, but its main role had narrowed to silent study. Brennan treats that quiet function as valuable, especially in a boarding-school setting where many pupils need calm space away from busy houses and playrooms, but argues that the library could also become a more welcoming and inclusive third place for students' social and emotional needs.

The intervention began with environmental and access-oriented changes rather than a named game program. Brennan streamlined cluttered areas, updated displays and signage, added plants, motivational posters, and artwork, and worked to make the library more visible and inviting from the corridor. The emphasis is on small, cumulative changes that shift the affective identity of the library: students should experience it not only as a place to complete coursework before deadlines, but as a student-centered space where wellbeing is visible and where pupils feel permitted to spend informal time.

The Zotero abstract and article metadata identify several later additions that connect the piece to GBLS. Brennan encouraged students to visit during breaks and introduced chess, computer games, and film screenings to foster friendships and conversations. These activities are not described in evaluative detail, but they matter because they frame games as part of a wellbeing ecology rather than as standalone entertainment. Chess and computer games appear alongside displays, social space, films, Mental Health First Aid training, and a Reading Well collection. The service logic is that playful and social activities can help students enter the library, connect with peers, talk more easily, and experience the library as a supportive third place.

For the larger GBLS project, Brennan is a modest but useful source for the emerging wellness, mental health, and care section. It shows how games can be folded into ordinary school-library wellbeing work without requiring a large program, new technology suite, or formal curriculum. The article also pairs well with third-place sources: the library is explicitly positioned between home and school, which is especially significant in a boarding context where those categories blur. Games, in this account, help widen the library's repertoire from silent study to community, conversation, relaxation, and belonging.

The limits are substantial. This is a reflective practitioner article, not a research study; it provides no participant data, outcome measures, or detailed description of the chess and computer-game activities. The attached PDF appears to include only the first page of the article, while the citation and abstract indicate pages 10-11. The source should therefore be cited cautiously and used as illustrative practice evidence rather than proof of effectiveness. Its value is in naming a service pattern: low-barrier game and media activities can support a wellbeing-oriented school library when embedded in broader changes to space, staffing, mental-health awareness, reading support, and student-centered culture.
