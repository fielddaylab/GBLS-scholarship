### Summary
# **Guo, S.-C., & Lan, Y.-J. (2023). Virtual world-supported contextualized multimodal EFL learning at a library. *Language Learning & Technology, 27*(2), 176-198. https://hdl.handle.net/10125/73508**

Guo and Lan report an action-research study of young English as a foreign language learners in a public-library setting in northern Taiwan. The article begins from the premise that libraries are informal learning spaces with reading resources, language programs, and emerging digital technologies, but that relatively little research has examined library courses that combine story reading with virtual construction for EFL reading. The study is therefore directly relevant to GBLS because it tests a library-based learning activity that combines picture storybooks, word games, story creation, and a 3D virtual world authoring tool.

The study compared two one-day library reading projects, each lasting six hours and each involving 19 learners from grades 4-6, ages 10-12. The first project used picture storybook reading, reading questions and answers, and English games such as memory games and charades. After the first project improved reading scores but did not produce satisfactory motivation and anxiety results, the instructors revised the design. The second project kept picture storybook reading but added Omni-immersion Vision (OIV), a desktop 3D authoring tool that lets learners navigate virtual sites, place interactive objects, create contexts, and communicate through text or oral modes. Learners first planned stories on posters and then worked in small teams to create 3D virtual environments connected to those stories.

The quantitative results show a useful but cautious pattern. Both groups improved on English reading tests: the picture-book group improved by 21.58 points on average, and the picture-book-plus-VR group improved by 26.84 points, though the difference in improvement between groups was not statistically significant. The affective measures were more distinctive. The PB+VR group showed higher intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, lower learning anxiety, and more positive attitudes toward technology than the PB group; the significant extrinsic-motivation gain suggests that creation in VR can help motivate young learners during informal library learning. The authors argue that VR reduced anxiety partly because learners focused on the environment, avatars, visual effects, and construction task rather than on foreign-language performance alone.

The qualitative analysis is especially useful for GBLS design. The researchers analyzed how learners moved from written notes to drawings to VR construction through semiotic and multimodal meaning-making. In VR, students used fewer written text elements and many more creatures, buildings, natural items, furniture, objects, vehicles, and interactive questions. The virtual environment gave them a larger canvas and new semiotic resources, but also required spatial skills and careful planning. Teams used bilingual text, bulletin boards, directions, and question-posing features to guide visitors through their story worlds. The study therefore treats the virtual world not merely as visual decoration but as a medium for transmodal redesign: students moved meanings across writing, drawing, 3D objects, spatial arrangement, interaction, and audience navigation.

For the larger GBLS project, Guo and Lan strengthen the argument that library game and immersive-technology programs can support literacy when they are embedded in a purposeful learning sequence. The article connects traditional library strengths, such as story reading and language programs, with playful activities and maker-like digital creation. It also offers a useful program pattern: begin with accessible texts, use low-stakes games and read-aloud work, introduce the virtual tool separately from the language task, have learners plan collaboratively on paper, then let them build and share virtual worlds that carry story meaning. This pattern is valuable for libraries considering VR, virtual worlds, Minecraft-like tools, or 3D creation as literacy supports rather than technology demonstrations.

The limitations are important. The two projects were held two months apart rather than as a randomized simultaneous comparison, each lasted only one day, and the sample was small. The PB+VR group began with higher pre-test scores and motivation, which complicates causal interpretation. The study also used one virtual construction platform, and some learner difficulties may have resulted from limited practice with 3D spatial positioning rather than the learning design itself. Still, the article provides one of the clearer empirical examples of a library-based virtual-world program with pre/post reading tests, motivation and anxiety measures, and analysis of student-created artifacts.
