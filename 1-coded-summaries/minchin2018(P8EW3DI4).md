### Summary
# **Minchin, P. (2018). In praise of tabletop games: Part 1. *Access, 32*(1), 41-45.**

Minchin's short professional article argues that tabletop games belong in school libraries because play is a form of self-directed learning and shared culture. The piece begins from a critique of older didactic models of education and the persistent assumption that play is trivial, childish, or opposed to seriousness. Minchin accepts that explicitly educational games can teach particular facts or systems, but he argues that libraries and educators miss much of the value of games when they treat them only as delivery mechanisms for preselected curriculum content. Like quality fiction, games can teach important habits, interpretive abilities, and dispositions even when they are not designed around conventional subject matter.

The article's most useful contribution is its vocabulary of multiple literacies. Minchin defines psychological literacy as understanding minds, emotions, vulnerability, self-knowledge, and other people as independent agents. He defines social literacy as awareness of power, influence, group dynamics, healthy and unhealthy patterns, and the ways emergent group forces shape individual choices. Systems literacy names the ability to understand rule-governed interactions, causes, effects, economies, ecologies, and other complex systems. Minchin then proposes "procedural literacy" as a distinct literacy: the ability to understand, observe, interpret, and use procedures, from queues and turn-taking to meeting protocols, electoral rules, and legal codes.

Tabletop games, in Minchin's account, are especially well suited to these literacies. Games that reward reading opponents' intentions foster psychological literacy. Multiplayer games develop social literacy because every game has an implicit or explicit social contract. Rule sets and simulations cultivate systems literacy because players repeatedly encounter causes, effects, constraints, and interactions. Tabletop games are also particularly strong procedural-literacy environments because they are rule systems governing human interaction. The familiar figure of the "rules lawyer" becomes evidence that games can train close attention to procedure, interpretation, enforcement, exception, and advantage.

The second major argument is that play itself should be understood as learning. Minchin draws on Huizinga's "magic circle," biological accounts of play as training for the unexpected, Diane Ackerman's claim that play is a favored way of learning, and Raph Koster's theory that fun is bound up with learning. The point is not that every game automatically produces desirable learning, but that play creates structured experimentation, feedback, resilience, pattern recognition, and meaning-making. For libraries, this means that games should be curated thoughtfully in the same way libraries curate literature, film, sequential art, and other cultural forms.

For the GBLS project, Minchin is valuable as a conceptual and advocacy source rather than as an assessment study. It gives language for connecting tabletop services to learning outcomes that are often hard to name: reading people, negotiating groups, understanding systems, interpreting rules, and practicing shared decision procedures. It also makes a library-specific fit claim. Libraries support self-directed learning, and genuine play also needs self-direction; libraries share culture, and tabletop games are cultural works that must be shared in order to be experienced. This helps justify tabletop collections and programs without reducing them to curricular instruments.

The limitations are clear. This is a professional essay, not empirical research, and it does not report a library implementation, participant data, or outcome measures. It also gestures to research and theory rather than systematically reviewing them. Still, it is useful in the review because it clarifies the learning theory behind tabletop GBLS and helps explain why a library might collect and facilitate games for intrinsic literacies, community learning, and cultural participation.
