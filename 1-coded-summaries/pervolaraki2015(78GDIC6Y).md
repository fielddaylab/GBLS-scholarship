### Summary
# **Pervolaraki, I.-E., Garoufallou, E., Siatri, R., Zafeiriou, G., & Virkus, S. (2015). How can video games facilitate information literacy? In S. Kurbanoğlu, J. Boustany, S. Špiranec, E. Grassian, D. Mizrachi, & L. Roy (Eds.), *Information literacy: Moving toward sustainability* (pp. 339-349). Springer. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-28197-1_35**

Processing note: the Zotero attachment is a full ECIL 2015 proceedings volume. This entry summarizes only the chapter "How Can Video Games Facilitate Information Literacy?" on pages 339-349.

Pervolaraki, Garoufallou, Siatri, Zafeiriou, and Virkus provide a systematic literature review on how video games have been adopted by educational institutions and libraries to support learning, literacy, and information literacy among adolescents and young adults. The chapter begins by acknowledging the long-standing concern that games may increase aggression or reduce prosocial behavior, but it shifts the review toward evidence that games can produce perceptual, cognitive, behavioral, affective, and motivational outcomes. Its central question for GBLS is how video games can become educational and library tools rather than simply entertainment media.

The authors use a systematic-review protocol adapted from Hemingway and Brereton: define review objectives, collect relevant literature, assess individual studies, combine results, and place findings in context. Searches were conducted from December 2014 to February 2015 across ERIC, JSTOR, ProQuest, PsycINFO, IEEE Xplore, ScienceDirect, Wiley Online Library, CiteSeerX, Emerald Insight, Google Scholar, dissertations, and conference proceedings, with citation chaining to reach saturation. The covered literature was published from 2004 to 2015, written in English, and focused on video games in public libraries, education, literacy, and information literacy.

The review is organized into five emerging themes. First, "video game literacy" treats games as cultural and social worlds in which players collaborate, solve problems, read, write, and participate in communities. The authors draw on Gee, Squire, Zimmerman, Steinkuehler, Shaffer, and others to argue that game literacy involves design, play, systems, social interaction, and situated expertise rather than only screen time. Second, "video games in education" summarizes evidence that games can support motivation, history learning, math achievement, computer science concepts, problem solving, and engagement, while also noting that educators still struggle to connect specific game tasks and skills to desired learning outcomes.

Third, the chapter discusses game design benefits. The reviewed studies suggest that adolescents who participate in making games can develop collaboration, engagement, critical thinking, hardware and software skills, and understanding of how technologies are produced rather than merely consumed. The authors note, however, that most research focuses on playing games rather than creating them. Fourth, the chapter reviews video games, reading, and writing. Studies of game manuals, forums, strategy sites, game reviews, MMOGs, and paratext creation suggest that adolescents may read and write extensively around games, sometimes performing above school-text reading levels when texts are tied to self-chosen game interests. Fifth, the public-library section gathers conceptual, practical, and limited empirical work on gaming programs, game collections, literacy, role-playing games, game selection, gaming policies, and library videogame events.

For the GBLS project, the chapter is valuable because it connects video games to information literacy without reducing the connection to a single library lesson. Information literacy appears in gameplay, game communities, out-of-game reading, collaborative information seeking, forum participation, wiki creation, strategy use, and the production of knowledge around games. This broadens the review's account of IL beyond orientation games and escape rooms. It also helps justify library videogame programs and collections as part of literacy ecology: they can attract adolescents and young adults, support social and collaborative learning, and provide a bridge between recreational interests and lifelong learning.

The chapter is also useful for its cautionary gaps. The authors argue that little is known about gaming and adolescents in library settings, that U.S. public libraries appear to have adopted videogame programs more readily than European libraries, that adult gaming in libraries is under-researched, and that studies of young people creating games remain limited compared with studies of game use. They also call for educator and librarian training so staff can move beyond unfamiliarity and fear of negative impacts. These gaps are directly relevant to the GBLS future research agenda, especially because later GBLS work still often relies on small cases, practitioner reports, and uneven evidence across library types.
