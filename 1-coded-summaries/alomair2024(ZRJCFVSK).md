### Summary
# **Alomair, Y., & Hammami, S. (2024). Enhancing the achievement of learning outcomes for foreign language learners based on gamification and card games. *International Journal of Computer Games Technology, 2024*(1), Article 8084687. https://doi.org/10.1155/2024/8084687**

Alomair and Hammami propose and evaluate a foreign language learning framework that combines gamification, card games, agent technology, and microlearning to improve student learning outcomes. The article begins from the premise that gamified and card game-based language learning often improves motivation but does not always align clearly with predefined student learning outcomes. In response, the authors design a framework in which foreign language learning activities are organized around stated outcomes for listening, speaking, reading, and writing rather than around engagement alone.

The proposed framework has two main components: a card drafting agent and a card set database. The card drafting agent receives a course identifier and a list of learning outcomes, retrieves relevant language-learning cards from the database, and generates a foreign language learning card set for each outcome. The card database stores listening, speaking, reading, writing, and prize cards. A typical card set contains four skill-related cards plus one prize card. Skill cards include microlearning-based content and an evaluation question; listening cards include audio, while speaking, reading, and writing cards provide learning statements followed by multiple-choice questions. Correct answers award points and move the learner to the next card set, while incorrect answers prompt the learner to choose another card from the same set.

The implementation was built with the Java Agent Development Framework for the card drafting agent, Java Server Pages for the web interface, and MySQL Server for the card set database. The evaluation used a quasi-experimental design with 120 intermediate-school students in Jouf, Saudi Arabia, divided into 60-student experimental and control groups. Students were 12 to 15 years old, had parental authorization, and were selected partly for computer and internet skills. The experiment took place during the second trimester of 2023 across two six-week periods. The framework was adapted to the English textbook used during the trimester and focused on two learning outcomes: knowledge and understanding of language concepts, and skill development across the four language skills.

The study assessed learning outcomes through quizzes, evaluation tests, and the Continuous e-Assessment Method algorithm. It also gathered student survey data on motivation and dialectal variation, plus instructor survey data on applicability and reliability. The reported results favored the experimental group: 72% of experimental participants improved on the knowledge-and-understanding outcome compared with 55% of the control group, and 90% improved on the skills outcome compared with 53% of the control group. Student survey data also suggested higher motivation in the experimental group, although dialect variation created difficulties for some learners. Instructor responses were generally positive, but some instructors argued that the framework should support rather than replace traditional teaching, and they raised concerns about device access, internet connectivity, technical proficiency, instructor training, and overreliance on online systems.

For the larger GBLS project, the article offers adjacent evidence about aligning game mechanics with learning outcomes, combining card-based play with microlearning, and treating games as instructional systems rather than simply motivational wrappers. These ideas could loosely inform library-created language, orientation, or information-literacy games. However, the article is not library literature. It does not discuss libraries, librarians, library collections, information literacy, library instruction, community programming, public-service settings, or library users. Its empirical case is a school-based foreign-language intervention in Saudi Arabia, and its contribution is mainly to educational technology and foreign language learning.

The source should therefore remain outside the main GBLS review. It may be useful background for future design work on outcome-aligned educational games, card-based learning, microlearning, or AI/agent-supported learning systems, but it should not be cited as evidence for game-based library services unless a later section explicitly reviews non-library education studies as adjacent design literature.
