### Summary
# **Association of College and Research Libraries. (2016). *Framework for information literacy for higher education*. American Library Association.**

The ACRL *Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education* is an official professional framework rather than a game-based learning article or a library program assessment. Filed by the ACRL Board in 2015 and adopted in 2016, it replaced a standards-and-competencies orientation with a more conceptual account of information literacy grounded in threshold concepts, metaliteracy, knowledge practices, dispositions, and local implementation. Its core value for the GBLS review is that it defines the learning domain many library games, escape rooms, scavenger hunts, orientation activities, and gamified instruction projects are trying to teach.

The Framework argues that information literacy can no longer be reduced to a prescriptive list of skills because students participate in a dynamic and uncertain information ecosystem. It defines information literacy as integrated abilities that encompass reflective discovery of information, understanding how information is produced and valued, and using information to create new knowledge and participate ethically in communities of learning. This definition is especially relevant to GBLS because it emphasizes inquiry, production, participation, ethics, and reflection rather than only locating correct answers.

The document is organized around six frames: Authority Is Constructed and Contextual; Information Creation as a Process; Information Has Value; Research as Inquiry; Scholarship as Conversation; and Searching as Strategic Exploration. Each frame includes an explanatory concept statement, knowledge practices, and dispositions. For example, Authority Is Constructed and Contextual asks learners to evaluate credibility in relation to community, need, bias, and context. Information Creation as a Process asks learners to look beyond format to the processes and purposes behind information products. Information Has Value links attribution, copyright, access, privacy, personal-data commodification, and information privilege.

The remaining frames are equally important for library game design. Research as Inquiry presents research as iterative, question-driven, uncertain, and open to multiple methods and perspectives. Scholarship as Conversation frames research as sustained discourse in which learners enter, evaluate, cite, and contribute to conversations shaped by authority and access. Searching as Strategic Exploration describes searching as nonlinear, iterative, affective, social, and dependent on mental flexibility, browsing, controlled vocabularies, search tools, and persistence. These frames map well onto game-based instruction because games can make iteration, uncertainty, collaboration, feedback, search strategy, and ethical decision-making visible.

The Framework's implementation guidance cautions against treating it as a single-session checklist. It asks librarians to work with faculty, teaching and learning centers, curriculum committees, instructional designers, and administrators to integrate information literacy developmentally across programs, courses, assignments, and co-curricular experiences. It also encourages local outcomes, assessment, piloting, reflection, and sharing of instructional materials. For GBLS, this matters because many library games appear in one-shots, orientations, workshops, or outreach events; the Framework reminds designers that game mechanics should connect to broader learning arcs and assessment plans.

For the larger GBLS project, the Framework should be used as a conceptual foundation for information-literacy games rather than evidence that games work. It helps translate GBLS design questions into learning questions: Which frame does the game address? What knowledge practices or dispositions are being practiced? Does the activity invite reflective discovery, strategic exploration, source evaluation, ethical participation, or metacognition? What debrief or assessment helps learners connect game action to information-literacy practice? The Framework is therefore useful in the review, but only as an anchor for instructional goals, not as an outcome study.
