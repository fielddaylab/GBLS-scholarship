# **Nelson, A. (2023). Review of the book *Let's roll: A guide to setting up tabletop role-playing games in your school or public library*, by L. Maxwell. *School Library Journal, 69*(5), 103.**

Nelson's short professional review evaluates Lucas Maxwell's *Let's Roll: A Guide to Setting up Tabletop Role-Playing Games in Your School or Public Library* for the May 2023 professional-reading section of *School Library Journal*. The review is not a research article, program case study, or direct account of a library TTRPG program. Its value for the GBLS project is instead bibliographic and field-facing: it records how a mainstream school-library review venue presented Maxwell's handbook to practitioners at the moment of publication.

The review frames Maxwell's book as a support text for library staff who are new to tabletop role-playing games and for staff who already understand gaming but feel uncertain about launching RPG programming. The named barriers are practical rather than theoretical: finding institutional support, marketing to patrons, and managing a chaotic program session. Nelson also identifies the book's supplemental materials, including a glossary of terms, a sample program proposal, case studies from school and public librarians, recommended reading lists, and brief reviews of simple gateway RPGs that require minimal preparation or expertise.

Nelson's assessment emphasizes tone and usability. The book is described as well organized, specific, gentle, and conversational. The review notes that Maxwell sometimes leans heavily on his own experience as a high school librarian, but it also highlights case study statements from librarians running TTRPGs in different library settings and with different age groups. The verdict recommends the title for school or public library professionals who feel trepidation about introducing an RPG program.

For the larger GBLS project, this review reinforces the practitioner-training problem around TTRPG services. It suggests that many librarians do not primarily need persuasion that TTRPGs exist; they need practical help translating the format into library workflows, administrative language, promotion, facilitation, and beginner-friendly game selection. The review also shows how professional literature positions TTRPG programming as relevant to both school and public libraries, and how anxiety about facilitation, institutional approval, and session management remains central to adoption.

At the same time, this item should not be used as substantive evidence in the literature review when Maxwell's handbook itself is available for direct processing. Nelson summarizes and recommends the book but does not add original data, independent program details, or a distinct theoretical argument. It is best retained as an adjacent professional-review record and a pointer to Maxwell (2023), not as a separate paragraph in `review.md`.
