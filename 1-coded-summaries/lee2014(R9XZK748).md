### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Clarke, R. I., Sacchi, S., & Jett, J. (2014). Relationships among video games: Existing standards and new definitions. *Proceedings of the American Society for Information Science and Technology, 51*(1), 1-11. https://doi.org/10.1002/meet.2014.14505101035**

Lee, Clarke, Sacchi, and Jett address a specific metadata problem for video games and interactive media: existing bibliographic and cultural-heritage standards can express some relationships between resources, but they do not capture the ways game players, collectors, scholars, and curators understand relationships among games. The article is part of the University of Washington GAMER Group's larger work with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum to develop a standardized metadata schema, controlled vocabularies, and a user-centered conceptual data model for video games.

The article begins from the premise that video games have become significant cultural objects and that more libraries, archives, and museums are collecting them. Unlike books, music, or films, games are shaped by fast-moving platforms, backward-compatibility problems, ports, remakes, re-releases, downloadable content, achievements, online modes, alternate endings, user-created modifications, and platform-specific versions. These relationships affect whether users can identify the game they want, understand what version they have, distinguish similar or related titles, or reconstruct the cultural and technical history of a game.

The authors review how several established standards and models handle relationships: Dublin Core, FRBR, RDA, CIDOC CRM, and Cataloging Cultural Objects. They find that each can express some relevant relationships, such as whole/part, version, derivative, accompanying, sequential, collection, series, influence, or related-work links. Yet the mappings are often too broad, too tied to physical artifacts or events, or too book- and museum-centered to describe games at the right level of abstraction. For instance, broad relationships such as "relation" or "related works" can technically link games, but they lose the distinction between a port, remake, reboot, prequel, sequel, side story, spinoff, crossover, spiritual successor, inspiration, and expansion.

The method combines three approaches: reviewing and harvesting relationship classes from metadata standards and game-related resources; consulting video game experts and enthusiasts from SIMM and local game companies; and examining sample game sets selected for varied genres, platforms, creation dates, and distribution methods. The sample sets included complicated families such as Shin Megami Tensei: Persona, Disgaea, Final Fantasy, Kingdom Hearts, Bejeweled, Civilization V, Pokemon, and Super Mario Bros., plus their related games and additional content.

The article's core contribution is a set of four grouping entities and 11 relationship types. The grouping entities are Series, Franchise, Universe, and Collection. A Series groups related games through numbering, continuing narrative, gameplay, themes, characters, settings, or timelines. A Franchise names intellectual property, related data, and shared content across cultural objects. A Universe is a broader intellectual or creative domain made from recurring ideas, themes, settings, archetypes, and related works, sometimes spanning beyond formal franchise boundaries. A Collection is an anthology of local releases grouped and published together.

The 11 relationship types are isPortOf, isRemakeOf, isRebootOf, isPrequelOf, isSequelOf, isSidestoryOf, isSpinoffOf, isCrossoverOf, isSpiritualSuccessorOf, isInspiredBy, and complements. These definitions matter because they separate relationships that may look similar in ordinary language but have different discovery, access, and preservation implications. A port converts a game to another platform; a remake re-releases an original game with enhanced graphics, stories, or gameplay; a reboot restarts a series or franchise while disregarding an earlier storyline; a prequel or sequel depends on narrative sequence; a side story gives an orthogonal or parallel perspective; a spinoff uses characters or archetypes; a crossover brings together entities from multiple games, series, franchises, or universes; a spiritual successor resembles a previous game as a separate intellectual property; inspiration captures broader artistic or commercial influence; and complements describes additional content that modifies, replaces, or adds gameplay features for a local release.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is important infrastructure literature rather than a program evaluation. It helps explain why library game collections need richer discovery systems than title, platform, publisher, and broad genre alone. Patrons may ask for the right version of a game, games connected to a universe, a sequel order, a game inspired by another game, a remake rather than a port, or additional content needed for play. Researchers and preservationists may need to reconstruct how game works, editions, local releases, platforms, expansions, and related media fit together. The article therefore supports GBLS claims about games as cultural media that require user-centered cataloging and relationship metadata.

The article's limitations are also clear. It is conceptual and metadata-focused, not a study of library users in a circulating collection, and it does not evaluate implementation in a public, school, or academic catalog. Still, its value for the review is strong because it gives specific terms and relationship distinctions that can inform cataloging, advisory, preservation, and cross-collection discovery for library game services.

Suggested review placement: Add to `# Major Areas of Game-Based Library Service` > `## Cataloging, Metadata, and Discovery`, immediately after the Lee, Cho, Fox, and Perti (2013) paragraph. Proposed text: "Lee, Clarke, Sacchi, and Jett (2014) extend the GAMER Group's user-centered metadata work by defining relationship metadata for games and interactive media. Their four grouping entities, Series, Franchise, Universe, and Collection, and 11 relationship types, including port, remake, reboot, prequel, sequel, side story, spinoff, crossover, spiritual successor, inspiration, and complements, show why library game discovery cannot rely on broad 'related work' links alone; patrons, researchers, and preservationists need to distinguish versions, platforms, narrative order, intellectual-property families, additional content, and influence relationships."
