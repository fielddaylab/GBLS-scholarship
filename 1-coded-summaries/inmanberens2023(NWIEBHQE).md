### Summary
# **Inman Berens, K., & Noorda, R. (2023). *Gen Z and millennials: How they use public libraries and identify through media use*. American Library Association.**

This report examines how Gen Z and millennials use public libraries and how library use intersects with broader media identities and practices. The PDF identifies Kathi Inman Berens and Rachel Noorda as the researchers and reports findings from two datasets: a March 2022 nationally representative Qualtrics survey of 2,075 U.S. Gen Z and millennial respondents aged 13 to 40, with quotas for race, gender, and region, and ethnographic observation at two Cuyahoga County Public Library branches. The report is written for library and publishing stakeholders, so its vocabulary is oriented around public-library relevance, digital collections, media use, identity, and the attention economy.

The headline finding is that younger adults and teens continue to use physical public libraries, but not always in book-centered ways. The report finds that 54% of Gen Z and millennials visited a physical library in the prior twelve months. It also emphasizes that 23% visited a physical library while not identifying as readers, and that 54% of those who did not identify as readers nevertheless visited the library. This is important because it reframes the branch library as a public space for safe, free hanging out, socializing, Wi-Fi, advice, makerspaces, media production, coding clubs, storytimes, job help, and browsing, not only as a place for self-identified readers.

The ethnographic observations make this point concrete. In teen lounge spaces, young patrons came to vibe, craft, game, check phones, chat, listen to music, show work to librarians or mentors, and move among media. Books lined the walls and remained available, but the social space was not organized around mandatory reading or school-like behavior. The report treats these lounges as spaces for relaxation, mental and social well-being, and learning untethered from formal academic mandates. For GBLS, that language matters: game-based services can function as invitations into the library's social and cultural commons rather than as detachable entertainment events.

The report also updates the cross-media argument from Noorda and Inman Berens's earlier work. Gen Z and millennials are described as media omnivores who discover stories across friends and family, talent and influencers, social media, streaming television and movies, online stores, physical bookstores, and public-library shelves. They also identify through overlapping media categories: 57% identify as readers, 53% as gamers, and 52% as fans. Among avid crossmedia discoverers, those identities become even stronger and more multiple. The report notes that nearly one-third of Gen Z gamers play video games as a way to talk with friends, and that fan identity is strongly tied to community and meeting others like oneself.

This has direct implications for game-based library services. Gaming is not positioned as a competitor to reading; instead, avid consumers are often avid across categories. Manga, graphic novels, webcomics, fanfiction, livestreaming, games, podcasts, social platforms, subscription services, streaming adaptations, and books form a connected media field. The report's finding that 59% would choose a graphic or manga version of a story over a text-only version, with even higher shares among Black and Latinx respondents, also supports treating visual and interactive formats as legitimate routes into story, identity, and community.

Digital collection findings add an access and equity dimension. The report finds that 52% of Gen Z and millennial physical library patrons borrowed from library digital collections, while many non-users did not understand how access worked. It also argues that long waits disadvantage BIPOC patrons because Black and Latinx physical library patrons use digital collections at higher rates than the general survey population, and 75% of respondents considered a wait of one week or less to be long. Although this portion is about ebooks and audiobooks rather than games, it is relevant to GBLS because game-related media ecosystems increasingly include subscription platforms, app stores, webcomics, serialized fiction apps, Audible exclusives, and other digital formats that libraries may not be able to license or expose easily.

For the larger GBLS project, the report supports a broad service model in which games are part of public-library media culture, youth and young-adult identity work, and branch-based social infrastructure. It suggests that library game collections and programs should be connected to manga, graphic novels, fandom, streaming adaptations, makerspaces, teen lounges, digital borrowing, social media, displays, and advisory work. It also cautions that younger patrons may not automatically understand what libraries offer, so GBLS should help communicate libraries' unique qualities: free access, fun, privacy, green reuse, and values-based public benefit.
