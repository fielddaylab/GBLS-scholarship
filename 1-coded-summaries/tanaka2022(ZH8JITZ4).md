### Summary
# **Tanaka, C. (2022). Indigenous video games in libraries. *Pathfinder: A Canadian Journal for Information Science Students and Early Career Professionals, 3*(1), 14-29. https://doi.org/10.29173/pathfinder57**

Tanaka argues that Indigenous video games belong in library collections and spaces as part of decolonizing library practice, digital literacy, cultural media access, and support for Indigenous creative production. The article begins from Indigenous Futurisms, defined through Grace Dillon as Indigenous perspectives in the context of technology, and connects that movement to video games as a rapidly growing form of new media. Tanaka's central claim is that libraries often overlook video games when thinking about Indigenous representation, even though games can share Indigenous Knowledges and traditions, support literacy and creativity, and make Indigenous-created media more accessible to patrons.

The first major section critiques historical representation of Indigenous Peoples in games. Tanaka discusses *The Oregon Trail*, *Europa Universalis*, and *Civilization* as examples of settler-colonial mechanics in which land, animals, materials, and Indigenous presence are rendered through extraction, conquest, erasure, or the figure of the hostile or helpful Other. In contrast, Indigenous-created and collaboratively developed games such as Elizabeth LaPensee's *When Rivers Were Trails*, *Never Alone*, and Maize Longboat's *Terra Nova* are presented as different models of game design. These games center Indigenous characters, Indigenous languages, cultural practices, relationality, community, and alternate forms of placemaking rather than progress through exploitation and ownership.

Tanaka then frames Indigenous video games as educational media. Video games are interactive, strategic, and agency-based: players make choices, solve problems, move through systems, and learn from consequences. In *When Rivers Were Trails*, for example, players encounter Ojibwe words, Traditional foods and medicines, emotional and spiritual wellbeing, General Allotment Act history, and Indigenous Ways of Being. Tanaka also discusses workshops by Indigenous creators such as Skawennati Fragnito and the Aboriginal Territories in Cyberspace project, where game creation becomes a form of storytelling, language work, self-representation, and technical learning. The article therefore treats gaming not only as consumption but also as a pathway into coding, storyboarding, design, art, project management, and digital authorship.

The article's most direct GBLS contribution is its list of library service recommendations. Tanaka suggests that libraries increase accessibility by setting up computer or console stations with Indigenous games installed; pursue community funding and partnerships with software and gaming companies; invite Indigenous Elders or cultural teachers to contextualize the knowledges represented in games; use cooperative play and live walkthroughs as discussion-based programming; host Indigenous gaming nights with Indigenous creators and industry professionals; offer gaming workshops for Indigenous Peoples interested in game creation; provide physical and online space for Indigenous creators; and raise professional awareness of Indigenous video games through conferences, workshops, and staff learning.

For the larger GBLS project, Tanaka gives one of the clearest examples of why games should be treated as cultural media, not just recreation or instructional novelty. The article links collection development, access infrastructure, programming, creator support, advisory, and staff education to questions of Indigenous sovereignty, representation, decolonization, and whose histories are archived or preserved. It also widens the GBLS evidence base by reminding libraries that digital-download indie games may not fit ordinary circulation models, so access may require installed stations, Steam accounts, programming, local partnerships, and deliberate promotion rather than simple acquisition.

The source is primarily conceptual and practical rather than empirical. It does not measure learning outcomes or patron use, and Tanaka explicitly situates the article as a starting point for larger discussion rather than a comprehensive Indigenous-authored framework. Its value for the review is therefore normative, critical, and service-oriented: it helps define what inclusive game collection practice should attend to, especially when games involve Indigenous Knowledges, Indigenous creators, and culturally specific forms of representation.
