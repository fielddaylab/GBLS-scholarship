### Summary
# **Farkas, B., Shang, Y., & Alhourani, F. (2022). Teaching tip: Teaching business process concepts in an introductory information systems class: A multi-level game-based learning approach. *Journal of Information Systems Education, 33*(4), 306-323.**

Farkas, Shang, and Alhourani describe a multi-level game-based learning approach for teaching business process concepts in an introductory information systems course. The article is grounded in information systems and business education rather than library service. Its learning goals are for students to define business process concepts, explain a business process to stakeholders, and describe the role of information systems, especially ERP systems, in business processes. The authors argue that conventional lecture, textbook, quiz, and LMS simulation approaches are too abstract and too isolated from the organizational contexts in which information systems operate.

The teaching method uses a sequence of three increasingly complex game-based exercises. Students first inspect bags of M&M candies to learn basic business process modeling concepts such as start, end, activity, and gateway. They then complete a role-based card-sorting and product-manufacturing activity to learn pools, lanes, roles, responsibility, extensibility, reuse, and cross-role dependencies. Finally, student teams operate a simulated company using an SAP ERP simulation, then diagram the underlying business process and the role of the information system in that process. The pedagogy is intentionally scaffolded: early games use familiar everyday activities and stronger instructor guidance, while later exercises increase complexity and reduce guidance.

The article provides some evidence of perceived effectiveness. Across two classes totaling 86 students, 63 students responded to an eleven-question seven-point Likert survey. The authors also discuss end-of-semester comments and their own observations. Students reported that the activities were fun, collaborative, hands-on, and helpful for understanding business process diagrams. The authors found moderate to strong correlations among several survey items about learning and studying, but they also acknowledge class-level differences, self-selection bias, possible delivery differences, pandemic-related disruption, and the absence of controls or comparison with other pedagogical methods.

For GBLS, this source is best treated as excluded rather than incorporated into the review. It does use game-based learning, scaffolding, role play, simulation, collaboration, and reflection, all of which are relevant to educational game design in a broad sense. However, the article is not about libraries, library instruction, library users, library collections, cultural heritage, game collections, information literacy, public programming, school libraries, public libraries, or academic library services. Its core domain is business process modeling in a business-school information systems course.

Because the GBLS review already includes library-specific and near-library escape room, instruction, VR, TTRPG, metadata, collection, and programming sources, adding Farkas et al. would dilute the scope without adding necessary library evidence. The article may remain useful as a background teaching-design reference if the project later needs a broad appendix on game-based learning pedagogy, but it does not warrant placement in the main GBLS literature review.
