### Summary
# **Forsythe, C. (2021). Game on! Reflections of a board game librarian: An examination and application of non-traditional collections and recreational play in the academic library. *Emerging Library & Information Perspectives, 4*, 143-156. https://doi.org/10.5206/elip.v4i1.13442**

Forsythe offers a practitioner reflection on creating and managing a board game collection and program in a community college academic library. The article is framed through the broader rise of non-traditional library collections: items such as tools, seed libraries, craft kits, technology kits, musical instruments, puzzles, and board games. Forsythe argues that these collections can attract new patrons, support library goals, promote social inclusivity and equity, increase comfort in the library, support programming, and create opportunities for community partnerships. Board games fit this logic because they provide shared, low-cost access to materials that can be expensive to own and difficult to store individually.

The article's local case comes from an academic library that already lent or supported several non-traditional resources, including video games, consoles, calculators, media equipment, 3D printing, and laser cutting. The library had also become a learning commons after merging with a learning centre, making collaborative learning, social exchange, and out-of-classroom learning central to its identity. Forsythe, working as a part-time reference specialist and unofficial "board game librarian," applied prior research on tabletop role-playing game collections to a board game collection of more than 25 games managed over nearly two years.

Forsythe organizes the case around practical collection and program decisions. The needs assessment began with alignment to institutional values: improving students' social and technical skills, providing a friendly and safe environment, attracting non-library users, and supporting collaborative learning. The library also considered its student demographics, especially a large international student population that was more likely to engage with programming than domestic students. This shaped selection because language-independent games such as *Tsuro* could reduce linguistic barriers and support inclusive participation.

Collection development depended on budget, selection criteria, and community knowledge. Games were purchased incrementally, three or four at a time, and recommendations were drawn from ALA GameRT, BoardGameGeek, and the League of Librarian Gamers. Forsythe used BoardGameGeek data to guide selection, especially number of players, user ratings, playing time, and weight or complexity. The local goals favored games for two or more players, games playable in 45 minutes or less, and lighter-weight games that students could learn during programming. The collection was then refined through observation and a short feedback survey about students' favorite games, enjoyment, willingness to return, and suggestions.

The circulation and preservation details are particularly useful for GBLS implementation. The collection was non-circulating, stored in a staff-only area, and used in-library by request or during programming. Drawing on Robson et al. (2018), Forsythe used clear book tape to protect boxes and rulebooks, component inventories under box lids, labeled bags for pieces, and RFID tags to keep games from leaving the library. Students could sign out games for in-library use with a manual tracking form and student ID. These details show how even a small tabletop collection needs policies, storage, preservation routines, inventories, and use tracking.

Programming made the collection visible and socially meaningful. The original plan was a monthly two-hour board game night, but student feedback pushed the program to three hours twice per month. Forsythe supplemented the library collection with personal games to fill gaps, test mechanics, and learn what students liked before purchasing. Marketing included posters, flyers, social media, word-of-mouth, and partnerships with the international centre and student association. Reported benefits included reducing library anxiety, turning attendees into library regulars, providing no-cost access to board games, and supporting language practice, global citizenship, problem solving, critical thinking, and social intelligence. Challenges included the need for volunteers, limited finances and marketing capacity, uneven attendance, and the interruption of in-person programming by COVID-19.

The article also addresses pandemic adaptation. Forsythe notes that library closures and restrictions reduced access to physical spaces and pushed libraries toward electronic resources and online programming, creating both access opportunities and barriers for patrons without reliable technology or broadband. The article highlights virtual board game programming at Valdosta State University's Odum Library, including Discord-based community spaces, trivia, virtual escape rooms, Jackbox Games, and online board game platforms. Forsythe compares Board Game Arena and Tabletopia, noting differences in game access, synchronous/asynchronous play, rule support, learning curve, and cost.

For the GBLS project, Forsythe is valuable as a concrete academic-library service model rather than an outcome study. It connects non-traditional collections, tabletop game selection, in-library use, recurring programming, student belonging, international student engagement, collection maintenance, feedback loops, and virtual adaptation. It also reinforces that a "game collection" is not self-executing: staff need selection tools, preservation practices, storage decisions, policies, partners, marketing, volunteers, and facilitation capacity. The evidence is reflective and local, but the implementation vocabulary is directly useful for libraries building or assessing tabletop game services.
