### Summary
# **Litsey, R., Harris, R., & London, J. (2018). Ctrl "C"-Ctrl "V"; using gaming peripherals to improve library workflows and enhance staff efficiency. *Journal of Access Services, 15*(1), 34-43. https://doi.org/10.1080/15367967.2017.1420481**

Litsey, Harris, and London discuss how gaming peripherals can be repurposed to reduce repetitive stress and improve staff efficiency in library workflows. The article is library-based and practical, but its focus is internal access-services operations rather than game-based patron services. The authors' starting point is that many library tasks, especially cataloging, circulation, document delivery, and interlibrary loan, require repeated mouse and keyboard actions that can create physical strain, psychological fatigue, and workflow inefficiency.

The article connects repetitive stress/strain injury (RSI), ergonomics, and Csikszentmihalyi's concept of flow. The authors argue that library ergonomics literature often focuses on tissue damage and individual physical conditioning, while paying less attention to psychosocial conditions. They suggest that gaming peripherals can help address all three parts of this RSI problem: reducing repeated movements, allowing customization to individual preferences, and helping staff stay in a more fluent work state through clearer goals, fewer interruptions, and greater control over repetitive tasks.

The practical case comes from Texas Tech University Libraries' Document Delivery department, which processed more than 100,000 requests in a year. The department incorporated gaming mice and gaming keypads, including Logitech G300s, G600, and G13 devices, into borrowing workflows. Staff mapped common commands such as copy, paste, select all, enter, and refresh to customizable mouse or keypad buttons. They also increased mouse DPI to reduce arm movement and improve pointer responsiveness. The aim was to reduce movement between keyboard and mouse, reduce repetitive clicking and keystrokes, improve speed and accuracy, and increase staff comfort.

To assess the change, the authors compared article-request turnaround times from two busy six-month periods: June 1 to December 1, 2015, before implementation, and June 1 to December 1, 2016, after implementation. They drew random samples of 800 article requests from each period because article requests were more labor-intensive than loan requests. Average turnaround time declined from 1.77 days to 1.68 days, a difference of about 2.1 hours. The authors interpret this as evidence that gaming peripherals improved borrowing workflow speed while also reducing repetitive strain.

The article's claims are modest and practice-oriented. It does not present a controlled study, does not isolate gaming peripherals from other possible workflow changes, and does not measure staff injury rates, perceived comfort, job satisfaction, accuracy, or flow directly. Its value lies in naming a practical intervention: technologies designed for gamers can sometimes be useful in library staff work because both contexts require rapid, repeated, precise computer actions.

For the larger GBLS project, this article is only tangential. It shows that libraries may borrow tools and design ideas from gaming cultures, and it adds an operations-adjacent reminder that staff ergonomics, customization, workflow burden, and sustainability matter whenever libraries adopt technology-intensive services. However, the article does not study games as collections, programs, instruction, community engagement, cultural media, or patron-facing services. It is therefore not evidence for game-based library services in the review's sense and should remain outside the main review.
