### Summary
# **Patino, D. M., Summerlin, E., Benn, S., Roldan, W., Joya, A., Dahya, N., Luke, J., Lee, J. H., & Yip, J. (2021). *Virtual Reality Art & Design Curriculum*. University of Washington Information School Digital Youth Lab. https://ischool.uw.edu/echo-glen-curriculum**

Patino, Summerlin, Benn, Roldan, Joya, Dahya, Luke, Lee, and Yip present a six-module virtual reality curriculum developed through the University of Washington Information School Digital Youth Lab for incarcerated young people living and studying in a juvenile rehabilitation center in Washington State. The curriculum was implemented in 2018-2019 in the school library at Echo Glen and was created with support from IMLS, Washington State Library, King County Library System, and Oculus. Although the item is a curriculum rather than a conventional research article, it is highly relevant to game-based library services because it documents a library-based, youth-centered model for moving VR from one-time technology exposure toward art making, world-building, reflection, and public exhibition.

The curriculum's stated goal is to teach young people about VR technology and digital art making while inviting them to explore art creation across 2D and 3D media. The six instructional days move from introduction to VR, to world-making, future building, VR and music, art, and VR exploration. The program then adds two modules focused on program and exhibit development, in which youth-created art and reflections were prepared for display in a local public library. That exhibit component matters for GBLS because it turns immersive play and creation into a public-facing cultural production process: youth participants were not only users of equipment but makers whose work could enter a civic library space.

The setting shaped the design. The authors explain that the curriculum was created for a highly restrictive environment where students' personal histories, crimes, and trauma could not be solicited or made central. Instead of framing the program around personal storytelling or empowerment, the team framed it around VR concept art design, creative world-making, and future building. The report repeatedly emphasizes an anti-oppressive framework: accepting the reality of structural power, centering intersectionality, asking who benefits from the work, seeking community feedback, reflecting on researcher and educator positionality, and revisiting ethical questions at every stage. These principles are translated into concrete facilitation choices, such as trauma-aware selection of VR experiences, purposeful outreach to girls and young women, attention to racial and gender dynamics, and a room layout that avoided placing headset users with their backs to peers.

The school library setup is one of the curriculum's most transferable pieces. Four VR systems were arranged so the participant in the headset faced out toward the room while the computer screen displayed the experience for spectators. Small groups sat at activity tables where peers could watch, discuss, draw, plan, and prepare for turns in VR. The curriculum therefore treats VR as social even when only one person wears a headset. Spectating, informal conversation, free play, table-based art making, and near-peer mentorship are part of the program design rather than accidental side effects. The modules also foreground rotation, short activities, safety, consent for touch, and the need for facilitators to model headset use before helping participants.

The curriculum's content is especially useful for libraries because it combines commercial VR applications with guided creative and reflective activities. Participants used or observed experiences such as Oculus First Contact, Henry, Google Cardboard stories, Tiny Town VR, Tilt Brush, Sculpt VR, Fantasynth, Beat Saber, Job Simulator, and Fantastic Contraption. Yet the point was not simply access to new technology. Each VR experience was paired with discussion, drawing, world-making, design prompts, or reflection. Future-building activities asked students to imagine libraries in the year 3000; art modules asked them to compare 2D, 3D, and VR creation; music modules invited reflection on how audio shapes game experience; and open exploration days gave participants choice while still connecting work back to the final exhibit.

The public-library exhibit, *Here But Not Here*, extends the curriculum into co-curation. Museology graduate students prepared with readings on virtual reality as an interpretive tool, programming with incarcerated populations, and DIY community-based exhibit development. Onsite sessions asked youth to answer questions such as what VR meant to them and why VR mattered to them. Activities included choosing words for feelings about VR, creating collages, customizing cardboard VR goggles, making Tilt Brush art, and telling stories about their relationship to VR. The final installation at Snoqualmie Library used youth words and artifacts, invited public conversation, and centered the theme that VR is real because students experienced unreal actions in ways that felt real.

For the larger GBLS project, this curriculum is strongest as a program-design and equity source. It does not provide a formal outcome study, and the authors note that research data collection in the juvenile rehabilitation setting was limited by consent barriers and other constraints. Still, it gives unusually rich detail about how a library-linked VR program can be built around anti-oppressive design, trauma-aware facilitation, youth agency, art making, social-emotional and cultural learning, and public cultural participation. It also connects several GBLS concerns that are often separated: technology access, game-like VR experiences, creative production, exhibition, school-library space, public-library partnership, and service to marginalized youth.

This source should be used carefully because it is a curriculum document and report, not peer-reviewed outcome evidence. Its value is in its specificity. For libraries considering VR, it shows that meaningful immersive programming requires more than hardware, software, and safety waivers. The design must answer who the program is for, what kinds of participation are safe and meaningful, how spectators and non-VR activities are included, how youth choice is protected, how artifacts are gathered and interpreted, and how public-library display can amplify participant voices without exploiting them.
