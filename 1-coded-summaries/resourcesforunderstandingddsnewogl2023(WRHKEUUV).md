# **Resources for understanding D&D's new OGL. (2023). *Google Docs*. https://docs.google.com/document/u/1/d/1afPW1kl9ygogfvnqDrfEC3LoTdVhr2vu5GdIsrH9yhU/edit?usp=drive_web&ouid=104872546336741826329&usp=embed_facebook**

This item is a one-page resource document about the controversy surrounding Wizards of the Coast's proposed changes to the Dungeons & Dragons Open Gaming License. The PDF describes itself as "a resource doc compiling a variety of resources to understand what's going on with Dungeon & Dragons Open Gaming License." Its content is organized as a list of external materials rather than as an essay, research article, program report, or practitioner guide. It includes headings for articles, forum discussions, news reports, podcasts, videos, and miscellaneous resources, with examples such as the Electronic Frontier Foundation, Gizmodo, the original WotC OGL, WotC's response to the leaked update, a Medium article by Noah Downs, a Reddit AMA by a third-party creator, Dicebreaker, Ars Technica, a Foundry Virtual Tabletop Reddit thread, videos on Ryan Dancey and the OGL, and the #OpenDND petition.

The document is relevant to the broader tabletop role-playing game ecosystem because the OGL debate shaped creator, publisher, and player anxieties about D&D's legal and economic infrastructure. For libraries that collect, program around, or teach with TTRPGs, the issue could matter indirectly: public libraries may need to understand why patrons, local creators, or game masters talk about open licensing, third-party publishing, digital platforms, and corporate control of D&D. It may also have indirect relevance for collection development if libraries support independent RPG publishing, local creator communities, zine culture, or tabletop game design workshops.

However, this PDF does not provide library-specific evidence. It does not discuss libraries, librarians, public-library programming, school or academic library services, D&D clubs, TTRPG facilitation, game collections, acquisitions, cataloging, intellectual freedom policies, assessment, patron outcomes, or community impact. It does not offer an argument about how the OGL controversy should affect library collections or services, nor does it document a library response to the controversy. Its value is as a current-awareness link roundup for people trying to understand a specific moment in D&D licensing history.

For the GBLS literature review, this item is therefore best retained as explicitly excluded literature. The review already has stronger sources for library TTRPG programming, including public-library D&D case studies, teen RPG guidance, LARP programming, and RPG collection/cataloging sources. If the project later develops a specialized subsection on intellectual property, licensing, open gaming, creator communities, or RPG collection policy, this resource list might serve as a pointer to primary and journalistic sources. In the present review, though, citing the resource list itself would add little evidence and could blur the distinction between library-service literature and general hobby-industry current awareness.
