### Summary
# **Lee, J. H., Clarke, R. I., Cho, H., & Windleharth, T. (2017). Understanding appeals of video games for readers' advisory and recommendation. *Reference & User Services Quarterly, 57*(2), 127-139.**

Lee, Clarke, Cho, and Windleharth address a central service problem for libraries that collect video games: games may be popular and increasingly present in library collections, but they are rarely integrated into readers' advisory or recommendation work. The article adapts the concept of "appeals" from readers' advisory and media studies to video games. Rather than asking only what a game is about, the authors ask what draws players in and what kinds of emotional, intellectual, sensory, social, and experiential responses games create. This makes the article directly relevant to GBLS because it turns game collections into advisory objects, not just circulating inventory.

The study draws on a large-scale survey about video-game-playing experience, information seeking, and game organization behavior. Survey invitations were distributed through game-related physical and online venues, university lists, forums, social media, and the Seattle Interactive Media Museum. A total of 2,163 people participated, and 1,257 completed the survey. For this article, the authors focus on responses about favorite games, reasons those games appealed to respondents, favorite genres, and reasons those genres were appealing. Open-ended responses were coded through an iterative consensus process, and genre/appeal relationships were examined quantitatively.

The authors define appeals as elements of media that draw people in by evoking particular emotional and intellectual experiences. They identify 16 video game appeals: narrative, challenge, sensation, fellowship, nostalgia, fantasy, exploration, mood, depth, creativity/innovation, expression, accomplishment, competition, submission, mastery, and learning. Narrative was the most frequently identified appeal, followed by challenge and sensation. Importantly, the appeals are not simply genre labels. They describe why a game matters to a player: story, characters, atmosphere, difficulty, social play, nostalgia, relaxation, self-expression, skill development, or other experiential qualities.

The discussion of individual appeals is particularly useful for library service. Narrative appeal includes story, character, lore, and the integration of story with gameplay. Challenge can be physical, intellectual, strategic, moral, or difficulty-based, and it depends on how much challenge a player wants at a given moment. Sensation includes visual, auditory, and physical stimulation, including music, art style, rhythm, and embodied interaction. Fellowship includes co-play, online play, local multiplayer, spectating, taking turns, and playing with family or friends. Mood and nostalgia show that games can be selected for emotional fit, memory, comfort, excitement, humor, horror, sadness, or escape, much as readers' advisory already attends to tone and mood in fiction.

Other appeals expand conventional library language for games. Exploration and depth describe player agency, open worlds, choices, consequences, complex systems, and the opportunity to discover places and mechanics. Expression and creativity/innovation describe customization, sandbox play, building, creation, and appreciation of unusual or experimental design. Accomplishment, learning, and mastery distinguish different goal-based motivations: earning status or completion, learning content or concepts, and practicing skills over time. Competition and submission further show that users may seek either direct comparison with others or opportunities to unwind and disengage.

The article also demonstrates why appeals should complement genre. Genre remains useful, but the survey showed that genres contain different appeal profiles and that appeal relationships can cut across genres. For example, narrative was especially important for role-playing games, exploration for action/adventure games, physical interaction for music/dance games, and challenge for strategy games. Puzzle and simulation games both had strong submission appeal despite seeming unlike each other. These patterns suggest that appeals can help librarians recommend across genre boundaries: a patron who likes exploration, fellowship, or mood may be open to games that look very different on the shelf.

For the GBLS project, this source is one of the strongest foundations for game advisory. It supports collection description, catalog enhancement, discovery, display design, cross-media recommendation, and staff confidence in talking about games. It also connects games to the broader movement toward whole-collection readers' advisory, where books, audiobooks, films, music, television, games, and other cultural objects are recommended through shared experience language. Appeals can become metadata, catalog facets, staff vocabulary, interview prompts, display themes, or local tags. This is especially important because patrons may not know game genres, platform histories, or mechanics vocabulary, but they can often describe what they want to feel or experience.

The study has limits. Its survey sample came from game-interested venues and is not necessarily representative of all public-library users. The appeals were derived from self-report, and the article proposes rather than tests advisory workflows. Even so, it gives GBLS a clear language for integrating games into readers' advisory and recommendation systems. It also pairs well with sources on cross-media engagement and game metadata: games need not be isolated as a special hobby collection if libraries can describe their experiential qualities in user-centered terms.
