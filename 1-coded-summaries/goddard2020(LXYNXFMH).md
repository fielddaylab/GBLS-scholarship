# **Goddard, J. (2020). Public libraries respond to the COVID-19 pandemic, creating a new service model. *Information Technology and Libraries, 39*(4), 1-4. https://doi.org/10.6017/ital.v39i4.12847**

Goddard's short editorial-board essay describes how public libraries responded to the COVID-19 pandemic by re-writing service rules and creating what the author calls a new service model. The article is not a study of games, play, or game-based programming. Instead, it surveys public-library adaptations during shutdown and reopening: electronic resources, virtual community engagement, real-time reference, contactless curbside service, broadband support, online learning resources, and advocacy for emergency library funding.

The opening claim is that public libraries demonstrated community value during the pandemic by helping patrons resume ordinary life, learn, grow, and stay entertained through resources available by library card. Goddard emphasizes that librarians maintained this level of service by improvising under crisis conditions. Once buildings closed, libraries promoted ebooks and other digital platforms. OverDrive usage rose sharply, new users increased, young adult and juvenile ebook readership grew, and services such as Brainfuse, Miss Humblebee's Academy, Gale Courses, and Gale Presents: Udemy saw increased use as children, families, and adults sought school support and skill development.

The article's infrastructure section matters most for general library-service context. Goddard notes that public libraries supported patrons without sufficient broadband by leaving Wi-Fi available outside buildings, strengthening wireless signals, hosting drive-up Wi-Fi hotspot locations, partnering with schools, and lending hotspots. This digital-equity frame is relevant to GBLS only indirectly: many virtual, hybrid, streaming, Discord-based, or online-game services depend on the same connectivity, device access, and staff-support conditions. However, the article does not discuss game access, game collections, online game communities, or game-program design.

The community-engagement section describes a rapid shift to virtual programming through Facebook Live and Stories, Discord, Instagram, YouTube, Zoom, and GoToMeeting. San Jose Public Library, for example, moved story time and reading programs online, created Spring Into Reading, provided a weekly recorded phone story time, and hosted more than 2,000 virtual events after the March 2020 lockdown. Oceanside Library in New York moved quickly from closure to multiple daily online programs, including story times, book chats, and book clubs, and later opened programs to patrons from outside the state through library partnerships. Goddard writes that patrons were exercising, listening to concerts, taking virtual vacations, learning skills, cooking, playing games, and reducing stress through lockdown programming.

For the larger GBLS project, this source is useful as a reminder that library play and game services are embedded in broader service-model shifts, especially the movement toward hybrid, virtual, and contactless delivery. The mention of Discord and patrons "playing games" points toward possible pandemic-era game programming, but Goddard does not develop that point. There are no program descriptions, patron data, design recommendations, game examples, collection practices, facilitation methods, learning outcomes, or analysis of game-based service. The article is therefore too general to support a substantive GBLS claim.

The concluding section argues that the pandemic-era service model should be preserved as part of the "new normal." Goddard recommends that community programs remain both physical and virtual, physical media and books be delivered both at the desk and through contactless service, and reference continue both in person and through chat. For GBLS, this is a broad service-design backdrop rather than direct literature: future game-based library services may likewise need hybrid access models, digital-equity planning, and online facilitation capacity, but those implications must be drawn from other, more game-specific sources.

# Wyatt, D., & Leorke, D. (2024). Playing in the "third place": How games and play are transforming public libraries. *Space and Culture, 27*(2), 254-273. https://doi.org/10.1177/12063312231223118

Unique identifier: `wyattPlayingThirdPlace2024`; Zotero item key `EU8B37X2`; attachment key `DUYSLXMW`.

Wyatt and Leorke (2024) examine games and play as part of the spatial, temporal, and civic transformation of public libraries. Drawing on observations of library spaces and interviews with 27 library staff and managers in Australia, Finland, and Singapore, with additional examples from the United States and elsewhere, the article argues that play is not merely an added program or collection format. Games and play are reshaping library architecture, furnishings, daily rhythms, atmospheres, acoustics, boundaries, and relations to urban policy.

The article begins by challenging a simple "third place" account of libraries. Library studies often describe the public library as a place between home and work, a voluntary civic space for informal gathering, learning, and sociality. Wyatt and Leorke argue that this language remains useful but also obscures contemporary changes. Libraries are now connected to digital urbanism, creative-city policy, smart-city agendas, entrepreneurial citizenship, co-working, maker culture, digital literacy, and redevelopment projects. In that context, play can support humanistic public life, but it can also be enlisted for instrumental goals such as attracting users, producing flexible workers, supporting creative industries, and demonstrating civic innovation.

The historical section is especially useful for GBLS because it links contemporary gaming to older library debates. Games, toys, puzzles, billiards, children's rooms, and playful materials have appeared in libraries since the nineteenth century, often justified as ways to attract new users, educate youth, provide moral guidance, and offer controlled alternatives to more suspect public leisure. The authors show that contemporary objections to gaming, noise, disruption, frivolity, violence, and replacement of books are not new. They recur whenever libraries expand beyond quiet reading and print-centered seriousness.

The Finnish cases make the argument concrete. Helsinki's Oodi Central Library includes a large digital and analog game collection, a PC Gaming Room, console rooms, VR games, retro/accessible arcade spaces, tabletop rooms, role-playing clubs, and game-literacy sessions. These are not peripheral add-ons: they change how visitors move through the building, where spectators gather, how gameplay becomes visible, how rooms are scheduled, and when the library is busy. Other examples show gaming zones near shelves and makerspaces, dedicated Minecraft rooms, after-school gaming peaks, evening board-game programs, and extended opening hours tied to play.

Wyatt and Leorke also discuss children and teen zones, playful architecture, and library-city boundaries. Singaporean libraries include immersive children's rooms, maker spaces, toys, augmented-reality books, costumes, and interactive walls; Melbourne and Singapore examples include castles, tree houses, animal seating, storytelling areas, and playful learning environments. New library buildings use colorful interiors, reading nooks, responsive installations, playful facades, outdoor play areas, and transparent connections to plazas, parks, playgrounds, waterfronts, and cultural precincts. In this sense, play spills beyond designated programs and becomes part of how libraries perform publicness.

For the larger GBLS project, the article provides a needed conceptual correction. It helps move the review beyond program lists by showing that game-based library services are spatial and institutional. A game night, a gaming room, a VR station, a Minecraft club, a makerspace, or a children's play area changes sound, flow, surveillance, privacy, dwell time, staff explanation, public expectations, and the boundary between the library and the city. GBLS planning therefore has to consider architecture, acoustics, scheduling, furniture, spectatorship, children and teen zones, safety, public criticism, and policy narratives about innovation and creativity.

The article's limits are also clear. It is qualitative, interpretive, and focused largely on well-resourced public-library examples, especially in Finland, Australia, and Singapore. It does not measure program outcomes, attendance, learning, belonging, or patron satisfaction. Its value is not causal evidence but analytic framing: it shows why libraries' increasing investment in play is socially consequential and why the public library as "third place" is both strengthened and unsettled by games.
