## Abstract

This article examines undergraduate students’ attitudes towards search data privacyin academic libraries, with a focus on the experiences and perspectives of membersof underrepresented groups. Using the qualitative framework of interpretivedescription and constant comparative analysis, 27 undergraduate students at VirginiaCommonwealth University—an urban research institution in the southeasternregion of the United States—were interviewed. Most students were comfortablewith search data collection if it benefited them; however, a notable number of thosewho opposed it expressed strong concerns about potential harm to vulnerable orunderrepresented groups. These privacy concerns were primarily voiced by membersof marginalized groups, emphasizing the importance of considering studentperspectives, especially those who may be most vulnerable to adverse effects ofdata use as academic libraries increasingly employ learning analytics and individualleveldata. The purpose of this article is to amplify those voices
